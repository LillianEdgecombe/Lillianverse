Anytime After Ten
Manchester – Islington, 2023

The radiator in Sarah’s studio had gone lukewarm again. It ticked like an anxious clock. She lay curled on the futon with a blanket over her head, her sketchpad half-buried under an open coat, and the tip of her tail twitching with each passing minute.
Emotionally immature. Derivative. Decorative.
That's what they said.
As if she were sewing curtains.
She stared at her phone in the total darkness of her dorm room and lit it up with a thumb. 10:14 PM
Late evening.
She hesitated. Fumbled with the blanket, fidgeted with her pillow, turned herself on her left side.
Still she could feel those words biting her hips.
She tapped the first name in her quick call list.
It rang three times. She almost hung up.
“Hullo, Sarah?” The voice came sleepy, not annoyed.
“Hey,” she said, trying to keep her voice steady. “Sorry, Dad. I know it’s late.”
“It never counts as late for you,” Miles said gently. “You all right?” She didn’t answer right away. Her throat was tight. “Sarah?” her father repeated, a concerned tone in his voice.
"Are you busy?"
"Never too busy for you," Miles replied, his voice relaxing just a little. "What's wrong?"
“Can I talk to you for a bit?” she kept asking.
“You can always talk to me.”
She pulled the blanket tighter. “It was a crit day.”
“Oh dear.”
“Yeah. Didn't go well.”
“What happened?”
She told him. "Professor Goodwin looked at my paintings for maybe thirty seconds and said they were 'derivative' and 'emotionally immature.' Can you believe that? I'm pouring heart and soul into any project and... He said I was hiding behind technique instead of saying something meaningful. That I was... that my work was 'decorative.'" The way the professor had stared at her bronze piece as if it smelled funny. The way one of the other students had smirked when she tried to explain her process. “'Don’t make your work about being an anthro. Let it stand without that.'” She let those words sit in the silence between them.
Miles finally said, “That’s bloody stupid.”
“I know.”
“You don’t sound like you know,” he argued.
“I don’t know what I sound like. But they... they sting," she acknowledged. She pulled her blanket tighter. "I'm 26, I have two degrees in Art, and yet... they keep seeing me like a scared pup? Like a girly-girl who can't manage her own emotions?" She sighed, passing one paw over her eyes. "Maybe he's right. Maybe I'm not as good as I thought. Maybe I am a scared pup, still. I mean, a bad crit and I'm calling Dad."
He made a thoughtful little hum, the kind she used to hear while riding in the passenger seat of his old van, the one that always smelled like lavender oil and axle grease. Then: “You remember the first time I pitched to the company board?”
“I was like... ten?" Sarah did the math. "I was still living in the warehouse, you haven't even found me.”
“Yeah, but I told you about it later.”
“You brought them cookies," Sarah remembered. She turned on he belly, the phone put in speakerphone mode. "They were still warm.”
He chuckled. “They were bait.” Sarah sniffed, half laughing. “I stood outside the conference room,” he continued, “in a too-big suit, holding my handouts like a bloody magician about to pull a rabbit out of a spreadsheet. And I wanted to puke. I could hear them laughing about something inside. I kept thinking, 'They’re going to see me. They’re going to know I don’t belong.'” She didn’t interrupt him. She never did when he told stories. She loved hearing his voice, especially when everything around her was dark. She found it after they saved her from the warehouse, when she came to live with Miles and Lillian, when she started having her own bedroom. When the nights stopped being scary, when she stopped needing to survive and started enjoying a life, enjoying having a father who would tell you bedtime stories. “I nearly turned and walked out,” he said. “But then your Mum—you know what she did?”
Sarah closed her eyes. “No. What?”
“She was with me. She leaned in, looked me square in the eyes, and said, ‘They’ve never had someone like you in there before. That’s their problem, not yours.’” Sarah swallowed. Miles said softly, “You’ve never had someone like you in that room either. But you showed up.”
“She said my work didn’t have bones,” Sarah whispered, tears slowly crawling out of the corner of her eyes.
“Love, of course your work has bones. It has teeth. Like that sculpture you did last month."
"The one with those sharks? Those were real shark teeth, Dad." 
"That's what I'm saying, pupper," Miles replied. Sarah could see he grin at the phone with her mind's eye. "You have that something that the art world needs. Who else would have used real shark teeth in a sculpture about... What was that? Capitalism?”
“They think it’s all... fur and girl feelings.”
“And that’s not allowed? I mean, you have fur, are a girl and have feelings... so what? Can't you express them?”
She let out a laugh that sounded like a sigh. “I hate that I care so much what they think.”
“It only means you’re paying attention to them," Miles replied. "Doesn’t mean they’re right.”
A long pause passed. The hum of city outside the window. Someone yelling at a dog. The radiator giving up. Sarah asked, quietly: “Did you really think you didn’t belong back then?”
Miles’s voice softened. “Every single time I walked into a room with suits and ties and people who knew how to pronounce char-... chalc-... charcuterie. But then I remembered... I knew things they didn’t. About rhythm. About people. About engines. And your Mum reminded me, even if I forgot.”
“Who reminds you now?”
“You do,” he replied quickly. "Your work. Your stubborness. Your care. Sometimes I wonder whether you're really Mum's biological daughter, in some way."
She stifled a laughter. "I sometimes wonder, too."
Miles began speaking again. "Sometimes the thing that makes your work meaningful is exactly what makes certain people uncomfortable. And sometimes those people happen to be in positions of authority."
"But what if he's right?," Sarah argued, sitting on her futon, letting her blanket fall and spread over it. She felt the damp, cold air on her fur, a refreshing sensation. "What if I am hiding behind technique?"
"Are you?" her father asked abruptly.
Sarah considered this, fidgeting with a tuft of fur. "I don't think so."
"What do you do when you paint or sculpt something? Are you looking for just some snappy technique?"
"No, Dad. I... I do what the piece needs. Shark teeth, clay, whatever. Sometimes a painting needs to be baroque, other paintings need cubism. Sometimes I... need to bend some iron wire to generate a suffering figurine." She lifted her head, a sudden realization in her mind. "I know why I sculpt the way I sculpt. I know what I'm trying to say."
"Then trust that."
"It's hard when someone whose opinion is supposed to matter tells you your work doesn't mean anything." Sarah sighed, trying to look around at her room in the dark.
"Here's the thing about opinions—even expert ones: they're still just opinions. Not..." Miles paused. "Hold on, love." Sarah heard a faint click on his end of the line, followed by the soft sound of a door closing. Then Miles came back, his voice closer to the phone. "Mum just brought me a glass of water. She didn't say anything, just looked at me. Don't worry, she's not listening anymore. What I was-oh, yes. Expert opinions are just opinions, not facts about your work or your worth."
"Dad?"
"Yeah?"
"Do you think I'm good? At art, I mean. Do you think I have whatever it is that makes someone a real artist?"
The question was so vulnerable that Sarah felt Miles' throat tighten even through the phone.
"Sarah, I've watched you make art since you were sixteen," Miles began, his voice mellow yet firm, the voice of a proud father. "I've seen you lose yourself in a drawing for hours, get so absorbed you forget to eat. I've watched you see things other people miss, then find ways to show them to us." He paused. "Art was one of the thing that kept you alive in that warehouse. So yes, I think you're good. But more than that, I think you're necessary. Your art is necessary."
Sarah was quiet. When she spoke, her voice was steadier. "Thanks, Dad. I needed to hear that."
"Anytime."
"I should go. I have to sleep, so that I can... do more necessary art tomorrow."
"Sarah?"
"Yeah?"
"Don't let his opinion change how you work," Miles said, in one of his most serious tones. "The world has enough art trying to be what other people think it should be. It doesn't have enough art brave enough to be exactly what it is."
“Thanks,” she whispered.
“You don’t need to thank me.”
“I’m glad I called.”
“I’m glad you did too.”
They stayed on the line. No rush. Just the quiet breath between two sides of the same old thread. Miles eventually said, “Want me to send you a voice note for the morning?”
“Sure, Dad.”
“Right then. It’ll say: ‘Make something messy. Then make it honest.’ That sound good?”
Sarah smiled into the dark. “Perfect.”
