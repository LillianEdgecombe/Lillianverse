# Miles Ferguson — The Week the Store Almost Broke

## Chapter 1 — Monday, 06:12 — Open Store, Open Wounds

The key turned with the familiar resistance of a lock that had seen too many winters. Miles Ferguson braced one shoulder against the glass of the automatic doors, listening to the quiet, reluctant clunk as the mechanism woke up. The strip lights inside the FreshMart hummed to life row by row, pale rectangles of brightness blooming over empty aisles and pallets of shrink-wrapped possibilities.

He stood there for a moment, just inside the entrance, hand still resting on the key. Forty-two years old, German shepherd, fur already damp at the temples from the drizzle outside. His back carried the faint ache of a weekend that had been more about spreadsheets than rest. The air inside the store smelled of detergent, cardboard, and the last ghost of yesterday’s baked bread. No customers yet. No staff yet. Just him and the systems.

He loved this moment more than he would ever admit aloud. The store as machine, waiting to be started properly. Shelves as ribs, aisles as arteries, stock as blood he could direct. He could feel the day as a flowchart in his head: deliveries, breaks, promos, who needed support on tills, who needed shielding from today’s inevitable nonsense.

His phone buzzed. He glanced down: 06:13. A notification from Head Office about a “special live announcement” at ten. He filed it away against the grey wall in his mind where all urgent-but-vague corporate messages lived.

“Morning, boss.”

Miles turned. Kaia, the ferret, squeezed through the half-open staff door from the back corridor, dark eyes bright above her high-visibility vest. She pulled the zipper of her hoodie up to her chin and shivered.

“You’re in early,” Miles said.

“Bus was either twenty minutes early or forty minutes late,” she said. “Figured I’d pick the one that doesn’t get me written up.”

He smiled, small but genuine. “Come on. Let’s get the shutters up. I’ll put the kettle on.”

They moved together through the routine: security checks, tills counted, baking timers set. Miles listened to the clack of Kaia’s claws on the polished floor, the whirr of the industrial ovens waking, the low roar of compressors behind the dairy section. Underneath it all, his own pulse kept time.

On the way back from the bakery, he passed the automatic doors again. Outside, the sky over east London was turning from charcoal to the flat blue of a tired Monday. A bus trundled past with an advert he’d seen a hundred times: a grinning human family loaded with shopping bags, a slogan about “fresh starts” pasted over their faces.

He thought, briefly, of another kitchen across the city. Loughton. Warm light, mismatched mugs, Lillian humming as she stirred porridge, her broad back to the window, the curve of her shoulders outlined against the morning. The smell of tea with honey. The sound of her voice when she told him to sit down and eat before he tried to fix the world.

He’d left her sleeping this morning. She’d worked late at the academy, then come home and watched him scroll through rota spreadsheets instead of watching the film she’d put on. He’d kissed her forehead in the dark and whispered, “Sorry, Lil,” and she’d made a soft, noncommittal sound that hurt more than any sharp words might have.

The door chime pinged once as he tested the sensors, pulling his mind back to the store. He straightened his name badge, rolled his shoulders, and walked towards the staff room. The day was starting whether he was ready or not.

## Chapter 2 — “Optimise 2030” Arrives

They gathered in the staff room just before ten, forty-odd bodies in hi-vis and polo shirts crammed among chipped lockers and notices about hygiene and mental health. The kettle steamed continuously in the corner, churning out tea in mismatched mugs. Someone had opened a packet of cheap biscuits, already being picked through like a foraging ground.

On the wall opposite the sink, a flat-screen TV that usually played training videos flickered to life, the FreshMart logo rotating in nauseous teal. A trumpet fanfare jangled through tinny speakers.

Miles stood near the front, arms folded, back to the microwave. His presence calmed some of the older staff, he knew. He could feel their eyes on him when the room went tense.

The image resolved into the smiling face of a human man in a navy suit, tie the exact teal of the logo behind him. “Good morning, FreshMart family!” he boomed.

“Oh, here we go,” muttered Felix, the rat from nightshift, under his breath.

Miles shot him a warning glance, then returned his eyes to the screen. The man introduced himself as Regional Performance Director for “Greater Europe & Strategic Growth”. He spoke in the rounded vowels of someone who had never packed a crate in his life.

“We are entering an exciting new era,” the man said. “Our new initiative, Optimise 2030, is going to transform how we serve our communities. Flexible staffing. Data-driven scheduling. A leaner, smarter future.”

On the screen, an animation showed bottom bars shrinking on a graph while a line representing “profit” soared upwards like a righteous bird.

“What does that mean,” whispered Kaia, standing beside Miles, “when they say ‘flexible’?”

“Nothing good,” Miles murmured, not taking his eyes off the TV.

The man’s words rolled on. “Using advanced algorithms and real-time footfall analysis, we will be matching staff hours precisely to customer demand. No more waste. No more dead time. Every minute of payroll will count.”

Miles felt his stomach tighten. He could already see the implications, numbers arranging themselves in cruel patterns behind his eyes: cuts in the early mornings when older staff liked the quiet, slashed closing shifts that let people finish properly, split shifts that would wreck bus timetables and child-care plans.

The screen shifted to bullet points. “Optimise 2030: Key Benefits.” Less overtime. Centralised scheduling. Performance flags for “underutilised staff”.

“Anyone else feel like we’re the ones they’re optimising out?” Felix muttered louder this time.

Laughter, bitter and quick, rippled round the room. Miles raised a hand. “Let’s just listen,” he said. “We’ll talk after.”

The man wrapped up with a grin. “We know change can be challenging,” he said, as if discussing a new brand of cereal rather than people’s livelihoods. “But together, we will build a future where every FreshMart colleague is in the right place at the right time, delivering excellence.”

The video cut to the static slogan: OPTIMISE 2030 — THE FUTURE IS EFFICIENT.

The screen went black. For a moment, nobody moved.

Then Sina, a middle-aged human woman from checkout, spoke up. “Does that mean my Wednesday mornings are gone?” she asked, voice tight. “I take my grandson to speech therapy. I can’t just… be ‘flexible’.”

Miles swallowed. “We don’t have the full details yet,” he said. “But—”

“We know what it means,” said Ajay, one of the shelf stackers, softly. “They’re going to cut.”

The room buzzed with anxious questions. Miles let the noise swell for a few seconds, then stepped forward.

“All right,” he said, voice firm enough to cut through the murmur. “Here’s what we’re going to do. I’m going to get the full schedule projections from Head Office before the end of the day. I want each of you to write down any times you absolutely can’t work — non-negotiable stuff like medical appointments, school runs, care responsibilities. Put it on paper, hand it to me. We’ll build something together before we let an algorithm do it to us.”

Kaia looked up at him, eyes wide. “Can you even do that?”

“We’ll see,” Miles said. “But they hired me to run this store. Not that bloke on the telly.”

He turned the TV off. The staff drifted back to their posts, carrying their fear with their tea. Miles stayed behind for a moment, staring at the dark screen, his own reflection faint over the ghost of the logo.

He could already hear Lillian’s voice in his head, dry and soft: You’re going to try and carry this whole thing yourself, aren’t you?

He rinsed his mug, set it down carefully, and went back to the floor.

## Chapter 3 — The First Cut

The email arrived at sixteen eleven: a polite bullet-shaped wound in Miles’s inbox.

SUBJECT: OPTIMISE 2030 — PHASE 1 ROSTER ADJUSTMENTS

He opened it standing by the office window, the hum of the shopfloor a distant tide on the other side of the glass. The document was a spreadsheet attachment, colour-coded in cheerful greens and oranges. At the top, a note: “These adjustments are informed by our predictive staffing model. Please implement by next Monday.”

He scrolled. Names, hours, red cells where lines had been crossed through. A column marked “UNDER-UTILISED” with percentages beside certain staff.

He saw Kaia’s name three lines down. Her weekly hours, already tight, sliced from thirty to eighteen.

For a long moment he simply stared at the screen. The numbers blurred; his vision tunneled. Eighteen hours meant she wouldn’t make rent. Eighteen hours meant buses and heating and food would become equations she had no way to solve.

He closed his eyes and exhaled slowly through his nose, counting to four the way Lillian had taught him the year Sarah moved out and the house felt unexpectedly hollow. In for four, hold for four, out for six. Keep breathing. Look at the whole system, not just the one break.

He opened his eyes again and scrolled the rest of the list. Older staff, carers, people whose bodies didn’t move as fast as the “average colleague” imagined by the algorithm — all marked for trimming. The model hadn’t seen faces, histories, medical notes. It had just seen gaps between peak hours and pay.

He clicked “Reply”.

“Hi,” he typed, fingers steady even as his heart thumped. “Thanks for sending this through. Before I implement, I need clarification on the criteria used for reductions. Several of these colleagues have protected characteristics and/or care responsibilities. We will need to conduct individual consultations and ensure we are compliant with employment law and equalities legislation.”

He read it twice. It sounded calm, almost bland. Underneath it was a growl.

He hit send, then picked up the phone and dialled Kaia’s extension.

“Frozen,” came her voice, breathless over the line.

“You got a minute?” he asked. “Staff room.”

When she came in, she had a crate of frozen peas balanced against one hip, condensation beading on her fur. She set the crate down by the fridge and wiped her paws on the thighs of her trousers.

“What’s up?” she asked. “If it’s about me nicking one of the custard slices that was going to be binned—”

“It’s not,” Miles said. “Though we’ll pretend I never heard that.”

He gestured to the table. She sat, the crate between them like an awkward third guest.

“They’ve sent the first round of cuts,” he said. No preamble; she deserved the truth without sugar-coating. “They want your hours down to eighteen.”

She stared at him. No sound, no twitch. Just stillness, the kind that meant she was calculating at speed.

“Okay,” she said eventually, voice flat. “So I quit, then.”

“No,” Miles said, too quickly. He leaned forward, forearms on the table. “I’ve already pushed back. I’m not implementing anything until I’ve spoken to them on an actual call. They’re not taking hours from anyone without a conversation, do you hear me?”

“And if they say it’s non-negotiable?” she asked. Her eyes were too bright now, the brittle shine of someone whose margin for error had been eaten alive years ago.

He hesitated. That was the wedge: the place where his principles and his mortgage and his career met head-on.

“Then I’ll fight it,” he said. “I’ll find every line in their own policies that says they have to consider your circumstances. You’ve been here how many years?”

“Since I was nineteen,” she said. “I’m twenty-six now. Seven Christmases.”

“Right,” Miles said. “You’re not a line on a spreadsheet. You’re the only one who can get the freezer section back in line in under an hour after a rush. You trained half the night staff. I’m not letting them treat you as disposable.”

Her throat worked. “You can’t promise that,” she said quietly.

“No,” he admitted. “I can’t. But I can promise you I won’t go along with it quietly. And if they push this through anyway, we will sit down and go through what options you have. I’m not leaving you to just... fall.”

It was not enough, he knew. It was all he had in this room right now.

Kaia looked away, jaw clenched. “My landlord put the rent up last month,” she said. “Said it was ‘market forces’. Funny how that always means I pay more, never less.”

Miles had no answer for that. He reached across the table, palm up; after a moment, she put her paw in his. Her grip was small and fierce.

“You shouldn’t have to be the one standing between us and them,” she said.

“I’m the one who’s paid to be,” he said. “Let me do my job.”

When she left with the crate, shoulders squared a little more firmly, Miles sat alone for a moment in the humming quiet of the staff room. Through the wall he could hear the beep of tills, the murmur of customers asking where the eggs had moved to this week. The store went on. It always did.

He looked at the spreadsheet again, then at the photo pinned above his desk: Lillian and Sarah on the Loughton sofa, Sarah’s golden head resting against Lillian’s broad shoulder, both of them laughing at something off-camera. His family, solid and alive.

He tapped the screen where Kaia’s name had been crossed out and whispered, under his breath, “Not on my watch.”

## Chapter 4 — Warning Signs at Home

By the time he got home, the sky over Epping Forest was a wash of purple and orange. The streetlights along their road had come on, casting soft halos over parked cars and the garden hedges. He could see the warm square of their kitchen window from the end of the street, steam ghosting the glass.

He let himself in quietly, the familiar click of the front door lock followed by the smell: garlic, onions, something slow-cooking and rich. The house sounded like them — faint music from the living room speakers, the clink of crockery, the tick of the old clock in the hall.

“Shoes,” came Lillian’s voice from the kitchen, without turning round. “You’re trailing the city all over my floor, love.”

He smiled despite the weight in his chest and toed his shoes off by the door. “Yes, ma’am,” he said. “Wouldn’t dare.”

When he stepped into the kitchen, the sight of her hit him like a physical exhale. Lillian Edgecombe stood at the hob, broad back to him, her 500-pound body wrapped in a dark green dress, apron strings digging slightly into the soft layers of her waist. Steam curled around her, fogging her glasses, silver threads in her black hair catching the overhead light.

She turned when she heard him, pushing her glasses up with the back of one flour-dusted paw. Her face softened.

“There you are,” she said. “I was about to send a search party down the Central line.”

“Long day,” he said, dropping his bag on the chair by the table. “Sorry I’m late.”

She set the wooden spoon down and wiped her paws on a tea towel before stepping closer. He could smell tomato and basil and the faint mineral tang of clay on her fur — she’d been in the garden studio at some point today.

“I know the look of a ‘long day’,” she said, studying his face. “This is a ‘something’s happening’ day.”

He hesitated, then shrugged, the movement more defensive than he intended. “Corporate are rolling out a new staffing system,” he said. “Algorithms and charts. I’ll tell you about it after we eat.”

“Which is Lillian-ese for ‘I’ll make some jokes over dinner and pretend it’s fine,’” she said. “Come here.”

He went. She wrapped her arms around him, pulling him in against her softness. His face pressed into the slope of her chest; his hands found familiar resting places at the small of her back and the side of her hip. Her grip was firm, grounding.

“You smell like freezers,” she murmured into his fur. “And cheap coffee.”

“It’s part of my charm,” he said.

“Mm. One of them.” She leaned back enough to look at him. “Do you remember what we agreed the last time something like this happened?”

He knew exactly which “last time” she meant: the year a different regional director had tried to force through a restructure that would have closed three local branches. He’d nearly worked himself into hospital over that one.

“We agreed I would not try to fix late-stage capitalism with my bare paws,” he said.

“And?” she prompted, one brow raised.

He sighed. “And I would ask for help before I reached the point of collapsing in the car park.”

“Good,” she said. “We haven’t had to test the second part yet and I would like to keep it that way.”

The kitchen timer beeped. She released him gently. “Sit,” she said. “Eat first. Then we’ll talk about Optimise Whatever and how you are not going to let it eat you alive.”

He sat at the table, watching her move. Lillian didn’t glide; she occupied space deliberately, each step considered. He watched how she shifted her weight as she bent to check the oven, one paw braced on the counter, the way the chair creaked a little when she settled into it opposite him with two plates of pasta.

“Sarah called,” she said, twirling spaghetti with an ease he envied. “She’s got a new piece in that group show in Manchester. Sent photos. Very abstract. Lots of welded metal and negative space. I told her you’d ring her later.”

Miles froze, fork halfway to his mouth. “Tonight?” he asked. “We were supposed to talk tonight?”

Lillian’s eyes flicked to his. “Yes,” she said slowly. “She texted you the reminder last week, love. Seven o’clock. You said you’d definitely be home in time.”

He checked the kitchen clock. 20:12. A small, cold stone dropped into his stomach.

“Shit,” he whispered.

Lillian’s gaze softened, but it didn’t lose its weight. “You’ve been missing meals and sleep over this job for years,” she said quietly. “I will not watch you miss our daughter as well.”

He put his fork down. Appetite gone, replaced by a tightness in his chest that felt a lot like shame. “I’ll call her after we eat,” he said. “If she’s still up.”

“You’ll call her even if she isn’t,” Lillian said. “You’ll leave a message. And then you and I are going to talk about what you can actually control and what you can’t.”

He nodded, throat thick. The sauce tasted good, but he barely registered it.

Later, when the plates were in the sink and the house had quieted, he sat on the edge of their bed with his phone pressed to his ear, listening to Sarah’s voicemail greeting — airy and fast, the way she had been at sixteen and still sometimes sounded now.

“Hey, dad,” she said when she picked up on the second try, breath already laughing. “You forgot, didn’t you?”

He closed his eyes. “I did,” he said. “And I am so, so sorry.”

## Chapter 5 — Logistical Fire #1

Tuesday began with a lorry that wasn’t theirs and a pallet of the wrong kind of milk.

Miles was halfway through rearranging the bakery rota when the intercom crackled. “Miles to goods-in, please. You’re going to want to see this.”

The loading bay smelled of diesel and wet cardboard. A towering human in a different supermarket’s uniform stood by the tailgate of his truck, scratching his head. Behind him, pallets of dairy products sweated in the cold air.

“This order’s for you lot,” the driver said when Miles approached. “FreshMart Eastborough. See?” He jabbed a finger at the manifest.

Miles took the clipboard and scanned it. “We’re FreshMart Eastborough,” he said. “But we’re not supposed to get this much organic oat milk or—” he squinted “—thirty crates of lactose-free cottage cheese.”

“It’s on the manifest,” the driver said. “If I take it back they’ll dock my pay for a failed delivery. I’ve got two more drops before eleven. So.”

So. Miles could see the chain of consequences unfolding: accept the stock, throw his storage plan into chaos, risk spoilage and write-offs; refuse it, cause trouble for a driver who looked like he’d already had enough of trouble for one lifetime.

He patted the side of the nearest crate, thinking like an engineer, not a victim. Storage capacity? Limited, but flexible. Demand? They could create some if they had to. Waste? Unacceptable if avoidable.

“All right,” he said. “We’ll take it. But I’m logging it as a misallocation, and someone up the food chain is going to explain where the budget comes from.”

Back inside, he pulled core staff into a quick huddle between aisles two and three.

“We’ve been gifted an unholy amount of oat milk,” he said. “We’ve got two choices. We can let it sit in the back and hope people find it, or we can make a song and dance about it.”

Felix raised a paw. “Please tell me you’re not about to suggest we literally sing and dance, boss.”

“No,” Miles said. “But I am about to suggest an endcap display and a ‘try something new this week’ promo. Sina, can you put together some tastings? Kaia, I need you to rework the chilled layout. We’ll make it look intentional.”

“You’re turning a screw-up into a feature,” Kaia said, half-admiring, half-exasperated.

“That’s the job,” Miles said.

By mid-morning, customers were sampling oat milk latte shots at a hastily arranged table, Sina’s cheerful patter turning confusion into curiosity. The new display at the front of the dairy aisle gleamed under the strip lights, all white cartons and carefully aligned labels. A hand-lettered sign — “COULD THIS BE YOUR NEW FAVOURITE?” — sat above it, written in Sarah’s looping style from an old poster she’d made for them years ago.

He texted her a photo, thumbs moving quickly.

Got surprise stock. Improvised a display. Used your old lettering. Store still standing. You’d be proud.

The reply came between customers.

Looks great, dad. :) Call later? I want to tell you about the sculpture. <3

He smiled, something loosening in his chest. He typed back.

Yes. Tonight. Promise. Alarm set and everything.

He meant it. He meant all of it. But as he moved through the day — dealing with a malfunctioning self-checkout, a customer complaining about the placement of gluten-free bread, a junior manager asking panicked questions about Optimise 2030 — he could feel the margins of his attention fraying.

By closing time, his legs ached and his brain felt like an over-used spreadsheet, cells full of error messages. Still, the store had made it through another day without burning down. In his world, that counted as a win.

He locked up in the dark, the automatic doors sighing shut behind him. In the reflection of the glass he saw himself: flannel shirt rumpled, fur damp at the edges, shoulders slightly rounded under an invisible weight.

He checked his phone. Two missed calls from an unknown number. One text from Lillian: Don’t forget Sarah, love. I will nag you until the end of time. x

He smiled, pocketed the phone, and headed towards the station.

## Chapter 6 — Corporate Visit Scheduled

The calendar invite landed like a small grenade on Wednesday morning.

SUBJECT: REGIONAL PERFORMANCE REVIEW — EASTBOROUGH FLAGSHIP

Attendees: Regional Performance Director (the man from the video), HR Business Partner, “Optimise 2030 Implementation Lead”, Miles Ferguson (Store Director).

Time: Friday, 10:00–13:00.

“They’re calling it a ‘review’,” Miles said in the office, reading the invite aloud to his deputy, Hana. “Which is corporate for ‘inspection, plus telling you why you’re wrong’.”

Hana grimaced. “Should we warn the staff to be extra… presentable?” she asked. “You know how they get about uniforms.”

He thought of Kaia’s worn trainers, of Ajay’s turban, of Sina’s nose ring that technically violated policy but had been part of her face longer than any of their current bosses had been around.

“We tidy the store,” he said. “We check the safety logs. We make sure the break room doesn’t look like a war crime. But I’m not turning this place into a showroom. They’re here to see how we actually run. Not some staged version.”

Hana gave him a look that said she both admired and feared his resolve. “You know they’ll mark us down for anything they can,” she said.

“Then let them,” he said. “Our numbers are solid. Our staff turnover’s lower than the regional average. Our customer complaints are down. If they want to criticise that because someone’s socks aren’t the right shade of black, they can do it to my face.”

He could hear Lillian in that, too: her refusal to shrink to fit anyone’s idea of acceptable. We belong where we go.

Still, that night, he found himself wiping down already-clean surfaces and re-straightening product displays until Kaia gently took the cloth out of his paw.

“You’re going to polish a hole in the glass,” she said. “Go home. Sleep. You’re no good to us if you arrive tomorrow looking like a raccoon that lost a fight with a spreadsheet.”

He laughed, reluctantly, and let her shepherd him out. On the train back to Loughton, the carriage lights flickered over his reflection: tired eyes, jaw clenched, phone clutched in one paw as if it were a lifeline.

He scrolled through old photos to distract himself. Lillian on stage at a gig, mic in hand, dress catching the light; Sarah at eighteen, covered in clay, grinning in front of her first big sculpture. His family, full of colour and weight and laughing mouths.

He thought of their kitchen again. Of the way Lillian’s chair had creaked slightly under her last night as she’d sat across from him and said, “You can’t keep doing these eighteen-hour days, Miles. You’re not a machine.”

He’d made a joke about being at least partly mechanical by now. She’d not laughed. Not much.

When he got home, she was on the sofa, laptop balanced on the broad shelf of her thigh, glasses perched low on her nose. Sheet music and staff notes glowed on the screen.

“You’re still up,” he said, surprised.

“Of course,” she said. “Someone’s got to make sure you actually get through that door.”

He sank down beside her, letting his weight lean not-quite fully into her side. The sofa dipped and shifted to accommodate them both; they’d replaced it last year with something sturdier, wider, more forgiving. A practical act of love.

“They’re coming Friday,” he said. “The big bosses. Your favourite man from the video.”

“I’ll bake something,” she said.

He blinked. “For them?”

“For you,” she said. “You handle them. I’ll handle you. That’s the division of labour we agreed on, remember?”

He smiled, then. It stayed with him even after he’d showered and slid into bed beside her, his body curling automatically towards her warmth in the dark.

## Chapter 7 — Promise to Sarah

Thursday began with rain and a list.

Miles stood at the kitchen counter, biro in paw, writing down everything that needed to be done before Friday’s visit. Fridge temp logs. Fire exits. Shelf gaps. Briefing with staff. He printed three copies: one for himself, one for Hana, one pinned to the noticeboard where anyone could see what mattered.

“You’re making a war map,” Lillian said, pouring tea into a large mug, the steam fogging her glasses again. She wore a soft black dress today, hair braided back. The line of her shoulders was straight, but he could see the concern in the set of her mouth.

“Just a list,” he said. “If it’s on paper, it’s not shouting in my head.”

She slid the mug across to him. “What time are you talking to Sarah tonight?” she asked.

“Eight,” he said. “I’ve blocked it out in my diary. I’ve told Hana to kick me out of the building at seven thirty if I’m still there.”

“Good,” she said. “Because she sounded… brittle, on the phone. Proud, but brittle. The sculpture means a lot to her.”

He nodded. He hadn’t admitted to anyone that he felt slightly intimidated by his daughter’s work — all sharp edges and negative space, nothing like the neat systems he understood. But he understood her need for his attention. That, at least, was simple.

At the door, pulling on his coat, he turned back. “I’ll be home in time,” he said. “I swear.”

Lillian watched him, eyes soft and sharp at once. “I believe you,” she said. “I also know your job. So I will be ringing you at seven to remind you. Don’t make me come down there in person.”

He laughed. “You’d terrify them,” he said.

“Exactly,” she said.

The day at the store moved in fast, jagged bursts. A broken pallet here, a complaint there, a new set of directives about end-of-aisle promotions that contradicted last week’s directives. Underneath it all, the ticking clock of the looming inspection.

At six forty-five, he was still in the back, rearranging the store’s rota on the computer while Hana sat opposite, cross-checking names against notes scribbled on the back of an old poster. They were trying to thread a needle: keep staff hours within Optimise 2030’s limits without wrecking anyone’s life.

“If we move Ajay’s shift on Tuesdays and give Sina the extra hours on Thursdays—” Hana began.

Miles’s phone buzzed. Lillian’s name lit the screen.

He answered on the first ring. “Hi.”

“Seven o’clock in fifteen minutes,” she said. “How’s my favourite over-functioning shepherd?”

“Buried in rotas,” he said. “But I’m leaving at half seven. Promise.”

“Hana,” Lillian called, voice raised enough that the phone’s speaker picked her up clearly. “If he’s not out of that building in forty-five minutes, I’m sending in the band.”

Hana laughed. “Understood, Lillian,” she said. “We’ll roll him home like a barrel.”

By seven thirty, Miles actually did leave, to his own faint surprise. The rain had stopped, leaving the pavements shining under the streetlights. On the train, he rehearsed questions about Sarah’s sculpture in his head, wanting to sound genuinely engaged rather than like a man whose brain was half in the stockroom.

At eight on the dot, he sat at the kitchen table, laptop open, video call window glowing. Lillian pottered in the background, ostensibly washing up but really keeping an ear on them.

Sarah’s face appeared: golden fur, hair tied up messily, smudges of what might have been paint or soot on her cheeks. Behind her, the blurred suggestion of a studio.

“Hey,” she said, and the little ache that lived in his chest whenever he thought about her eased for a moment.

“Hey, love,” he said. “Show me this famous sculpture, then.”

She did. She walked him around the piece with the camera, explaining angles and tensions and the way light fell through the gaps. He listened, asked questions, felt his way into her language. He told her about the oat milk fiasco, about Sina’s tasting table, about Kaia’s dry commentary on corporate slogans.

For half an hour, the store shrank to the size of anecdotes. For half an hour, he was just her dad again, not a director or a buffer between algorithm and human.

When they hung up, Lillian came to sit beside him, thigh pressed against his, the sofa sighing under their combined weight.

“See?” she said softly. “The world didn’t end because you left a spreadsheet undone for an hour.”

He leaned his head against her shoulder. “Not yet,” he said.

“It won’t,” she said. “And if it tries, we have very good lawyers. And a band.”

He laughed, tired and real. The week wasn’t over. The storm hadn’t truly hit. But for this moment, in this house, he was held.

## Chapter 8 — Non-Compliance Warning

Friday morning began with a red exclamation mark.

Miles was in the office at seven, tie tightened properly for once, going through the last of the temperature logs for the inspectors. The store hummed outside the glass like a subdued beehive. His email pinged.

SUBJECT: Reminder — Roster Implementation Deadlines

The body of the message was only three lines long.

Dear Miles,

Our systems show that Optimise 2030 Phase 1 roster adjustments have not yet been fully implemented at Eastborough. Please note that non-compliance with centrally mandated scheduling may impact your performance evaluation.

Regards,

Regional Performance Director

He read it twice, jaw tightening. No name at the bottom, just the job title. As if a person didn’t need to sign their own threats.

He clicked “Reply” before he could talk himself out of it.

“Dear [Name],” he typed, refusing to write “Dear Regional Director” like he was addressing a building. “As per my previous emails, we are conducting individual consultations with impacted colleagues to ensure legal compliance and appropriate accommodations. As soon as those conversations conclude, I will confirm which changes can ethically be implemented.

I remain committed to both operational efficiency and staff welfare. I trust that Optimise 2030 is designed to support both.”

He hovered over “Send” for a moment. The last sentence was sharper than it looked; it implied that if the system didn’t support staff welfare, it was failing its own stated purpose. Lillian would have been proud.

He hit send.

A minute later, another email arrived, this one from HR. More words, more soft threats about “alignment” and “consistency across the region”.

He forwarded that one to his personal account to show Lillian later. She understood institutional language better than anyone he knew.

At eight, he walked the floor. Shelves were tight, gaps neatly front-faced. The bakery smelled rich and warm. Staff moved with the focused energy of people who knew they were being watched.

“You ready?” Hana asked, falling into step beside him.

“Never,” he said. “But the fridges are in range and no one’s on fire. That’s the best we ever get.”

## Chapter 9 — Staff Meeting Meltdown

He had wanted to talk to them after the inspectors left, when he knew how bad the verdict would be. But the email about non-compliance burned a hole in his pocket. He couldn’t ask them to stand shoulder to shoulder with him without telling them what was coming.

So at nine, an hour before the suits arrived, he called another staff meeting.

They squeezed into the break room again, the air thick with steam and nerves. High-vis vests hung over the backs of chairs. Someone’s tuna sandwich added a sharp note to the smell.

Miles stood by the kettle, arms folded. He didn’t bother with a PowerPoint. Just his voice.

“You all know about Optimise 2030 by now,” he began. “You know some of the proposed cuts. I wanted you to hear from me where we are.”

He outlined the basics: his emails, the non-compliance warning, the fact that he had not yet implemented the reductions. As he spoke, he watched their faces — eyes flicking to each other, shoulders tightening.

“So you’re refusing?” Ajay asked. “Like, properly refusing?”

“I’m delaying,” Miles said. “Calling for proper consultations. Using the policies they wrote to hold them to their own standards.”

“And if they sack you?” someone asked from the back.

The question hit the air like a thrown glass.

He swallowed. “If they decide that protecting you makes me expendable,” he said, “that will say more about them than me. But until that happens, I am your director. My job is to run this store well and to look after the people who make that possible. That is what I’m doing.”

“What about my Wednesdays?” Sina’s voice shook now, anger and fear braided together. “You said you’d try, Miles. I can’t move those hospital appointments.”

“I know,” he said. “And I meant it. As long as I have any say, those Wednesdays stay yours.”

It was too much. He knew it as soon as the words left his mouth. A promise he couldn’t guarantee, said to keep her from falling apart.

“You can’t say that,” muttered Felix. “You can’t promise things you don’t fully control. They’ll just hurt worse when it all goes to hell.”

The room sparked. Voices rose — frustration, fatalism, desperation. A younger colleague demanded why longer-serving staff should be protected when he could barely pay his own rent. An older worker snapped back about loyalty and years put in. Someone mentioned unions. Someone else mentioned how long organising would take.

Hana tried to calm them, but the volume climbed anyway. The break room became a pressure cooker. It wasn’t really about hours anymore; it was about all the cuts and squeezes and humiliations of the last decade, bubbling out at once.

Miles raised his voice. “Hey. Hey.” When that didn’t work, he slammed his paw against the table hard enough to rattle the mugs.

Silence fell, shocked and sudden.

He hadn’t meant to hit the table. His palm stung.

“We are not turning on each other,” he said, breathing hard. “They would love that. They would love for us to sit in here and fight over scraps while they call it ‘efficiency’. I am not watching that happen in my store.”

He let the words hang. Then, quieter: “I can’t promise to beat their system. I won’t lie to you about that. But I can promise to keep you informed. To tell you the truth, even when it’s bad. And to keep pushing back as hard as I can without losing the job that lets me do any of this. That’s what I’ve got.”

Kaia’s eyes met his, dark and steady. She nodded, just once. Others followed, reluctantly. The heat in the room cooled from boil to simmer.

“All right,” Miles said. “We’ve got inspectors in less than an hour. Whatever we think of them, if they see a store that runs well and a team that knows what it’s doing, that strengthens our hand. We’ll have that argument later. For now, we work.”

They filed out, tension still crackling around their shoulders. As the door swung closed behind the last of them, Miles braced his paws on the table and bowed his head.

He had just promised too much and not nearly enough.

## Chapter 10 — First Bad Night

The inspectors came. They clicked pens and took notes, asked pointed questions in soft voices, peered into fridges and fire exits. Miles walked them through every system, every checklist, every contingency plan. He was polite, firm, and utterly exhausted.

By the time they left, it was late afternoon. He’d missed lunch entirely. His only sustenance was three cups of coffee and a custard slice Kaia had shoved into his paw between questions.

“We’ll be in touch with a written summary,” the Regional Director said as they stood by the door. “I’m sure we can bring Eastborough fully into alignment.”

The smile didn’t reach his eyes.

When the automatic doors finally closed behind them, the whole store seemed to exhale. Hana sagged against a shelf. Someone whooped quietly in the bakery.

Miles forced a smile, thanked everyone, and sent them back to their tasks. There were still customers to serve, deliveries to receive, a sick call to cover.

He took the late shift himself. It seemed simpler than pulling someone else in on overtime he might not be able to authorise next week.

By ten that night, the store echoed. The fluorescence took on a harsh buzz. He walked the aisles with a roll of labels in one paw, correcting tiny misprices no one else would probably have noticed.

When he finally locked the doors and completed the last of the closing checks, it was nearly midnight. The car park was a wet, quiet expanse under orange sodium lights. His breath steamed in the cold.

He sat in the driver’s seat of his car and didn’t start the engine. The silence felt thick. His hands rested on the steering wheel, knuckles pale under fur.

He thought about going home. About Lillian, probably asleep by now, one paw stretched into his usual space out of habit. About Sarah’s new sculpture, which he hadn’t had the energy to look up properly after their call.

He thought about Kaia’s hours, Sina’s Wednesdays, the warning in his inbox. He imagined losing this job and what that would do to their mortgage, to the quiet stability of the house in Loughton. He imagined staying in it at any cost and what that would do to him.

His chest tightened. His breath shortened. He recognised the feeling — the same one that had crept up on him the year of the almost-closures.

Not again, he thought. Please, not again.

He forced himself to breathe the way Lillian had taught him. In for four, hold, out for six. He counted the rhythm like a drum pattern. Slowly, the panic receded enough for him to turn the key in the ignition and drive home, hands trembling only a little.

Sleep, when it came, was thin and fragmented. He woke at three, then four thirty, his body convinced he’d overslept, his mind already in the aisles.

## Chapter 11 — Missed Zoom Call

Saturday morning, his phone pinged while he was in the loading bay wrestling with a stubborn pallet truck. He glanced at the preview — a message from Sarah — and told himself he would read it properly as soon as he’d got the crates safely inside.

By the time he did, an hour had passed and the notification had slid off the front of his mind.

He saw it properly at four in the afternoon, standing in the staff room with his paw halfway to the coffee jar.

Hey, can we do a quick call tonight? Got feedback from the curator + I kind of want your take. 7pm okay?

Sent at 09:42.

He checked the time. 16:03. Seven was possible, he told himself. He would make it possible. He replied.

7 works. I’ll be home. Proud of you, love.

The rest of the day blurred. A fridge alarm went off. A child was sick in aisle five. A colleague called in, crying, because their brother had been laid off at another branch. An email arrived with a “draft” version of the inspectors’ report, full of suggestions about “optimising staff utilisation”.

At six forty-five, he was still in the office on a call with HR, arguing about the wording of “underperforming sections”.

“I’m saying that you can’t call our community donations ‘non-core activities’ in a public-facing report,” he said. “We’ve been doing that food-bank partnership for five years. It keeps people alive.”

“Miles, this is standard language,” the HR Business Partner said. “We can look at the phrasing, but the substance has to stay. Charity work isn’t a KPI.”

He checked his watch. 18:52.

“I have to go,” he said. “We’ll pick this up Monday.”

“We really need to sign off this week—”

“Monday,” he repeated, and hung up.

He left ten minutes later than he’d meant to. The train was delayed. By the time he jogged up their front path, panting slightly, it was seven twenty.

He burst into the living room, laptop bag banging against his hip. “I’m here,” he called. “I’m—”

Lillian was on the sofa, laptop open on the coffee table. The screen showed a video-call window with Sarah’s name and the words Call Ended.

Lillian looked up at him, eyes tired.

“She waited,” she said. “Fifteen minutes. She’s gone back to the studio. Said she’ll ring tomorrow instead.”

The guilt landed like a physical blow. He sat down heavily on the edge of the sofa, paws hanging between his knees.

“I tried,” he said weakly.

“I know,” Lillian said. “And I know you are juggling flaming knives down there. But from her end, it just looks like you forgot. Again.”

He rubbed his face. “I’m trying to keep people’s hours, Lil.”

“I know,” she said again. “But you’re letting them take yours. And hers.”

He wanted to argue that it was temporary, that once the worst of Optimise 2030 was past he’d have more space. But he remembered thinking the same thing about the last initiative, and the one before that.

“I’ll call her,” he said.

“Not tonight,” Lillian said gently. “She’s welding. She’ll call when she’s ready. And you… you’re not fit for real conversation right now.”

He hated that she was right. He hated even more that he could feel himself resenting the truth instead of changing in response to it.

## Chapter 12 — Breakdown in the Car

Sunday was supposed to be his easier day. Fewer deliveries, shorter opening hours, a slightly softer pace.

Instead, it was the day everything in him finally said no.

The morning passed in a blur of petty crises. A card machine crashed. An elderly customer slipped near the wet fish counter and had to be helped up gently, her embarrassment sharper than her bruises. The bakery oven decided to beep an error code they’d never seen before.

By lunchtime, Miles’s patience was stretched thin as clingfilm. He snapped at Felix about a misplaced display. He was curt with a regular who wanted to chat about football. Even Hana gave him a wary look.

“You all right?” she asked quietly as they passed each other near the cereals.

“Fine,” he said. “Just tired.”

At four, the last customer left. They locked the doors. The store fell into the peculiar silence of a place that was usually public suddenly private.

Miles did the closing checks on autopilot. Fire exits. Fridges. Safe count. Sign-off sheets.

He made it as far as the car park before his body refused to take another step.

He sat in the driver’s seat, keys in the ignition, and found he couldn’t remember how to start the engine. His paws rested on his thighs, heavy as if someone had poured concrete into his bones.

The sky outside was a flat grey. Rain threatened but hadn’t yet arrived. The car park lights flickered on with a hum.

He stared at his own reflection in the windscreen. A tired German shepherd in a too-neat shirt, shoulders hunched, eyes hollow.

Something in his chest cracked.

The tears came without drama, without sobs. Just a silent overflow that blurred the world into streaks of light. His breath hitched, then stuttered, then turned into little gasps.

He pressed his paws to his eyes, ashamed, even though there was no one to see.

For years, he had prided himself on not being the kind of man who broke. He was the drumbeat under everyone else’s melody, the quiet system that held. He fixed things. That was the point of him.

Now, alone in a car park behind a supermarket, he felt as if the system had finally failed. Not because of some dramatic catastrophe, but because he had been asked to carry too much for too long.

His phone buzzed where it lay face-down on the passenger seat. He ignored it. It buzzed again. On the third buzz, he picked it up purely to stop the sound.

Lillian.

He answered and couldn’t speak for a moment. The only sound he managed was a choked breath.

“Miles?” Her voice was soft, immediately alert. “Love, where are you?”

He swallowed. “Car park,” he managed. “At work.”

There was a pause long enough for him to imagine her sitting at the kitchen table, tea cooling, the Sunday paper folded half-read.

“Are you safe?” she asked.

He nodded, then realised she couldn’t see him. “Yeah,” he said. His voice sounded wrong even to his own ears. “Just… can’t seem to move.”

“Stay there,” she said. “I’m coming to you.”

He opened his mouth to protest — the drive, the petrol, the indignity of his wife having to rescue him from his own workplace — but his throat closed around the words.

“Okay,” he whispered instead.

He sat there, breathing in the smell of his own fur and car upholstery, until he saw the familiar shape of their hatchback pulling into the far corner. Lillian got out slowly, coat pulled tight, hair up in a loose knot. Even at a distance, she was unmistakable — wide-hipped, deliberate, a moving centre of gravity.

She tapped on the window once. He unlocked the door. The car dipped slightly as she eased herself into the passenger seat.

“Hi,” she said, as if they’d just bumped into each other on the street.

“Hi,” he said, voice rough.

She took his paw without ceremony. “There it is,” she murmured, thumb brushing over his knuckles. “The point where you finally notice how much this is hurting you.”

He laughed, a broken sound. “Took me long enough.”

“You’re very committed,” she said. “Stubborn as an old hymn.”

They sat in silence for a while, the car ticking as the engine cooled, rain beginning to patter softly on the roof.

“You don’t have to go back in tomorrow,” she said finally. “You know that, right?”

“I can’t just not go,” he said automatically. “They need me.”

She turned to look at him properly, eyes sharp behind fogged glasses. “They need you alive,” she said. “They do not need you shattered.”

The word hung between them like a diagnosis.

## Chapter 13 — Lillian’s Quiet Intervention Begins

On Monday morning, Lillian did something she’d never done in the twenty-odd years he’d known her: she walked into his store in the middle of the day, unannounced, not as a customer but as herself.

Miles was at the customer service desk, dealing with a complaint about a mispriced Easter egg, when the automatic doors sighed open and let her in. She was dressed for work at the academy: long dark dress, structured coat, scarf in deep blues and golds. She walked with the calm assurance of someone who knew she had a right to be wherever she chose.

Heads turned. She wasn’t the sort of woman you could fail to notice. She had always joked that her body entered a room before she did; today, she used that gravity like a shield.

“Excuse me one moment,” Miles said to the customer, then crossed the floor towards her, heart thumping.

“Lil,” he said. “What are you doing here?”

“Shopping,” she said, deadpan. “Obviously. Where else can a girl find twelve litres of oat milk and a crisis?”

He snorted despite himself. “You should have told me you were coming. I would have—”

“What?” she asked. “Polished the staff? Hidden the stress in aisle nine?”

He opened his mouth, then closed it.

“I’m not here to embarrass you,” she said, softer now. “I’m here because last night you cried in the car and then tried to pretend it was a minor software glitch. I’m not leaving you alone with this.”

She pulled a folded piece of paper from her bag and pressed it into his paw. He unfolded it: a printout of his contract, sections highlighted in yellow. Sick leave. Stress-related absence. Duty of care.

“I had a chat with our HR at the academy,” she said. “Then with your cousin Brad, who owes me three favours and knows a good employment lawyer. You have options.”

He stared at the highlighted clauses. The words swam a little. He could feel the eyes of his staff on them from a distance — curious, concerned.

“I can’t just walk out,” he said.

“I’m not asking you to,” she said. “I am asking you to tell your GP what’s happening. Today. And to start writing down what they’re doing to you and your people. Paper is power, remember?”

He thought of all the times he’d seen her annotate contracts, syllabuses, festival agreements, turning bureaucratic documents into something that served her students and her band rather than the other way round.

“I’ve got three hours until my next class,” she said. “I will sit with you in that back office while you ring the surgery and make the appointment, if that’s what it takes. Or we can do it in the staff room. I’m not precious.”

He looked at her. At the way she stood, rooted, unmovable. At the way the fluorescent light caught in the streaks of silver at her temples. At the quiet fury burning behind her gaze — not at him, but at what was being done to him.

“Okay,” he said. The word felt like stepping off a ledge. “Okay.”

He turned to Hana. “Can you cover the floor for half an hour?” he asked. “I need to make some calls.”

She nodded, relief flickering across her face. “Of course,” she said. “Go.”

In the cramped office, Lillian sat on the spare chair, knees spread slightly, fullness of her skirts spilling over. She looked far too large for the space, in a way that made the office feel small rather than her too big.

“Dial,” she said gently.

He did. His voice shook more talking to the GP’s receptionist than it had talking to the Regional Director. Saying the words “stress” and “panic attack” out loud to a stranger made them solid in a way emails hadn’t.

When he hung up, appointment booked for Wednesday, he looked at Lillian and saw pride in her face.

“That’s my lad,” she said. “Now. Show me this famous cold storage everyone keeps mentioning in your late-night rants.”

He laughed, properly this time. On the way through the back corridors, he introduced her to staff. She learned names in seconds, eyes warm, handshake firm. Kaia straightened up, suddenly shy. Felix cracked a joke about VIP visits. Lillian responded in kind.

Her presence changed the air. Not because she was some miraculous fix, but because she reminded them — and him — that Miles existed outside these walls, loved and anchored and seen.

## Chapter 14 — Cold Storage Breaks

It happened, of course, on the worst possible day: the morning the final inspectors’ report was due and the first whispers of a transportation strike hit the news.

Miles arrived to find Hana standing outside the walk-in freezer, arms folded, breath misting in the air.

“Don’t panic,” she said as soon as she saw his face. “But you’re going to panic.”

He looked at the display on the temperature monitor. It blinked an angry red. The internal thermometer read six degrees above where it should have been.

“How long?” he asked.

“At least an hour,” she said. “Maybe more. We only noticed when Kaia said the ice cream felt soft.”

He did the maths quickly. A certain number of minutes above a threshold, certain products becoming unsafe, write-off thresholds, paperwork. Money down the drain. Ammunition for anyone who wanted to argue that his store wasn’t under control.

“Get everything perishable into the other units,” he said. “Prioritise meat and fish. Use the old bakery chiller if you have to. Log everything. We’re going to minimise the loss.”

“The other units are already packed,” Hana said. “We might have to get creative.”

They did. Within fifteen minutes, the back corridor had turned into a triage ward for chilled goods. Staff hustled crates back and forth, voices overlapping, condensation blooming on fur and skin. Kaia directed the flow like a tiny, fierce general.

“That shelf is at four degrees; stack the chicken there. Ice cream into the display freezers for now, we’ll sort later. No one leaves anything on the floor. If we’re throwing it, it goes in a labelled crate, not straight in the bin. I am not sorting through a mystery puddle later, I swear to God.”

Miles moved among them, lifting, directing, checking thermometers. Sweat prickled under his shirt despite the chill. The cold storage unit itself hummed sullenly, fans whirring without effect.

“Have you called maintenance?” he asked between armfuls of yoghurt.

“On hold,” Hana said, brandishing her phone. Tinny elevator music leaked from the speaker.

Of course that was the moment the Regional Director chose to walk in, flanked by the Optimise 2030 Implementation Lead and a different HR partner than last time.

“Miles,” the Director said, voice too bright for the refrigerated chaos. “We thought we’d do a quick site walk before we finalise the report. Is this a good time?”

Miles straightened, fur damp, breathing hard. He looked at the crates of food, at his staff’s determined faces, at the broken freezer behind them.

He forced a smile that was mostly teeth. “It’s an excellent time,” he said. “You’re about to see exactly how we handle a crisis.”

## Chapter 15 — Cold-Storage Fiasco

They walked through the cold corridor together, the sound of their footsteps echoing off metal and plastic.

“We had a failure in the main unit,” Miles explained, keeping his tone even. “We caught it quickly. We’re triaging stock to safe temperatures. Losses will be logged. Maintenance are—”

“This represents a significant risk,” the Implementation Lead said, frowning at the blinking red display. “Temperature deviations of this magnitude—”

“—are why we inspect twice a day and react immediately when we find a fault,” Miles said. “If you’d arrived twenty minutes later, you’d have walked into a mess. Instead, you’re seeing it contained.”

Kaia appeared at his elbow, clutching a clipboard. “Updated list,” she said, slightly out of breath.

He took it and handed it straight to the Director. “Here,” he said. “Product by product, time out of range, whether it’s been moved or written off. You can see that we prioritised high-risk items.”

The Director scanned the list, eyebrows lifting despite himself. “You did this… how quickly?”

“Started logging fourteen minutes ago,” Kaia said, cheeks flushing under her fur now that three pairs of managerial eyes were on her. “We’ve got another list for moved items.”

“Ms…?” the Implementation Lead asked.

“Kaia,” she said. “Freezer section lead.”

“Well,” the Director said slowly. “This is… impressively organised.”

“It would be more impressive if the freezer hadn’t broken in the first place,” the Implementation Lead muttered.

Miles bit back the urge to point out that the unit was older than the Optimise programme, and that he’d been requesting its replacement for two years. Instead, he took a breath.

“We work with the equipment we’re given,” he said. “And we build systems robust enough to cope when it fails. That’s the job.”

They finished the walk-through with the Director unusually quiet, taking more notes on staff initiative than on minor housekeeping details. When they returned to the office, he asked Hana and Kaia to join them.

“I want to be very clear,” he said, once they were all squeezed into the small space. “The situation this morning could have been disastrous. The fact that it wasn’t is due to your staff’s quick action and Mr Ferguson’s contingency planning.”

Miles blinked. Compliments from this man felt more disorienting than criticism.

“That said,” the Implementation Lead cut in, “we can’t ignore the underlying question of why we have such a risk-prone piece of equipment on site—”

“Which is why we will be authorising the replacement of this unit this quarter,” the Director said, overriding him. “And reviewing similar units across the region.”

Miles exhaled slowly. That, at least, was a concrete win.

When the suits finally left, Kaia leaned against the wall and let out a long breath.

“I thought I was going to throw up on his shoes,” she said.

“You did brilliantly,” Miles said. “If they don’t put you on every ‘best practice’ slide they write after this, they’re idiots.”

Her mouth quirked. “You’d better not let them cut my hours after that,” she said.

He didn’t answer out loud. But something in him hardened around that vow.

## Chapter 16 — Retaliation

The written report arrived two days later. It was five pages long, filled with phrases like “opportunities for improvement” and “alignment with strategic objectives”. He read it three times before the shape of what they were really saying came into focus.

They praised the store’s “community engagement” and “staff cohesion”. They commended the handling of the cold-storage incident. They noted “strong local leadership”.

Then, in the final section, they slid in the knife.

We remain concerned, the report read, about the pace of implementation of Optimise 2030 staffing efficiencies at Eastborough. While we understand that individual consultations are ongoing, the delay in enacting centrally approved roster changes may result in Eastborough underperforming on labour cost metrics compared to similar stores.

We recommend that Mr Ferguson receive targeted coaching to support alignment with strategic priorities. Continued non-alignment may necessitate consideration of alternative leadership arrangements at Eastborough.

He printed the last paragraph out and took it home, the paper crisp between his fingers.

After dinner, while Lillian loaded the dishwasher and hummed under her breath, he laid the sheet on the table between them.

She read it once, lips pressing into a thin line. Then again, more slowly.

“Ah,” she said. “There it is.”

“There what is?” he asked.

“The bit where they say, ‘You’re very good at your job, but if you insist on doing it in a way that doesn’t sacrifice your people on our altar, we will find someone else,’” she said. “Dressed up, of course, in management poetry.”

He rubbed his temples. “If they move me, if they push me out… what happens to everyone there?”

“You are not the only source of care in that building,” she said. “You have built a team that knows what to do. You are important, but you are not the entire universe.”

He knew she was right. It didn’t make the thought any less terrifying.

“Brad’s friend called,” she added. “The employment lawyer. She says this is… suggestive, but not yet concrete. They’re testing you. Seeing how much you’ll bend.”

“And what does she suggest?” he asked.

“Document everything,” Lillian said. “Every conversation. Every time you propose an alternative. Every time they ignore your duty-of-care concerns. If they do try to move you, you’ll have a record.”

He looked at the paper again. At his own name on the line about “alternative leadership arrangements”.

“Maybe I should just shut up and implement it all,” he said quietly. “Maybe that’s what good soldiers do.”

Lillian’s head snapped up. “You are not a soldier,” she said. “You are an engineer. Your job has never been to follow orders blindly. It’s been to build systems that work without breaking the people inside them.”

Her voice was calm, but it carried the same steel he’d heard when she’d stood on picket lines years ago.

“If they wanted a Yes-Man, they shouldn’t have hired you,” she said.

## Chapter 17 — Transportation Strike Begins

The first day of the transportation strike was oddly quiet.

No lorries. No deliveries. The loading bay stood empty, echoing. The usual morning rush of pallets and shouting and reversing alarms simply… didn’t happen.

On the news, images of blockaded depots and picket lines filled the screens. Commentators argued about supply chains and just-in-time logistics. Miles watched for five minutes in the staff room, then turned the volume down and stared at the muted crawl of headlines instead.

“How long do you think it’ll last?” Hana asked.

“Long enough to hurt,” he said.

The store could operate on existing stock for a couple of days, maybe three at a stretch, if they were careful. But after that, shelves would gape. Customers would panic. Management would start sending emails about “maximising on-shelf availability under challenging circumstances” as if food could be conjured from empty crates.

He walked the aisles that morning with a different eye. Not looking for misaligned labels now, but for fragility. Which items ran out fastest. Where they could redirect demand.

He pulled Kaia aside in the cold section. “We’re going to need to think like a small shop,” he said. “Make what we have last. Rotate stock more often, push what’s plentiful, be honest when we’re out.”

She nodded, jaw set. “I grew up on corner shops,” she said. “We can do that.”

He rang local suppliers — the bakery round the corner, the independent greengrocer who technically competed with them but appreciated their occasional bulk orders. He asked what they could provide if the main deliveries didn’t resume.

“You’re supposed to be the big guy,” the baker said, half-joking. “Isn’t it backwards for you to lean on us?”

“We live in the same streets,” Miles said. “People will still need bread. I don’t care whose van it comes off.”

By midday, they had a rough plan: local bread deliveries by hand, veg topped up by a late-night run to the wholesale market if it was still open, signs ready to explain empty spaces honestly rather than blaming “unforeseen circumstances”.

He felt oddly energised. This, at least, was a problem he understood: finite resources, creative routing, the maths of scarcity.

“You look almost happy,” Hana observed that afternoon as they shifted stock.

“I like solving things,” he said. “When the problem isn’t ‘How many ways can we make people’s lives worse while calling it progress’.”

Of course, Head Office saw it differently.

At four, an email arrived with the subject line: TRANSPORT STRIKE RESPONSE — CUSTOMER RETENTION STRATEGY.

It spoke of “leveraging loyalty schemes” and “maximising high-margin items still available”. It mentioned nothing about vulnerable customers, about people on fixed incomes who couldn’t simply swap to whatever was left.

Miles forwarded it to Lillian with the subject: You’d love this one. Then he went back to the floor, to the real work.

## Chapter 18 — General of a Small War

By the second day of the strike, Eastborough felt less like a supermarket and more like a siege.

Shelves thinned. Customers arrived with lists already half-crossed out in their heads. Anxiety hung in the air like static. Rumours spread faster than facts.

“They say there’s no more flour anywhere,” one woman whispered at the bakery counter.

“My mate in Croydon says their FreshMart’s run out of nappies,” another customer said at the tills. “What are we supposed to do?”

Miles found himself fielding questions that had nothing to do with his official job description. Did he think the strike was justified? Was it safe to buy frozen veg if the lorries hadn’t run? Had he heard anything from “higher up” about when things would be normal again?

He answered as honestly as he dared. He refused to demonise the drivers. He refused to make promises he couldn’t keep. He brought a pallet of long-life milk out himself and stood by it, explaining to an anxious older man how long it would keep.

Behind the scenes, he orchestrated what felt like a small war.

He drew maps of stock flows on the whiteboard in the office, arrows showing where to move tinned goods to hide gaps, circles around items to push in place of missing ones. He scheduled extra breaks for staff at the front lines, knowing that being the person who had to explain “No, we don’t have that” fifty times in an hour was its own kind of labour.

He coordinated with the local bakery to deliver bread in multiple small batches rather than one big drop, so shelves never looked completely bare. He partnered with the greengrocer to share produce, explaining to customers that some of the apples now came from “down the road” rather than “the warehouse”.

In the evenings, he emailed Head Office detailed reports: what they were doing, what worked, what didn’t. He framed it as “learnings” and “local initiatives”, knowing that language was more likely to be read.

Hana watched him, half admiring, half worried.

“You’re in your element,” she said one night as they stood by the staff room kettle at nine p.m., both too wired to go home yet. “You know that, right?”

“Crisis is simple,” he said. “You either fix the thing in front of you or you don’t. It’s the slow stuff that eats me.”

“You can’t live in crisis forever,” she said.

“No,” he agreed. “But for now, it’s keeping us from falling apart.”

He went home even later that night. Lillian was still awake, reading, a mug of cooling tea on the bedside table.

“General Ferguson,” she murmured as he slid under the duvet. “How goes the front?”

He rested his forehead against her shoulder. “We’re holding the line,” he said. “For now.”

“And the general?” she asked.

He hesitated. “The general is… very tired,” he said.

She kissed the top of his head. “We’ll talk tomorrow,” she said. “When you’re not made entirely of caffeine and adrenaline.”

## Chapter 19 — “You’re Disappearing”

Tomorrow came, and with it a small, ordinary moment that finally jolted him into seeing what she had been seeing all week.

It was Thursday morning. He was halfway out the door, one arm in his coat, phone already in his paw. Lillian stood by the kitchen counter, breakfast plates stacked in the sink, the tea towels hanging slightly askew.

“Miles,” she said.

He paused, hopping slightly as he tried to shove his foot into his shoe without sitting down. “Mm?”

“Look at me,” she said.

He did. Her gaze was steady, the sort she used on students who were about to talk themselves out of taking up space.

“You’re disappearing,” she said.

He blinked. “I’m right here,” he said, attempting a joke. “Large German shepherd, takes up most of the bed. Hard to miss.”

She didn’t smile.

“You’re here,” she said. “Your body is here. Your paws are making tea. Your key is turning in that lock every morning. But the rest of you…” She searched for words, then gestured vaguely around his head. “The rest of you is always somewhere you’re not. In an email. In a rota. In the next crisis.”

He opened his mouth to protest, then caught himself. Because she was right. Conversations with her had become half-sentences interrupted by texts. Meals were swallowed while his mind ticked through tasks.

“I don’t know how to do it any other way,” he said quietly. “If I let go, things break.”

“Things will break anyway,” she said. “Because they built them to. Because profit and exhaustion are baked into the design. You holding your breath is not going to change that.”

He leaned against the counter, suddenly unsteady. “What am I supposed to do, then?” he asked. “Walk away and watch them cut everyone’s hours? Pretend I don’t know how to stop certain kinds of damage?”

“You’re not supposed to walk away,” she said. “You’re supposed to step back far enough to see which parts of this are yours to carry and which aren’t. You’re supposed to remember that you are not a martyr. You are my husband. You are Sarah’s dad. You are a drummer in a band and a man who makes the best roast potatoes in Essex. You are not only a buffer between your colleagues and a spreadsheet.”

His throat tightened. Tears threatened again; he blinked them back.

“I don’t know how to come back,” he said. It was the closest he’d come to a confession.

She reached out and took his paw, pressing it flat against the soft slope of her belly, the centre of her warmth.

“Start here,” she said. “Remember what this feels like. Remember what we’ve built that has nothing to do with FreshMart. Then we’ll make a plan.”

He swallowed. “What kind of plan?”

“One that involves you taking two days off,” she said. “In a row. On purpose.”

The idea felt so radical it might as well have been a foreign language.

## Chapter 20 — Turning Decision: Two Days Off

The GP’s surgery smelled of disinfectant and old magazines. Miles sat in the waiting room between a coughing child and an elderly man reading a fishing magazine, his paws restless on his knees.

Lillian had come with him, but she sat a few seats away, nose in a novel, giving him the illusion of privacy while still anchoring him in the room.

When his name was called, he felt the old, childish urge to bolt. Instead, he stood up and walked into the consultation room, heart rattling.

The doctor — a middle-aged fox with kind eyes — listened as he haltingly explained the last few months. The long hours. The panic in the car. The pressure from above and below. The way he’d started waking up already exhausted.

He expected skepticism. Instead, he got a nod and a printed questionnaire about stress and sleep, which he filled in with a kind of embarrassed honesty. The scores, apparently, were not ideal.

“This is not uncommon in people in your position,” the doctor said gently. “You’ve been under sustained stress for a long time. Your body is telling you it’s had enough.”

Miles stared at his paws.

“I can sign you off for a couple of weeks,” the doctor continued. “Give you time to rest, talk to your employer, maybe adjust some things before you go back.”

The thought of not being at the store for that long made his chest clamp. Who would handle the strike? The rotas? The vulnerable staff?

“I can’t be away that long,” he said. “They’ll… things will go wrong.”

The doctor studied him. “Then let’s start smaller,” she said. “I’m going to sign you off for two consecutive days. Not your usual single day off where you spend half of it thinking about going back. Two days where you are not available to them. In that time, I want you to rest. Talk to your family. Decide what needs to change.”

Two days. It sounded absurdly little, and impossibly much.

“They’re going to be furious,” he said.

“They will survive,” the doctor said. “You might not, if you keep going like this.”

He took the note. The paper felt heavier than it should.

Back at home, he laid it on the kitchen table. Lillian read it, then looked up at him with eyes that shone.

“Good,” she said simply. “We’ll make them count.”

Telling Hana was the hardest part.

“Two days?” she said when he showed her the doctor’s note in the office. “In a row?”

“Apparently that’s a thing people do,” he said, attempting levity.

Her face crumpled for a moment. “You should have done this weeks ago,” she said. “But I’m glad you’re doing it now.”

“You’ll be all right?” he asked.

She snorted. “We’re not children, Miles. You trained us. We know how to run a store. Go sleep.”

He wrote a long email to Head Office, attaching the note, referencing duty of care, making it as formal and unarguable as possible. Then he switched his work phone off for the first time in years.

The silence that followed was terrifying and strangely clean.

## Chapter 21 — Sarah’s Good Call

The first morning of his enforced rest, he woke at six out of habit, heart racing, then remembered he wasn’t going in. He lay in bed listening to the rain against the window, Lillian’s slow breathing beside him, and forced himself not to reach for his phone.

At nine, Sarah called.

“Mum sent me a photo of your sick note,” she said as soon as he answered. “You okay?”

“Define ‘okay’,” he said, trying to sound lighter than he felt.

“Not having a heart attack in a car park,” she said. “That’s my baseline for now.”

He winced. “Your mother talks too much.”

“She worries,” Sarah said. “We both do.”

He could hear studio noises in the background — the clank of metal, distant voices, music leaking from someone’s speaker. He pictured her there, mask pushed up on her head, paws stained with soot and oil.

“I’m… tired,” he admitted. “But the world’s still turning without me in the aisles, so that’s new.”

She laughed softly. “Weird, isn’t it? When you realise the machine doesn’t actually depend on you bleeding on the gears.”

He blinked. “That’s a bit dramatic,” he said.

“Comes with the job,” she said. “Listen… I’ve been thinking about the sculpture. The one I showed you.”

He felt a prick of panic. “You said the curator liked it?”

“She did,” Sarah said. “That’s not the point. The point is… it’s about you, partly. About the way you hold everything up. The way you make space for Mum and me, and for all those people at the store, and forget that you’re part of the load.”

He swallowed. “I thought it was about… architecture,” he said weakly.

She laughed. “It is. You’re very literal, you know that? It’s about structure. And weight. And which beams get reinforced and which ones people just hope won’t crack.”

He listened, quiet.

“You taught me that care is a system,” she said. “Not just feelings. You made sure I always had food and a warm bed and train tickets home when I needed them. You taught me that designing for people’s bodies matters — chairs, beds, cars. But somewhere along the line, you stopped being part of the design. You turned yourself into infrastructure.”

He let out a breath he hadn’t realised he was holding.

“I don’t know how to stop,” he said.

“You don’t have to go from hero to selfish bastard overnight,” she said. “Maybe just… start by letting other people hold some things. Let Hana be a manager. Let Kaia be the freezer queen. Let Mum be the terrifying HR presence she was born to be.”

He smiled faintly. “She does enjoy a contract,” he said.

“You’re allowed to be just… Miles,” Sarah said. “Not ‘Director Ferguson’. At least sometimes.”

There was a pause. He heard her inhale.

“And dad?” she added, voice softer. “I know you missed both those calls. That hurt. But this—” she paused, searching for words “—this hurts more to watch. I’d rather have you a bit less available but actually here than half in the room all the time.”

The honesty cut and healed at once.

“I’m sorry,” he said. “For all of it.”

“I know,” she said. “Now do something with the apology, yeah? Sleep. Go for a walk with Mum. Touch your drum kit. Remember who you are when you’re not under those bloody strip lights.”

He laughed, a real sound. “Yes, artist,” he said.

“Good,” she said. “Send me a photo when you’re out in the woods or something, so I know you’re not secretly rearranging the tinned tomatoes.”

After they hung up, he sat at the kitchen table for a long time, staring at his paws. Then he stood up.

“Walk?” he asked Lillian, who was already putting on her boots.

“I thought you’d never ask,” she said.

They went to Epping Forest, to the paths where the trees arched over them like a green nave. The air smelled of damp earth and leaves. He walked with his hand in hers, feeling the weight of her arm, the soft friction of her coat against his.

For two hours, he didn’t think about rotas.

## Chapter 22 — Staff Rally

On the second day of his leave, the group chat lit up.

Hana: Morning, boss. Just an update. We’ve opened without burning anything down.

Kaia: Freezers behaving. For now. I’m watching them like a hawk with a spreadsheet.

Felix: Old Mrs Patel brought biscuits “for the nice manager dog who finally took a day off”. We told her you’re on secret missions.

Miles read the messages with a strange mix of relief and longing. He typed back:

Proud of you lot. Don’t let Hana steal all the biscuits.

More messages followed throughout the day. Photos of the new joint display they’d cobbled together with the local greengrocer. A shot of a handwritten sign: DUE TO STRIKES, SOME ITEMS ARE MISSING. WE’RE DOING OUR BEST. THANK YOU FOR YOUR PATIENCE. (And below, in smaller letters, Kaia’s addition: AND THANK YOU, DRIVERS.)

Hana called at lunchtime.

“We’re fine,” she said before he could ask. “Actually… we’re more than fine. People are being weirdly kind. I think they can tell we’re all stretched.”

He listened as she talked through the morning — the small crises she’d handled, the decisions she’d made without checking with him first. He could hear pride in her voice, and something steadier: competence.

“We’ve been doing this together for years,” she said. “You know that, right? It was never just you.”

He closed his eyes. “I forget,” he said.

“We know,” she said. “That’s why we’re reminding you.”

At the end of the day, Kaia sent a photo that made his throat tighten. The staff, all of them who were on shift, crowded in front of the staff room whiteboard, grinning and pulling faces. On the board, someone had written in thick marker:

DAY 1 WITHOUT MILES: STORE STILL STANDING.

Underneath, in different handwriting:

DAY 1 WITH MILES RESTING: GOOD.

He showed the photo to Lillian. She smiled, eyes bright.

“They’re rallying,” she said. “Not just for the store. For you.”

He nodded, unable to speak for a moment.

## Chapter 23 — Confrontation with Corporate

He went back on the third day to a store that felt both familiar and subtly rearranged.

Shelves were still stocked as well as circumstances allowed. The makeshift joint displays with local suppliers had become part of the rhythm. Customers greeted him with, “You all right now, love?” in tones that suggested they’d been told something in broad strokes.

In the office, the Regional Director waited, flanked again by HR and the Implementation Lead.

“Miles,” the Director said, standing to shake his paw. “Good to see you back.”

“I wasn’t gone that long,” Miles said. “The team did fine without me.”

“Yes,” the Director said. “They did. Which is part of why we’re here.”

They sat. The air in the little room felt thick. Lillian’s highlighted printout of his contract lay face-down in his bag like a talisman.

“I want to address the Optimise 2030 situation,” the Director began. “You’ve been slower than most to implement the changes. At the same time, your store’s staff engagement and customer satisfaction scores are among the highest in the region, even under strike conditions.”

Miles said nothing. Let him talk.

“We could insist on full immediate compliance,” the Director went on. “We could treat this as a performance issue. But I suspect that would be… counterproductive.”

The HR partner cleared her throat. “We’ve reviewed your file,” she said. “The feedback from your colleagues. It’s clear that your leadership style is… valued.”

The Implementation Lead looked as if he’d swallowed something sour.

“So here’s what we’re proposing,” the Director said. “We’ll grant Eastborough a six-month ‘pilot’ status. You’ll implement a modified version of Optimise 2030 — your negotiated rota approach, your community partnerships — and we’ll compare your outcomes to fully optimised stores. If your labour costs don’t exceed a certain threshold and your performance metrics remain strong, we’ll consider adopting some of your practices more widely.”

Miles stared. “You want to turn us into a case study,” he said slowly.

“Innovation has to come from somewhere,” the Director said, as if the idea had originated with him. “Think of it as… an opportunity.”

“And if we ‘underperform’?” Miles asked. “If your numbers don’t like what happens when we treat staff like humans?”

The Director’s smile thinned. “Then we will revisit leadership arrangements,” he said. “I’m being honest with you.”

It was more honesty than Miles had expected.

He thought of Lillian’s words. Of Sarah’s sculpture. Of the photo of his staff under the DAY 1 WITHOUT MILES sign.

“All right,” he said. “We’ll pilot. On my conditions.”

The Implementation Lead bristled. “Your conditions?” he asked.

“We don’t cut hours for anyone with documented care responsibilities without an alternative agreed plan,” Miles said. “We protect existing accommodations. We prioritise stability in the rota for people who need it. We share the data both ways — if you’re going to measure us on cost, we’ll measure on retention and engagement.”

The HR partner looked intrigued. The Director considered.

“You drive a hard bargain,” he said. “But… all right. Within reason.”

It wasn’t a revolution. It was a wedge. A small, hard piece of resistance hammered into a vast machine.

He could work with that.

## Chapter 24 — The Staff’s Gift

The day the new freezer unit arrived, the staff threw what they insisted on calling a “launch party”.

“It’s a fridge,” Miles said, watching the shiny new stainless steel be manoeuvred into place.

“It’s a symbol,” Kaia said. “Of the fact that sometimes, if we shout loud enough and run fast enough, we get equipment that doesn’t try to kill us.”

They brought in homemade biscuits and crisps and a bottle of non-alcoholic bubbly from the drinks aisle. Someone stuck googly eyes on the freezer door and a paper crown on top.

“Say something, boss,” Felix called when they’d all crammed into the cold corridor, breath steaming.

Miles raised his mug of suspiciously sweet fizz. “May it keep things cold and our paperwork minimal,” he said. “And may no one have to write another emergency triage list for a long time.”

Laughter rippled. They clinked mugs against metal and each other.

After the mini-ceremony, as the others drifted back to their stations, Hana tugged on his sleeve.

“We’ve got something for you,” she said. “Staff room.”

He followed her, bemused. The room was empty when they went in, save for a large, flat object propped against the far wall, draped in a tea towel.

“We did this on our breaks,” Hana said. “And a bit after hours. Don’t tell corporate; they’ll want us to log it as a ‘team-building initiative’.”

She whipped the tea towel away.

It was a board. About a metre wide, framed in simple wood. The surface was an intricate collage. Old rota sheets cut and layered into a background. Photos of staff over the years, laughing and serious and mid-work. Little handwritten notes: “Thanks for swapping shifts.” “You caught that leak before it flooded the aisle.” “You saved my Wednesdays.” In the centre, in large letters made from cut-out packaging, someone had arranged the words:

YOU MADE THIS PLACE KINDER.

His vision blurred. He blinked hard, but the tears came anyway.

“We know you’re going to say it’s a team effort,” Kaia said, appearing in the doorway. “And it is. But you started it. You kept fighting for us even when they told you not to.”

“We wanted you to have something that isn’t a spreadsheet,” Felix added. “Something you can put up in that office so the next time an email tells you you’re ‘non-compliant’, you can look at it and remember what actually matters.”

Miles reached out and ran his paw over the surface. The paper was sealed under clear varnish, smooth and solid.

“I…” He cleared his throat. “I don’t know what to say.”

“Say you’ll stay,” Hana said, half joking, half not.

He looked at them — at their tired, stubborn, hopeful faces. At the collage of small acts of care.

“I’ll stay,” he said. “As long as I can do this job in a way that makes sense. As long as we’re doing it together.”

Kaia nodded. “Good,” she said. “Because we’ve got a freezer to boss around.”

## Chapter 25 — Bittersweet, Warm Ending

On Sunday evening, a week after his tearful breakdown in the car park, Miles stood at the front doors of the store and watched the last customers leave.

The strike was easing. Deliveries had resumed, patchy but present. The shelves were still scarred with gaps and substitutions, but the panic had ebbed. The staff moved with a new kind of confidence, knowing they’d held the line without him for a couple of days and with him the rest of the time.

He turned the key in the lock, feeling the now-familiar resistance. The lights above the aisles dimmed. The store exhaled.

“You all right?” Hana asked, coming to stand beside him.

He nodded. “Yeah,” he said. “I think I am.”

They walked the store together one last time. Not looking for faults, for once, but just… seeing it. The hand-drawn signs. The local bakery’s bread on their shelves. The freezer humming steadily. The collage in the staff room, already slightly smudged at the edges where people had touched it.

“You know they’re going to try something else in six months,” Hana said. “Another optimisation. Another programme.”

“I know,” he said. “And we’ll see what we can bend without breaking.”

He drove home through streets that glowed with the soft lights of Sunday dinners. In their kitchen, Lillian stood by the oven, apron tied over her dress, cheeks flushed from the heat. The table was already laid: roast chicken, potatoes crisped at the edges the way he’d taught her, vegetables in mismatched bowls.

“Look who’s home before nine,” she said, smiling.

“Miracles do happen,” he said.

They ate. They talked about small things: a new student of hers, a neighbour’s garden, a silly viral video Sarah had sent of a sculpture that collapsed mid-unveiling. They did not talk about Optimise 2030. Not directly.

After dinner, he went out to the shed at the bottom of the garden. The drum kit sat where it always had, slightly dusty, waiting. He sat on the stool, adjusted the height, picked up the sticks.

At first, his hands felt clumsy. Then muscle memory took over. He fell into a slow, steady rhythm, the kind of beat that underpinned so many of Lillian’s songs — solid, supportive, unshowy. He played until his shoulders loosened, until his breath synced with the pattern.

When he looked up, Lillian was standing in the doorway, arms crossed, leaning on the frame. She was smiling, eyes soft.

“There he is,” she said. “The man I married.”

“I never went anywhere,” he said. “Just… got a bit lost in the stockroom.”

She stepped into the shed, the space narrowing around her in a way that felt comforting rather than cramped. She rested one paw on the cymbal, letting it shiver.

“You know this isn’t over,” she said. “The fight. The nonsense. The way they’ll keep trying to push.”

“I know,” he said. “But I’m not going to do it alone anymore. And I’m not going to let them convince me that the only good I can do is measured in labour cost percentages.”

She nodded, satisfied. “Good,” she said. “Because I like you much better when you remember you’re a person.”

Later, lying in bed with her warm weight a steady presence beside him, he thought about the week. About the breakdown and the strike and the freezer fiasco. About the staff’s gift. About Sarah’s voice in his ear, telling him to stop turning himself into infrastructure.

The world outside their house was still unfair. People were still being cut and squeezed and optimised. His two days off hadn’t changed that.

But his corner of it — this store, this team, this house — felt a little kinder. Not because he’d saved anyone, but because he’d refused to let certain kinds of harm pass through him unchallenged.

He reached for Lillian’s paw under the duvet. She laced her fingers with his without opening her eyes.

In the dark, listening to her breathing, he let the thought form fully for the first time.

I didn’t fix the world, he thought. But I made this corner kinder. And I’m still here.

He exhaled, slow and deep.

For now, for tonight, that was enough.
