Hello Mum, Dad. It's Sarah.
Everything's sound here. Brighton’s much the same as ever. They’ve given me back the room I had when I first started my degree, but... God, it feels weird being back here as a PhD student. 
I’m chuffed that... well, that they accepted me. Never took it for granted, did I? 
But... anyway, I’m rambling. Sorry.
I’ve got the boxes Dad left in Mum’s study. Thanks for packing them up for me.  I opened them expecting my old stuff, but instead I found... well, it’s your own private exhibition, isn't it? I thought it one of the things I asked Dad to sort out, but I’ve been sitting here looking at it for over an hour and... wow.
I knew I had bits and bobs of yours in my room—I know I’d pinched some of your old sketches and photos to run tests on before I started at uni—but I’d never seen the whole lot. 
I’d never seen this.
(Short pause)
Mum, there’s a whole life... a whole world you never told me about. 
I’m looking at the photos you took when you first went to art school. The Polaroids.
(Long pause)
The ones you put in the box labelled 'SHAME.' You wrote that, didn't you, Dad?  I recognised your engineer's handwriting immediately. (Soft, watery laugh) And I recognised the caption you wrote for the series: 'Self-portraits, 1997. This is what courage looks like.' I didn't... Mum, it’s like looking at an open wound. Still bleeding. How long did you carry that feeling around with you? I can see it in your face. Or... no, I can see it in your whole body. It’s like you were doing everything you could to make yourself small. Unseen. As if you didn't want to exist. If I’d been... if I’d known you back then, if we’d been friends... I would have held you so tight. 
But I reckon you did all the hard work yourself, didn't you? As usual. 
Then I moved on to the second box, the one labelled ‘DEFIANCE.’ I recognised the drawings I’d nicked from you, and I finally connected the dots with the stories you used to tell me. The art academy where you modelled—that's where these are from, right? The character in these... she doesn't look like the same person hiding in the photos from '97. You were reborn there. It's obvious.  Your face, your gaze, your shoulders are loose... You were reclaiming the world. Reclaiming yourself. 
(Laughter through tears)
Though, I have to say, it’s hard to connect the serious art-model in these drawings with the badger I know—the one wandering around our house in Islington in a dressing gown, singing her own songs and making toast straight out of the shower! And then reading what Dad wrote: ‘Life figure study, 2003. She is learning to be seen.’ God, that kills me. But the thing that properly set me off... were the ones that came after. 
Like the one Dad labelled: 'Jonathan Harlow's personal collection, 2004. Comparing Venuses.' It's you, Mum, standing right under uncle Jonathan's painting. You aren't naked, but that dress shows everything, even covered in sequins! And you have this light in your eyes, behind your glasses... it speaks volumes. It speaks of your joy. Of how happy you were to be there, and to be seen. Seen twice: once as a person, and once as a work of art. If you've still got doubts, Dad, don't worry: I reckon from that moment on, Mum learned to see herself.
And he was right to talk about Venuses, plural. Because underneath the Rokeby Venus—or the Harlow Venus? I don't know what we call it—(Sniffle)—there is another one in flesh and blood. 
And the photos you took for uncle Jonathan’s other projects... 'Study for project, 2007. Staying between their lines. When she learned to laugh. She stopped holding her breath.' Dad, have you ever thought about curating a proper gallery? You’ve got a knack for interpreting what you see. I actually saw some of the photos from this project years ago in a book I was using for inspiration, and I remember thinking, 'Blimey, this anthro looks so much like Mum!' Or the one where Dad snapped you at the front door of the music academy you're teaching at. 'Professor Edgecombe, 2007. Soon to be running the place.' You look absolutely radiant. 
And then... there's the last series. The one Dad called ‘ACCEPTANCE.’ They're no less beautiful, but this... this is the family I know. This is you. 
(Pause. Sound of shifting papers)
Okay, come on. I admit it. You’ve made me cry. 
Mum, I found the photo of the two of us at the potter's wheel.  That vase we tried to throw together? Do you remember? I'll never forget it. We’d just arrived in Loughton, and you showed me how much fun it was to just make a mess with the clay. We got so filthy my dungarees are still stained, and I’ve washed them a hundred times in four years! And then... there's that simple black and white one I took of you. 
The one where you're standing with your back to us, with the winter sun coming in from the window on the right.  Do you remember it? The one Dad wrote on: 'The Potter, 2024. Finally home in her own skin.' 
I don't know how the world made you feel when you were my age... or before I came along... but they were wrong.  You are wonderful. I think you always have been. 
There is... there is one last index card in the bottom of the box, though.  I can’t find the photo that goes with it. Maybe you forgot to pack it? Or maybe... maybe there isn't a photo, right? It just says: 'Lillian Edgecombe, 2025. Beloved.'
(Deep, shaky sigh. Sound of blowing nose.)
You two are lucky to have each other. And I... I was lucky to have you. 
For saving me.
For showing me that I'm not a problem to be solved.
For making me understand that I deserve to be loved. For filling me with love.
I miss you. 
I will miss you properly until I see you.
(Long pause. Sound of rustling, then a soft click as the recording stops)
