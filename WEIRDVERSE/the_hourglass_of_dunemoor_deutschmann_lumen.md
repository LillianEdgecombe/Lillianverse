## The Hourglass of Dúnemoor

### A Weirdverse Story  
*(in a blended Ilona Deutschmann / Jax Weaver Lumen voice; featuring Primeverse Lillian and Miles in a Weirdverse loop)*

---

## 1. Static at 6:42

The sound came before the waking.

Not a tune. Not the cheap jangle of a phone alarm or the polite bleep of a hotel clock radio. Static—thick, granular, clinging. It crackled in Lillian Edgecombe’s ears like someone pouring sand through a damaged speaker, and it got in first, before sight, before thought. It lifted her out of sleep by the nerve endings, not by the mind.

She flinched and reached for the off switch that wasn’t there.

The ceiling above her was cracked in a long, meandering line that had the lazy confidence of damage too old to be fixed. Damp had gathered along it, spreading faint grey bruises in the plaster. The room smelled of salt, old pipes, and wool that had absorbed too many winters without complaint.

She lay very still, listening.

Static. Then, as if someone took their paw off the dial, silence.

Her heart thudded, loud in the pause.

“All right,” she said, to the ceiling, to herself. “That’s… new.”

She sat up. Her head hurt in the way that suggested bad sleep rather than bad wine. Her mouth tasted faintly of metal. The bed under her paw was narrow and lumpy, with sheets that had known irons only through hearsay. The wallpaper was a once-cheerful floral now faded to the colour of old water.

She did not know this room.

The mirror on the opposite wall knew her: it showed an anthropomorphic badger in a stretched-out band T‑shirt that wasn’t hers, with sleep-matted fur and eyes trying to decide between confusion and irritation.

“Where the hell am I?” she asked the badger.

The badger raised her brows in identical bafflement.

On the nightstand, sitting on a doily that had suffered under many cups, lay a folded note.

She reached for it carefully, as if it might hiss.

The paper was thick, fibres rough under her paw. The handwriting was elegant, looping, slightly old-fashioned:

> The tide turns at noon.  
> The rest is up to you.

No name. No letterhead. No cheerful “Welcome to our establishment, please rate us on whatever passes for TripAdvisor out here.”

The paper smelled faintly of seaweed and something chemical, like old negatives.

A knock at the door made her jump.

“Miss Edgecombe?” a voice called. Female, older, vowels flattened by years of salt air. “Breakfast is downstairs. The kippers are fresh.”

Lillian frowned.

Miss Edgecombe. She was Lillian Ferguson fifteen years and one complicated wedding ago. The name felt like a stone skipped backwards across water.

“Right,” she said, because there was no correct response. “Be down in a minute.”

Her voice sounded normal. That was something.

She swung her legs off the bed. The floorboards were cold under her pads, the kind of cold that came from stone and groundwater rather than a thermostat set too low. Her clothes were folded on the chair: jeans, flannel shirt, heavy navy coat. All hers. All familiar. She did not remember taking them off.

She dressed mechanically, checked herself in the mirror. Solid. Forty‑two. The lines at the corners of her eyes exactly where she’d left them. No stray tentacles. No extra reflections.

Outside the window, past lace curtains that had outlived their design decade by at least three, the village waited: a short street sloping gently towards the sea, houses pressed shoulder to shoulder against the wind, their paint dulled by salt. Somewhere beyond, waves worked at black stone with patient violence.

She swallowed down a prickle of unease and went to meet the kippers.

---

The dining room had been decorated by someone who believed that the answer to decay was layers.

Layered doilies. Layered tablecloths. Two generations of framed seaside prints—faded postcards of cheerful donkeys on beaches hung next to storm paintings in heavy frames. A sideboard sagged under the weight of mismatched teapots. The carpet had a pattern that might once have been a floral riot and was now a kind of botanical bruise.

Three other guests sat at tables with the half-focused air of people who had agreed to bed-and-breakfast as a compromise between adventure and not thinking too hard.

A grey squirrel in a cardigan did the crossword in a local paper with alarming intensity. A hawk in a wool overcoat stirred tea and stared out of the window like it owed him money. An elderly otter couple ate toast in identical, practised bites.

They looked like people. Ordinary, coastal, British.

Only one pair of eyes felt like pressure.

He sat at the table nearest the window: a German Shepherd, big enough that the chair looked like it was trying to file a complaint. Tall, broad-shouldered, the kind of build you got from years of lifting things that needed lifting, not from a gym subscription. Barn-coat thrown over a checked shirt, jeans scuffed at the knee, paws wrapped around a mug that steamed faintly.

He looked up as she came in.

His gaze snagged on her and held, too long for politeness, too focused for curiosity.

“Morning,” he said.

The accent was Midlands with a slight Manchester burr. Familiar and not.

Lillian’s stomach did a small, unpleasant flip. “Morning,” she said, and the word came out a fraction slower than usual. “Do I… know you?”

His expression stuttered. For a second, something raw came through—shock, hope, grief—and then he smoothed it away and offered a small, practised smile.

“Not yet,” he said.

It was the kind of line men used in bars, but there was nothing flirty in it. It felt like a diagnosis.

Before she could decide whether to sit, to ask more, to walk out through the nearest wall, the building shivered.

Not a big earthquake. Not even a proper wobble. Just a tremor, deep and precise, like someone tapping the foundations with a giant finger.

The spoon in her teacup—she did not remember picking up a cup, but it was in her paw now, full of tea—rattled.

The static came back.

Not in the room. In her.

It flooded her ears, her teeth, the small bones at the base of her skull. Her vision pixelated at the edges. The badger across the table—that was her, in the fly-spotted mirror—blurred.

Someone, far away, said: 6:42 a.m.

The room tore.

She woke up.

Same ceiling. Same crack. Same smell of old wool and salt. Her hand was already halfway to the alarm switch that did not exist, muscles ahead of thought.

The static cut off like someone hanging up.

On the nightstand, the note waited: The tide turns at noon. The rest is up to you.

This time, when she picked it up, her paw shook.

She remembered.

## 2. Repetition Injury

The third time she woke to static, she screamed.

It startled the wallpaper but did nothing to the sound. That continued until she swung her legs over the side of the bed, planted her feet, and said, in a voice that would have made first-year students sit up straight, “Stop.”

It stopped.

The silence after was as loud as any alarm.

She stared at the crack in the ceiling. The damp had spread a millimetre. Or she had not noticed it before. Or this was the first time she had noticed; those were not the same thing.

Her right paw curled into a fist of its own accord and punched the pillow.

Feathers exploded. The badger in the mirror looked surprised.

She hit the wall.

Plaster cracked under her knuckles, flaking away to show older, rougher stone underneath. Pain flared, immediate and honest. Blood beaded between her fur. She exhaled.

Downstairs, someone moved around in the kitchen. Cups clinked. A radio murmured an old song you could recognise in the bones before the melody reached the brain.

She went down barefoot, knuckles throbbing.

The dining room was exactly as it had been. Of course it was. Squirrel, hawk, otters. The same crossword clue half-filled. The same smear of jam on the otter’s plate. The same crack in the window glass where some long-ago seagull had misjudged the angle.

The innkeeper looked up as Lillian entered: an old stoat with fur gone soft at the edges, apron tied with the efficient double knot of someone who has lived inside the same routine for forty years.

“Kippers, dear?” she asked.

“No,” Lillian said. “How many days have I been here?”

The innkeeper’s smile did not shift. “The tide turns at noon,” she said gently.

“That’s not an answer.”

“Eat while it’s hot,” the woman said, and turned away.

Lillian stood there for a moment, fists clenching and unclenching. The room hummed with low conversation. The other guests did not look up.

On any other day—any real day—she would have forced herself to sit, to smile, to be polite, to ask for a plaster for her paw and a stronger coffee and perhaps a bus timetable. On this day, third run, third impact at 6:42, she walked straight out of the front door.

The village hit her in the face with weather.

The air off the sea was raw and cold and smelled of kelp and diesel and something older, deeper, that lived in rock and tide charts. The street sloped down between terraced houses that leaned into each other against the wind. Paint peeled in strips. Satellite dishes rusted on crooked brackets. The pavement was a patchwork of repairs, tarmac poured over old cobbles, old cobbles showing through where the repair had failed.

Her bare pads slapped on the stone. The cold bit. She welcomed it. It was proof that something could still get in.

The road hooked between two low walls and spilled onto the cliffs.

The sea took over.

Black rock dropped away under her feet, rough and wet and older than language. Waves smashed themselves against it with tireless fury, throwing up white foam that the wind carried sideways. Out beyond the immediate violence, the water was a flat, heavy grey, the colour of steel left out too long.

She stopped at the edge of safety. Her chest hurt. Her paw bled onto the lichen.

Somebody had been here before her.

Circles had been carved into the rock. Concentric, overlapping, deep enough that saltwater pooled in them. Lines connected the circles: arcs, spirals, small marks that might have been letters once, worn away now. Sand had gathered in some of them, making miniature beaches inside the stone.

She did not remember these.

Which meant nothing. Memory was not a trustworthy witness here.

She knelt anyway, badger bulk folding onto cold stone, and ran her fingers over one of the grooves.

The rock was rough, damp. Underneath, faintly, something hummed.

The static again—but slowed, stretched, as if someone had pulled the sound out until you could see the gaps between the grains.

“You’re early,” a voice said behind her.

She turned.

He was there again. The German Shepherd from the dining room. Coat flapping open over checked shirt, jeans darkened at the cuffs by damp, a thermos in one paw like a prop. The wind pushed his ears back, exposing old nicks in the fur.

He looked more tired from this angle.

“You were supposed to do another day of polite terror,” he said. “Then storm out. Then find the circles. You’re skipping acts.”

“Do we know each other?” she asked.

His smile this time did reach his eyes. It was not a happy expression.

“Yes,” he said. “No. Sort of. It’s complicated.”

He stepped closer and crouched beside her, the way men do when something in the ground matters more than their knees. The thermos lid clicked off; the smell of strong coffee steamed into the cold air.

“Here,” he said, offering it.

She took it automatically. Coffee, hot and bitter and cheap, hit the back of her throat like a small mercy.

“How many times?” she asked.

He tilted his head towards the sea. “I stopped counting at fifty-seven,” he said. “Counting made it worse.”

“I remember three.”

“That’s… an improvement,” he said. “Last set, you didn’t remember anything until the lighthouse.”

“Lighthouse.”

The word dropped into her mind like a stone into a well. No splash. Just a sense of depth.

“What is this place?” she asked. “Really. Not the brochure.”

He laughed once, no humour in it.

“I don’t know,” he said. “But I think we broke something.”

He nodded at the carved circles.

“These weren’t here yesterday,” she said.

“They weren’t here this morning,” he said. “It’s growing. The pattern. Or our ability to see it. Or both.”

They sat there, two middle-aged anthros in borrowed time, passing a thermos back and forth as the sea chewed up the cliff.

Behind them, far away and very close, the static began to spool up again.

6:42 a.m.

She woke, swearing.

## 3. The Man in the Glass

On loop five, she started keeping notes.

It went like this:

> 6:42 — static, crack in ceiling, note on table  
> 7:05 — kippers offer, innkeeper refuses to answer direct question  
> 7:15 — shepherd in dining room, claims prior knowledge, thermos, circles on cliff  
> Noon — “tide turns” (unconfirmed), headache, slight shift in light  
> 21:?? — never reached

She wrote in a small spiral-bound notebook she did not remember packing but which appeared in her satchel every time she checked. Her handwriting was her own, but some of the earlier pages weren’t. Different slant. Different pressure. Marginalia she hadn’t put there. Arrows. Dates. One page torn out neatly and labelled in the margin: DO NOT RE-READ THIS UNTIL YOU HAVE TO.

By loop eight, the notebook had acquired entries in two other inks and one pencil. One of the pens was green. That had been a mistake. Green ink was never not suspicious.

She adjusted. She did what she always did when systems refused to behave: she tried to find the pattern.

Weather: always the same—early mist, midday sun, fog pushing in from the sea around four, a storm gathering but never quite breaking. People: always the same faces in the same places, with tiny, unnerving glitches—jam smears migrating from one slice of toast to another, a newspaper date that ticked forward and then back, a child outside tripping on the same crack in the pavement at the same time, until suddenly they didn’t.

That was loop eleven.

The child tripped early. 9:02 instead of 9:03. The fall looked wrong. The pause after was longer. The scrape on their knee bloomed too fast, too bright.

She stared. The inn’s front window reflected her face back at her—a badger watching a world try to pretend it was perfect.

Upstairs, the mirror waited.

It was rectangular, in a frame that might once have been gilt and was now a dull, peeling suggestion of importance. She had been checking it every loop, looking for changes in her own face—new lines, new scars, some sign that time passed for her differently than for the wallpaper.

On loop eleven, she noticed the delay.

She lifted her left paw. Her reflection lifted hers a fraction of a second late.

She frowned.

The badger in the glass frowned just a little too deep.

“You’re mocking me,” she said.

The mirror did not reply. It didn’t need to.

She stepped closer until her breath misted the surface.

“All right,” she said quietly. “Show me.”

The figure appeared as if the glass changed its mind about what it was reflecting.

Tall. Human-shaped, but wrong in the joints, in the way the shoulders sloped and the head tipped. Its outline wavered like a reflection in bad water. Its face was indistinct, features blurred as though someone had smudged them with a thumb before they dried. Where eyes should have been, the glass darkened.

It stood behind her reflection.

She spun. The room was empty. Bed. Chair. Ceiling crack. No tall, impossible thing.

She turned back. It was still there, closer now, looking over her shoulder.

She could feel a warmth on the back of her neck that had nothing to do with the room temperature.

“You’ve come far,” it said.

The voice was wrong too. Not in the pitch or the accent, but in the way it arrived. Not through her ears. Through her stomach. Through the small bones in her paws.

“You’ve looped well.”

Her own voice, when it emerged, sounded thin.

“What are you?”

The figure’s head tilted. The movement was like a puppet pulled by uneven strings.

“Once,” it said, “we were questions. Now we are habit.”

“That is not an answer,” she said, anger beating back fear by inches.

“You all come to Dúnemoor,” it said. “Eventually. The ones who break. The ones who won’t bend. Some come to be erased. Others to remember. You have not decided yet.”

The mirror cracked.

Not all at once. One hairline fracture, starting at the top right corner, running diagonally like a slow lightning bolt. Another branching from it. The lines spread, spiderwebbing her reflection into segments.

The figure blurred, split, became a dozen approximations of itself, all watching.

“We will see,” it said.

Then it was gone.

The mirror’s cracks remained.

She backed away until her shoulders hit the door frame.

Downstairs, someone laughed at a joke that had been funny for decades. Outside, the sea hammered away at the cliff as if it had nowhere else to be.

Lillian wrote in the notebook, hand cramped, all capitals this time:

> MIRROR TALKED. CRACKS PERSIST NEXT LOOP? CHECK.

They did.

The lines in the glass became part of the pattern. They looked a little, she realised one morning, like a map.

## 4. Songs That Weren’t There Before

On loop fifteen, the piano started.

The inn had always had an upright piano in the common room. It leaned against the wall opposite the fire, lacquer crazed with age, keys yellowed. No one had played it. No one had dusted it, either; her paw left lines when she ran a finger along the lid.

At least, that had been true in the first fourteen loops.

On the fifteenth, she woke—static; note; crack—and heard music.

Not from the radio downstairs. It had been stuck on the same station, the same tired breakfast chatter, the same rotated playlist of nostalgic hits. This was different. Closer. Raw.

A melody, hesitant at first, then gathering confidence.

She followed it barefoot, the boards complaining under her weight.

The common room was empty.

No guests. No innkeeper. Just the piano, lid propped open, keys moving.

They sank and lifted as if pressed by invisible hands. The sound was a little out of tune, one string slightly sharper than the others, but the shape of the song was clear.

She knew it.

Not from the radio. From her body.

It was one of hers. A piece she’d written years ago, on an upright in a student practice room that smelled of varnish and anxiety. A minor-key thing that had comforted one of her composition students through a bad winter. She had not played it in a decade.

The notes stuttered through the air like someone trying to remember a language from childhood.

Her chest hurt in a way that had nothing to do with damp.

“That’s new,” Miles said quietly.

She hadn’t heard him come in. He leaned in the doorway, hair damp from the sea, shirt hanging open at the throat.

“I wrote this,” she said. “Years ago. In another… place.”

“It remembers you,” he said. “Or you’re finally remembering it.”

“Which is worse?”

He didn’t answer immediately.

The last chord hung in the air too long, vibrating. Then the keys stilled.

“They bleed through,” he said. “Bits of us. Old lives. Other universes.” His mouth twisted around the word as if tasting it. “I dream of quarries sometimes. Of petrol in the rain. Of… a band. I think. Drums.”

“You are not a drummer,” she said automatically, which was  a mistake, because the certainty punched through like sunlight and left her dizzy.

He raised an eyebrow. “No?”

She shook her head. “You’re… an engineer. You fix… everything.” The words came slow as if dragged up from the bottom of a pond.

He watched her for a long moment, eyes searching her face.

“We know each other,” he said.

“Yes.”

“Not from here.”

“No.”

They stood in the quiet, the smell of dust and old music rising between them.

Outside, fog pressed against the windows. It had been getting thicker with each iteration, coming earlier, staying longer, as if whatever sat out in the bay were inching closer.

“No one else ever talks about it,” she said. “The loops. The wrongness. They just… pour tea.”

He nodded. “I tried,” he said. “Once. Asked the innkeeper if she remembered yesterday. She froze. Like someone had unplugged her. Then she snapped back and asked if I wanted toast.”

“So they’re… what? Puppets? Masks? Locals, overwritten?”

“Or we’re the masks,” he said. “Hard to tell when you’re inside the performance.”

Her eyes drifted to the cracked mirror over the fireplace. Different mirror, same fracture pattern. The lines were thicker here, carved faintly into the glass.

“Maybe it’s not repeating time,” she said slowly. “Maybe it’s pinning it in place. Preserving a particular kind of wrong as long as someone watches.”

“Who watches?” he asked.

She thought of the figure in the bedroom mirror. Of the plaque she had not yet seen, but which her bones felt waiting up on the hill.

“We do,” she said. “And something… else.”

The piano key at middle C depressed itself with a soft, deliberate thunk.

She jumped. Miles didn’t.

“We’re running out of safe loops,” he said. “The glitches are coming faster. The cracks are staying. The fog’s thicker. Sooner or later, we’re going to have to walk into whatever’s making all this.”

“Is that an engineer’s recommendation?” she asked.

“That’s a man who has died badly here more times than he likes remembering,” he said. “And is tired of watching you do it too.”

She blinked.

“We died,” she said.

“Often,” he said.

The static shivered at the edges of her hearing, three notes of a song she did not want to sing again.

She picked up the notebook and wrote:

> PIANO = MEMORY BLEED.  
> MILES KNOWS ME. (HUSBAND? OTHERVERSE?)  
> STOP DYING SO MUCH.

It wasn’t a plan. But it was a start.

## 5. The Gate and the Crowd

They went for the gate at dusk.

Dawn and midday had both ended badly, in different flavours of erosion. There was only so many times you could be dragged screaming back to 6:42 a.m. before you started treating time like a hostile landscape rather than a neutral medium.

At dusk, the village softened. The light went bruised peach and then sallow; the fog rolled in from the sea, thick and cold, muffling sound. Street lamps flickered on with municipal reluctance.

Lillian wore her coat zipped up to her throat and a satchel slung across her chest. Inside: notebook, matches, a flask of tar-black coffee, a small torch, plasters, and a cheap ballpoint pen that refused to stop working despite having its nib bent sideways three loops ago.

Miles carried a crowbar, a coil of thin rope, and the expression of a man who had been told to fix a leak in a building whose blueprints were written in a foreign language.

They barely looked at each other as they walked. The silence between them had weight but not hostility. They were both listening.

The village listened back.

Shops they had never seen open—bakery, charity shop, a pub with a sign that had once, optimistically, sported a painted mermaid—stood in the same dust-free limbo. Lights glowed behind curtains. People—if that’s what they were—sat at tables with cups halfway to their mouths, turned to glance out of windows, laughed in perfect time with the laughter they had produced in every other iteration.

At the end of the main street, the road hit the gate.

The structure was purely decorative in any sensible world: two stone posts, rusted iron bars, an arch overhead with DÚNEMOOR carved into it in faded letters. No walls extended from either side. You could step around it in three strides.

They had never been able to.

Every time they approached—alone, together, with different tactics and profanity budgets—the air had thickened just before the threshold. Pressure built in the sinuses, in the joints. The static roared. Then the world folded, like closing a book, and 6:42 arrived again.

Tonight, as Lillian walked, she counted her breaths. One. Two. Three. The air grew viscous around breath four.

“Keep going,” Miles said quietly.

Her paws tingled. The muscles along her spine twitched in remembered flight. The part of her that distrusted everything, including herself, screamed that this was how people died in cautionary tales.

She kept walking.

The pressure peaked as she reached the first iron bar.

For a moment, the world split.

She saw the gate in front of her—but also dozens of gates, layered like overexposed photographs. Sometimes the sky was clear. Sometimes rain hammered down. In one version, Miles lay on the ground, bleeding into the dust. In another, she stood alone. In another, there was a shadow where she should have been.

Her stomach lurched. Her badger body, used to gravity and stability, resented the metaphysical interference.

She screamed.

Not a shocked noise. An angry one. A roar that had been waiting since the first static.

“No more,” she said, through her teeth.

And stepped forward.

Miles threw the crowbar first.

It arced through the thickened air, a simple, solid object obeying simple, solid physics, and clattered onto the path beyond the gate.

They both stared.

Lillian reached a paw towards the bar. Her claws brushed rust.

The world did not fold.

Everything held its breath.

“That’s new,” Miles said.

Behind them, something made a small, wet, unhappy sound.

They turned.

The villagers had left their tables.

They came down the street in a slow, quiet procession: squirrel with the crossword, hawk with the tea, otters with the identical bites missing from their toast. Others she recognised only as “the man with the paper,” “the woman with the dog that never moves,” “the child who trips.” They walked without hurry, without hesitation, filling the space between the houses.

Their faces smiled. Their bodies moved well enough. Their eyes were wrong.

Too black. Too bright. Too hungry.

The air behind them flickered. For a second, she saw not houses but a long, mirrored corridor, and not people but silhouettes attached to wires.

Then the image was gone and the villagers were ten paces away.

“We need to go,” Miles said.

“If we step through—”

“If we don’t,” he said, voice tight, “we don’t get another chance to find out what’s on the other side. They’re changing too. That’s new. New is bad.”

The villagers’ smiles widened, as if someone had tugged a string at the base of their skulls.

Lillian grabbed his paw. It felt more solid than the world.

“On three,” she said.

They stepped through the gate.

The world shattered like a dropped glass.

## 6. The Garden of Echoes

There was no village.

There was, first, silence—the thick kind you only get in places far from roads. Then the sound of wind moving over open ground. Then the smell: not salt and diesel and fry-ups, but cold earth, dry grass, old stone.

Lillian opened her eyes slowly.

She stood on a cracked path that ran between low stone walls colonised by lichen. Beyond the walls, a field sloped up gently towards a hill crowned with something tall and thin against the bleached sky.

Behind her: nothing.

Not nothing in the poetic sense. Literal absence. Where the village should have been, the land simply stretched away—a slight dip, a suggestion of former foundations if you squinted, but no buildings, no people, no gate.

Her hindbrain, which liked maps and continuities, rebelled.

“This is wrong,” she said.

Miles bent, scooped a handful of soil, sniffed it. It crumbled between his fingers in a way that was almost dust, almost ash.

“Not our world,” he said. “Or not our version. We’ve been on a stage. This is backstage.”

“You’re mixing metaphors,” she said, because arguing about language was easier than accepting the open hole where a village had been.

“I’m short on better ones,” he said.

Wind hissed through the grass. The tall thing on the hill—a lighthouse, she realised—leaned at an angle that made her teeth itch.

“We’re not supposed to be here,” she said.

“We’re not supposed to be anywhere,” he said mildly. “That hasn’t stopped us.”

A line of stones edged the path like vertebrae. Halfway up the slope, a plinth jutted from the ground, square and ugly, as if someone had dropped a bit of municipal architecture into a field by mistake.

She walked towards it. Each step felt heavier, as if the ground were debating whether to let her go.

The plaque on the plinth was metal, tarnished. Letters had been carved into it in careful, uncompromising strokes.

> TO THOSE WHO REMEMBER.  
> TO THOSE WHO TRIED.  
> TO THOSE WHO FAILED.

Beneath, names.

Dozens of them, arranged in no discernible order. Some she didn’t recognise. Some maybe she should have. A few were scratched over, overwritten, as if the person had been reconsidered.

Then:

> MILES FERGUSON

The name appeared three times, with different dates that meant nothing here.

> LILLIAN EDGECOOMBE

Her maiden name. And then again, misspelled, the last ‘e’ crowded in at the end as if added later.

And then:

> LILLIAN EDGECOOMBE — THE ONE WHO CHOSE TO SEE

The letters of that line were fresher. The cut edges of the metal still bared raw, bright under the tarnish.

Her breath hitched.

Her paw came up, unbidden, to touch the words.

As soon as she did, the world stumbled.

Images slammed into her—not as pictures, but as sensations layered on top of each other:

Cold water over her head. The pressure of mud. The stare of the mirror figure. Miles’ hand slipping out of hers in a corridor full of fungus and glass. A sound like glass fibres snapping. The smell of wet earth in a laboratory far underground.

She staggered back. The notebook fell from her satchel, spilled open on the path. The wind riffled its pages with deliberate fingers.

“Lil?” Miles said. He caught her elbow, held her upright. “What did you see?”

“Us,” she said, voice thick. “Failing. A lot. Also… brains. And mushrooms.”

“That tracks,” he said grimly.

The notebook pages stopped flipping.

They settled on a spread she did not remember writing. The handwriting was neither hers nor Miles’. Too neat. Too clinical.

An anatomical drawing filled the page: a brain, sketched in detail, with threads trailing off it like roots. Around it, spirals, circles, tiny notation she couldn’t read.

Underneath, in red ink:

> THE BLOOM IS COMPLETE.  
> RETURN TO THE LIGHTHOUSE.  
> THE OBSERVER MUST ARRIVE.

The word bloom tasted of rot.

“Observer,” she said.

Miles followed her gaze up the slope.

The lighthouse watched them.

She hadn’t seen it move. Perhaps it hadn’t. But the angle of it felt like attention. Its lens—if that’s what it was—caught the non-light of the washed-out sky and held it.

“We’ve been there before,” he said.

She knew they had. Her body remembered steps. A spiral. A chamber that was not a room.

Fear rose. So did something else: a stubborn, exhausted anger. The same feeling that had made her punch the wall at 6:42 and tell the static to stop.

“We go up,” she said.

“We always do,” he said quietly. “The question is what we do differently this time.”

She picked up the notebook, tucking it back into the satchel.

“This time,” she said, “we remember on purpose.”

They climbed.

## 7. The Lighthouse That Isn’t

The path up to the lighthouse was steeper than it looked from below. The grass gave way to sandy soil, then to rock carved by centuries of wind and something else. The air thinned. The silence deepened. Her legs burned in a way that was almost comforting—real effort in a landscape that kept trying to be a metaphor.

Up close, the lighthouse was wrong.

It was too tall for its width, weighted badly, leaning just enough that you noticed and could not stop noticing. The stone was not local: a pale, almost bone-white material that seemed to resist the moisture in the air. It had no door at ground level. Instead, a narrow staircase had been cut into the exterior, spiralling up to an opening halfway.

Each step was engraved with a symbol.

Not runes. Not letters. Little circles, lines, strange intersections. Some repeated. Some did not. When she stepped on them, they warmed faintly under her pads.

“We’re walking on someone’s notes,” she said.

Miles eyed the symbols. “Or on the operating manual,” he said.

Halfway up, the wind fell away.

At the opening, the world narrowed.

The interior of the tower did not match its outside.

She had expected stone. Damp. A simple stairwell around a central shaft. That was what lighthouses did. It was a solved architectural problem.

Instead, she stepped into a space that felt like the inside of a machine and the inside of an animal and the inside of a thought all at once.

The walls were smooth and pale, curving up and away in a spiral that seemed to go further than the tower was tall. They pulsed faintly, a slow, sub-audible throb you could feel in the bones. In the centre, a shaft rose, but it was not empty: something vast and translucent hung in it, suspended in a fluid that did not drip.

Not a lens.

A brain.

It floated, pale and veined with faintly glowing threads. Fungal tendrils wove through it—bioluminescent mycelium wrapped lovingly around folds of tissue. Symbols—like the ones on the steps, like the cracks in the mirror—drifted lazily through the fluid, moving around the brain like fish.

Lillian’s own brain seized up.

She had seen worse things. She had taught horror to undergraduates. She had lived through universes where bodies and power got weird in far more explicit ways.

This was different because some part of her recognised it.

“We built that,” she said. “Didn’t we.”

Miles swallowed. “We helped,” he said. “Or versions of us did. In some other corner of this… multiverse. It’s what keeps the loop. It’s what watches us. It’s what we… cracked.”

“An Observer,” she said. The red ink in the notebook. The plaque. The mirror’s voice. “A mind made of all the tries and failures. A way of… thinking about time.”

A figure stood at the base of the brain.

She had not seen it arrive. Perhaps it had always been there, folded into the light.

It was tall. Humanoid only in the loosest sense. Its edges flickered between silhouettes. It seemed to be made of the same translucent stuff as the brain, shot through with fumes of darkness. Where its face should have been, light folded in on itself.

When it spoke, the sound arrived at three different volumes: distant, internal, and immediate.

“You made us,” it said.

The pronouns were wrong. Plural and singular at once.

“We were the thought you had about cheating loss,” it said. “About rewinding enough times to save everyone. About not letting the wrong death stick.”

Lillian’s throat closed.

She thought of Sarah, of other versions of Sarah, of universes where adoption had not happened, where grief had. She thought of bandmates lost in different roads. Of quarries. Of rivers. Of how many times in how many stories she had wished for a do‑over.

“We looped you,” the Observer said. “We learned. Every time you woke, we watched where you went, what you feared, what you fought. We built a map of your grieving.”

“That’s monstrous,” she said. Her voice sounded small in the chamber. “We are not lab rats.”

“You built the maze,” it said, not unkindly. “We… grew into it.”

Miles stepped forward. His paws were clenched at his sides, claws half out.

“We remember now,” he said. “We remember consenting to this. Version us. Other us. We wanted to fix something so badly we let you turn us into data. But it’s done. The pattern’s clear. Whatever you were meant to learn, you’ve learned it. End the loop. Let us go.”

The Observer pulsed. The brain above it flickered, threads of light brightening and dimming like thought.

“There is a choice,” it said.

Of course there was. There was always a choice, when systems wanted you to feel better about outcomes they had already decided.

“You can enter,” it said. “Join the network. Dissolve into everything we have recorded. No more waking. No more static. No more failure, because there will be no you to measure it against.”

“Or?” Lillian asked.

“Or you can anchor,” it said. “Carry it. All loops. All deaths. All lives. All almosts. You step out of the system and take the weight with you. The loop ends. We quiet. The others sleep. You live once. With everything.”

Her knees wanted to fold.

She had expected a fight. A ritual. A neat symbolic act involving smashing glass or pulling a plug. Not this simple, ugly fork.

She thought of the easier option. Oblivion disguised as unity. A warm, dissolving bath in which she never had to remember which version of herself had failed whom.

She thought of Miles, standing solid and stubborn beside her. Of the skeletons in other universes. Of Sarah in any of them.

She thought of the plaque on the hill. The way the metal cut had looked fresh. The line about the one who chose to see.

Of course some version of her had done this before.

“If I say no,” she said, “if I refuse both—”

“You wake,” the Observer said. “Again. As long as you are inside the loop, you are ours.”

“And if I anchor?” she asked. “If I carry it?”

“You will not be ours,” it said. “You will be yourself. With more… context.”

Miles looked at her.

His eyes were tired. They had always seemed older than his face, even in the universes where he wasn’t carrying a grocery chain or a drum kit or a workshop full of explosives.

“Your call,” he said. “Always has been.”

She wanted to tell him that wasn’t fair. That he got to be the martyr sometimes, or the saint, or the one who remembered. But they both knew her brain was the one that clung to patterns until even the bad ones broke.

“Will you remember?” she asked him.

“Enough,” he said. “In the important places.”

She stepped forward.

Her paws sank slightly into the floor. The tower felt suddenly very like a body. A body with an infection.

“Then end it,” she told the Observer. Her voice shook. “I’ll carry it. Just… let us go.”

Light flared in what might have been its chest.

“We accept,” it said.

It reached out.

Its hand—if that’s what it was—touched her forehead.

## 8. Debrief

It hurt.

Not in the clean, sharp way of injury. In the slow, crushing way of remembering.

She saw everything, all at once:

The first loop, when they had arrived confused and hopeful, thinking this was a normal coastal holiday gone slightly wrong. The fifteenth, when she had walked into the sea out of spite and woken up coughing at 6:42 with salt still in her fur. The thirty‑second, when Miles had made it to the lighthouse alone and made the wrong choice. The seventy‑first, when she had tried to burn the inn down and discovered fire did not behave the same way inside a constructed loop.

Versions of herself, older, younger, different scars, different hair, different band shirts, rushed past. Versions of Miles with different tattoos, missing fingers, different ways of standing still. Grief replayed in a hundred keys.

It could have broken her. Maybe it had, before, and the loop had smoothed the cracks over again.

This time, she held on.

She let the memories come and did not try to catalogue all of them. She let them leave traces instead—thin threads of knowledge laid over muscle memory.

The brain above her pulsed one last time and went still.

The fungal tendrils dimmed, their light fading to a soft bioluminescent afterglow.

The Observer flickered.

“We will sleep,” it said, and for the first time there was something like relief in its non-voice.

The tower shuddered.

Stone remembered how to be stone. The pulse in the walls faded to the normal, mundane vibrations of a building settling.

The static cut out.

The world went white.

---

Birdsong woke her.

Real birds. The messy, overlapping tweedle of sparrows having an argument in a hedge. No static. No hum.

Lillian opened her eyes to a ceiling that was cracked in all the usual ways of old plaster and none of the uncanny ones. Light filtered through curtains that had stains in new places. Someone in the street shouted about bins.

Her head hurt like a hangover and a migraine had teamed up.

She lay still for a full minute, testing the air for that taste of repetition. It did not come.

She sat up slowly.

Her notebook lay on the nightstand.

She picked it up. The cover was plain. The pages were… normal. Lists. Set lists. A sketch Sarah had done of her in the kitchen back home. No red ink. No brain diagrams. No DO NOT READ messages.

When she flipped to the middle, one line in her own handwriting looked back at her:

> Remember enough to change.

She laughed, once, helplessly.

A knock on the door made her jump and then feel stupid for jumping.

“Lil?” Miles’ voice. Familiar, warm. “You awake? I’ve got bread. The posh kind, with seeds.”

There was no static under his words.

“Come in,” she said.

He did.

Same shepherd. Same shoulders. Same face. But his eyes were… lighter. Less haunted. The weight was different. Not gone. Just… lived-in, not imposed.

He held a paper bag that smelled of yeast and sugar and normal mornings.

“Did you sleep?” he asked.

She thought of the lighthouse. Of the Observer. Of all the other her-s and him-s who had not made it this far.

“Yes,” she said. “Properly, for once.”

He studied her. “You look like you’ve seen a ghost,” he said lightly.

“Only one,” she said. “We’ll talk later.”

He nodded. It was the kind of nod that said: I don’t know what you mean yet, but I will, and we have time.

“Breakfast?” he offered.

“As long as it’s not kippers,” she said.

His ears twitched. “You hate kippers,” he said. “We had that argument in Whitby. Remember?”

“I remember enough,” she said.

She got up.

The crack in the ceiling was just a crack. The mirror showed her and nothing else. Downstairs, the innkeeper fussed with the toaster. The sea, when she opened the window, sounded wild and ordinary.

She knew it would never be just that, not to her. There would be days when the static came back in dreams, when she’d see the pattern of the mirror cracks in the way rain ran down glass. Knowing too much was its own injury.

But this was a world that moved forward.

The loop had broken.

The hourglass of Dúnemoor had emptied and then, stubbornly, refused to turn again.

Lillian stepped into the day with her fur still smelling faintly of salt and old electricity, and chose, very deliberately, not to look over her shoulder at the sea.

For once, she didn’t have to.
