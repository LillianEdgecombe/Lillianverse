# Fangs of Autumn

## 1. Crimson Wood

The priest arrived first, as if the forest had been waiting for him.

A sea of crimson maple leaves lay thick over the earth, rustling under a bruised sky. The late-afternoon light was a pale, exhausted thing, diffused through low cloud like a dying ember under ash. Wind moved through the trees in narrow streams, not gusts but threads, hissing softly, as if the branches were conferring.

The priest’s robes had once been white. They had seen too many fires since. Sun-bleached, smoke-stained at the hem, they dragged through the leaves as he walked, sandals scuffing. Behind him, trailing from a lacquered chest strapped to his back, unspooled a rope of shide: zigzag paper charms, hand-cut, hand-folded, stiff against the damp.

Every few paces he stopped, knelt, and set a trap.

They were iron things, lotus-shaped, rusted and polished again, old and re-sanctified. Each he laid with careful hands, fingers lingering on each petal as he murmured the same words, not quite a prayer and not quite a curse.

“Let no fox pass. Let no tanuki twist the veil. Let masks return to earth, and rot.”

The shrine waited ahead of him, half-consumed by moss. The torii leaned, its crossbeam sagging as if drunk on the weight of offerings it no longer received. Under the eaves, ema plaques—tiny wooden tablets scrawled with aging wishes—swayed on a wind that barely touched the leaves. The roof tiles were broken in places; ferns had taken the gaps. It looked, Lillian thought, like a throat that had tried to heal over around a foreign body.

She watched from the trees.

Her robes were the colour of old leaves: ochre, russet, gold edged in black where the hem had brushed too many fires. Her hair was pinned up with simple combs, not the lacquered ornaments the city women favoured, and her face was painted as for mourning—white, with a trace of red at the mouth that said nothing about joy.

She did not speak. She had learned that words, once heard, had a way of writing themselves into other people’s memories in shapes she could not control.

The priest’s traps pulsed faintly when he set them, a brief shimmer under the rust, like old iron remembering having been more than metal. She could taste the spell on the air: salt, sake, ink, and the bitter tang of fear disguised as righteousness.

He reached the foot of the shrine steps and stopped. His breath steamed. The rope of shide had shortened. The chest on his back looked lighter, bones of its frame more defined beneath the lacquer.

He spat a mouthful of sake onto the shrine floor.

Something inside the shrine shuddered. It was not a sound, not precisely. A change in pressure, a ripple through the leaves. The forest shifted its attention.

“Let masks return to earth,” the priest repeated. “And rot.”

It was not clear whether he meant hers.

He paused then, still as if listening to something only he could hear. His head turned, slowly, toward the trees where she stood.

“Show yourself,” he said. “Beast.”

His voice was roughened by years of shouting at things that did not listen. It carried anyway. Dry leaves trembled. A crow barked once in the distance, then fell silent.

Lillian did not move. Her hands rested in her sleeves, one thumb brushing the edge of the mask hanging from her hip. Wooden, oval, painted with the suggestion of her own features—an approximation, like a memory drawn by someone who had seen her once.

The priest sniffed, as if testing the air.

“I know you’re there,” he said. “I smelled your grief.”

He raised a wand etched with seals, the paper worn soft from handling. He snapped his wrist; one of the lotus traps snapped shut with a groan that echoed strangely in the quiet wood. Bark peeled from the nearest tree as if struck by heat.

The air thickened. The wind drew back.

The priest lunged, straight toward her, as if pulled by the thread of his own conviction. His wand swung down.

It met another staff.

The iron rings at the head of the shakujō chimed as they took the blow. The sound ran through the clearing, clean and dissonant, cutting the chant in half.

Miles had arrived without ceremony. He rarely did anything with it.

Tall, broad-shouldered, fur the colour of autumn wheat and old bruises, he wore a traveller’s robe patchworked from sturdier cloth with a monk’s care. His face was canine—Akita Inu, if anyone had been inclined to categorise; broad muzzle, dark eyes, ears that refused to lie flat even when he tried. His staff had the worn polish of having been used more often to keep people from falling than to knock them down.

“Not every leaf is a demon,” he said. “Not every shadow is yours to cleanse.”

The priest snarled, teeth yellowish in the low light.

“That thing wears a woman’s face,” he said, jerking his chin toward the trees.

“Then it’s braver than most men I know,” Miles said.

Their staffs sparked when they met again. His shakujō’s iron rings struck the seals on the priest’s wand. The charm-paper sizzled. The smell of scorched ink joined the damp-leaf scent.

Lillian watched the exchange with a stillness that cost effort. Her body wanted to move, to slip further back into the trees, to retreat into mist and become someone else. Years of survival had taught her that disappearing at the right moment was as important a skill as any spell.

But the traps were new. The priest was new. The way his shide rope flickered when he spoke suggested the involvement of powers that predated any village and would outlast any single priest. She could not assume that what had saved her before would work again.

“You defend it?” the priest spat. “You, mongrel? No wonder the gods cursed your face.”

The insult rolled over Miles like water over stone, leaving a slick trace and little else. He had heard worse, and more imaginative, from children with less cause.

“You’ll trip over your own traps if you keep shouting,” he said.

He shifted his weight, not quite stepping back, not quite pressing forward. The priest’s wand dipped. The moment stretched.

Lillian slid her thumb along the edge of her mask. It warmed, faintly. The forest held its breath.

Then the priest stepped back with a hiss, as if some tension inside him had been cut. His eyes darted to the shrine, to the unmoving doors, then to the ancient torii. Whatever agreement he had with the powers he served did not extend to breaking certain boundaries without invitation.

“You’re already lost,” he said to Miles. “You just haven’t realised how far.”

He turned and limped away, shide rope dragging. The traps he had set shuddered once, as if offended, but did not spring.

When he had gone far enough that his footsteps no longer disturbed the leaves, all the lotus traps snapped shut at once.

Rust flaked away from their teeth. The iron bloomed briefly bright, then crumbled, collapsing inward into piles of red dust that the next wind rolled into the moss.

On the stone at the base of the shrine, where no foot had stepped, new scratches had appeared. Miles noticed them first, because he knew where to look for the traces of those who preferred not to be seen.

Hunter meets hunted,
the leaf does not ask the wind
if it meant to fall.

He read the characters silently, lips moving. The verse tucked itself into his mind the way such things always did. He did not need to turn his head to know that she had moved, that the presence in the trees had shifted, that he could no longer say with any confidence where Lillian stood.

He tapped the staff once against the earth, a small gesture of thanks—not to the priest, not to any shrine, but to the line of verse. Then he turned away, leaving the traps to their rust.

High above them, a crow shook its feathers and muttered to itself, as if filing a note on the day: priest, dog, mask, leaf. It would remember. The forest always did.


## 2. Riverbank

The river remembered more than it told.

It moved through the valley with the patient insistence of someone tracing the same line of a story over and over until all other versions wore away. Pebbles shifted under its current, carrying dust, stray words, fragments of incense ash and bone.

Miles sat on the bank at dusk, his paws planted in the damp earth, his staff laid horizontally in front of him. The fire at his side was small, more embers than flame, its smoke rising in a lazy twist toward the first stars.

He was tracing characters into the dirt with the butt of the staff, half prayer and half habit. Simple things: numbers, fragments of sutra, the outline of a ward-sign he had once written a hundred times on paper and now, for lack of ink, rehearsed in soil.

“Five breaths to center,” he murmured. “One breath to remember. Water knows my name. Earth does not.”

It was an old saying from the monastery where he had almost become something else. The rhythm still calmed him, though the meaning had never felt quite right. Water forgot plenty. Earth was very attached to its dead.

Steps rustled in the reeds behind him, then stopped. No accompanying breeze, no breath he could hear. The fur along his neck rose, then settled.

He did not reach for his staff. He did not turn, yet.

“You’re quiet,” he said instead. “For someone the priest scented at thirty paces.”

Silence answered. It was not an empty one. It had edges.

He glanced over his shoulder.

Lillian stood at the edge of the firelight.

The robes were simpler tonight, travel cloth tied easily at the waist, colours muted to river-soft browns and greys. Her hair was unadorned, tied in one knot at the nape of her neck. The mask hung at her side, cord looped around her wrist. In her hands she held a wrapped bundle of food.

Grilled mushrooms, rice balls, pickled plum. Enough for two people who had not planned to meet.

She crossed the last few steps to the fire and knelt, placing the bundle down within reach but not, he noticed, offering it. That would require a kind of presumption she had not yet granted herself.

Her knees did not disturb the grass.

He nodded once, the kind of thank you the road taught—economical, sincere, without the fuss of temple etiquette.

They ate without commentary. The rice was slightly burnt at the edges, which he appreciated. Burnt rice reminded him that someone had been distracted by something else while cooking. It made the food feel lived in.

Crickets started their thin, rehearsed chorus. One faltered, as if forgetting the next line, then resumed. The river hummed its usual monotony. Miles watched Lillian watching the water.

“Not that I am complaining,” he said eventually, “but you are more silent than I expected.”

She tore a piece of mushroom with small, precise fingers. Her eyes reflected the firelight, not as an overlay but as an additive quality, as if something inside her welcomed flame.

When she spoke, it was in verse.

“Tongue is for liars,” she said. “Leaves speak true when they fall free. I fall like they do.”

The words were haiku, but the voice was not ornamental. It bent the form around something practical: a reluctance to commit simple statements to air.

He had met other yōkai who liked poetry. Kitsune, especially, were fond of it, curling phrases around themselves the way they curled around their tails. But Lillian’s verses sat closer to bone. They felt like the tips of longer thoughts she had refused to say.

On the opposite bank, a fox sat on a stone, white fur sharp against the dark. Its tails—three, he counted—flicked in idle rhythm. It watched them with the decided concentration of someone trying to determine whether what they were seeing would matter later.

Lillian’s shoulders tightened.

“Friend of yours?” Miles asked.

She shook her head, once.

“Foxes see through masks,” she said, again in the cadence of verse. “But seeing is not knowing. They know only fear.”

The fox snorted, offended in a way that suggested it understood more than he did. It vanished behind a cedar without disturbing the branches.

“They’ll talk,” Miles said.

“They always do,” she said. “Talking is what saves them, and what kills them.”

He considered that.

“And you?” he asked. “What saves you?”

She glanced at him, then back at the river.

“The spaces between questions,” she said.

They listened to the water. He did not ask the next question. It felt important, somehow, not to.

Later, when the fire dwindled and the night took the edges of the trees, they lay down on opposite sides of the embers. He watched the flicker through half-closed eyes. On her side, her silhouette blurred at the edges, as if the darkness had more claim on her than the light.

The river moved on, carrying their reflections without keeping them.


## 3. The Shrine That Still Breathes

Dawn came reluctantly to that part of the forest, sliding in thin bands between branches as if remembering another morning it had regretted illuminating.

The path Lillian chose was not the obvious one. It had been obvious once, perhaps, when the village had still sent girls up with offerings and boys up with dares and old men up with complaints. Now it was half-swallowed by leaf litter, the stones at its edge crooked and half-buried. Pale mushrooms, thin as fingers, pushed up between them.

Birds did not sing along this stretch. They watched from higher boughs, heads cocked, as if counting.

Miles walked a pace behind her, his staff finding the gaps in the stones without looking. His other hand brushed, now and again, the paper seals hanging from his belt. They rustled against each other softly, like an argument kept sotto voce out of respect for the sleeping household.

Lillian’s feet were bare. He noticed, because he had spent his life in bodies that left prints and bodies that didn’t, and he knew the difference. The leaves did not move around her steps. They acknowledged her weight and then decided, quite rationally, not to record it.

“I know this place,” he said when the torii came into view.

This gate was not merely old. It was tired. The wood had blackened not from fire but from time and touch and rain. The crossbeam cracked along one side, a vine bleeding rust-coloured sap down its length. On either side of the path, the trees leaned back, not away in fear but outward in something like reverence. Their branches framed the shrine like hands around a secret.

“A monk came here once, to cleanse it,” Miles said. “Never left. They say the shrine took his face. Gave it to something else.”

Lillian tilted her head, the way she did when evaluating the difference between a human story and what might be underneath.

“Faces are paper,” she said. “Masks for gods, beasts, or silence. What matters is breath.”

She stepped under the torii. The air on the far side was thicker, wet in a way that had nothing to do with dew. Insects hummed inside the walls. The shrine’s doors were half rotted, fibres peeling back from the frame.

They opened by themselves.

The smell hit first. Rot, yes—the sweet, cloying stench of rice offerings gone black and sake turned to syrup—but under it, something metallic and old, as if the room had once been washed in blood and the memory had not quite left.

Inside, bowls of rice lay on stone altars, surfaces moving with insects. Flowers, once carefully arranged, had withered into tongues curled back in disgust. In the centre stood a statue of Jizō, child-protector, guardian of travellers, but something in the carving had been overwritten. The face was smooth, worn blank, features eroded not by time but by hands. Moss grew along the shoulders, creeping down the outstretched arms.

Miles stopped at the threshold.

“This isn’t right,” he said.

The paper charms hanging from the rafters twitched.

Then they twisted. Not in the wind—there was none—but as if called. They curled and writhed, edges rasping against each other. Whispering began, low at first, the scrape of dry paper on dry paper. Then, beneath it, voices.

“Traitor,” they hissed. “Dog-face. Mask-breaker. Does she wear yours? Do you still dream?”

Miles’s hand tightened on his staff. His ears flattened. This was old magic, but it had been badly maintained, fed with fear and neglect. It had soured.

From the deeper dark of the shrine came a shriek: wet, birdlike, twisted at the end into something that might once have been human.

Lillian stepped past him.

“This is not your fight,” she said, verse edged like a blade. “Let the dead judge the bleeding. I will close their eyes.”

The charms fell silent at her words. The whispering cut off, like a stream dammed with bare hands.

She knelt before the blank-faced Jizō, palms flat on the stone, fingers splayed. Her hair slid forward, hiding her painted face. For a moment, she looked entirely human in posture, if not in presence.

Then her shadow changed.

It lengthened, claws extending at its edges where her hands remained still. The curve of her back sharpened. Her breath deepened, ribs moving in a rhythm older than any sutra.

Miles watched from the threshold, one foot on the worn wood, one still on the the stone path outside, caught between duties. He should not have let her go in alone. He knew that. He also knew there were places where his presence would only make the bargaining worse.

A sound came from under the statue. Not words, not speech. Creaking, like wood under pressure, like old bones realigning. The air in the shrine shifted, heat sliding along his fur.

Lillian spoke again, low and steady, each word a stone dropped into a well.

“They do not rest here,” she said. “But if I call the old name, they might sleep a while.”

The name itself was not one he recognised. It slid past his ears like water, leaving the sense of having been addressed, but not personally.

The statue exhaled.

Moss peeled back from its arms, curling like green smoke. The air cleared. The smell of rot retreated to the bowls where it belonged. The paper charms sagged, strings no longer taut with borrowed voices.

Outside, a breeze moved through the trees at last, tentative, as if asking permission.

Miles did not move his hand from his staff.

“That name,” he said. “It wasn’t one we learned in the monastery.”

Lillian rose. Her expression was composed, but there was a flicker of something older in her eyes: weariness, or recognition, or grief.

“There are names you only learn when people try to erase you,” she said.

She stepped past him, back under the torii. As she crossed the threshold, the vines overhead shifted, sliding back across the beam. The gate closed around the shrine like a mouth re-learning how to shut.

He watched her walk ahead along the path, bare feet leaving no marks. He felt the first clean doubt settle into his bones.

He had suspected, from the first poem on the shrine stone. Now he knew with the sort of knowing that did not yet have words: Lillian was not merely someone the priest hated. She was someone his entire system had been built to fear.


## 4. Bones in the Roots

The orchard lay beyond the furthest rice fields, where the land rose and the soil turned stony. Once, someone had loved it. The rows had been straight. The trees had been pruned. Children had climbed the lower branches, hands sticky with juice. The village festivals had hung lanterns here.

Now the path was choked with grass. Persimmons clung, overripe, splitting on the branch. The smell was sweet and rotting, with an undertone of iron that did not belong.

Crows hopped between the trees, silent. Their eyes tracked Miles and Lillian as they walked, a moving point of interest in a landscape that had lost its story.

The tree at the clearing’s centre was older than the rest.

Its trunk twisted upon itself, bark blackened and wet-looking in places even on a dry day. Roots crawled out of the earth like limbs, some knotted like fists, others curving up and back down like ribs. The grass around it was sparse, bone-pale, brittle underfoot.

Lillian stopped at the edge of the ring of roots.

“Fruit falls for no one,” she murmured. “Roots remember broken names. I do not belong.”

Miles crouched by one of the thicker roots, fingertips brushing moss aside. Something pale gleamed in the dirt.

He dug with deliberate care, claws curled in to keep from scratching what lay beneath. Soil gave, grudgingly.

He lifted a bone into the light.

“Humerus,” he said. “Human.”

More digging revealed more. A ribcage, partial, then a jawbone with two teeth missing. There was no order to the way they lay. They had not been buried with the overt intention of honour. They had been discarded here as part of a spectacle.

He found the mask last.

Cracked, one side caved in, the paint flaking away in irregular patches of red and black. It was the type used in exorcism plays, to show the audience where to direct their fear. It had never protected anyone.

He set it gently on a root. The air stilling around it felt like attention.

“They used to bring yōkai here,” he said. “Make a show of it. Tie them to this tree. Cut them open like fish. Call it cleansing.”

He did not look at her as he spoke. Some confessions needed room around them.

“I watched once,” he continued. “I was young. Stupid. A weasel yōkai, that time. Didn’t fight. Didn’t speak. Had eyes like…”

He stopped, because all the comparisons his mind offered were parts of his own history, and he was not ready to connect those threads.

“I was supposed to help,” he said instead. “Hold the ropes. Chant the lines.”

He looked down at his hands. Fur, claws, fingers scarred from rope burns and bad landings.

“I let her go,” he said. “Cut the ropes in the dark. Sent her into the trees.”

The orchard breathed once, in.

“Next night I woke up like this,” he said. “Fur, ears, tail. They called it a curse. They said the weasel had taken my face. Or the priest’s god had punished me. I still don’t know which story is more insulting.”

Lillian’s robes shifted as the wind changed. She had not moved closer to the bones, but her body leaned, slightly, toward him.

“You carry your shape,” she said, “like a soldier bears a wound. Not healed, not shameful.”

He huffed a short, humourless laugh.

“You speak like you’ve seen a thousand winters,” he said.

She met his gaze then, properly.

“One thousand,” she said. “No less. But not one with hands like yours. Warm. Rough. And afraid.”

Something eased in his chest at the admission. Not relief, exactly; more the sense of a weight having shifted from his ribs to a place he could better balance it.

They camped that night away from the tree, under younger branches that had not yet learned to hold bones. Lillian lay near the fire, hair unpinned, mask set beside her like a sleeping face. She watched the flames until correspondence between their movements and her thoughts blurred, then closed her eyes.

Sleep did not come as a gift. It had to be taken.

Her dream began in water.

Black, still, thick as oil. Reeds rose in endless rows. The sky above was not a sky so much as a ceiling of dark, unmarked clay.

She walked. Her robes trailed, dragging ripples that closed too quickly. Figures emerged at the edges of her vision: other mujina, bodies shifting between animal and human, fur and skin and ceremony. Their faces were painted in red, not with pigment but with old blood. They whispered from the trunks of trees, from the underside of leaves, from holes in the world.

“Did you bury your name?” they asked. “Will you tell him what you are? Will you let him see your grave?”

One stepped forward.

It wore her face, older, the edges eaten away as if by salt. Its mask was carved from bone she recognised without wanting to know why. It knelt, close enough that she felt the dream-breath on her cheek.

“What you buried,” it said, “still breathes.”

She woke without sound, lungs burning. The orchard around them lay in grey pre-dawn. The tree with the bones stood as before, unbothered.

From one of its higher branches, the execution mask she had seen earlier now hung. She was sure Miles had left it on the ground.

She did not wake him to tell him.


## 5. Fire Beneath the Lanterns

The village clung to the mountainside the way moss clung to north-facing stones: stubbornly, in defiance of angles and common sense. Its houses were low, roofs heavy with tile and lichen. Smoke rose in thin columns from a few chimneys, straight up on a windless evening.

When Miles and Lillian walked in, the doors shut.

Not loudly. Not with the theatrical slam of scripted fear. Quietly, one after another, as if responding to a breeze only they felt.

“We’ll keep moving,” Miles murmured. “Ask for food. A roof. No more.”

Lillian’s kasa hat hid most of her face. It did not hide the way her body held itself: coiled, restless under the robe. Her tail pressed against cloth, a curve that wanted to flick. She walked with a cane, the limp exaggerated. The disguise was good. It would not fool trained eyes.

The teahouse at the end of the main street had seen better paint. Its sign leaned. The lanterns outside were patched with paper in slightly different shades of red. Someone had cared enough to repair them, which was in its way a type of prayer.

Inside, the air was thick with camellia oil, old smoke, and the faint sourness of spilled sake. The keeper, a man whose fur had gone thin at the muzzle, blind in one eye, took their coins without comment. He had the look of someone who had outlived too many winters to bother being surprised by what walked through his door.

“Room in the back,” he said. “Walls thin. Sleep loud, if you want privacy.”

Miles managed a smile. Lillian’s did not reach the surface.

They had barely settled—robes hung from pegs, staff propped by the door, a small brazier coaxed into life—when the drum began.

Not festival rhythm. Not the easy, complex patterns of celebration. This beat was slower, deliberate, spaced like footsteps descending into a grave. Each strike echoed off the mountainside and back into the hollow between houses.

Miles’s ears twitched. His tail went low.

“We need to leave,” he said.

He moved to the door. The first lantern outside flared brighter, then dimmed as a shadow passed.

The priest from the crimson wood stood in the courtyard, robes cleaner now, sleeves rolled, hair tied back. Flanking him were five onmyōji in ceremonial dress. Their faces were painted with careful lines of white and black, mouths thin, eyes narrowed. Their sleeves bore inked sigils, each a net for something that did not consent to be caught.

Each carried a lantern and a scroll.

“The beast-boy returns,” the priest said. “Still walking upright. Still stinking of foxblood.”

Miles stepped into the doorway, filling it.

“I haven’t harmed anyone,” he said. “There’s no cause—”

“You were warned,” the priest cut in. “The dog who spares monsters becomes one.”

He gestured. The onmyōji moved, not in unison—they were not soldiers—but with the practiced coordination of men who had trained in circles together, backs to the same teacher. Paper charms fluttered from their sleeves to the ground, tracing a ring around the teahouse.

Each charm hummed low in the throat of the earth, a sound only those attuned to such things would hear. Lillian, inside, heard them like nails scratching her bones.

She rose. Her hand went to the mask. Her claws, usually so carefully hidden, slipped out through her fingers, black and curved.

“Five flames in still dark,” she whispered. “Do they know the oil spills? Do they smell their smoke?”

Outside, one onmyōji stepped closer to Miles. He lifted a cleansing fan carved from bone, prayers etched along its ribs. It glowed faintly, the light unsteady.

“Kneel,” he said. “Let us purge you. Or we rip the spirit from your skin.”

“I’m not possessed,” Miles growled. “This is me.”

“Then it will be a mercy,” the priest said.

Inside, the air around Lillian vibrated with the effort of not moving. Her body wanted to spring, to tear through screen and circle, to reduce the ring of charms to ash. Her haunches bunched. Her ears flattened.

She could kill them. That was not arrogance. It was mathematics: their distance, her speed, the brittle quality of their wards after too many years of being used as excuses rather than tools.

Her claws lifted. The mask slipped.

She saw, through the narrow gap in the screen, Miles standing alone in the courtyard, staff still lowered. He was not begging. Not bargaining. Merely existing where they had decided he ought not to be.

“If I kill them,” she thought, “he will never look at me the same way again.”

The thought did not come as verse. It came as a blunt stone. It settled, heavy, in the space where she usually hid her fear.

The teahouse keeper stepped out.

He wore no charms. No armour. His hands were empty.

“This is sacred soil,” he said. “My blood on it. My wife’s bones under it. Take your hate elsewhere.”

The words had the cadence of having been rehearsed, not for this moment, but for some future point where someone would need to remember that they had a right to stand where their dead lay.

The onmyōji paused. Their spells did not care particularly about wives or bones. The kami they borrowed from did. To offend the local dead carelessly was to invite a different kind of trouble than they had been paid to cause.

The priest hissed, frustration audible.

He snapped his fan shut.

“Next time,” he said to Miles, “the cleansing won’t be offered. Only fire.”

He turned. The circle of charms extinguished one by one, a ripple of small deaths in the dust.

They left.

Inside, Lillian’s claws retracted by degrees. Her breath, which had been standing in her throat like a trapped bird, escaped.

Later, when the drums had ceased and the village had resumed its attempt at pretending nothing unusual ever happened within its borders, Miles sat by their small fire, wrapping a bandage around a cut on his arm.

“They knew something,” he said. “About you.”

Lillian knelt beside him. The light from the coals threw her face into planes and shadows. The mask rested between them, its painted features neutral, uninterested.

“I have many names,” she said. “None spoken while someone loves. This one… is for you.”

She lifted his hand, bandaged and clumsy, and folded her fingers around it. The contact was brief; the warmth lingered longer.


## 6. The Mask Cracks

The path above the village wound into mist. Stones, half-fallen, marked where pilgrims had once walked to a higher shrine. Now the fog used them as handholds, clinging, shifting between them, heavy with unsaid prayers.

Lillian walked alone.

She told herself it was because she needed air. Because the teahouse walls had pressed too close, their thinness no comfort against the thought of paper charms. Because Miles’s breathing, steady in sleep, had started to sync with hers and that scared her more than any priest.

The truth was that the voices had returned, and they did not like to be drowned by conversation.

Her steps were slow. She did not disturb the moss. The moon above was half-veiled, its light a diffused smear.

“Your feet don’t leave prints,” a voice said from the mist.

The priest stepped out as if he had been waiting in the white for hours, only now solidifying. His wand was gone. In its place, he held a single paper seal between two fingers, careful not to touch the ink.

He threw it at her feet.

It burned as it landed, a circle of white fire flaring up around her. Symbols leapt from the ash into the air, hissing, forming a ring that locked around her ankles. The force was not physical. Her muscles could have broken iron. This was a different kind of bind, one written not on her body but on the rules of the place.

She tried to step forward. Her legs did not obey. It felt like the moment in the mirror-room long ago, when she had realised that the reflection looking back at her had nothing to do with what she actually was.

“Speak your true name,” the priest said. “Let the record end you.”

“Your paper cannot hold the shape of the forest,” she said. “I am not ink-bound.”

The circle tightened. Heat licked up her calves, then her thighs.

He stepped closer, jade knife in his hand, blade white, edge stained faintly red from past use.

“You deceive the weak,” he said. “Seduce the cursed. Feed on grief. I will end your hunger.”

He swung.

Mist erupted.

It poured up from the ground, from her lungs, from the gap between what she had shown and what she had hidden. With a sound like bark splitting in winter, the circle shattered.

Her body twisted.

Silk tore as her shoulders broadened, spine lengthening. Fur burst along her arms and back, black-striped over silver. Her face elongated, muzzle emerging, teeth sharpening. It was not sudden, not for her. Every change echoed an older one. For the priest, it must have been as if the air had grown claws.

Her paw struck his chest. Claws, long and obsidian, slid between ribs with surgical precision.

The forest went quiet.

The priest’s eyes widened. For a moment he looked almost relieved. Then the strength went out of his knees. He slid down the length of her arm and settled at her feet, a bundle of blood and cloth and the end of a particular line of hatred.

“Lillian.”

His voice came from behind her, hoarse with exertion. Miles, staff in hand, stood a few paces away, chest heaving, fur damp with more than mist. He had run. He had not been fast enough to prevent the strike. He had been fast enough to witness it.

Her body trembled. The mujina form was still new in this place after so long a discipline of passing. It wanted to run. It wanted to disappear between roots. It wanted to finish the job.

She turned.

He did not raise his staff.

He did not flinch.

His eyes held something rawer than fear: recognition layered over horror, the sudden rearrangement of someone he thought he knew into something larger and more dangerous and still, somehow, that person.

“Not what you prayed for,” she said, her voice deeper now, syllables rolling from a throat built for growls. “Not demon, nor goddess. Just fur in falling dusk.”

A red leaf drifted down between them, landing on his shoulder. He did not brush it off.

He stepped closer.

She lowered her head, throat exposed. It was not a gesture of submission so much as one of choice: I will not run. If you strike, it will be with my consent.

He knelt.

“I did pray,” he said, quietly. “Once. For something to show me whether I’d been cursed or chosen. This will do.”

The leaf on his shoulder shifted in the wind, kept its place, and turned brown.


## 7. Rain on Stone Faces

Rain came in thin, patient strands, soaking their fur, their robes, the ground. It did not hurry. The forest had time, and so did the weather.

They walked a mountain path that could hardly be called a road anymore. Stone markers stood at intervals, half buried, their carved characters moss-softened into suggestions. Jizō statues, child-guardians, marked the way—some wrapped in tattered red cloth, some bare, all streaked with water.

Lillian stayed in her half-shape, midway between human and mujina. It was not the easiest form, but it was the truest she could bear for now. Her claws remained, but shorter. Her face lengthened in profile, but her eyes were still the ones she had painted in the mirror centuries ago.

Miles’s gait had changed. The tension that usually sat in his shoulders had eased. His ears lay flatter under the rain, more from the weight of water than worry.

They stopped at one statue where the cloth had rotted almost entirely away. Someone long ago had tied it with care. The knots remained, though the fabric had become little more than a stain.

They sat.

The rain tapped on stone, on fur, on the hollow bamboo of his staff.

“It wasn’t wrath,” Miles said. “Not really. Not what cursed me.”

Lillian watched his profile. The slope of his nose, the way his jaw clenched and unclenched.

“I loved a spirit,” he said. “A long time ago. Not even for long. Just enough.”

He ran one finger along the statue’s arm, tracing a line carved there: a child’s name, barely legible.

“The village thought I’d been taken,” he said. “Tainted. So they dragged me out. Held prayers. Burned her tree. Left me half-animal.”

He laughed once, the sound short and sour.

“They told themselves the kami had done it,” he said. “That their god marked me. Easier than admitting they’d done something irreversible.”

He turned to meet her eyes.

“The kami didn’t do it to punish me,” he said. “If they did it at all. They didn’t want me dead. They just didn’t want me to forget.”

Lillian lowered her gaze. Her hand rose, slowly, and removed the ruined mask from where it hung. The wood had cracked in their flight. The paint was flaking.

She laid it in the mud before Jizō.

“I was never whole,” she said softly. “But you look, and do not flinch. That undoes my name.”

They sat in silence. No vow. No promise. The rain continued, misting their eyelashes, beading ear fur.

Behind them, bowls of old offerings in the underbrush had filled with water. Rice, scattered and swollen, floated like pale larvae. The world did not pause to observe their gesture. It had other rituals to maintain.

Before them, the path descended into cloud.


## 8. The Last Mirror

Beneath the oldest shrine—older than the one with the blank-faced Jizō, older than the execution tree, older, perhaps, than the village itself—there lay a room the monks who had tried to catalogue such things had listed simply as forbidden.

The approach to it had half-collapsed. Stones had tumbled, corridors narrowed. The air thickened with humidity and the smell of old fire. Lizards watched from cracks, unblinking.

The room itself had no decorations. No offerings. No obvious altar.

It had a mirror.

Bronze, if one were generous, though much of its surface had darkened beyond identification. Taller than a man, as wide as three. It stood on three legs shaped like roots, its rim carved into waves and teeth. The metal did not reflect. It pulsed.

It breathed.

Lillian stopped at the threshold. Her body, still held in that half-state, tensed as if about to flee and as if rooted in place. The voices that had grown quieter in the days since the orchard swelled again, crowding the small room.

Hide again, sister, they murmured. The world burns the named. Give up the face. It was never yours.

The mirror’s surface rippled. Shapes rose within: faces, masks, animal snouts, human eyes sewn shut. Children in prayer robes who became foxes in their next breath. Women whose hair turned to ink that drowned them. Men whose names peeled off their skin in strips of paper.

The mirror opened.

The surface bulged, then split, revealing a darkness that was not absence but density. White hands slid out, too many fingers, each nail lacquered like obsidian. They beckoned.

Return, they said without sound. Forget. Become all and none. The mask is safety. The mask is silence. Be wind again.

Lillian stepped forward.

Her outline flickered. For a moment she was the woman in mourning paint who had watched the priest set his traps. Then she was the mujina of old legends, badger fur striped with shadows, eyes like coins at the bottom of a well. Then she was something else entirely: blank, a suggestion of a face, the idea of a self without specifics.

Miles fought behind her.

The shrine had not let them come this far without protest. Its wards, untended for years, had become resentful. They slammed shut like jaws around him, illusions rising to confuse his steps: corridors that led back to where he had started, voices calling his name in tones of accusation and desire, hands that tugged at his fur.

His staff was almost spent. Each charm he burned took some of his strength with it. His body flickered as the old mirror sought to parse him: human boy again for one step on the stones, Akita monk for another, something in between when he stumbled.

“Lillian!” he shouted, voice tearing at the edges. “Don’t let them call you back.”

The mirror brightened. The hands stretched. The room’s air turned syrup-thick, hard to breathe.

Her body began to dissolve. Mist rose from her shoulders, coiling, streaming toward the mirror’s open mouth.

Join us, the ghosts chanted. Join us. Join us.

He dropped his staff.

He stepped forward, unarmed, one paw out. His knees shook. His lungs burned. The illusions ripped at him, showing him versions of himself in other lives: a man who had knelt at the execution tree and done nothing; a boy who had turned in the weasel girl; a monk who had closed the shrine doors and left the yōkai outside.

“You told me what you were,” he said. His voice cut through the chanting not by volume but by being the only thing in the room that was not shaped by the mirror. “But I saw what they didn’t.”

He took another step. His chest collided with something unseen. He forced breath past it anyway.

“The way you pick mushrooms,” he said. “How you hum when you think. The mud under your claws. The curve of your haiku when you’re tired.”

Lillian’s outline jerked.

“You are not a lie,” he said. “You’re not my wish. You’re not their shame. You are Lillian.”

The mirror screamed.

It was a high, grinding sound, like a thousand kiln-cracked bowls shattering at once. The bronze frame shook. The hands flailed, fingers grasping at nothing.

Lillian’s hand—paw—something between—reached out toward him. The ghosts swarmed it, claws trying to drag it back. Her form thinned where they tugged.

She breathed in.

“I was almost gone,” she said, her voice steadying. “But someone named my silence. I return as me.”

Her body snapped back into cohesion. Mist condensed. Fur darkened. Stripes reasserted themselves. Her eyes cleared.

She stepped away from the mirror.

It cracked.

Lines spread across its surface, jagged and branching. The hands scrabbled at the edges, then withdrew. Shards of metal fell, slow as leaves, each piece catching one face, one mask, one story. None showed hers.

When the last piece hit the floor, the room exhaled.

The chants stopped. The air lightened. The smell of old incense and older anger faded, leaving only damp stone and their two breaths.

Miles’s legs gave out. He dropped to one knee. The world tilted for a moment, then centred on the feel of the stone floor beneath his paws.

Lillian knelt beside him.

Their foreheads touched, lightly, an acknowledgment and an agreement. They stayed like that until the first drop of water fell from the cracked ceiling, landing on the back of his neck, cold enough to make him gasp.

“We should leave before the mountain remembers it can fall,” he said.

“We should,” she agreed.

They did not move for another few breaths.


## 9. Autumn’s Judgment

When the shrine collapsed, it did not do so with drama.

There was no choir of thunder, no sudden gust. Stones loosened. Beams, already half-rotten, surrendered. The weight distributed itself differently. Gravity did the rest. From outside, the sound was not a catastrophe but the shrug of an old building deciding its work was done.

The ground shivered. Dust rose.

Where there had been walls and torii and prayer ropes, there was now a clearing ringed in ash and splintered wood. The air smelled of char long after the visible fire had gone.

Lillian crouched in the centre with her fur smoking, breath ragged. The fight with the mirror had left marks that would not show on the surface but would ache in weather changes.

Under her, Miles convulsed.

The curse had chosen its moment badly or well, depending on one’s sense of timing. His body twisted between shapes, not in the smooth shift of yōkai but in the tearing pull of something inflicted. His limbs stretched, then shortened. His muzzle elongated, then tried to retreat. Bones popped; skin followed as best it could.

Sound ripped from his throat that had very little to do with the present and everything to do with remembered voices: villagers chanting, the crackle of burning branches, the weasel girl’s silent eyes. He tried to bite back the noise. It escaped anyway.

Lillian curled around him.

Her body, larger in this form, made a ring, head and tail meeting almost over his chest. She pressed her flank to his shaking side, claws digging into the soil to anchor herself.

She did not speak to the curse. It was not hers to negotiate with. She spoke to him, without words, the way she had once spoken to pups huddled in burrows during storms: I am here. The sky is loud. You are still.

The forest stilled.

Leaves held their breath mid-fall. Crows stayed silent. Wind coiled low, then lay down like a dog at their feet.

The kami observed.

It did not appear as a figure, because it did not need to. Its awareness moved through roots and fungi and the thin veins of water that threaded the hill. It felt where the shrine had cracked. It felt where the mirror had been broken. It felt, with some surprise, the hole where a girl’s soul should have been in its catalogue and was not, because she had chosen to stand in a ring of ash instead.

This was not how it had been arranged.

You should have died caged, it said, not in language but in pressure on the bones. He should have died forgotten.

Lillian did not bow. Her neck was already bent over Miles. To move would have been to abandon her position. She had vowed, once, never to cower in front of any presence again. She kept to that.

He called your name, the kami noted. You did not hide.

The curse fluttered under her paws, uncertain now. Its logic had been clear: boy loves spirit; village punishes; gods are blamed. It had tied itself to that narrative, drawn its strength from it. Now the other end of the story had been cut.

She did not petition. She did not make bargains. She had been a functionary of forces like this for long enough to know how they loved the sound of their own contracts. She merely stayed where she was, sharing breath with someone the world had tried repeatedly to make into a lesson and who stubbornly remained a person.

Miles’s spasms slowed.

His body settled into the Akita form. The lines of his face eased. His fur, matted with sweat and dust, lay flat. His breathing steadied, in and out, catching only now and then on some internal bruise.

He opened his eyes. They were his eyes, not the mirror’s, not the villagers’. He saw her muzzle close to his, whiskers wet with sweat and rain, smoke curling from her shoulder fur.

“You came undone once,” she said. “I am here to hold the thread. No more mirrors now.”

The kami receded.

It did not reward. It did not punish. It adjusted.

A gust of wind, casual now, lifted the last stones at the edge of the clearing and tumbled them into a ravine. Moss would claim them later. Autumn’s bright fury dimmed. The first bite of winter edged the air.

Autumn ended there.

Something else began that none of the priests’ scrolls had charted.


## 10. Under the Red Moon

Weeks later, the forest had begun, tentatively, to heal.

Saplings leaned toward gaps where old trees had fallen. Fungi colonised dead wood with the quiet efficiency of those who understood long projects. The ash at the former shrine site had filtered into the soil, souring it for a generation and feeding the next.

Above it all, the moon had turned red.

Not blood-red, despite village women’s eager labeling, but rust-red, the colour of dried iron and late persimmons. It hung low and large in the night sky, a watchful eye over the valley.

They had built a shelter near a hot spring that hid half up a slope, steam rising from it even on cold mornings. The water remembered nothing. It carried traces of minerals, warmth from the earth, a faint smell of sulfur. It offered comfort without commentary.

The lean-to was a modest construction of bamboo and pine, roof sloped to shed rain, walls woven tightly enough to keep the worst cold out. Miles had argued with himself over every joint, every knot. Lillian had watched with bemused patience, occasionally handing him another length of rope.

One evening, he sat at the edge of the spring, ankles submerged. The heat climbed his legs, easing aches he had stopped admitting to himself he had. The staff lay beside him on the stones, stripped of charms, whittled down to its simplest use: balance.

Lillian crouched by the fire.

She favoured her mujina form at home, where there were fewer eyes to misinterpret it. Her fur glowed orange in the firelight, black stripes deepening into lacquer. She turned sweet potatoes in the coals with a stick, murmuring to them as if coaxing children to sleep.

He listened, half more to her tone than the words.

“What will you do,” he asked eventually, “now that the mirrors are gone?”

She looked up.

The question hung between them, heavy with all its possible meanings. Who are you now. Who will you be. What do we do with what is left of us when the roles we were forced into have been burned.

“Live,” she said.

The word was small. It felt enormous.

She added, after a moment, back in the pattern he had come to recognise as both shield and intimacy:

“Let the world forget. I have found one who sees me. Names bloom where you look.”

He snorted, a soft, involuntary laugh.

“You’ve stopped hiding,” he said.

“You never started,” she said.

They ate.

The potatoes were hot enough to burn his tongue. He welcomed the pain. It grounded him more reliably than most prayers he had been taught.

Later, when the fire had burned down to a banked glow and the steam from the spring had risen to meet the cold, they lay side by side under the lean-to. Not touching. They did not need to. Their bodies curled in mirror-image, tails twitching in sleep-wavering rhythms.

Above the open edge of the shelter, the red moon watched. It did not bless, or curse, or speak. It bore witness.

Lillian, drifting toward sleep, murmured one last verse.

“If I must be myth,” she said, “then let me be whispered of in your dreaming voice.”

The wind moved through the trees, catching ash from the old shrine scar, lifting it, thinning it, carrying it elsewhere. Somewhere, in a village that had learned to shut its doors more quietly and open them more carefully, children told stories in which a badger woman and a dog-faced monk walked through autumn woods and argued with gods.

Those stories would change.

Autumn would come again.

The forest would remember the traps that had crumbled, and the mirror that had cracked, and the way two small bodies had held each other in a ring of ash under a watching sky and, against all arrangement, refused to disappear.
