## The Perfect Corpse and Other Routine Failures

### A Weirdverse Necropolis Story  
*(in the style of Ilya Evtushenko; featuring a Weirdverse Lillian Edgecombe)*

---

## 1. Procedural Tuesday

On Tuesdays, the bones rioted.

Officially, the tower called it *Minor Reanimation Irregularities (Recurring, Weekday-Linked)* in the incident log. Unofficially, everyone called it Tuesday.

Lillian Edgecombe—necromancer, administrator, reluctant employer of the dead—stood at her workbench and watched a thundercloud attempt drama outside the narrow window. It failed. Lightning licked at the horizon, thought better of it, and went to harass some other, more impressionable hillside.

Inside, the air already smelled of the day’s work: candle smoke, damp stone, singed linen, the faint iron tang of old blood that had been filed under *legacy fluids* in the tower’s maintenance reports. Somewhere below her, in the skeleton barracks, two dozen bone-racks rattled in disharmonious anticipation.

She lifted her chipped teacup, took a sip of black licorice tea, and looked down at the slab.

The corpse was almost perfect.

It had taken her three years of requisitions, midnight excavation permits, and one deeply uncomfortable ethics committee review to assemble him. The committee had eventually agreed that, technically, no single original owner could lodge a complaint any more, and the Council of Fleshed Citizens was several districts away and busy arguing about tram fares.

He had the chest of a sailor who had sung himself hoarse in seven ports and one war. The hands of a harpist, calluses worn in places that remembered music. Legs built from people who had survived long walks in bad weather. One eye was green, the other an onyx button that had once adorned a funeral coat; it blinked occasionally of its own accord, in the same way old buildings sometimes creaked without wind.

He also hummed.

Not any song Lillian recognised; not even on key. Just a low, insistent vibration that filled quiet moments like water seeping under a door.

“Today,” she told him, and herself, and the tower’s ancient bones, “is the day.”

Her voice did not echo. It had learned not to, in here.

She set down the tea, wiped her paws on her smock, and raised her own hands over his chest. Thread glowed faintly in the dimness—stitched sigils, careful knots. Beneath the slab, the old machinery hummed: gears that predated the city, pipes that carried more than water, a slow, patient pulse the tower claimed was just thermal expansion.

Down in the barracks, a whistle blew.

Immediately, someone began screaming.

Lillian closed her eyes briefly.

“Scraps,” she said.

The lab door banged open. Scraps clattered in: skeleton, apron, chef’s hat, clipboard. He carried a tray of pickled mandrake heads and a raven that had failed its necromancer entrance exam three years running.

“You bellowed,” he said, which in Scraps’ language meant good morning, how can I help ruin this.

“Where is the spleen?” Lillian asked.

Scraps looked at the slab. At his clipboard. At the tray. He tilted his skull in the manner of a clerk confronted with a field not present on their form.

Then he shrugged, and the tray slid out of his hands and hit the flagstones. One mandrake began to weep. The raven escaped, flapped in circles, and left a complaint in the air.

Outside in the corridor, voices rose.

“MISSY,” came Sawbones’ tenor, each syllable like a tapped glass. “You cannot *just glue eyebrows to your skull*—that’s not… medically accurate!”

Missy shrieked back, outraged and echoing. “I have a right to self-expression! We don’t all want to be dusty toothpicks, you know!”

The tower shuddered, not from thunder but from cumulative incompetence. Somewhere below, something exploded with theatrical restraint. A broom screamed. It did that most Tuesdays.

Lillian took another sip of tea. The cup had been chipped on three separate occasions, each time in a different small uprising. The chip-line mapped a history of compromise.

“Just once,” she told the corpse, “I’d like to finish a Tuesday without arson, poetic declarations, or any furniture starting a spontaneous polka.”

The corpse groaned softly.

She looked at him. “Agreement or threat?”

One mismatched eye blinked, once, with the grave deliberation of a committee adopting a resolution.

Lillian’s mouth curled. “We’re getting close,” she said.

Under the tower, far below the foundations, old machinery shifted its weight. No one in the building mentioned the way the floor seemed to breathe, but more than one custodian had started keeping earplugs in their pockets, just in case the pipes ever tried to speak.

## 2. Undead Labor Force 43-B

By ten o’clock, the skeletons had filed a formal complaint.

The Grand Hall of Bickering was not on any official blueprint. The Council archives would tell you that Bickering Hall A had burned down in a minor procedural disagreement about parking permits, and Bickering Halls B through F had been repurposed as redundant storerooms for forms that no one needed but no one dared throw away.

Nevertheless, the hall existed.

It had benches. It had an imposing dais. It had banners nailed to its damp stone walls, lettered in wonky capitals:

- WE BLEED … METAPHORICALLY  
- NO PAY, NO POLISH  
- TIBIA TODAY, TIBIA TOMORROW, TIBIA FOREVER

Several of the skulls incorporated into the chandeliers still belonged to their owners, who had opinions about the acoustics.

Lillian stood at the front of the hall, arms crossed, robe still smoking faintly from an experiment that had gone technically right and politically wrong. Her fur itched from residual necrostatic charge. Her back ached, which the occupational health forms would have described as *ergonomic misalignment due to extensive corpse lifting (chronic)*.

In front of her, Undead Labor Force 43-B rattled themselves into order.

The spokesperson stepped forward: a skeleton in a waistcoat, with a clipboard in one hand and a jar containing a single floating eyeball in the other. The eyeball belonged to the union’s legal advisor and was rotated between members for meetings as needed.

“Esteemed Necromancer Lillian Edgecombe, Mistress of the Tower, Chief Sorcerer of Section Nine, Acting Head of Reanimation Logistics,” the skeleton intoned, voice like dropped cutlery. “We, the Undead Labor Force 43-B, hereby present our formal demands.”

Scraps leaned in at Lillian’s elbow. “They want dental,” he whispered.

“They don’t have teeth,” Lillian whispered back. “Most of them, anyway.”

Missy, from three rows back, clicked her jaw in offended solidarity. Several teeth fell out and bounced down the aisle. Sawbones made a note in his little black book that served as both medical log and poetry journal.

“Item one,” the spokesperson—Tibbits, if she remembered correctly—continued. “New uniforms. The current loincloth provision is undignified and leaves us vulnerable to drafts.”

There was a clatter of agreement.

“Item two: formal job titles. We are not ‘minions.’ We are *spectral specialists in decomposition logistics*.”

There was a smattering of applause.

“Item three: predictable rota scheduling and one day off per week to pursue personal projects, such as bone-based poetry, interpretive anatomy, or continuing professional development in moaning.”

From the back, a skeleton with a cravat and three mandibles shouted, “AND TIBIA INSURANCE!”

Laughter. Someone threw a femur in the air and caught it again, which would have been more impressive if it hadn’t been their own.

Lillian raised a paw. The hall fell more or less quiet, partly because one of the chandeliers chose that moment to detach itself from the ceiling and crash onto a row of protestors. No one was hurt, but three skulls were now slightly more avant-garde than before.

“Your tibias are functionally indestructible,” she said. “Your spines regenerate in six minutes. You do not require dental. You are, with all due respect, dead.”

“Union membership status is independent of life,” Missy snapped. She had new lace sleeves over her arm-bones and eyebrows drawn onto her skull in grave-ink. “We have needs.”

“You have hobbies,” Lillian said. “That is not the same thing.”

Sawbones held up a scroll. “We have a constitution,” he announced.

The scroll burst into blue flame, dissolved into ash, and reconstituted itself as a slightly longer scroll.

“See?” Sawbones said. “Resilient.”

Lillian pinched the bridge of her snout. The bridge pinched back. The tower’s ambient necromantic field liked to get involved.

“Fine,” she said. “You have my recognition.”

The hall buzzed. Tibbits looked smug. Missy struck a pose.

“I recognise you,” Lillian continued, “as a problem.”

Rattling applause.

Before Tibbits could launch into a motion about committee chairs, the sound cut across them all: a scream from deep in the laboratory wing.

Everyone stilled.

It was not a living scream. It was not a ghost’s. It was the sound of a body remembering that it had been alive, then forgetting again violently.

The chandeliers swayed. Dust drifted down from the high beams.

Lillian’s posture changed. Her annoyance folded neatly and slid into a familiar drawer; her focus sharpened.

“Meeting adjourned,” she said, voice flat. “Anyone not helping me contain whatever that was will be reassigned to mop duty.”

“Mops are a tool of oppression,” hissed someone in the third row.

“Mops,” Lillian said, “are the reason we survived the Great Viscera Spill of Last March. Move.”

Bones scattered. Banners fell. Scraps scooped up his clipboard, his missing femur, and what was left of his dignity and clattered after her.

In the lab, the humming had stopped.

The corpse was breathing.

## 3. Tea with the Baron and a Policy Reminder

The necropolis ran on tea and bylaws.

The tea was easier to understand.

Later that week—because even rising corpses had to be fit around the visit schedule of aristocratic oversight—Lillian sat in the smaller of the tower’s reception rooms, hands wrapped around a cup that steamed faintly of wormwood and ashroot.

The room had been decorated, at some point, by someone with a grudge against subtlety. Torn velvet curtains framed a stained-glass window depicting the Four Horsemen playing croquet. Teacups hovered at shoulder height, refilling themselves from a pot shaped like a screaming fetus. A stuffed raven played harpsichord in the corner with its wings, unwillingly.

Across from Lillian, Baron Athanasius Hollowgrave, Sixth Earl of Deathmere-on-Murk, floated three inches above his chair. The aristocracy considered this both a mark of status and a necessity to avoid the damp.

“Lady Edgecombe,” he said, voice like rust on a locked gate. “It is always… enlightening to visit your establishment.”

“It wasn’t strictly my choice,” Lillian replied. “Your emissary rode a severed horse through my conservatory and screamed in Latin. The begonias are traumatised.”

He made a small amused clicking sound. “They will adapt. Plants respond well to a sense of purpose.”

On cue, the fern in the corner stiffened as if standing to attention.

The Baron gestured. A skeletal butler with eight arms poured tea with practised elegance. The butler’s job description, as filed with Necropolis HR, read: *Hospitality, light assassination if required, incidents reporting*. There was a separate form for each.

Lillian accepted her cup.

“To what,” she asked, “do I owe the unholy pleasure?”

Hollowgrave steepled his fingers. They clicked together like a mild curse.

“Rumours,” he said. “Paperwork. Concerns.”

“All my favourite things.”

“You have been requisitioning unusual… components,” he said. “You have filed for discrete permissions under Articles 12, 33, and the seldom-invoked 47-C, which, as you know, pertains to the assembly of entities falling under the category *Significantly Integrated Remnant Constructs of Unclear Allegiance*.”

“The clerk at Records smiled when I handed that one in,” Lillian said. “I think it made their decade.”

“I am glad someone enjoys bureaucracy,” the Baron said. “The Deep Bureau does not enjoy anomalies.”

He set his cup down on a saucer that flinched in anticipation.

“They worry,” he continued. “About ambition. About order. About the possibility that a certain young necromancer with a record of… creative interpretation of regulations might create a being beyond their models.”

“They should worry,” Lillian said. “Their models are twenty years out of date and written by men who thought skeletons were furniture.”

“Be that as it may,” Hollowgrave said languidly, “Article 47 of the Concord of Eternal Night is clear on attempts to build a Perfect Corpse.”

“I shredded that concord last month to line the graveyard beds,” Lillian said. “The dahlias loved it.”

There was a pause in which the room politely revised its opinion of her.

“You are, then,” he said, “attempting it.”

She considered lying. Then she considered the fact that Baron Hollowgrave had almost certainly already read the sigils that pulsed in the walls four floors down, and had possibly heard the humming in his own marrow.

“Yes,” she said.

“Good,” he said, and smiled.

The cups froze mid-air. Even the harpsichord seemed to miss a note.

“Good?” Lillian repeated.

“My dear Lady Edgecombe,” Hollowgrave said, “if the Deep Bureau had its way, we would still be using chalk circles and morally dubious apprentices. Cities must occasionally be shaken. Systems must be stressed, or they ossify.”

“We have enough ossification,” Lillian said. “Most of it in the barracks.”

He inclined his skull. “I have read your incident reports,” he said. “Skeleton Unionization Attempt 3A was particularly… imaginative.”

“They wanted dental,” she said. “They do not have gums.”

“Desire is rarely anatomically accurate,” he said. “Regardless. Build your Perfect Corpse. Just be aware that once you introduce a new category of being into the necropolis, it will attract forms.” His voice dropped. “Policies. Committees.”

“You’re warning me that if I succeed,” Lillian said, “I will have to attend more meetings.”

“And write minutes,” he agreed.

She shuddered.

“Why are you telling me this?” she asked. “If you serve the Deep Bureau—”

“I do not serve them,” he said, polite offense in every polished bone. “I serve the balance. And my own interest in an interesting city. Do be careful where you store its heart.”

He rose. Or rather, he increased his hover-height.

“Good luck with your corpse, Lady Edgecombe,” he said. “And with the skeletons. I hear they are considering a poetry night.”

“Bone Words,” Lillian said. “Every Thursday.”

“Terrifying.”

He bowed, vanished in a curl of sarcasm and ozone, and left a faint taste of old policy in the air.

Lillian sat for a moment with her tea. The fern rustled in sympathetic horror.

“Scraps,” she called.

The door burst open. Scraps arrived with a biscuit missing a corner and a quill he was not officially allowed to borrow.

“Yes, mistress?”

“Add a note to the risk register,” she said. “New category: *Unprecedented Entities and Related Admin*. Likelihood: high. Impact: worse than glitter.”

Scraps blanched, which was impressive for someone made entirely of bone.

“Worse than glitter,” he repeated softly.

He made the note.

## 4. Corpse Rebellion, Contained in Triplicate

All great undead rebellions begin with a missing part.

In the East Wing, the part was a femur. According to the Maintenance Log, it belonged to Skeleton 17-B (Tibbits), left leg, last seen being used as a pointer during a safety briefing. According to the skeletons, it had been “redistributed by fate.” According to Lillian, someone had misfiled their own limb.

The riot began just after Lillian finished restitching the Perfect Corpse’s eyelids (“for a more mysterious expression” per her project notes) and just before the scheduled fire inspection.

A shriek echoed down the corridor, hit the East Wing vaulting, and bounced.

Lillian dropped her needle. “Scraps.”

Scraps appeared in the doorway already mid-anxiety. “Terribly sorry, mistress. I tried to warn them not to unionize—again—but then someone mentioned moisturiser and—”

“Moisturiser.”

“They say the dry look is out.”

Lillian strode down the corridor with the tight-shouldered gait of a woman whose budget meeting had been rescheduled three times by fate and once by a minor demonic incursion.

The East Wing corridor was tiled with old restraining orders, archived under *Obsolete Magical Constraints – Decorative Use Only*. Their text flared faintly under her feet as she passed.

At the barracks door, a barricade had been erected: overturned candelabras, bookcases, a toppled cart of labelled bones.

Behind it, skeletons waved banners made from old surgical gowns:

- NO BONES ABOUT IT: FAIR ROTAS NOW  
- EQUAL POLISHING RIGHTS  
- WE ARE MORE THAN AMBIENT RATTLES

On top of a tea trolley in the centre, Tibbits wore a monocle and a tea cosy as a cape.

“Down with femur favoritism!” he cried. “We demand even limb distribution!”

“You are made,” Lillian said, “from seventeen different donors. You are lucky you are symmetrical.”

“Gregor has three mandibles and a silk cravat,” Tibbits retorted. “I have a spinal kink and no kneecaps.”

Gregor—the cravatted skull in question—waved. One of his mandibles clacked in embarrassment.

“Gregor,” Lillian said, “was assembled for the cello section. You were assembled for… personality.”

A murmur. Tibbits faltered.

“You think I have personality?” he asked.

“Don’t fall for it,” Scraps whispered. “She said the same about the liver in the laundry basket.”

The liver in question had since been promoted to Head of Odours.

“You have a manifesto,” Lillian continued, as if correcting a form. “That’s slightly different.”

“We have demands,” Tibbits said, gathering himself. “Redistribution of limbs! Tuesday pizza nights! A plaque for Skronk, who dissolved himself in acid during a trust exercise!”

“The Skronk Memorial Avoidable Incident,” Scraps said solemnly.

Lillian looked at the barricade. At the banners. At the timer in her head that tracked how long until the fire inspector would arrive, and how much paperwork would be required if they found an unapproved skeleton uprising on the premises.

“Pizza nights,” she said. “Fair rotations for polishing. A plaque for Skronk. In return, no more dismembered protests in the summoning circles, and someone other than me updates the rota board so it does not, once again, list three people as On Fire.”

There was a pause while this sank into communal bone.

“We can do rotas,” Sawbones said. “We have bullet points.”

“Fine,” Tibbits said. “We accept.”

The barricade collapsed in a clatter of semi-contrite bones. A skeleton band struck up a tune with spoons and a ribcage. Someone attempted to crowd-surf and immediately fell apart; it was, in fairness, traditional.

Later, after the inspector had been diverted with a tour of the tower’s more presentable hauntings, Lillian slumped in her armchair with a goblet of mulled tar.

Across the room, the Perfect Corpse lay on his slab, half-sewn, humming faintly.

Scraps sidled in and handed her a flyer.

“Bone Words,” he said. “Thursday nights. Tibbits is calling it an anatomical arts initiative.”

Lillian stared at the flyer. There was an illustration of a skull rhyming.

“…I will need more goblets,” she said.

Under the slab, the tower hummed approval. Or indigestion. It was hard to tell.

## 5. Recital, Glitter, and Unscheduled Evaluation

The necropolis council, like all councils, believed in culture as long as someone else did the cleaning afterwards.

So when Lady Tarquin of the Boneclad Barrows announced an inspection, the Council appended a note: *Please demonstrate evidence of staff engagement and extracurricular development*. There was a form. There was a rubric. There were metrics.

Lillian, against her better judgment, gave the skeletons a stage.

The ballroom had been closed for years. It smelled of dust and old decisions. Cobwebs hung from the chandeliers like bylaws no one had updated. A draft came from somewhere the tower did not acknowledge in its floorplans.

They scrubbed it, begrudgingly. They rehung tattered drapes. They persuaded the chandeliers not to fall. By evening, the hall glowed with phosphorescent foxfire and stubborn optimism.

The programme, as submitted to the Council, read:

> ANATOMICAL RECITAL  
> Featuring: Undead Labor Force 43-B  
> Programme:  
> – “Ode to My Missing Fibula” (sonnet) – Tibbits  
> – “Mandible Shadows” (haiku) – Gregor  
> – “Cartilage” (interpretive movement) – Ensemble

Lady Tarquin arrived precisely on time, borne on a floating palanquin of carved bone. Her shawl was woven from semi-sentient worms who stared at everyone with bureaucratic curiosity.

“Charmed,” Lillian said, because the etiquette guide insisted.

“I do hope,” Tarquin said, “this won’t involve tap-dancing intestines. The Gut Waltz of ’79 was… memorable.”

“We are intestine-free,” Lillian said. “Per Regulation 12-B.”

The lights dimmed.

Tibbits stepped forward, bones polished, monocle gleaming.

“Ode to My Missing Fibula,” he intoned. The sonnet was unexpectedly good: funny, mournful, anatomically accurate. There were rhymes for shin that the tower would puzzle over for days.

Gregor followed with his haiku:

> Mandible shadows  
> in moonlight, I feel so vast.  
> Where is my spleen now.

The audience—necromancers, banshees, one ghoul who played the bansuri on weekends—applauded politely. Lady Tarquin dabbed at her monocle.

Then the interpretive dance began.

Accounts differ.

Scraps would later insist that he had *meant* to cue the slow waltz from the approved playlist, not the cursed polka infused with whale-song that he’d been saving for an emergency. Skronk’s cousin claimed he’d been pushed onstage with the trachea-kazoo. Gregor maintained that the glitter had been a metaphor.

Whatever the cause, the result was catastrophe.

The music lurched into a frenzied, off-key polka. Skeletons flailed in time and violently out of it. Ribcages flew. Vertebrae rattled on the floor like castanets. A skeleton attempted a dramatic leap, disassembled mid-air, and fell onto a lich with a plate of devilled eggs.

Then someone threw glitter.

Glitter is not, strictly speaking, a forbidden substance in necromantic practice. It is, however, discouraged. It is persistent. It lodges in sigils. It confuses the dead. It never, ever goes away.

Within seconds, the hall was a shimmering disaster. Skeletons sparkled. The banners sparkled. Lady Tarquin sparkled. Her worms shivered, offended at having acquired sequins.

Lillian walked onto the stage.

“This,” she said, glitter slowly settling into her fur, “is not what we rehearsed.”

“We added jazz hands,” Tibbits said, proudly.

Lady Tarquin rose. Bones straightened around the room. Even the music faded into a guilty cough.

“…Delightful,” she said.

There was a beat of disbelief.

“Chaotic. Horrifying. Entirely unplanned. Approved,” Tarquin added. “You have achieved authentic staff expression. The Council will be delighted. Possibly concerned. Both are acceptable outcomes.”

The hall erupted in ghostly cheers. Confetti—different from glitter only in its willingness to eventually fall—rained from nowhere in particular.

Lillian allowed herself one long, silent exhale.

Backstage, she leaned against a pillar.

“Well,” Scraps ventured. “At least nothing exploded.”

Skronk’s cousin hiccuped a small gout of flame into a bucket.

“Yet,” Lillian said.

The tower chuckled, a low vibration in the stones.

Far below the ballroom, in the workshop where sigils glowed faintly on stone, the Perfect Corpse twitched.

## 6. Activation Protocol

The activation ritual had eighteen steps in the official procedure and forty-three in Lillian’s notes.

The official document, stored on parchment in a drawer labelled *Dangerous, Do Not Misfile*, listed the basics: components, incantations, authorised practitioners. It had a signature line for a supervisor who had been dead for a century.

Lillian’s notes began with:

> 0. Make tea.  
> 0(b). Check no one is rioting.  
> 0(c). Check cupboards for Formerly Perfect Corpse (Poetically Unstable).

It was almost midnight when she finished step seventeen.

The workshop lights had dimmed of their own accord. Candles burned with blue edges. The tower’s hum had deepened, like someone clearing their throat at a meeting.

On the slab, the Perfect Corpse lay motionless, stitched and sanctified. Sigils traced his limbs. Threads glowed along his veins. The humming that had haunted the lab for months now vibrated in his bones.

Scraps hovered near the door, clutching a copper cup of embalming espresso.

“So,” he whispered, “what happens if he, you know… wakes up wrong?”

“Define wrong,” Lillian said.

They both glanced at the cupboard in the corner.

It bore, in three languages and one set of pictograms, the label: DO NOT OPEN – CONTAINS FORMERLY PERFECT CORPSE (NOW POETICALLY UNSTABLE). A faint scratching occasionally came from within, accompanied by rhyming couplets.

“Worst case,” Lillian said, “we file another incident report.”

“Under which category?” Scraps asked.

“*Unexpected Ontological Outcome*,” she said. “Now hush.”

She placed her paws on the corpse’s sternum. The sigils under her fur lit in response, a faint ache where they intersected old scars.

“Ego te exspectavi, Corpus Perfectus,” she murmured. “Surge et iudica.”

Lightning did not strike from outside. Instead, it crawled along the chandeliers, down the chains, into the slab. The walls shook. The pipes under the floor rattled like teeth.

The corpse inhaled.

It was not a natural breath. It sounded like bellows taking in their first air; like a file being pulled out of a too-tightly packed drawer.

The chest rose. Ribs creaked.

His eyes opened.

One jade, one onyx. Both sharp.

He looked at the ceiling. At the candles. At Lillian.

His first word, when it came, was more commentary than exclamation.

“…Darlings,” he said, voice dry as old theatre curtains. “What exactly am I wearing?”

There was a collective intake of breath from the assembled skeletons, apprentices, and one offended fern.

Tibbits, who had overseen the design of the ceremonial robe—composed of repurposed velvet, very fine lace, and three discarded theatre backdrops—drew himself up.

“It’s vintage Regency meets Ritual Chic,” he said. “It’s bespoke.”

The Perfect Corpse sat up. Something squelched.

He swung his legs over the side of the slab, flexed his fingers, and examined himself with professional interest.

“Muscles… responsive,” he murmured. “Joints… mostly cooperative. Soul integrity…” He tilted his head, listening inward. “Partial, but within acceptable parameters.”

He looked at Lillian.

“You look exhausted,” he said. “Have you been eating?”

Lillian blinked. “Are you… judging me?”

He smiled. It was not entirely reassuring.

“I am the Perfect Corpse,” he said. “It appears to be in the job description.”

Scraps leaned toward Sawbones. “I don’t know whether to be proud or afraid,” he whispered.

“Both,” Sawbones said. “File it under both.”

## 7. Performance Review

The tower did not have a form for *newly awakened entity complaining about decor*, but Lillian suspected someone would write one soon.

For now, she improvised.

She offered the corpse—who, after some discussion, agreed that “Perfect Corpse” was a job title, not a name—a chair. He declined. He preferred standing, apparently, or leaning in a way that reminded Lillian of actors on old posters in the archives.

“Why am I here?” he asked.

It was not an existential question. It was practical, the way someone might ask why a meeting had been called.

“You are a proof of concept,” Lillian said. “An answer to a question: can we build a being who remembers life, functions in death, and does not turn into a hazard report within twenty-four hours?”

“Ambitious,” he said.

“The Council prefers ‘innovative’ on grant applications,” she said.

He considered this. “What do you expect me to do?” he asked. “Lead a parade? Redeem the necropolis? File my own expense claims?”

“Start by walking,” Lillian said. “Then we’ll see.”

He walked.

His gait was too smooth for a corpse assembled from parts. Some of it was the stitching. Some of it was the tower. Underneath the floor, the old machine hummed in sympathy, aligning. Lillian felt it through the soles of her paws: the tower adjusting, slightly, to a new weight.

He touched the wall, fingers tracing an old crack.

“There’s something underneath,” he said.

“Several somethings,” Lillian said. “Don’t worry about it.”

He smiled. “You worry about it,” he said. “You’re very loud in here.”

He tapped his own chest.

Lillian did not answer.

“You have staff?” he asked, instead, looking at Scraps, Tibbits, the others.

“Bones,” Lillian said. “Staff implies benefits.”

“They riot,” Scraps said helpfully.

The Perfect Corpse tilted his head. “You riot?”

“We engage in constructive collective bargaining,” Tibbits said.

“Mostly about polishing,” Gregor added.

“And existential recognition,” Missy said. “And moisturiser.”

The Perfect Corpse nodded slowly, as if cataloguing a small, unexpected culture.

“Do you like it here?” he asked them.

“It’s… work,” Scraps said. “We have rotas now.”

“There is poetry,” Tibbits said. “Bone Words, Thursdays.”

“It beats being furniture,” said a chandelier.

The Corpse looked at Lillian again.

“You built something,” he said. “Not just me.”

“I inherited the tower,” she said. “I built the part that apologises for it.”

He laughed.

“You are tired,” he said. “You should sleep.”

“I have a report to file,” she said.

He looked like he might argue. Then he didn’t. “Show me the mop,” he said.

“You want to…?”

“If you’re going to keep me in a place,” he said, “I should know how its floors feel.”

Scraps fetched a mop.

The Perfect Corpse took it with the unembarrassed grace of someone who had never been told that certain tasks were beneath them. He began to clean, humming under his breath, and the humming shifted from vague vibration to something like a tune.

The tower watched, through pipes and stones and sigils.

In the Deep Bureau, far below the necropolis, an old machine marked a file: *CASE 47-C: STATUS – IN MOTION*.

## 8. Ball, Exit, and Aftercare

The Council insisted on a ball.

There was, in the bylaws, a clause that any successful deployment of a new magical category required “a suitable civic celebration.” The definition of suitable had been written by a committee that liked bunting and feared improvisation.

Lillian did her best.

The ballroom, freshly glittered from the recital, shimmered. Skeletons polished themselves. Banshees tuned. The golem DJ prepared a playlist that translated mortal power ballads into ghostly violin.

The Perfect Corpse wore a velvet tailcoat and an expression halfway between amusement and impending departure. Lillian had given up trying to fix the nose; it had grown on her. Or on him.

“You look like you belong,” she said, standing beside him at the balcony rail.

“Now that is a dangerous sentiment,” he said. “For both of us.”

Down below, Tibbits spun Lady Tarquin’s reanimated ostrich in circles that were technically waltz-adjacent. Scraps monitored the cake. Gregor wept into the punch for sentimental reasons.

“I remember more now,” the Corpse said quietly. “Fragments. I was an actor. Or a priest. Or a man who sold umbrellas in a very wet city. It blurs.”

“You don’t have to be who you were,” Lillian said. “Half the tower isn’t.”

He smiled sidelong. “And you?”

“I was supposed to be a music professor,” she said. “In a universe where the dead stay where they’re put.”

“This is more interesting,” he said.

“That is one word.”

He watched the dancers.

“The spell is fraying,” he said. “You know that.”

“Yes.”

“You built me to last longer.”

“I built you to be possible,” she said. “Lasting is up to the rest of it.”

He considered this, then extended a hand.

“One dance?” he asked.

She hesitated. Then, because there was no form to forbid it, she set down her goblet and took his hand.

They danced. The music swelled. The tower hummed along, just out of tune.

Slowly, as they turned, he faded.

It was not dramatic. Parts of him simply stopped catching the light. A fingertip. A shoulder. The shadow of a jaw.

“Tell them,” he said, voice thinning.

“I will,” she said.

“Fix the nose, next time,” he added, and even now there was humour.

He vanished on the downbeat.

The music stopped. The hall exhaled.

Someone sniffled. It might have been Scraps. Or the fern.

Lillian stood for a moment in the space where he had been. Then she picked up a broom.

“All right,” she said. “Let’s clean. The gargoyles wake in an hour and they don’t care about civic milestones.”

The skeletons saluted. The banshees fetched cloths. Lady Tarquin, still glittered, quietly helped put away chairs.

Work resumed. It always did.

Later, alone in her office with the tower humming around her, Lillian filled out the form:

> PROJECT: CORPUS PERFECTUS  
> OUTCOME: Partial success. Entity achieved self-awareness, ethical reciprocity, limited tenure.  
> RISKS: Elevated. Systems now aware of new category.  
> RECOMMENDATION: Proceed with caution. Document kindness carefully.

She signed, dated, and filed it in the drawer marked *Things That May Matter Later*.

Outside, the tower’s stone skin cooled in the night air.

Someone on the wall walk swore they saw a figure bow from the battlements—a long, theatrical bow, as if thanking an audience that might, someday, understand the joke.

The tower said nothing.

It adjusted its foundations, just slightly, making room.
