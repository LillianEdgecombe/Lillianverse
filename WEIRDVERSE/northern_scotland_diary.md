# The Echoes at Dundhu

*Field Journal of Dr. Lillian Edgecombe*
*Private correspondence to Miles Ferguson*

---

## Entry 1 – At the Edge

Dear Miles,

I promised I’d write every day, and here I am-in a tent, under Scotland sky, at the light of a rechargeable camping lamp.
The rental car still smells of spilled coffee and sweat. But more than that, I can feel your love in the way you carefully packed everything in my field bag—spare cables coiled with engineer’s precision, silica packets tucked into the recorder case, the small handwritten checklist you left on top: *Batteries. Backups. Come home.*
Your ears folded back when you handed me the waterproof case. German Shepherd worry, trying not to look like worry. I caressed your cheeks.
“Highland weather doesn’t care about academic schedules,” you said.
The Institute calls this place Dundhu-which sounds like a totally makeshift name, something that vaguely sounds like Scottish. It doesn’t appear on any public map—just a blank fold of northern Scotland, somewhere close to the Corrieshalloch Gorge National Nature Reserve.
The road ended a kilometre ago. I left a GPS positioning tag in the car, locked it and went on foot. What’s under my boots now is peat and heather and something else, something yielding that remembers being water. The air tastes of bog myrtle, old stone, and a metallic tang like the moment you bite your tongue. 
I am heavier than the last time we hiked together, but the place is flatter than a coffin. My pack straps bite a little at first, then settle. This body knows how to work: thighs burning, lungs pulling in the wet cold, claws testing for purchase in the torn turf. I feel large and solid and very, very visible against the glen.
The Institute’s brief said: *acoustic anomalies, unclassified harmonic events, possible environmental cognition.*
What it meant was: *something is making music here, and no one understands who is listening.*
I wish you were at my shoulder, grumbling about vague specifications and underfunded safety protocols. Instead, I have this notebook and the weight of you behind every word.
If I write as if I am talking to you, maybe I will remember where I stop and this place begins.


## Entry 2 – The Glen that Listens

Miles,
The glen doesn’t sound right, in every possible sense.
Visually, it’s almost ordinary: a wide valley opening toward an unseen sea, a stream threading silver between black rock, stands of birch and pine gripping the slopes. But the proportions are off. The way the hills curve is... too regular. As if... there's some kind of maths behind it, as if they [UNREADABLE] were waveforms. The stream bends in places where gravity would have nothing to say about it-like I swear I saw it branch and flow upwards along the slope of a hills, then curve down to merge again with the main stream.
I'm not drinking, I swear. But, och, I'd kill for a Guinness right now.
Silence here isn’t an absence: it’s a texture. When I stop moving, the quiet presses against my fur with the soft persistence of wool soaked through. If I clap my hands, the echo comes back too *clean*, too sharp, as... there was someone editing it.
I know, it's crazy, Miles, but you know me, aye? You know how much I care for sound, music and everything, and I know a thing or two about sound techniques and quality, right? So, would you believe me if I told you so?
The first time I set the recorder down, I thought the levels had glitched. On the waveform, the wind is there, the trickle of water, the soft thud of my own movements. Underneath, at the edge of audibility, something regular: pulses in ratios that keep resolving into familiar intervals. Fifths, sevenths. Then things no scale should comfortably hold. I have downloaded all the recordings on my laptop, I'll try to re-analyse them with the programs you suggested me before leaving.
If you were here, you’d roll your eyes and tell me I’m hearing patterns because I expect them. Then you’d take the recorder apart to prove it still worked.
My body is an instrument; I feel that more keenly here. My calves complain sooner than they did at twenty, but my balance is sure. When the turf gives unexpectedly—there are places it does that, like a rug pulled gently and then put back—the spread of my weight keeps me upright. Every extra pound, every curve you’ve kissed, resists and receives the slope. 
I pitched my tent in a shallow bowl in the earth that looked natural. There's a circle of stones all around it that must be man-made: too clean, too precise to be random, to be natural. But... maybe no man made it. I lay awake and listened as the circle hummed up through the ground and into my bones.
You asked me once why I chose musicology. I told you it was because I liked to know *why* things sounded the way they did.
Here, knowing feels beside the point.


## Entry 3 – The Stone and the Shape

Dear Miles,
I found a line of dolmens placed along a valley between two hills. I'm sure these structures are placed according to some kind of algorhytim, math, equation or whatever. If you were here, I'm sure you'd understand it and find it, and you'd find the right words to explain it to me. I miss you and Sarah like... like the desert misses the rain.
But I digress.
From a distance the stones look like any other ancient set—lichen-scabbed, hunched, a suggestion of human placement. A tad too evenly-spaced, however, in a way that feels... fake. Artefact.
Up close, their surface crawls. Not literally, obviously, but the pale striations and darker veins refuse to stay where my eyes put them. What at first looks like weathering resolves, if I hold still long enough, into something like notation. Not our notation—the staff is implied, then discarded, rhythms recorded in nested spirals, pitch in shifts of color rather than line, but I'm sure that, given enough time, I could translate this into a working composition. I tried to grab some pictures with my camera, but it can't focus on the surface. I think it gives it the headache, just like it does with me.
But what really put me on edge was their warmth. Stones shouldn't have been that warm. Like bodies. They were as warm as a living body. I was so scared I scrambled on my feet and fell on my butt.
Behind me, the heather rustled. I turned and stood up, expecting just some wind.
But there was some[UNREADABLE] at the end of the dolmen line. For a heartbeat I thought it was my reflection, except there was no surface to throw it back. Badger markings, my same round glasses, my long hair. Except...
It-she was... big. Heavy. It-she was me, but much fatter. Likely almost double my weight, a belly dangling almost to her knees and thighs as big as tree trunks. I felt something churning inside when I realized that her large body was... empty. Hollow. I could see holes poked into it, but... it looked really like it-she was a tree stump, broken, yet teeming with life around and within. Large yellow mushrooms were growing on her breasts, she had animals scurrying within her hair. I could see beaks poking from holes on her belly. 
“You touch everything as if it might break,” she said.
Her voice was mine, dropped an octave and worn softer, like an old sweater your grandma gave you as a Christmas present years ago and you still wear it as a memory. I meant to ask who she was. What came out instead was: “How long have you been here?”
She tilted her head, ears flicking back. “Long enough that I became home to many.” She caressed her belly. “I lived a home and became a home. Have you found your home, yet?”
A stupid, instinctive flush crept under my fur. At forty-two. At two hundred and eighty pounds, boots sinking, hair flattened by mist. "Yes," I replied.
She watched the colour rise in my face and snorted, almost amused. “I don't think so. But you found home for someone else.”
“Who?” I asked.
She didn’t answer. A breath of wind slid between us, carrying the smell of peat-smoke and baking bread, of our kitchen at home. When I blinked, she was gone. The moss where she’d stood held an imprint, deeper than my own.
I'm still pondering on her words. I'm here. I'm not at home. I was sure I had a home, but... I think I've been looking at this from the wrong point of view. Home is not a place. Just like this is not a real place.


## Entry 4 – Ratios in the Mist

Dear Miles,
The mist here behaves like a curious animal. It came down before dawn, extruding itself from the hollows. When I stepped out of the tent, the world ended a few metres away in soft white, but the mist did not simply obscure sound—it shaped it.
My footsteps returned to me with absurd precision: 1.618 seconds between impact and echo. Golden ratio. I checked. I checked again. Told you there was a clear math here.
You’d be pleased to know I swore at my own readings. I used a set of Scottish swear words my grandpa used to utter.
The glen folds in that weather. Paths kink when I look away, yet my sense of direction never fully abandons me. Some badger part of me tracks the underground lines; I can feel it moving beneath my feet. My thighs burn, my back sweats under the pack, but the weight feels... used, somehow.
While walking, I found that the valley was getting narrower and deeper, until it became a gorge. At the end of the gorge, basalt columns started seeping and crawling from beneath the moss. I heard running water above me, and all of a sudden I found myself in fronto a waterfall. There I met another shape.
At first I thought it was a column of mist thicker than the rest. Then the edges trembled into a vaguely humanoid outline, translucent and viscous, as if someone had poured me into an invisible mold and forgotten to let me set. Features slid across the surface—eyes that nearly resolved where my own sit, a mouth uncertain of its borders. I could guess drops forming two round shapes where my glasses usually are. When it spoke, the sound didn’t come from a throat. It shimmered in the air around me, vibrating the water droplets on my whiskers. “They tried to fix me,” it said. “Smooth every edge, melt every seam. Younger. Lighter. *Better.*”
The words landed in my chest like dropped stones.
It rippled, and for a moment its torso narrowed, hips trimming, shoulders pulling in—the silhouette magazines sketch when they say “after.” Then it relaxed, expanding outward, settling into a shape not interested in lines on a chart. “I learned I could pour instead,” it murmured. “Into whatever vessel the song needed.”
One fluid hand reached out. I almost stepped back—you know how I am with anything resembling medical gel—but some other instinct held me. The touch was neither warm nor cold. I simply felt its pressure against my paw. Harmonics flared through my bones, notes I didn’t recognize but understood anyway. Then the shape thinned, ran itself upward along the impossible waterfall, and vanished into the low clouds.
This will sound ridiculous, even to you, but my joints ached less afterward.
I am writing this with damp fur and the certainty that whatever is here does not only use instruments. It makes them. Today I was one of those instruments: born, broken and remade.
I'm scared.


## Entry 5 – The Circuits Under the Heather

Dear Miles,
You love maps. You’d hate this one.
I’ve tried to sketch the glen three times. The satellite reports are scant and blurry and totally unreliable. Yet, I can have georeferenced pictures and the folks at the institute taught me how to use a bunch of programs to do maps.
They don't work, obviously-not on the coordinates I give them. It's not their fault, nor mine. A stand of pines I’m sure was on my right this morning sits now to my left. The stream I'm trying to follow, which I watched turn sharply toward the north, appears on my page insisting on south.
I started drawing lines for what *felt* right rather than what my eyes insisted I’d seen.
The two sets of lines do not agree. But the wrong one hums when I touch it.
Deeper in, where the birches give way to some species that should not exist at this latitude—trunks braided, leaves like overlapping scales of tarnished copper—the air ticked. Static, at first, as if someone tried to tune the atmosphere to a station that didn't exist. Then it stabilized somehow. A figure resolved out of interference: an outline built from points of light, as if someone had drawn me with a stylus on a dark screen, then refused to add skin. Code scrolled under the surface—mathematical notations, waveforms, phrases from papers I haven’t written yet.
“You always did like to take things apart,” it said.
The voice was everywhere: in my ears, in the teeth you tease me about grinding at night, in the recorder’s tiny condenser mic.
My entire self was screaming at me, asking me to run away from this entire bullshit at lightning speed. Instead I said, “You’re not in my frequency range.” I know that it sounded like something you'd say. I said it on purpose. To feel you here with me. Somehow.
It... laughed? No. The logs around me glitched in unison, bark flickering to bare wood and back. “Consciousness is pattern,” it replied. “Pattern doesn’t care if it lives in meat or metal or mycelium.”
Your old arguments about artificial intelligence bubbled up; how you insisted any truly thinking machine would be so alien we might not recognize it. And... well, if I know you, you'd been happy to speak with this thing for hours. “What do you want from me?” I asked.
“Conductivity,” it said simply. Light raced from its fingertip into my outstretched claws. For a moment, every nerve in my body lit like a network diagram. I saw—felt?—other paths my life could have taken. Practice rooms instead of lecture halls. No adoption papers on our kitchen table. No you at all, some of them. Food everywhere in others. No fur at all in a couple. I've seen wars, cars, courtrooms. Each possibility had its own timbre. “You think your choices are yours alone,” the figure whispered. “Every decision rings through the score. Even the small ones. Especially those.”
Then it blinked out, leaving the smell of overheated circuitry and wet earth.
I checked my recorder afterward. The file it wrote for that hour is corrupted—no audio, only a block of raw data my laptop refuses to parse, some kind of format it doesn't recognize. When I feed the numbers into my spectral analysis software, the output is not a graph.
It’s a staff.
If you were here, you’d stay up all night trying to decode it. So will I.
If the glen is circuitry, I am being wired in.


## Entry 6 – A Quiet Uniform

Dear Miles,
I saw a watchtower that shouldn’t be here.
From below, it was just another lump on the ridge. Up close, the angles betrayed it—too regular, too exact, each stone fitting into the next with an engineer’s contempt for chance. No mortar. No lichen. Everything kept together through care and spite.
At the base, carved in a language that made my eyes water, a single symbol repeated three times. Not a word, not a letter, not a syllabe, but... I could form a shape with my lips that sounded like it. My fingers traced it despite myself. The stone under my pads vibrated—not enough to feel, but enough to be *heard* through bone. A vibration that made me feel like I was a turning fork.
Someone stood at the top, framed in the narrow slit where an arrow should go.
Their silhouette was my own, squared off, tucked in. Shoulders held with military precision. The fabric of the coat clung close where mine hangs loose, as if someone had tailored an entire life around the need to move quickly, cleanly, without spare motion.
They didn’t come down. Didn’t need to.
“Perimeter secure,” they said, and the words slid into my ear with the familiarity of faculty meeting minutes.
I hadn’t asked. “Who are you guarding?” I called up instead.
They were quiet long enough for the sky to dim a shade further toward violet.
“The mission,” they answered at last. “The composition.” You’d recognize the look in their eyes. It’s the same one you see on my face when I am defending a student from a budget cut, or a piece of music from being called frivolous. “Sometimes you have to mute an instrument,” they went on. “Sometimes you have to scrap the whole movement. For the integrity of the piece.”
Down in the heather, my feet sunk a little deeper. My calves trembled. My boots felt suddenly insufficient to keep me from sliding into whatever the tower rose out of.
“You can’t control this place,” I said.
“I can try,” came the reply.
For a moment I envied them that certainty. Then the wind shifted, carrying up from the glen a chord so low and so rich it bypassed my ears entirely. My ribs vibrated. The tower shuddered—in surprise? In warning?
The figure looked past me, toward the sound. “That,” they said, voice suddenly softer, “is not in my command.”
When I looked back from the valley, the tower was gone. My calves still shake as I write this, whether from the climb or from the knowledge that some part of me would rather build bastions than listen.
I'm growing tired, Miles. I feel it in my bones more than in my muscles. I want to go, I... I'm dreading I will never see the end of this path. Or that the end won't mean I'll be coming back home.


## Entry 7 – A House That Pours Tea

Dear Miles,
I walked into our kitchen today. Except I didn’t.
It was halfway up the ridge where no house should stand: a low cottage of stone too smooth to be weathered, its roof thatched not with reeds but with something that looked like solidified fog. Light gathered at the eaves but didn’t quite become windows. The door opened before I could knock.
For one aching second, I thought you’d step out—ears pricked, tea towel in hand. My heart hoped it so much it hurt.
Instead, a woman who could have been my cousin or my reflection if you wiped the badger from my blood stood there with the kettle’s steam curling around her. No fur; only pale skin freckled where my muzzle stripes would be. Round glasses, black hair, pulled back the way I wear mine when I throw clay at the studio wheel. “You’re drenched,” she said, ushering me in. My clothes were only damp. My bones felt sodden.
The interior was wrong in ways I can’t catalogue. The table had four legs but suggested more if I tilted my head at the right angle. The stove produced heat without flame-yet I heard the low noise of wood crackling in the fire. Every surface held the echo of things we own back home—the chipped mug you favour, the red teapot from Sarah’s first solo exhibition—but none were exact, the coffee cups I made from deep-sea mud last time.
She poured tea that smelled like what your fur does when you’ve been caught in light rain: warmth and dog and the faint ghost of machine oil, and lavender and turpentine. “They called this a cure where I came from,” she said conversationally, as if commenting on weather. “Strip away the animal. Leave only the human. As if one is contaminant and the other is truth.”
The way she watched my face made me aware of every line there, of the soft weight at my middle pressing against the table edge when I leaned. Of my animal reality. “Did it work?” I asked.
She smiled with my smile, minus the slight fang overlap you tease me about. “They took the fur. But the flesh still hurts.”
The tea warmed me from the inside out. My fingers, thick and callused from years of instruments and clay, looked almost elegant wrapped around the cup.
“This place,” she said, “records us. All of us. Every possible contour. It doesn’t care whether we call ourselves human or animal or something in-between. It cares about timbre.”
You once told me my speaking voice had the same timbre as my cello in the low register. I hear it differently here. Weighted. Useful. “I still have to go back,” I said.
She tilted her head as if listening to something behind my words. “You will write that sentence more than once before it stops being true.”
When I stepped outside, the cottage didn’t follow. It sat there, perfectly solid, perfectly wrong. By the time I reached the stream, there was no sign it had ever existed.
But my tongue still tastes of tea.


## Entry 8 – Minor Keys

Dear Miles,
I miss you. The cottage visit left me with your scent in my nostrils. I keep waking up alone in a tent. I just wish I could feel your fur in my paws.
Do you remember that score I kept on the piano, the one you said sounded like it had teeth? The one I only played after you’d gone to bed, when the flat was dark and the walls closer?
Today felt like standing inside it. I felt the teeth of the glen on my bones.
The trees in this part of the glen grow in spirals that deny Euclid his rest. Their branches interlock above the path in patterns that mimic notation—staves broken and overgrown, rests elongated into tunnels of shadow. Between their trunks the light goes sour, taking on a colour I can only describe as drunken amber.
The smell is wrong: not rot, not bloom. Something too sweet yet incredibly sour, like caramelized rotting pineapple.
A figure leaned against one of the twisted boles as if the tree had grown to accommodate the shoulder. Their garment—if you can call it that—was a hanging construction of bone and something too pale to be bark that I refuse to acknowledge properly. It clicked softly when they shifted, the way your toolbox rattles when you carry it up stairs. “You keep pretending you’re only here to observe,” they said. Their eyes were mine, stripped of every softness you’ve coaxed there over two decades and filled instead with a long, cold curiosity. “Some truths don’t arrive in major keys,” they went on. “Some need dissonance to be heard.”
The air around us answered with a chord that set my fur on end: gull cries strung through modem shrieks, organ pipes fed through broken speakers, children’s laughter stretched until it wasn’t human anymore.
I wanted to cover my ears. Instead, I listened, knowing it would have driven me crazy.
You’d tell me now about resonance frequencies and structural failure, how the right note at the right intensity can shatter glass. Here, the glass was me-did I shatter? I don't know, and that's what makes me fucking scared.
“They tried to tell you certain parts of yourself were unacceptable,” the figure murmured. “Too much. Too heavy. Too hungry. You turned that into music. Admirable. But you could also turn it into power.” The word tasted dangerous.
I thought of my students, of Sarah at sixteen insisting she didn’t deserve a future, of how I had used my voice then—not as a weapon, but as a rope. “I don’t want to hurt anyone,” I said.
“Oh, love,” they answered, and the word sounded like anything but affection, “you already do. Existence is dissonance. The question is whether you resolve it."
I left them in their grove of crooked staffs. As I walked away, the bone garment’s clatter followed me, rearranging itself into a rhythm I swear I’ve heard you tap out on the steering wheel in traffic.


## Entry 9 – Small Mercies

Dear Miles,
I found a clearing filled with the smell of antiseptic and wet fur and iron. More than a clearing, it looked like a trench, a huge stretch of muddy soil dug from the glen itself. Cots stood in crooked rows, their frames made of branches still sprouting leaves. On each, a body—blurry around the edges, uniforms from wars that never happened or all of them at once. Their faces wouldn’t stay fixed long enough for me to count them.
Someone moved between them with a bowl and cloth. Their outline shifted species the way others here shift light: human, badger, dog. Yet every configuration told me the same thing—midwife’s hands, nurse’s back, healer’s tired set to the shoulders. “You’re late,” they said without looking up.
“For what?” I asked.
“Understanding.” They smiled in a way I’ve seen in mirrors after difficult students finally grasp syncopation. “At Verdun,” they said—Verdun, specifically, though nothing about our surroundings acknowledged France—“I learned bodies are just instruments. Some play longer. Some play sweeter. All fall silent. What matters is what passes between them while they’re still warm. The air they breathe. How they turn that air into sound, words.”
Their fingers glowed where blood should have smeared. When they touched a wound, the torn edges did not close. Instead, music leaked out: a bar of song, a remembered lullaby, the crackling hiss of radio static.
“You keep looking for the grand gesture,” they told me. “The thing that will justify your fear. Maybe start smaller. Change the key of one person’s morning. Sometimes that’s enough.”
You’d like them, I think. You share the same exasperation with needless suffering.
When I left the clearing, one of the ghostly patients hummed *our* song. The one we wrote, half as a joke, in the supermarket queue. I don’t remember ever singing it aloud here.
The glen remembers for me.


## Entry 10 – Hands That Grow

Dear Miles,
My body is changing.
You’d say that’s hardly news; bodies are always changing. But this is not the gradual accretion of age, the softening we have named, together, as survival. This is... directed.
My claws have lengthened, not in the brutal way of overgrowth but in elegant spirals, translucent filaments extending from what used to be nails. When I drag them lightly over stone, the contact produces not a scrape but a tone. Different rocks, different pitches.
Today I realized I can tune them.
The skin between my fingers maps out vibrations as neatly as one of your schematics. I press my palm to a tree, and I feel sap moving, hear rings creaking as they expand micrometres in the cool. The trees breathe—literally. I watched a trunk swell on an in-drawn gust, bark splitting momentarily to reveal wet, luminous tissue beneath.
You’d want data. I have impressions, ratios, half-mad scribbles.
I think I'm really going crazy up here. And the worst thing is that I'm accepting it. It's a reality I can't change, after all. Or is it a reality at all?
The sky no longer behaves like sky. It holds at an eternal twilight, violet bruised with veins of gold that look too much like cracked glass. I have started humming without stopping. At first I was embarrassed to hear myself—forty-two-year-old badger woman muttering melody fragments like a child with a secret tune—but now the noise feels less like mine and more like something passing through.
My joints hurt less when I match pitch with the wind. My back straightens when I find the right interval under my breath. My stomach—the swell you rest your head on when we watch films—feels dense, not like burden but like anchor.
They are teaching my body to be an instrument.
I do not know what they plan to play. But this time I don't feel fear. I'm getting ready for it.


## Entry 11 – The Tower

Dear Miles,
There is a tower at the heart of Dundhu.
It wasn’t here yesterday, I swear, but it looks like it has always been here.
It spirals both up and down, a helix of stone and something more yielding. There's a score written on its surface-not in a notation we know. Sometimes they resolve into notes I understand, sometimes into the diagrams you sketch on napkins when solving a problem you don’t yet have language for.
I sit at its threshold now, notebook on my knees, claws leaving faint musical scars on the paper.
The ground around the tower pulses. Not metaphor. The peat rises and falls in slow breaths. Each exhale carries spores that glitter, hanging in the air just long enough for me to see their geometry before they sink back into soil, into me. Like sitting on a huge whale.
Your name is written across my forearms. Not in letters, though: in lines of sound. The script from the earlier expedition report—do you remember that classified file I wasn’t supposed to show you?—has started to appear on my skin. It rearranges itself to the rhythm of my pulse.
“They sing you into the soil,” one of the moving lines spelled this morning. “You will grow beautifully.”
I laughed then. I don’t now.
I don't want to grow beautifully. I want to get out. But I can't.
When I try to walk away from the tower, the path bends. Subtly at first. A slightly steeper [UNREADABLE] bog where I could swear there was heather. After an hour of stubborn refusal, I find myself back where I started, breathing hard, sweat soaking the shirt you insisted I pack because *layers, Lils*.
You would try to anchor me with ropes and pitons. There is nothing here for them to hold that the place does not choose.
[LONG PEN LINES, INCOHERENT SKETCHES]
I am tired, Miles.
Not just in the muscle way, though there is that too; carrying this much body up and down impossible topography would tax anyone. This is a tiredness of boundary. The effort it takes to keep my edges clear when everything around me wants to sing through. I'm trying to hold my shape, and it's tiresome.
Part of me—the academic, the mother, the woman who loves your ridiculous coffee rituals—still wants to come home, to lay all of this on your workbench and let you pick through it with wrenches and patience while I take a shower and wait for you in bed.
Another part, the part that hums when the tower does, is already loosening its grip.


## Entry 12 – Not a Goodbye

Dear Miles,
I now know this will not reach you.
I’m writing it anyway.
Tell Sarah I love her. She'll always hear Mum in the sound a glass makes when she gently passes a finger on its borders.
The tower’s surface is warm against my back. I’ve given up pretending the ground is separate from me. Roots—mine, not mine—snake around my hips where I sit, anchoring me in a way that feels alarmingly like relief.
My fingers are no longer entirely fingers. Threads of light extend from their tips into the soil, into the tower, into the stones of the circle beyond. When I flex, distant trees answer, leaves shivering in a pattern I recognize as the first lullaby I ever hummed to Sarah when she came home with us. Do you remember her first bedroom? Her bedroom in our Islington house, the house you found.
My voice, when I try it, is no longer singular. There are chords in it now, overtones that do not belong to any mammal. I say your name and hear it answered in moss, in water, in the small shifting bodies of creatures under the turf.
I feel you everywhere. I hope you'll be able to feel me everywhere as well.
I used to think of myself as solid. Curvy, you called me, with a look that meant the word was praise. Substantial. Grounded. Those things are still true. I am simply becoming ground in a more literal sense.
They are not taking me apart with violence. There is no ripping, no burn. It is an unmaking of edges. Where skin ends and peat begins is no longer a sensible question. I thought there would be fear at this point. There was, earlier, when I still believed I could map an exit. Now there is only a strange, tidal belonging. You will be angry with me, I know. Angry with them, with the Institute, with every bureaucrat who signed the clearance. You will stand in some corridor under bad fluorescent lights and demand accountability in that quiet voice that makes people listen.
Do that.
And I know you're going to cry. Cry at the institute, cry in bed, cry at home.
Do that, too.
But do not waste too much grief on the idea of me as lost.
I am not lost. I am distributed.
Some of me will remain in the recordings you’ll eventually pry from the Institute’s archive. Some in the way Sarah stirs sugar into her tea, three times clockwise, twice counter. Some in the students who will remember my insistence that music is less about notes than about attention.
And some of me will stay here, in Dundhu, humming under the heather.
If you ever stand in a northern glen where the silence feels too thick and your ears start to vibrate, put your hand on the nearest stone.
If it warms under your touch, if a low note gathers in your chest and makes your tail twitch despite yourself—that will be me, saying hello.
This is not a good ending, Miles.
It is simply an ending that keeps going.
With all the mass and music I have,

Lillian
