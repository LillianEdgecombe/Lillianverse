## The Exchange

### Chapter 1 — A Normal Morning

On the morning the universe went sideways, Miles woke up to the sound of the kettle boiling twice.

He lay there for a moment, on his back in the wide bed he had reinforced years ago—wood smoothed, joints double-bolted, each slat tested with his own weight and Lillian’s combined—listening to the familiar series of clicks and hums that meant his life was in order. The central heating sighed. A lorry downshifted on the main road. Somewhere in the house, a floorboard settled.

And the kettle boiled. Twice.

The first whistle was the usual one, faint through the bedroom door, rising in a small, urgent question. The second came eight seconds later, identical but wrong, like an echo played back on a slightly different device.

Lillian shifted beside him, fur rustling against the duvet. Her belly pressed warm against his flank as she rolled onto her side and reached for the glasses on the bedside table.

“Did you hear that?” she asked, voice thick with sleep.

“The kettle?” he said. “Yes.”

“Twice,” she added.

He listened again. The house had returned to its usual background hum. No third whistle. No ghostly kitchen appliance.

“Maybe Sarah’s here,” he said. “Making tea upstairs as well.”

“She told me she’s in Manchester until Thursday,” Lillian replied. “Unless she’s developed teleportation. In which case I’m proud, but I still want notice.”

She pushed herself up, broad badger shoulders rolling under the cotton of her sleep shirt, and swung her legs over the side of the bed. The mattress dipped and held, exactly as he had calculated. Miles watched the way she planted her paws one after the other, stretching her back, the early light from the curtains outlining her in a pale, familiar glow.

“I’ll check,” he said.

He padded out of the room, tail flicking once to avoid the doorframe, and made his way downstairs. The handrail was smooth under his paw, worn to a satin shine by years of mornings like this. The air smelled of house: their house, which meant wood polish, clay dust, the faint citrus of floor cleaner, and the lingering memory of last night’s garlic.

In the kitchen, the kettle sat on its base, perfectly innocent. Steam still curled from the spout. It had boiled once. The display agreed. The universe, according to the appliances, was behaving.

He pressed the button again just to be sure. The second boil sounded normal. The first one, whatever it had been, sat in his memory like a misfiled document.

He made tea. Two mugs, because if you lived with a badger who taught from nine and rehearsed from seven you did not skimp on caffeine. Dark tea with honey, no milk, the way she liked it. He carried the mugs back upstairs carefully, shoulders slightly angled so his bulk and the tray fit through the doorway without tapping the frame.

Lillian sat on the edge of the bed, brushing her hair. Her glasses caught the light. The bed creaked as he joined her; it was the good creak, the one that meant *I am holding you* rather than *please reconsider your life choices*.

“Any sign of spectral baristas?” she asked.

“Just the kettle,” he said. “Looking innocent.”

She took the mug, wrapping her paw around it, claws tapping lightly against the ceramic. Steam fogged her lenses.

“Maybe I dreamt the second whistle,” she said. “My dreams have been very administrative lately. Forms. Emails. Kettles filing grievances.”

He smiled. “What did the kettle complain about?”

“Being asked to perform outside its scheduled hours,” she said gravely. “Lack of clear communication regarding tea time.”

He laughed, then paused as his phone buzzed on the bedside table.

Two notifications popped up simultaneously. One from Brad—**u up?**—which was always an ominous start to any day, and one from his store manager in Leyton—**Emergency rota question. Call when you can.**

He checked the time. 7:14. The alarm on his phone read 7:13 in small, accusing numbers. The clock on the wall said 7:16.

“Our clocks disagree,” he said.

“They’re allowed a margin of error,” Lillian said. “We’re not running a nuclear facility.”

He frowned anyway. Time and kettles and schedules were his domain. Small discrepancies bothered him the way a slightly uneven drum pattern would have bothered him at seventeen.

Downstairs, the letterbox clacked. Once. Then again, fractionally off-beat, as if someone had put the same post through twice.

Lillian’s ears flicked.

“Did you hear—”

“Yes,” he said.

They looked at each other, mugs halfway to mouths, and then both started laughing, because what else was there to do? People got odd about the full moon. Old houses made noises. The brain misfired.

“We’re just tired,” Lillian said, finishing her tea in three firm gulps. “You’ve been juggling three stores and a broken freezer. I’ve been dealing with trustees who think diversity is a new brand of yoghurt. Our neurons are allowed to misbehave.”

He nodded. The explanation settled on his shoulders like a sensible coat.

In the bathroom, the tap dripped twice in quick succession where it usually dripped once, and the mirror reflected the two of them brushing their teeth in a way that made perfect sense—same badger, same shepherd, same fur, same toothbrushes.

Still, when Miles left for work, he double-checked the locks twice. Just in case the house had decided to be creative about doors as well.

Outside, the sky over Loughton was its usual pale grey. The air smelled of wet pavement and exhaust. The world, as far as the street could tell, was ordinary.

Inside the house, in the quiet kitchen that now held the memory of a duplicated whistle and mismatched clocks, something shifted just enough to count.

The kettle, innocent as ever, waited.

### Chapter 2 — The First Arrival

By the time Miles got home that evening, the duplicated sounds had downgraded themselves from “unnerving” to “mildly annoying.”

The fridge made a soft double-click when it cycled on. The front gate squeaked twice when Sarah came by to pick up some clay from the shed. The telly turned itself off and then on again during the weather, causing Lillian to mutter something unkind about signal gremlins.

“Maybe we have a poltergeist with OCD,” she said, poking the remote. “Everything must be done twice or it feels wrong.”

“If there is, it can help with the laundry,” Miles replied, stacking folded towels with precise corners. “At least then we’d get something out of it.”

He was halfway through checking the boiler pressure—because the last thing they needed was an actual broken appliance on top of phantom noises—when the doorbell rang.

It rang once. Then again. Two tasteful chimes, slightly out of sync.

“If that’s a parcel,” Lillian said from the kitchen, “I swear on all things ceramic that I am not signing for another mystery box from your cousin.”

Miles wiped his paws and went to the door. He opened it, ready for Brad with some ill-advised gadget, or a neighbour with a petition about the bins.

Instead, he found himself looking at himself.

The German Shepherd on the doorstep had his face, his build, his fur—short, dense, dark sable blending into tan—but rearranged around a different centre of gravity. He stood a fraction straighter, as if someone had tightened a screw in his spine. His clothes were familiar in cut—dark trousers, a shirt—but cleaner lines, no rolled sleeves, no faint smear of plaster dust on the cuff. His eyes were the same warm brown, but there was a layer behind them like tinted glass.

“Hello,” the other Miles said.

“Right,” Miles said.

He thought, briefly and unhelpfully, of mirrors and double-kettle whistles and parallel universes where he had said yes to different things. Then he did what he always did when confronted with something that made no immediate sense: he checked his environment.

Same front step. Same hydrangeas, slightly battered by last week’s storm. Same decorative hedgehog statue Sarah had insisted on buying. No camera crews, no prank show vans, no obvious wiring. Just him, the doorframe, and another version of his own shoulders.

“Can I help you?” he asked, because politeness was a default he could cling to.

The other Miles hesitated, then gave a small, almost formal nod.

“I believe I may be lost,” he said. “Or you are. It depends on your definition.”

His voice was the same in timbre but different in cadence—shorter sentences, less Manchester seeped into the vowels, more deliberate pauses.

“Okay,” Miles said slowly. “That’s… specific and unhelpful.”

Behind him, Lillian called, “Who is it?”

“Me,” Miles said. “Apparently.”

There was a silence from the kitchen that contained at least four entire arguments with the laws of physics.

“Bring you in,” Lillian said at last. “We can’t leave you on the step. The neighbours will talk.”

Miles stepped aside. The other Miles entered the hallway with the careful assessment of someone used to reading rooms as potential weaknesses. His eyes flicked over the shoe rack, the coat hooks, the slim console table that Miles had built to fit exactly between door and stairs. He registered the structural decisions, the load-bearing walls, the line of sight from front door to back.

“Nice house,” he said.

“Thank you,” Miles replied automatically.

They stood there for a moment, two German Shepherd men in a corridor built for one, both slightly angled to accommodate their backs and tails.

Lillian emerged from the kitchen, apron on, wooden spoon in paw. She stopped dead.

“Right,” she said. “Okay. No one panic.”

“I am not panicking,” Miles said.

“You are holding your shoulders like the boiler’s exploded,” she said. “Hello. I’m Lillian. Apparently still Lillian. Are you… also Miles?”

The other Miles nodded once.

“Miles Ferguson,” he said. “Different context.”

He extended a paw. Lillian shook it, her own paw dwarfed briefly between two nearly identical sets of fingers. Her fur bristled just a little, not with fear, but with the sensory overload of meeting a familiar engine running a different program.

“Okay,” she said again. “Context we can work with. Are you hungry?”

Miles looked at her.

“You’re offering him food?” he asked.

“He’s you,” she said. “Of course he’s hungry. And this is my house. I don’t care if the universe has decided to copy and paste you; it doesn’t get to override my hospitality.”

The other Miles watched them with a small, almost amused tilt to his head.

“I wouldn’t say no to tea,” he said. “If it’s not too much trouble.”

“It’s already made,” Lillian said. “Apparently twice.”

She turned and walked back towards the kitchen. Both Miles followed, one slightly behind the other, like a strangely specific procession.

In the kitchen, the kettle sat on its base, steam rising as if it knew it had become a character in a story. The mugs on the counter—Lillian’s favourite chipped blue one, Miles’s plain grey—looked suddenly insufficient.

“We need another mug,” Miles said.

“We have eight,” Lillian said. “You just like pretending we live in a minimalist advert.”

She reached into the cupboard and pulled down a third mug. It was one of Sarah’s, hand-thrown, slightly lopsided, glazed in a swirl of green and gold. She set it on the counter between the others.

“Right,” she said. “Tea first. Then explanation, if possible. If not, we will at least have tea while being confused.”

The other Miles watched her move around the kitchen, noting the way she adjusted her stance at the table to accommodate her belly, the way she reached for the honey without looking, as if its place in the cupboard was mapped into muscle memory.

“You handle anomalies remarkably calmly,” he said.

“I work at a music academy,” she replied. “And I married this one. Reality has been optional for some time.”

“Thank you,” Miles said.

He set about making tea for three, paws moving in automatic, comforting patterns. Kettle, mugs, tea, honey. The other Miles watched, then stepped in with unerring precision to catch a spoon that slid towards the edge of the counter. Their hands brushed. The hair on Miles’s arms lifted.

“You move like me,” Miles said.

“You move like me,” the other replied.

Lillian watched them both. The kitchen felt smaller and larger at the same time, as if the walls had shifted a few centimetres out and the air had thickened.

“We’ll figure it out,” she said. “One kettle at a time.”

Outside, the street remained stubbornly ordinary. A dog barked. A bus sighed at the end of the road. Somewhere, in some other version of Loughton, another kettle boiled, slightly out of sync.

### Chapter 3 — Multiplication

The second Miles arrived twenty minutes later, which was rude, because they hadn’t even finished processing the first.

They were at the kitchen table, three mugs of tea in a neat line. Funverse Miles—though he didn’t have the label yet, just the uncertain feeling of being downgraded to version one—sat in his usual chair, the one whose legs he’d adjusted to account for his weight. The other Miles sat opposite, posture straight, paws folded, eyes moving with almost professional politeness.

“So,” Lillian said, stirring her tea. “Let me see if I’ve got this right. You are Miles Ferguson. You are… from somewhere else.”

“Yes,” the other Miles said.

“You work with… what did you call it?”

“Operations,” he said. “Security, intelligence, some field work.”

Miles tried to imagine himself in a suit that wasn’t for weddings, doing anything involving “field work” that didn’t mean gardening. His brain rejected the image on technical grounds.

“And you woke up this morning,” Lillian continued, “and found yourself at our gate.”

“At a gate,” he corrected. “This address. Different details. Same core structure.”

He said it like he was reading off a report. His tone was calm. His paw, wrapped around Sarah’s green-and-gold mug, was very slightly too tight.

“You’re taking this very well,” Miles said.

“I am not,” the other replied. “I’m delaying my reaction until it’s useful.”

Before Lillian could respond, the front door opened.

Opened. Not knocked, not rung. The handle turned, the latch clicked, the door swung in.

“Sorry, sorry!” called a familiar female voice. “I know, I know, keys, but the vibe outside is weird and—oh. Oh, this is a lot.”

Lillian stood up so fast her chair scraped. Miles half-rose, instincts fighting between *intruder* and *put the kettle on*.

Another Lillian stood in the doorway.

She was Lillian, obviously. Same broad badger build, same long black hair, same glasses. But this version wore a bright red dress that looked like it had opinions about structural engineering, a leather jacket draped over her shoulders, and trainers that had seen more nights out than staff meetings. Her eyeliner could have cut glass. She carried a tote bag covered in band stickers.

“Wow,” she said, taking in the kitchen. “It’s like stepping into a parallel universe where I bought the sensible tiles.”

Funverse Lillian stared. For a moment, she saw her own face in someone else’s day and understood, viscerally, how Miles must have felt.

“You’re me,” she said.

“Unless I’ve accidentally wandered onto one of those prank shows,” the new Lillian replied. “In which case, points for production design.”

She dropped the tote bag onto a chair, which creaked in protest.

“Name?” the spy Miles asked, automatic.

“Lillian Edgecombe,” she said. “Funverse, apparently. Or that’s what the slidy, wobbly thing in my hallway whispered when it spat me out. I tried to text Sarah, but my phone turned into a coaster and then back into a phone, so I thought I’d just… pop round.”

“You don’t live here,” Miles said, somewhere between reassurance and accusation.

“No,” she said. “I live in Islington. Or I did, this morning. Now I’m in your kitchen. Nice mugs.”

There was a pause. The house, which had barely recovered from accommodating one extra German Shepherd, now had to resize itself for an additional badger with very strong opinions about colour palettes.

“We need more chairs,” Funverse Miles said.

“We have more chairs,” Lillian said.

They devolved briefly into furniture logistics. Sarah’s old stool was brought in from the studio. A folding chair emerged from the under-stairs cupboard. The table shifted a few centimetres to the left to accommodate everyone’s knees. It became a choreography: bodies and wood, paws and tails, chairs receiving weight in new patterns.

By the time they were all seated—two Lillians, two Mileses, one table trying its best—the doorbell rang again.

One chime. Two. Three.

“If that’s a third me, we’re instituting a rota,” Funverse Lillian said. “My ego can’t cope with that much self in one room.”

It was, as it turned out, a third Miles.

This one arrived wet.

He stood on the doorstep in a coat that had once been waterproof and now looked like it had absorbed several memories along with the rain. His fur was damp, clumping slightly. His eyes were darker than the others’, not in colour but in effect. He smelled faintly of damp concrete and the particular hospital antiseptic that never quite leaves your nose once you’ve encountered it.

“Hello,” he said quietly, when Miles opened the door. “I think I’m in the wrong… everything.”

Miles, who had been prepared for almost anything except himself with trauma, stepped aside.

“Come in,” he said. “We’re… figuring it out.”

The house swallowed another version of him. The table, already crowded, somehow made room for one more.

In the hallway, unnoticed, the thermostat clicked twice, picking an arbitrary number between realities.

### Chapter 4 — Assessment

By mid-afternoon, the house had upgraded from “cozy” to “structurally overpopulated.”

They had three Mileses: Funverse, Spyverse, Horrorverse. Two Lillians: Funverse and the one who would later be labelled Islingtonverse in Sarah’s sketchbook, because of her stories and her earrings. The air smelled of wet fur, tea, and the faint metallic tang of impending plumbing failure.

“We need a system,” Funverse Miles said, which was what he always said when the world turned into a pile of unsorted objects.

“Agreed,” said Spyverse Miles, which was unnerving.

They stood in the living room. It was the only space large enough for everyone to breathe without someone’s elbow becoming a conversational topic. The sofas sagged under badger and shepherd weight; the old rug did its best to hold its ground.

Spyverse Miles took in the room quickly. His gaze skimmed the exits, the windows, the potential weapons (lamp, heavy plant pot, ornamental drum), and the variables (duplicate selves, unknown mechanism). Then he turned back to Lillian. Funverse Lillian. The one whose house this was.

“With your permission,” he said, “I’d like to run a quick assessment.”

“By all means,” she said. “I’ve got sausages defrosting; we might as well be efficient while reality has its little episode.”

He nodded, as if she’d just signed off on a mission brief.

“We have multiple incoming versions of the same individuals,” he said, pacing slowly between the coffee table and the sofa. “All of us report a similar phenomenon: waking in our own spaces, then experiencing a… displacement. We arrive here. Same address. Same basic layout. Different details.”

“In my world,” Islingtonverse Lillian said, “the shed is a studio. Also, there’s a mural on the side of the house. And a neighbour who plays the trombone loudly at 2 a.m. If that helps.”

“In mine,” Horrorverse Miles said softly, “the shed is… locked.”

The room’s temperature dropped half a degree, or perhaps that was just everyone’s fur reacting.

Spyverse Miles didn’t flinch. “Mechanism is unknown,” he went on. “There’s no obvious portal, no external device, no visible energy source. But we can assume some kind of multiversal convergence focused on this locus.”

“This locus is my living room,” Funverse Lillian said. “Which, while flattering, is inconvenient.”

“My working hypothesis,” he said, “is that something about your configuration—your relationship, your… household—makes this place a stable anchor. Or unstable, depending on your feelings about duplicate selves.”

Funverse Miles rubbed his muzzle. “So we’re… what? The multiverse equivalent of a well-designed junction box?”

“You’re a node,” Spyverse Miles said. “High-connectivity. Low failure rate. The kind of place other systems default to when theirs go wrong.”

“You’re talking about my marriage like it’s a router,” Lillian said.

“He’s not wrong,” Funverse Miles muttered.

She shot him a look. “I know we do a lot of load balancing, love, but I refuse to be described as ‘high throughput’ in my own house.”

Islingtonverse Lillian snorted. “Speak for yourself.”

Spyverse Miles continued, unflappable. “For now, we prioritise safety. No one leaves the house alone. We establish zones—sleeping areas, shared spaces, quiet rooms. We avoid overloading the boiler.”

Funverse Miles perked up. “I can do zones,” he said. “We can put one Miles in the shed—”

“No,” all three Lillians said at once.

“Right,” he said. “No sheds.”

Horrorverse Miles had been silent, taking in the room with slow, precise sweeps of his eyes. Now he spoke.

“In my world,” he said, “we had… something like this. Once. People moved where they shouldn’t. Came back… different. Or didn’t come back.”

He turned his gaze to Funverse Miles. It was like being assessed by a version of yourself who had sat through more funerals than training sessions.

“The house is important,” Horrorverse Miles said. “It’s… soft. Keep it soft.”

Lillian’s throat worked around a sudden lump. She thought of the kettle, the worn sofa, the way the walls held their laughter. Softness as infrastructure.

“We will,” she said. “As long as we’re in charge, we will.”

Spyverse Miles gave a small, almost imperceptible nod. For all his talk of nodes and anchors, he understood that part.

“Then we proceed,” he said. “We wait. We observe. We feed everyone. We don’t panic.”

“You sound like a very competent fire warden,” Islingtonverse Lillian said. “Have you considered a career in event management?”

“At this point,” Funverse Miles said, “this is an event.”

Somewhere between the boiler and the upstairs bathroom, the pipes rattled twice, as if clearing their throat.

### Chapter 5 — The Quiet One

Horrorverse Miles slept on the sofa that first night.

He chose it himself, without argument, as if the idea of taking up a bed in someone else’s world felt like a presumption too far. When Lillian offered the guest room, he shook his head once, a small firm movement.

“I don’t… sleep well,” he said. “It’s better if I’m where I can see the doors.”

Funverse Miles recognised the logic, even as his heart clenched at the flatness of the statement. He had his own habits—checking locks twice, making sure the shed was bolted, knowing where the fire extinguishers were at work—but this was… more.

They found him a blanket, the heavy one Sarah had knit during her first winter in Manchester. He sat on the end of the sofa, back to the arm, knees drawn up, paws folded loosely. His coat, now dry, still smelled faintly of damp stone. His eyes followed every movement in the room with the quiet intensity of someone who had lived too long waiting for the next impact.

“Are you sure you’re warm enough?” Lillian asked, hovering with the blanket.

“I’m fine,” he said.

It was the kind of “fine” that meant *I have recalibrated my expectations down so far that this counts as luxury*.

“You’re in my house,” she said. “We do not do martyrdom in my house. That’s a rule.”

He looked at her, a long, weighing look that took in her solid presence, her stubborn kindness, the way she stood between him and the draught from the hallway without seeming to realise it.

“I’ll… try,” he said.

She draped the blanket over his legs, tucking it in at the sides. His paws flinched, just a fraction, at the unexpected contact. Then they stilled. His shoulders dropped by half a centimetre.

Funverse Miles watched from the doorway. He knew what it felt like to be taken care of by her without fanfare; he had not expected to feel a spike of something like protectiveness towards himself.

Later, when the house had settled and the other versions were distributed across spare rooms and inflatable mattresses, Miles lay in bed, listening.

He heard the gentle creaks of furniture under unfamiliar weight. The distant rumble of traffic. The double-click of the boiler.

From downstairs came the almost-silence of Horrorverse Miles: no restless tossing, no muttered dreams, just a steady, awake breathing. A guard dog posture in a house that had never asked for one.

“He’s not sleeping,” Miles whispered.

Lillian shifted beside him, tail brushing his shin.

“He’ll sleep when his body convinces his brain we’re not going to explode,” she murmured. “That takes time.”

“What happened to him?” Miles asked, before he could stop himself.

She sighed, a soft exhale.

“Something worse than a cabinet going viral,” she said. “Something that made him lose softness and keep going anyway.”

Miles stared at the ceiling. He thought of the multiverse like a crooked bookcase, some shelves overloaded, some empty, some holding versions of himself with more scars.

“Do you think I’d be like that,” he asked quietly, “if things had gone differently?”

Lillian considered.

“I think you would still be you,” she said. “But with less soup.”

He huffed a small laugh.

“We’re going to have to make a lot of soup,” he said.

“We are,” she agreed. “Starting tomorrow. Feeding protocol. It’s the only way we’re going to get through this without someone trying to fix the existential crisis with power tools.”

He thought of three Mileses and a boiler. He winced pre-emptively.

Downstairs, Horrorverse Miles shifted slightly, the blanket rustling. His eyes stayed open, watching the dark room like it might bite.

### Chapter 6 — Feeding Protocol

Breakfast was chaos.

Not loud chaos—not yet—but the quiet, dense kind where too many bodies tried to occupy a space designed for three and a half. Chairs scraped. Mugs multiplied. The kettle, popular and overwhelmed, boiled continuously, its element humming like an overworked heart.

Lillian took one look at the crowd in her kitchen and flipped an internal switch.

“Right,” she said. “We’re doing this properly. Miles, baseline, you’re with me. The rest of you, sit. No one touches the knives unless I say so.”

Spyverse Miles opened his mouth, possibly to argue about operational chains of command, then closed it. Horrorverse Miles sat where she pointed, back to the wall, eyes on the room. Islingtonverse Lillian perched on the counter, swinging her legs.

“I can help,” she said.

“You can chop vegetables and not set anything on fire,” Funverse Lillian said. “There’s a hierarchy.”

“Rude,” Islingtonverse Lillian said, and reached for the chopping board anyway.

Funverse Miles moved around the kitchen in his usual pattern—fridge, counter, cupboard—except now his route had to include detours around duplicate paws and tails. He adjusted chairs, shifted the table a few centimetres, nudged the bin with his foot so it lined up flush with the cupboard.

“We’re going to run out of plates,” he said.

“We’ll stagger,” Lillian said. “First wave eats, second wave washes, third wave judges.”

“I volunteer as tribute for judging,” Islingtonverse Lillian said.

“You can judge and wash,” Funverse Lillian said. “We’re a non-hierarchical breakfast.”

She cracked eggs into a large bowl, the shells making soft taps against ceramic. The smell of frying onions rose from the pan. Bread slid into the toaster in batches. The kitchen filled with the sounds of a household scaled up beyond its usual parameters: chairs receiving more weight, the scrape of cutlery, the low murmur of overlapping voices.

Spyverse Miles watched the flow like a logistics exercise. His eyes tracked Lillian’s movements, noting how she made the space work, how she positioned people so they weren’t in each other’s way. Horrorverse Miles watched her too, but with a different intensity, as if measuring the distance between her and the door and the knives.

“You’re feeding us as if that will fix it,” he said quietly, when she came near with a plate.

“It won’t fix it,” she said. “But it will mean no one passes out while we figure it out. Also, if the universe insists on bringing you all to my table, the universe can watch me do what I do best.”

“Which is?” he asked.

“Make sure people leave nourished,” she said. “Even if they didn’t know they were hungry.”

He looked down at the plate: scrambled eggs, toast, grilled tomatoes, a frankly ambitious amount of sausages. Something in his posture softened by half a degree.

“Thank you,” he said.

“You’re welcome,” she replied. “Eat. That’s an order.”

At the table, the conversation had devolved into banter about multiversal grocery stores.

“So you’re telling me,” Islingtonverse Lillian said to Spyverse Miles, “that in your world, Miles is some kind of spy-engineer hybrid who also runs logistics for a shadowy organisation, and you still get him to take the bins out?”

“Yes,” Spyverse Miles said. “It’s all about how you frame the mission.”

Funverse Miles snorted. “You turn it into a quest.”

“Waste management operation,” Spyverse Miles corrected.

Horrorverse Miles ate slowly, as if reminding his body that food was allowed. He chewed carefully, swallowing between other people’s jokes.

Funverse Miles watched him from the corner of his eye. The same jawline, the same shoulders, the same paws. Different wear patterns.

Lillian moved between stove and table, hips brushing chairs, tail swaying. She refilled plates, topped up tea, kept the flow moving. It felt, in a strange, crowded way, like the first time she’d fed Sarah and Miles together—a rehearsal for a family she hadn’t yet learned how to name.

“This is… nice,” Islingtonverse Lillian said, mouth full. “I mean, apart from the ontological crisis. Ten out of ten breakfast.”

“We aim for eleven,” Funverse Lillian said.

Spyverse Miles looked at her, then at his plate.

“My world doesn’t do this,” he said quietly, half to himself. “Food is… fuel. Not… architecture.”

“It is here,” she said. “Welcome to our building code.”

Somewhere between the second and third round of toast, the kettle cut out mid-boil and then started again, as if remembering itself.

No one commented. They were busy chewing.

### Chapter 7 — Lillian Meets Herself

Horrorverse Lillian arrived in the afternoon, as if the day had decided the emotional weather wasn’t sufficiently complex.

The doorbell rang once. Just once, this time. The sound sank into the house like a stone.

Funverse Lillian wiped her paws on a tea towel and went to answer it herself. The hallway felt narrower than usual, shoulders brushing the walls. Her heart beat in a steady, measured rhythm; she had performed at festivals, confronted boards, held Sarah’s hand in hospital waiting rooms. She knew how to walk towards a threshold.

The woman on the doorstep was her, as surely as the others had been. Same height. Same broad, solid body. Same round face. But the small differences made her feel like she was looking at a statue carved from denser stone.

Horrorverse Lillian wore a dark coat, buttoned to the throat despite the mild weather. Her fur lay flatter, as if it had learned not to bristle unless absolutely necessary. Her hair was braided back tightly, not in the loose, heavy plait Funverse Lillian favoured on busy days, but in a neat, functional rope. Her glasses were practical, simple frames. Her eyes were… steady. Not cold. Just heavily guarded.

“Hello,” she said.

Her voice was lower, roughened by use and by shouting in places that didn’t echo kindly.

“Hello,” Funverse Lillian replied. “You must be me.”

“I was hoping you’d say you were a cousin,” Horrorverse Lillian said. “But yes.”

They looked at each other in the threshold for a long moment. The air between them hummed with recognition and difference.

“Come in,” Funverse Lillian said. “We’ve got spare mugs and a rapidly failing understanding of causality.”

Horrorverse Lillian stepped inside. She moved like someone used to tight corridors and unreliable doorframes, body angled, assessing the floorboards for creaks. Her coat stayed buttoned.

In the living room, the Mileses fell silent.

“Oh,” Islingtonverse Lillian said softly. “Oh, you’re…”

“Later,” Funverse Lillian said, without looking away from her counterpart. “Kitchen first.”

She led the way. Horrorverse Lillian followed, eyes flicking over the photographs on the wall—wedding, band, Sarah at various heights and haircuts. Her gaze snagged on the one of all three of them at the beach, Miles mid-laugh, Lillian in a swimsuit, Sarah holding a dripping sandcastle.

There was a small pause. Then she moved on.

In the kitchen, Funverse Lillian gestured to a chair. Horrorverse Lillian sat, back straight, paws folded.

“Tea?” Funverse Lillian asked.

“Please,” Horrorverse Lillian said. “If it’s not… too much trouble.”

“It’s boiled twice already,” Funverse Lillian said. “It’s bored.”

She set about the familiar ritual, grateful for something for her paws to do. Kettle, mugs, leaves, honey. The clink of ceramic grounded her more effectively than any breathing exercise.

“How bad is it?” she asked, eyes on the teapot.

Horrorverse Lillian considered the question.

“There are worse worlds,” she said. “We’re not… at the bottom of the pile. But it’s… thinner there. Less… soft. Less… you.”

Funverse Lillian glanced up, met her own gaze, and saw the spaces where softness had been shaved away to keep moving.

“What did you lose?” she asked quietly.

Horrorverse Lillian’s jaw tightened. Her paws flexed once on the table.

“Later,” she said. “If ever. For now, we deal with what’s in front of us. Which is… an anomaly centred on your household.”

“Apparently we’re a node,” Funverse Lillian said.

“You are,” Horrorverse Lillian said. “In more ways than one.”

She took the tea when it was offered, wrapping her paws around the mug like it was a briefing folder. She sipped, eyes closing briefly as the heat hit her tongue.

“You still make it strong,” she said.

“Of course,” Funverse Lillian said. “We’re not animals.”

There was a tiny twitch at the corner of Horrorverse Lillian’s mouth. It might have been the ghost of a smile.

“You look… well,” she said, after a moment. “Strong. Settled.”

“I am,” Funverse Lillian said. “We are.”

Horrorverse Lillian’s gaze flicked briefly to the doorway, where, inevitably, a Miles-shaped shadow lingered.

“You have him,” she said simply.

Funverse Lillian’s chest ached.

“Do you?” she asked, before she could stop herself.

Horrorverse Lillian’s paws tightened around the mug. The tea rippled.

“Not in the way you do,” she said. “Not anymore.”

Silence settled between them. It wasn’t comfortable, exactly, but it was… respectful. Like the quiet between musicians who know the next note will hurt if they’re not careful.

“I’m sorry,” Funverse Lillian said.

“Don’t be,” Horrorverse Lillian replied. “Your happiness doesn’t hurt me. It… helps. To know there’s a version where we didn’t… lose everything.”

The kitchen blurred slightly at the edges for Funverse Lillian. She blinked, hard.

“We’ll keep it safe,” she said. “This house. This version. For as long as we can.”

Horrorverse Lillian nodded once.

“Good,” she said. “Because whatever brought us here? It’s… not benevolent. It’s… curious. And curiosity, in my experience, is hungrier than malice.”

Funverse Lillian thought of the kettle, the duplicated sounds, the way the walls seemed to flex.

“Then we feed everyone before it gets ideas,” she said. “Including you.”

“I don’t need—” Horrorverse Lillian began.

“Sit,” Funverse Lillian said. “Drink your tea. You can be uncompromising with the universe after you’ve eaten something.”

Horrorverse Lillian’s eyes flicked down to the table, then back up.

“Bossy,” she said.

“Consistent,” Funverse Lillian said.

The kettle, naturally, boiled again.

### Chapter 8 — Power Enters the Room

If Horrorverse Lillian was density, Rich/Spyverse Lillian was polish.

She arrived in a car that did not belong on their street. The neighbours, who had mostly decided that the slightly odd couple with the music and the big dog were harmless, peeked through curtains as a sleek, dark vehicle eased into the narrow road and stopped outside the house. The engine purred once, then died.

Funverse Miles watched from the front window, already bracing.

“Is it wrong that I recognise the sound of money?” he asked.

“Only if you start barking at it,” Lillian said, joining him. “Is that… me?”

The woman who got out of the car was definitely her. Same body, same fur, same face. But everything about her was curated: the coat, perfectly tailored; the jewellery, understated and unquestionably expensive; the way she walked, like the pavement had signed a contract promising to support her.

She rang the doorbell once. The sound was crisp.

“Right,” Funverse Lillian said. “Let’s see what I look like with a bigger budget.”

She opened the door.

Rich/Spyverse Lillian smiled. It was a controlled, warm, interview smile.

“Hello,” she said. “I hope I’m not interrupting.”

“Only our ongoing existential crisis,” Funverse Lillian said. “Come in. Wipe your boots. The universe may be collapsing, but my floors are still mine.”

The other Lillian stepped inside, glancing around with quick, appreciative eyes.

“Charming,” she said. “Very… lived-in.”

Funverse Lillian heard what she didn’t say: small, cluttered, non-optimised. She also heard something else: genuine fondness, like someone looking at an old rehearsal room.

In the living room, the assembled Mileses and Lillians shifted to make space. Rich/Spyverse Lillian took it automatically, sitting where she could see everyone.

“So,” she said. “We have a convergence. Interesting.”

“That’s one word for it,” Islingtonverse Lillian muttered.

Rich/Spyverse Lillian’s gaze skimmed over Spyverse Miles, ticking boxes. “You’re mine,” she said. “Or close enough.”

“Approximately,” Spyverse Miles said, utterly unruffled.

Horrorverse Lillian watched from an armchair, body still. Her eyes narrowed slightly.

“You’re very calm about all this,” Funverse Lillian said to her rich counterpart.

“Multiversal anomalies aren’t new,” Rich/Spyverse Lillian said. “In my world, they happen whenever someone with enough money and not enough supervision gets bored. The difference here is that we’re all in one place, and… you’re at the centre.”

She looked at Funverse Lillian with interest that bordered on clinical.

“You have… built something,” she said. “A life, a relationship, a household. It holds. Even under this. That has… value.”

Funverse Lillian bristled.

“I’m not a property,” she said. “This isn’t a development opportunity.”

“Everything is a development opportunity,” Rich/Spyverse Lillian said mildly. “The question is who benefits.”

She leaned forward, paws steepled.

“You have options,” she said. “We all do. We can ride this out and hope it resolves on its own, leaving a manageable amount of trauma. Or we can intervene.”

“Intervene how?” Funverse Miles asked warily.

“By leveraging the convergence,” Rich/Spyverse Lillian said. “We could… redirect the flow. Build a… facility here. A place where versions can… land safely. Be assessed. Repatriated. Or… redeployed.”

Spyverse Miles frowned, and it was a small comfort to Funverse Lillian that even he looked uneasy.

“You’d turn my house into a… hub,” she said. “A processing centre.”

“With funding,” Rich/Spyverse Lillian said. “With staff. With protections. You’d be compensated, of course. Your students would have scholarships for generations. Your family would never worry about money again.”

The offer hung in the air like a glittering, poisoned chandelier.

Horrorverse Miles shifted. “At what cost?” he asked.

Rich/Spyverse Lillian looked at him, not unkindly.

“The cost is always the same,” she said. “Access for some, exclusion for others. Control. Choosing who gets through.”

Funverse Lillian thought of the Royal Academy rejection letter. Of the students who walked into her office with shoulders already braced against disappointment. Of the way Miles checked the load-bearing walls of every room he entered.

“No,” she said. “Absolutely not.”

Rich/Spyverse Lillian tilted her head.

“You didn’t even ask for details,” she said.

“I don’t need them,” Funverse Lillian said. “If the solution to the multiverse is more bureaucracy and a front desk, I want no part of it.”

“You’re turning down stability,” Rich/Spyverse Lillian said. “Power.”

Funverse Lillian’s fur bristled, not with fear, but with something like pride.

“I have power,” she said. “I have a house full of people who trust me to feed them and keep them safe. I have a husband who will take apart the boiler before he lets it hurt us. I have a daughter who will draw this mess into something that makes sense. I don’t need your version of power.”

Rich/Spyverse Lillian regarded her for a long moment.

“Interesting,” she said softly. “Very interesting.”

Then, unexpectedly, she smiled. A real one, small and sharp.

“Good,” she said. “Someone needs to say no.”

She leaned back.

“For what it’s worth,” she added, “if anyone from my world tries to… commercialise this, call me. I can make their lives… complicated.”

“I’ll keep that in mind,” Funverse Lillian said.

The room exhaled. Horrorverse Lillian’s shoulders dropped a fraction. Spyverse Miles made a mental note about allies in adjacent universes.

Outside, the expensive car sat on the curb like a misplaced chess piece. Inside, a badger who had turned down a multiversal development deal went to put the kettle on.

### Chapter 9 — The Sarahs

The Sarahs arrived in tandem, as if the universe finally remembered there was a third point to this triangle.

Funverse Sarah came in through the back door, as she always did, shedding her coat on the chair nearest the garden like a golden retriever dropping fur on every surface. Her sketchbook was tucked under one arm; charcoal smudged her paws.

“Mum, Dad, I brought clay and existential questions,” she called.

Islingtonverse Sarah appeared in the front hallway simultaneously, hair piled up in a messy knot held together with pencils, wearing paint-splattered overalls that had not seen a washing machine in some time. She held a different sketchbook, bulkier, its spine reinforced with tape.

“This is not my stair carpet,” she said to no one in particular. “Also, I think my bus took a wrong dimension.”

They met in the kitchen doorway and stared.

“Oh,” they said together.

“You’re me,” Funverse Sarah said.

“You’re soft focus,” Islingtonverse Sarah replied. “Like, emotionally. Wow.”

They grinned at each other, identical in the tilt of their heads, the way their ears flicked with curiosity.

“I’m Sarah,” Funverse Sarah said. “I mean, obviously. Loughton.”

“Islington,” the other said. “Art school. Slightly more piercings. Same parents, different postcode.”

Funverse Lillian watched them with something like awe. She had expected to feel protective, uneasy, maybe even jealous on Sarah’s behalf. Instead, she felt… delighted.

“Tea?” she said, because she could not greet multiversal versions of her daughter with anything less.

“Yes,” both Sarahs said promptly.

They sat at the table, sketchbooks between them like peace offerings. The Mileses hovered at a respectful distance, deeply aware that they were in the presence of a phenomenon that required careful observation and no sudden dad jokes.

“So,” Islingtonverse Sarah said, flipping open her sketchbook. “How weird is it here?”

Funverse Sarah considered.

“On a scale of one to ‘Mum’s band accidentally starting a small revolution,’” she said, “we’re at ‘Dad times three and a half.’”

Islingtonverse Sarah snorted. “Solid mid-tier chaos.”

They compared sketches.

Funverse Sarah’s pages were full of careful studies: Lillian’s hands on a mug, Miles’s back as he worked in the shed, the curve of the sofa under their combined weight. She captured weight and line and the way light fell on fur.

Islingtonverse Sarah’s book was messier and larger, filled with bold, aggressive lines and chaotic compositions. Buildings bent at wrong angles. Figures blurred into each other. There were recurring motifs—doors, kettles, overlapping silhouettes.

“You draw them like they’re… holding,” Islingtonverse Sarah said, studying a sketch of Miles and Lillian on the sofa. “Like the house is wrapped around them.”

“They do,” Funverse Sarah said. “Hold, I mean. Each other. Me. The walls. It’s all… part of the system.”

“I draw them like they’re… breaking the frame,” Islingtonverse Sarah said. “Like the house can’t contain them.”

“Because in your world, it didn’t,” Horrorverse Lillian said quietly from the doorway.

Both Sarahs looked up, startled.

“You’re… Mum,” Funverse Sarah said.

“One of them,” Horrorverse Lillian said. “Don’t worry. I’m not here to… argue about grades.”

Islingtonverse Sarah’s eyes went wide, then narrowed with artist’s focus.

“Can I draw you?” she asked.

Horrorverse Lillian blinked.

“Now?” she said.

“Yes,” Islingtonverse Sarah said. “You stand like… all your corners are inside. I want to see if I can get that on paper.”

Horrorverse Lillian hesitated, then nodded.

“Ten minutes,” she said. “Then we talk about… protocols.”

Funverse Sarah watched her counterpart sketch, charcoal moving in swift, confident strokes. The likeness emerged quickly: the set of the shoulders, the way the jaw tightened, the small strain around the eyes. It was her mother and not her mother, all at once.

“You’re brave,” Funverse Sarah said, quietly.

Islingtonverse Sarah shrugged.

“I just draw what’s there,” she said.

Funverse Sarah looked at her parents—the ones who belonged to her, the ones who had fed her, held her, sat through her early, terrible sculptures and her later, better ones. She looked at the other versions, carrying other histories.

“Exactly,” she said. “That’s the brave part.”

### Chapter 10 — Boiler Room

The boiler had the decency to wait until after lunch before developing a personality.

Funverse Miles had been expecting it. You didn’t cram three versions of yourself, three versions of your wife, and two of your daughter into a house and expect the plumbing to sit quietly in a corner. Systems, he knew, had limits.

The first sign was the noise: a low, complaining rattle from the cupboard under the stairs where the boiler lived, followed by a whoosh that sounded like a sigh under protest.

“Ah,” Spyverse Miles said, head cocking. “There it is.”

“Do not say ‘I’ve got this’,” Funverse Miles said. “That’s how things explode in films.”

“I wasn’t going to,” Spyverse Miles said mildly. “I was going to say we should assess the situation.”

“Together,” Horrorverse Miles added quietly.

“Absolutely not,” Funverse Lillian said. “I am not losing all three of you to a boiler mishap. Pick one Miles and one Lillian. The rest of you stay where I can see you.”

They ended up with all three Mileses in the hallway anyway, because some events are inevitable.

The boiler cupboard was small and full of pipes. The air was warm, tinged with metallic tang and the faint smell of dust. Funverse Miles opened the door, kneeling to peer inside. Spyverse Miles crouched beside him, eyes scanning the gauges. Horrorverse Miles hovered just behind, close enough to grab them if something went wrong.

“Pressure’s high,” Funverse Miles said. “Not danger-level yet, but not… comfortable.”

“Relief valve?” Spyverse Miles suggested.

“Thought of that,” Funverse Miles said. “It sticks sometimes.”

“Your maintenance schedule is inadequate,” Spyverse Miles said.

“I’ve been busy dealing with multiversal houseguests,” Funverse Miles said. “It’s not like the manual has a section for ‘when your boiler is exposed to alternate versions of itself.’”

Horrorverse Miles leaned closer, sniffing.

“In my world,” he said slowly, “we had… an incident. Pressure built. Pipes burst. People… got hurt.”

All three of them went very still.

“Okay,” Funverse Miles said. “So we definitely don’t do that. We bleed off some pressure. Gently. No heroics.”

He reached in, paws careful, and turned the valve a fraction. The boiler hissed, then settled into a lower hum.

“See?” he said. “Manageable.”

The light above them flickered twice.

“You say that,” Islingtonverse Lillian called from the kitchen, “like the house isn’t listening.”

“If the house is listening,” Funverse Lillian replied, “it can make itself useful and stop duplicating noises.”

The boiler, perhaps offended, made a strange, gurgling sound.

“That’s not… normal,” Spyverse Miles said.

“Depends on your definition,” Funverse Miles said. “Ours has always been a bit dramatic.”

He tapped the gauge gently, the way you tap a sulking drum. The needle shivered, then settled.

“I think,” he said, “we’re okay. For now.”

Horrorverse Miles rested a paw on his shoulder.

“You’re good at this,” he said quietly.

Funverse Miles snorted. “At what? Making friends with boilers?”

“At… keeping things going,” Horrorverse Miles said. “At… seeing where the cracks might be and… holding them together. I thought… that was just me.”

Funverse Miles swallowed.

“It is you,” he said. “And me. And him. Apparently we’re a… multiversal brand.”

Spyverse Miles huffed softly. “We adapt,” he said. “Different fields. Same core function.”

For a brief moment, in the cramped, warm cupboard, three versions of the same man existed in perfect alignment: paws on pipes, eyes on gauges, shoulders bearing different worlds and the same instinct.

Then the boiler hissed again, bringing them back.

“Right,” Funverse Miles said. “We’ll schedule a proper check when the universe calms down.”

“Assuming it does,” Horrorverse Miles murmured.

“It will,” Funverse Miles said. “Because if it doesn’t, I’m not renewing its warranty.”

The joke was terrible. The other two smiled anyway.

### Chapter 11 — Hallway Moment

The house slept uneasily that night, and so did its occupants.

At around three in the morning, when the street outside was quiet and the boiler had settled into a low, steady murmur, Funverse Miles woke up with the bone-deep certainty that someone was standing in the hallway.

He slid out of bed carefully, not disturbing Lillian, and padded to the door. He opened it a crack and peered out.

Spyverse Miles stood halfway down the corridor, leaning against the wall. Horrorverse Miles sat on the top step of the stairs, blanket around his shoulders, paws dangling loosely over the edge. The light from the landing lamp—left on low, because no good ever came of stumbling in the dark—cast them all in soft, yellowed tones.

“Couldn’t sleep either?” Funverse Miles asked.

“Sleep is inefficient when your environment is unstable,” Spyverse Miles said.

“Can’t turn it off,” Horrorverse Miles said simply.

Funverse Miles stepped out, closing the bedroom door behind him. The laminate floor was cool under his paws.

“We’re going to be in trouble tomorrow,” he said. “Lillian has a strict policy about husbands who function on four hours.”

“You’ll nap,” Horrorverse Miles said. “On the sofa. She’ll put a blanket on you and pretend not to watch.”

Funverse Miles blinked.

“You know that?” he asked.

Horrorverse Miles’s mouth twitched.

“It was… the same,” he said. “Before.”

There was a silence heavy with things not said.

“How did you… lose her?” Funverse Miles asked, then winced. “Sorry. That’s—”

“It’s fine,” Horrorverse Miles said. “Or… it isn’t. But words don’t make it worse.”

He looked down at his paws.

“We had… something like this,” he said. “A convergence. Only… it wasn’t centred on our house. It was… bigger. Cruder. People came through who… didn’t care about soft places. They wanted… leverage. She got in their way.”

Spyverse Miles’ jaw tightened. “I can imagine the type,” he said.

“She told them no,” Horrorverse Miles said. “Like she told everyone no when they wanted to… use us. They didn’t like that.”

Funverse Miles felt a wave of nausea, as if the floor had tilted.

“Did they—” he began.

Horrorverse Miles shook his head once, sharply.

“She made sure I didn’t see,” he said. “She always did that. Even at the end.”

The landing light hummed quietly. Somewhere downstairs, the fridge clicked.

Spyverse Miles spoke into the silence.

“We don’t let that happen here,” he said.

“We don’t,” Funverse Miles said, with a conviction that surprised even him. “Not while I have working paws and a toolkit.”

Horrorverse Miles gave a small, tired laugh.

“You think you can fix everything with bolts and careful planning,” he said.

“I know I can’t,” Funverse Miles said. “But if the choice is between doing nothing and doing what I know how, I’ll pick the toolbox.”

He sank down onto the top step beside Horrorverse Miles. Spyverse Miles joined them, leaning his shoulder against the wall in a posture that looked almost casual.

“What does it cost you?” Horrorverse Miles asked. “Being… like this. Carrying… everyone.”

Funverse Miles considered.

“Sleep,” he said. “Some back pain. The occasional anxiety spiral. But… I’m not alone. That helps.”

He glanced back at the closed bedroom door.

“She notices,” he said. “When I’m… fraying. She asks. She sits. She makes soup. She drags me to the sofa and takes the remote away.”

“Must be nice,” Horrorverse Miles said, without bitterness, just observation.

“It is,” Funverse Miles said. “I’m… trying not to take it for granted. Even now.”

Spyverse Miles shifted, the movement small but telling.

“In my world,” he said, “I chose the job over… this. Over… domesticity. It felt… safer. Less to lose.”

“And?” Funverse Miles asked.

“I lost things anyway,” Spyverse Miles said. “Just different ones.”

The three of them sat there, variations on a theme, the hallway holding them like a narrow, imperfect chapel.

“Maybe that’s the constant,” Horrorverse Miles said. “We all… lose. The variable is what we build before.”

Funverse Miles nodded slowly.

“Then we build as much as we can,” he said. “So when something comes to take it, it has to work very hard.”

Spyverse Miles looked at him, then gave a small, respectful nod.

“You’re not just coping,” he said. “You know.”

Funverse Miles blinked.

“What?” he asked.

“You think you’re just… improvising,” Spyverse Miles said. “But you’ve built a system that holds under multiversal stress. That’s… impressive.”

Funverse Miles felt heat creep into his ears.

“It’s just… life,” he said.

“Exactly,” Horrorverse Miles said. “That’s the hard part.”

They sat there until the landing light timed out and clicked off, plunging them into dimness. None of them moved to turn it back on.

Eventually, one by one, they drifted back to their respective beds and sofas. The house, briefly, felt less alone in its strangeness.

### Chapter 12 — The Table

On the third day, they managed a proper meal with everyone at the table.

It was an achievement on par with negotiating a peace treaty. Chairs were borrowed from neighbours under vague pretences about “having people over.” The table was extended with a folding section Miles had built years ago and never expected to need for this many versions of himself.

Lillian presided at one end, Funverse Miles at the other. The various other thems arranged themselves in between like alternate chords in a progression.

The spread was ambitious: roasted vegetables, two kinds of stew, bread in absurd quantities, a salad that existed mostly for colour. The air was warm and thick with the smell of food and fur. Conversations overlapped and braided together.

“In my world,” Islingtonverse Lillian was saying, “we did a residency in Berlin. The shed never recovered.”

“In mine,” Rich/Spyverse Lillian said, “the shed is a security outbuilding with biometric locks.”

“In mine,” Horrorverse Lillian said, “we don’t talk about the shed.”

That closed that branch of the conversation.

The Sarahs compared notes on tutors and gallery spaces. Spyverse Miles and Rich/Spyverse Lillian exchanged information in clipped, efficient phrases that made Funverse Lillian roll her eyes.

“You two could run a coup with a napkin and a ballpoint pen,” she said.

“We have,” Rich/Spyverse Lillian said. “Twice.”

Horrorverse Miles ate quietly, but not as warily as before. He reached for seconds without asking, which Funverse Lillian counted as a win.

At one point, Funverse Miles looked around and experienced the strange, dizzy sensation of seeing his life refracted: one version of himself laughing at a story, one analysing escape routes, one staring at his mug like it might bite. One version of Lillian gesturing animatedly, one leaning back with the relaxed confidence of someone whose signature made buildings appear, one sitting very still and watching everyone else.

“If the neighbours could see this,” he murmured.

“They’d complain about the parking,” Lillian said.

There were moments—small, bright, sharp—when understanding surfaced without needing to be named.

When Rich/Spyverse Lillian complimented Funverse Lillian’s seasoning, the approval landed somewhere old and raw. When Spyverse Miles stood up to clear plates without being asked, Funverse Miles saw a version of himself that had learned service in different hierarchies and still defaulted to washing up.

When Horrorverse Lillian asked Sarah—both Sarahs—about their work, her questions were precise and practical, and the girls answered with the respect given to someone who had survived long enough to have opinions.

No one talked about how they would leave. No one talked about whether they could.

They talked about clay and gig venues and drainpipes. They passed dishes, poured tea, navigated elbows and tails.

For a little while, the multiverse shrank to the dimensions of a table and the reach of a ladle.

### Chapter 13 — Vanishing

The first disappearance was almost gentle.

Spyverse Miles was in the middle of making a point about evacuation protocols in multilevel buildings when his sentence cut off halfway through a word.

He didn’t fade or glitch. There was no flash of light, no dramatic swirl. One moment, he was leaning against the kitchen counter, paws tracing plans in the air. The next, the space he occupied was just… air.

His mug dropped, hit the floor, and shattered.

For a moment, no one moved.

“No,” Funverse Lillian said, voice flat. “Absolutely not. We are not doing that.”

“He’s gone,” Rich/Spyverse Lillian said, staring at the empty space as if it might reform.

“He was mid-breath,” Funverse Miles said, ears flat. “He didn’t… finish.”

The house hummed with wrongness.

“Maybe he got sent back,” Islingtonverse Sarah said, too quickly.

“Maybe,” Horrorverse Miles said. “Or somewhere else.”

Lillian’s paws clenched around the back of a chair.

“We should mark it,” she said. “Where he was. For when—if—he comes back.”

She fetched a piece of chalk from the drawer where they kept spare candles and rubber bands. She drew a small, neat circle on the floor where his paws had been.

“There,” she said. “You were here.”

It did not fix anything. It made a shape around the absence.

The others began to vanish over the next hours, as if whatever force had brought them was slowly retracting its experiment.

Islingtonverse Lillian disappeared mid-laugh, hand extended towards a plate of biscuits. Her laugh cut off like someone pressed pause. The biscuits clattered to the table.

“Rude,” Funverse Lillian said, on her behalf.

Rich/Spyverse Lillian went next, sitting very still in the armchair, a notebook on her lap. One blink and she was gone, notebook falling closed.

“She knew,” Horrorverse Lillian said. “She’s good at exits.”

Horrorverse Miles vanished standing in the garden, looking up at the sky. One moment, there was a large, damp-furred shepherd silhouette; the next, only night and hydrangeas.

Funverse Miles stood where he had been, paw outstretched, fingers closing on nothing. His chest hurt in a way that felt like landing from a sprint without warning.

“He didn’t say goodbye,” he said.

“He didn’t have time,” Lillian said. “None of them did.”

The Sarahs clung to each other in the hallway, sketchbooks pressed between them like shields.

“What if we go next?” Funverse Sarah whispered.

“Then we’ll deal with it there,” Islingtonverse Sarah said. “That’s what we do. That’s what they taught us.”

“I don’t want to leave them,” Funverse Sarah said.

Islingtonverse Sarah rested her forehead briefly against hers.

“You might not,” she said. “Or you might. Or it’s just me. Or neither. We can’t… control it.”

“I hate that,” Funverse Sarah said.

“Me too,” Islingtonverse Sarah said.

In the end, Islingtonverse Sarah vanished in the studio, charcoal in hand, halfway through a line. The mark on the paper ended in an abrupt, unfinished stroke.

Funverse Sarah cried in the garden, hard and quiet.

Horrorverse Lillian did not vanish. Neither did Funverse Lillian, or Funverse Miles, or Sarah. For a while, that felt like a reprieve. Then it began to feel like being left alone after a party where half the guests had been stolen.

“Is it over?” Miles asked, when the house had been still for an hour.

“I don’t know,” Lillian said. “I don’t know how to measure ‘over’ when the variable is reality.”

They moved through the house, taking stock. Spare beds empty. Extra mugs unused. Chalk circles on the floor.

“We should pack away the chairs,” Miles said, eventually. “Before someone trips.”

“Not yet,” Lillian said. “Leave them. For a bit.”

The house, bereft of its extra occupants, felt both too big and too small.

### Chapter 14 — Silence

The silence the next morning was a different kind of wrongness.

No extra breathing in the living room. No faint rustle of someone turning over on the sofa. No duplicated footsteps in the hallway. Just the three of them—Lillian, Miles, Sarah—and the house that had briefly hosted a convention of their alternate selves.

The kettle boiled once. The front gate squeaked once. The thermostat clicked, obedient and alone.

Miles walked through each room, checking.

In the spare room, the bed was unmade, sheets creased in the shape of someone who no longer lay there. On the bedside table, a glass of water sat half-drunk, a ring left on the wood.

In the studio, Islingtonverse Sarah’s sketch lay open on the easel: Horrorverse Lillian, captured in bold strokes, half her face fully rendered, the other half just beginning to emerge. The line where she had vanished ended in a sharp, lonely mark.

In the garden, the grass was flattened where Horrorverse Miles had stood. The air smelled of damp earth and, faintly, the memory of his coat.

Miles touched each trace gently, as if pressing on bruises.

Lillian, in the kitchen, found the chalk circles on the floor and left them. She wiped the table where crumbs remained from the shared meal. She straightened chairs, then deliberately left two a little skewed.

Sarah sat at the table with her own sketchbook open, staring at a blank page.

“I don’t know how to draw absence,” she said.

“You already have,” Lillian said softly, nodding towards the half-finished sketch in the studio.

“That’s interruption,” Sarah said. “It feels… different.”

Miles joined them, paws washed, fur damp.

“We could pretend it was a strange dream,” he said. “People do. About less.”

“We could,” Lillian said. “But then the universe would get away with it.”

He sat down heavily, the chair accepting his weight.

“What do we do instead?” he asked.

“We remember,” she said. “We talk about them. We put them in the art. We adjust the boiler. We… keep going.”

Sarah looked up.

“And if it happens again?” she asked.

Lillian met her gaze.

“Then we decide again,” she said. “Where the line is. What we’re willing to give. What we’re absolutely not.”

The house listened, its walls holding their voices.

Outside, the street was normal. Bins, buses, neighbours. Inside, a multiverse’s worth of echoes settled into the furniture.

### Chapter 15 — Accounting

A week later, the house had reverted to something like its old rhythms, but the new grooves remained.

Miles found himself setting three mugs out sometimes before correcting to two when Sarah wasn’t visiting. His paws would hover over a third plate at dinner before he laughed at himself and put it back.

He started a list in a notebook—not a to-do list, for once, but an accounting.

*Things I learned from other me’s:*

1. My coping mechanisms count as skills.  
2. I am allowed to not like being watched and still be useful.  
3. Being the one who notices cracks is not a character flaw.  
4. The line is not always where I think it is. Sometimes it’s further out. Sometimes closer in.

He wrote slowly, sitting at the kitchen table, pen scratching quietly. The house smelled of soup. Again. Some things did not change.

Lillian came in, wiping her paws on a towel.

“What are you writing?” she asked.

“Minutes from the multiverse meeting,” he said.

She chuckled, leaning over his shoulder to read. Her fur brushed his cheek.

“That’s good,” she said, tapping the first point.

“Feels… arrogant,” he said.

“It’s accurate,” she said. “You’ve been keeping us together for twenty years. It’s nice to have external verification.”

He turned the pen between his fingers.

“Do you…” he began, then stopped.

“Do I what?” she asked.

“Do you ever wonder,” he said, “if there’s a version of you out there who looks at me and thinks I’m… not enough?”

She blinked, then frowned.

“No,” she said. “Because that version of me would be wrong.”

He huffed a small laugh.

“Seriously,” she said. “All those other Lillians? Variations on a theme. Different budgets, different wars, same core. None of them looked at you and saw… less. They saw… different. And most of them wanted what we have.”

He thought of Horrorverse Lillian’s eyes in the kitchen, and the way Rich/Spyverse Lillian had looked at their table.

“Point taken,” he said.

Sarah wandered in, charcoal on her paws again, as if the week had looped.

“I finished the piece,” she said. “From the sketch.”

They followed her to the studio.

On the easel stood a canvas that made Miles’ throat tighten.

The painting showed the hallway, the three Mileses sitting on the stairs in the half-light. Their faces were turned towards something outside the frame—maybe the bedroom door, maybe an invisible threat, maybe each other. The lines of their shoulders echoed and diverged. The landing light cast a circle around them like a spotlight and a target.

Behind them, barely visible at first glance, the suggestion of other figures—Lillians and Sarahs—built into the layers of paint, like impressions left when people leave a room.

“It’s called *Load-Bearing*,” Sarah said.

Lillian exhaled slowly.

“It’s beautiful,” she said.

Miles stared.

“I look… tired,” he said.

“You are,” Sarah said. “But you’re also… there. All of you. That’s the point.”

He swallowed around the lump in his throat.

“Can we… keep it?” he asked.

She smiled.

“It’s yours,” she said. “Obviously.”

They hung it in the hallway, above the stairs. From then on, every time Miles went up or down, he saw himself seeing himself, layered with versions who had survived different things.

It was, in its own way, a grounding exercise.

### Chapter 16 — Doorbell

The doorbell rang on a Sunday, three weeks after everything, and the three of them jumped.

Lillian looked up from the book she was reading on the sofa. Miles froze halfway through tightening a loose screw on the coffee table. Sarah, visiting for the weekend, paused mid-text, thumb hovering over the screen.

The bell rang once. Just once. A polite chime.

“If it’s another me,” Sarah whispered, “I’m charging rent.”

“If it’s another me,” Miles said, “I’m making him take over the grocery inventory.”

Lillian stood, smoothing her dress, and walked to the door. Her heart beat steadily. Her paws were dry. She had, she realised, developed a new kind of calm—the kind that came from knowing they had already survived a very specific kind of weird.

She opened the door.

It was their neighbour, Mrs. Patel, clutching a Tupperware box the size of a small galaxy.

“Hello, dear,” she said. “I made too much biryani. Again. Please save me from myself.”

Lillian laughed, the sound coming out lighter than she expected.

“Come in,” she said. “You’re in luck. We have a whole multiverse worth of appetite.”

Mrs. Patel blinked.

“Is that a new band?” she asked.

“Something like that,” Lillian said.

Miles took the Tupperware, paws careful; it was warm through the plastic. The smell hit him—spice and rice and home in someone else’s flavour profile. His stomach growled on cue.

“Thank you,” he said. “You’re a lifesaver.”

“You always say that,” Mrs. Patel said, smiling. “One day you’ll have to prove it.”

Sarah appeared behind them, peering over Lillian’s shoulder.

“Is that Mrs. Patel’s biryani?” she asked. “We are truly blessed.”

They all laughed, the sound rolling out into the hallway, brushing past the painting on the wall. The Mileses in the frame looked down impassively, but Miles liked to think they approved.

Later, after Mrs. Patel had gone and the house smelled of spice and fur and shampoo, they sat at the table with plates heaped high.

“Do you think it’s over?” Sarah asked, between mouthfuls.

“I think that whatever did this has moved on to annoying someone else,” Lillian said. “Or gone back to sleep. Or written a report and filed it under ‘inefficient experiment, do not repeat.’”

“We should be so lucky,” Miles said.

He looked around at the kitchen—the same worn table, the same chipped tiles, the kettle behaving itself. The extra chairs had been stacked away but not buried. The chalk circles on the floor had faded, scrubbed at by time and mopping, but in certain light you could still see where someone had once stood.

“If it happens again,” he said, “we’ll handle it.”

Lillian reached across the table, her paw warm on his.

“We will,” she said. “Because that’s what we do.”

Sarah lifted her glass of water.

“To us,” she said. “The multiverse’s favourite junction box.”

Miles groaned. “Please don’t let that catch on.”

“Too late,” Lillian said. “It’s canon now.”

They clinked glasses, paws, cutlery. The house, which had been a node, a hub, an anchor, settled comfortably back into being what it had always been: a place where people arrived, were fed, and left changed.

Outside, the streetlamp flickered twice, for old time’s sake, then steadied.

Inside, in the soft circle of their kitchen, a badger, a shepherd, and a golden retriever ate biryani and laughed, carrying the weight of other lives lightly, for the moment.

The kettle, unremarkable and blessedly singular, boiled once.
