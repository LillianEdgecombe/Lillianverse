# The Choir of Defiance

## Chapter I — The Almighty's Blunders

The storm had been gathering since dawn, a slow congregation of malice that began as whispers in the heather and grew to proclamations of wrath by the time it reached the crumbling battlements of Edgecombe Manor. It was the sort of storm that made sensible folk bar their doors and cross themselves and made the very stones remember they had once been mountains. The wind came first, howling down from the peaks like a pack of starved wolves, followed by clouds the color of old pewter and twice as heavy.

By evening, the tempest had arrived in earnest, beating its way across the Highland moor with the persistence of a debt collector. It struck the manor like a fist against a door. The ancient stones groaned in their mortar beds; the ivy that had spent decades conquering the east wall now clung desperately for their lives. Windows that had weathered three centuries of Highland fury now bellied inward like the cheeks of a starving man.

Somewhere in the labyrinthine chimneys, a network of flues and passages that had been added to and modified by six generations of Edgecombes, each with their own peculiar ideas about how smoke ought to behave, a rook had taken shelter and was now protesting the weather in a language that predated sermons, predated Latin, predated perhaps even the notion that birds ought to be polite about their grievances. Its cries echoed through the house like the complaints of a disgruntled ancestor, which, given the Edgecombe family history, was entirely possible.

In the dining hall, beneath a chandelier that had been installed during the reign of the second George and had chosen sometime during the reign of the third to lean a few degrees toward perdition—whether from age, earthquake, or simple philosophical disagreement with the concept of hanging straight—Lillian Edgecombe dined with the appetite of a besieging army and the table manners of a woman who had long since decided that propriety was a luxury she could not afford.

The dining hall itself was a testament to the Edgecombe family's complicated relationship with both wealth and taste. The walls were paneled in oak that had been dark when it was installed and had since achieved a blackness that seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it. Portraits of dead Edgecombes glowered from their frames, each painted by artists who had clearly been instructed to emphasize the family's capacity for righteous indignation over their capacity for beauty. The carpet, once a respectable burgundy, had faded to the color of dried blood and bore the stains of three centuries of spilled wine, dropped food, and the occasional theological argument that had escalated beyond mere words.

The table itself was a massive thing of carved mahogany, scarred by decades of carving knives, heated serving dishes, and the occasional fist brought down in emphasis during particularly heated discussions about the nature of divine providence. Tonight it groaned under the weight of Lillian's feast: a goose that had been twice-basted, thrice-buttered, and blessed with herbs that had been gathered under circumstances the local rector would have found deeply troubling; a ham glazed with honey and studded with cloves like a medieval mace; potatoes that had been roasted in duck fat until they achieved a golden perfection that bordered on the obscene; and vegetables that had been cooked with such skill and attention that they seemed to glow with their own inner light.

She was a tall, monumental badger-woman, all bristling authority and velvet hair, enormous in black silk that had been tailored to accommodate both her impressive girth and her even more impressive sense of drama. Her sleeves were rolled to the elbow like a surgeon's before some exultant trespass against propriety, revealing forearms that spoke of years spent kneading bread, wringing necks, and gesticulating with sufficient vigor to make strong men step backward. Her hair, a lustrous black shot through with silver threads that caught the candlelight like captured starlight, was pinned up with what appeared to be a collection of antique hairpins, though closer inspection would have revealed them to be small surgical instruments of uncertain provenance and definitely questionable purpose.

Her round spectacles, perched on the end of her substantial snout, reflected the candlelight in a way that made her eyes seem to burn with an inner fire. Those eyes, dark as Highland peat and twice as deep, held the sort of intelligence that made bishops nervous and theologians reach for stronger drink. They were the eyes of a woman who had read every book in her considerable library, understood most of them, disagreed with half, and was prepared to argue with God Himself about the remainder.

Before her lay the remnants of her feast. The goose, which had begun the evening as a thing of beauty and was now half-vanished, bore the marks of her surgical precision. She carved with operatic flourishes, as if each slice were a triumphant aria in some grand performance that only she could hear. Her knife—a wicked thing of Sheffield steel with a bone handle worn smooth by generations of Edgecombe hands—moved through the meat with the confidence of long practice and the enthusiasm of genuine appetite.

Between bites, she sang to the storm with her mouth full, her voice a rich contralto that had once graced the choir of St. Giles Cathedral before certain theological disagreements had made her presence there... inadvisable. The songs she sang were not hymns, though they had the cadence of sacred music. They were her own compositions, theological arguments set to melody, complaints against the Almighty rendered in four-part harmony.

"D'ye hear it, then?" she cried, pausing in her carving to gesture toward the windows with a fork that dripped with goose fat. Her cheeks shone with drippings, and her eyes blazed with the fervor of a woman who had found her calling and intended to pursue it with the single-minded determination of a terrier after a rat. "Yon Almighty beatin' His pots an' pans, as though thunder were invention! Och, it's a poor show, that. A child could dae it wi' a copper sheet and a boot heel. I could dae better, and I will, by Saint Andrew's whiskers."

The thunder that punctuated her declaration seemed almost apologetic, a halfhearted rumble that spoke more of divine embarrassment than divine wrath. Lillian snorted with derision and took another bite of goose, chewing with the satisfaction of a woman who had just made her point and found it delicious.

"You are speaking to the sky again," said Sarah from the far end of the table, where she sat in black like a candle's shadow, a slender golden retriever whose fur caught the gaslight like spun silk despite her deliberate attempts to appear as morbid as possible. Her wavy hair, the color of autumn wheat, was tied back with a strand of ribbon that had been stolen from a funeral wreath—not metaphorically stolen, but literally pilfered from the grave of one Hamish MacLeod, who had died of consumption and whose mourners had possessed an unfortunate taste for overly elaborate memorial decorations.

Sarah's dress was black as a minister's conscience and twice as unforgiving, cut in the severe lines that she favored and adorned with the sort of buttons that looked as though they had been carved from the bones of particularly melancholy saints. Her hands, delicate as a pianist's but stained with ink and charcoal, moved across her sketchbook with the precision of a surgeon and the passion of a lover. She sketched her mother with a pen that hissed and scratched, the way knives talk when they meet in a drawer, capturing not just Lillian's physical presence but something of her spiritual enormity as well.

"Look fierce again, please," Sarah continued, her voice carrying the sort of dry wit that made vicars nervous and made her mother proud. "No—fiercer. Imagine it is tea with an English dean."

The suggestion had the desired effect. Lillian's expression transformed from mere indignation to something approaching righteous fury, the sort of look that had once caused a bishop to flee Edinburgh rather than face her in theological debate. Her spectacles flashed in the candlelight, and for a moment she looked less like a woman dining and more like an avenging angel who had decided that the Almighty's work needed significant editorial revision.

"Aye, that's the look," Sarah murmured, her pen flying across the paper. "The look that says 'I've read Aquinas and found him wanting.'"

Lillian shoved the goose platter aside with a gesture that sent gravy cascading across the mahogany like a small, delicious flood. She leaned upon the table with meat-slick fists, her considerable weight making the ancient wood creak in protest, and bellowed upward with the sort of voice that had once filled cathedrals and was now employed in the considerably more ambitious project of filling the space between earth and heaven.

"Hear me, ye perfumed English heavens!" she roared, her Scottish accent thickening with passion until each word seemed to carry the weight of Highland stone and the fury of Highland wind. "I dinna bow to a mediocrity in a clouded wig! Ye've made a universe in the manner o' a timid clerk—small-minded, orderly, a tasteful hush o' dust—an' I mean to outdo ye with noise, with life, wi' a choir ye cannae bear to hear for shame!"

The words hung in the air like a challenge thrown down before the throne of the Almighty Himself. Outside, the rain flung itself against the tall windows with renewed vigor, as if the storm had taken personal offense at being called mediocre. Somewhere in the distance, a shutter tore loose from its moorings and slapped against the stone wall like a disgraced fish, adding its own percussion to the symphony of the tempest.

The dining hall seemed to hold its breath, waiting for divine retribution or divine response. The portraits of dead Edgecombes looked down with expressions that suggested they had heard such blasphemies before and were curious to see how this particular theological argument would resolve itself. The chandelier swayed slightly, whether from the wind or from the sheer audacity of Lillian's declaration, it was impossible to say.

Sarah continued sketching, apparently unmoved by her mother's challenge to the Creator of the universe. She had heard such declarations before, had grown up with them as other children grew up with lullabies. To her, Lillian's theological rebellions were as natural as breathing, as inevitable as the tide, and considerably more entertaining than anything the local church had to offer.

The door at the far end of the hall sighed open with the weary resignation of wood that had been opening and closing for three centuries and had long since given up any pretense of enthusiasm for the task. Through it stepped Miles Ferguson, bringing with him the peculiar patience of a man who had endured worse climates, worse roofs, worse employers, and considerably worse sausages than anything Scotland had to offer.

He was a lank German Shepherd in a sodden coat that had once been brown but was now the color of Highland mud mixed with Highland rain and seasoned with Highland despair. His long limbs moved with the careful economy of a man who had learned to conserve energy for the battles that mattered, and his shoulders bore the permanent stoop of someone who had spent years bending over engines, over wounded men, over the countless small mechanical failures that seemed to plague any household that contained Lillian Edgecombe.

His face was weathered like old leather, marked by years of sun and wind and the sort of experiences that left permanent lines around a man's eyes. Those eyes, pale blue as winter sky, held the sort of weary wisdom that came from having seen too much of the world and having decided that the best response was a combination of dry humor and stubborn competence. His ears, pointed and alert despite his generally resigned demeanor, twitched at the sound of Lillian's theological pronouncements, but his expression remained carefully neutral.

Mud had welded his boots to the floor with an audible argument, a squelching protest that spoke of paths turned to soup and drainage systems that had given up any pretense of functionality. He stood in the doorway for a moment, dripping steadily onto the ancient carpet, and surveyed the scene before him with the air of a man taking inventory of the latest disaster he would be expected to remedy.

"Storm's turned the upper path into a soup," he announced, his voice carrying the sort of accent that belonged to no single country but had borrowed a drawl from one place, a corner of grit from another, and a lonesome prairie wind from somewhere else entirely. It was the voice of a man who had traveled far, seen much, and had learned to adapt his speech to whatever company he found himself in, though the underlying cadence spoke of American origins and experiences that had left their mark on both his tongue and his soul.

"The privy drain's givin' up the ghost," he continued, with the matter-of-fact delivery of a man reporting on the weather, "and the roof above the east nursery is thinkin' about resignin' also. Smells like Monday, tastes like Thursday, and sounds like the devil's own percussion section."

He paused, considering the scene before him: Lillian in her theological fury, Sarah sketching with the concentration of a monk illuminating manuscripts, the remains of the feast scattered across the table like the aftermath of a very civilized battle. His expression suggested that this was, by Edgecombe standards, a relatively quiet evening.

"Did ye bring the cogs?" Lillian asked, her voice carrying the sort of eagerness that other women reserved for news of grandchildren or particularly scandalous gossip. She dabbed at her chin with a napkin that had once been white and had since become a palimpsest of victories, each stain a testament to meals conquered and proprieties abandoned.

Miles nodded and began the process of extracting himself from his sodden coat, a procedure that involved considerable wrestling with wet wool and the sort of muttered commentary that suggested the coat had developed opinions about its treatment. Beneath the coat, he wore a vest of brown leather that had been patched and re-patched until it resembled a map of some particularly war-torn continent, and a shirt that had once been white but was now the color of old parchment.

He deposited a canvas satchel on a chair with the gravity of a physician unveiling surgical instruments, though the contents were considerably more mysterious and potentially more dangerous. The satchel itself was a thing of beauty, crafted from canvas that had been waxed and re-waxed until it could have shed water in a hurricane, and fitted with brass buckles that gleamed like captured sunlight despite the general gloom of the evening.

"Cogs," he said, beginning to unlace the satchel with the sort of ceremony that the occasion seemed to demand, "bellows, valves, a length o' gut—don't ask from where, because the answer involves a conversation with the butcher in Benroy that I ain't eager to repeat—some catgut strings from the fiddler who died last winter, and a mouthpiece that used to belong to a cornet, assuming the cornet belonged to a drunkard with more enthusiasm than skill."

As he spoke, brass gears winked in the gaslight like a handful of stolen suns, each one polished to a mirror finish and inscribed with markings that suggested they had been crafted by someone with both considerable skill and a peculiar sense of humor. The bellows was a thing of blackened leather and brass fittings, constructed with the sort of attention to detail that spoke of German engineering and American pragmatism working in unlikely harmony.

"Also," Miles added, producing a small wooden box with the air of a magician revealing his final trick, "a box of biscuits, if we're bein' domestic about it. Mrs. MacReady in the village made 'em, and she says they're blessed, though I suspect that's more wishful thinking than theological fact."

Sarah did not look up from her paper, but her pen paused for a moment in its scratching. "Mama intends to be blasphemous about it," she observed, her voice carrying the sort of casual certainty that came from long experience with her mother's theological projects.

"Blasphemy," Lillian intoned, rolling the word around in her mouth like a fine wine, savoring its texture and its implications, "is but the courtesy of speakin' up when the landlord's furniture is ugly." She began to clear the table with her forearms, a gesture that sent bones and carving knives and the gravy boat in a perilous migration across the mahogany surface. Plates clattered, silverware sang against crystal, and the remains of the feast rearranged themselves with the sort of chaos that suggested the laws of physics were merely suggestions in the Edgecombe household.

"Bring the bellows," she commanded, her voice carrying the authority of a general ordering a charge. "I'll show ye a lung worthy of Genesis."

## Chapter II — Flesh Is Just Clay

The kitchen had always been the heart of Edgecombe Manor, but it had never before been asked to serve as both laboratory and cathedral, workshop and shrine. It was a vast chamber that had been carved from the living rock of the Highland hillside, its walls lined with stone shelves that had held provisions for three centuries of Edgecombes, each generation adding their own peculiar requirements to the architectural conversation. The ceiling was a maze of blackened beams, hung with copper pots that had been polished to mirror brightness and iron hooks that had supported everything from sides of beef to the occasional theological argument that had required suspension pending further consideration.

The great hearth dominated the eastern wall, a cavern of stone and iron that could have roasted an ox and frequently had. Tonight it burned with a fire that seemed to possess opinions about the proceedings, crackling with what sounded suspiciously like commentary and occasionally spitting sparks that arranged themselves in patterns that suggested either divine disapproval or simple coincidence, depending on one's theological inclinations.

The long oak table that occupied the center of the room had been cleared of its usual burden of bread and butter and the remnants of civilized dining, and now bore the components of Lillian's first experiment in divine improvement. Brass gears lay scattered across its surface like the scattered thoughts of a clockmaker's fever dream, each one polished to a mirror finish and inscribed with markings that suggested they had been crafted by someone with both considerable skill and a peculiar sense of humor about the nature of mechanical precision.

The bellows that Miles had brought was a thing of beauty and terror, constructed from leather that had been blackened by age and use until it resembled the hide of some mythical beast, fitted with brass valves that gleamed like captured sunlight and connected by a network of tubes and pipes that suggested the circulatory system of a creature that had never been intended by nature. It was the sort of device that belonged in the workshop of a master craftsman or the laboratory of a natural philosopher, not in the kitchen of a Highland manor, but then again, the Edgecombe family had never been particularly concerned with the conventional boundaries between the sacred and the profane.

Lillian's prototype was a marvel of theological audacity and mechanical ingenuity, though it would have made both theologians and engineers reach for stronger drink. At its heart was a human skull—the provenance of which was best left unexamined—that had been fitted with a brass throat piece that gleamed like captured starlight. The throat piece itself was a work of art, engraved with patterns that seemed to shift in the candlelight, suggesting now musical notation, now anatomical diagrams, now symbols that belonged to no earthly alphabet.

The skull's jaw had been hinged with brass fittings that allowed it to open and close with mechanical precision, and within the cavity of the mouth, a goose's wishbone had been suspended on catgut strings, positioned to serve as a tongue that would never know the taste of earthly food but might, if Lillian's theories proved correct, know the taste of celestial harmony. The entire assembly was connected to the bellows by a network of tubes and valves that suggested the respiratory system of a creature that breathed music instead of air.

"Now then," Lillian said, rolling up her sleeves with the air of a surgeon preparing for a particularly challenging operation, "let us see if the Almighty's monopoly on breath can be broken by a woman wi' sufficient determination and a proper understanding of pneumatics."

She positioned herself beside the bellows with the confidence of a conductor taking her place before an orchestra, though this orchestra consisted of brass and bone and the accumulated theological grievances of three centuries of Edgecombes. Her hands, large and capable and stained with the honest work of creation, grasped the handles of the bellows with the sort of reverence that other women reserved for prayer books or love letters.

Miles took his position at the valves, his long fingers moving across the brass fittings with the precision of a pianist and the caution of a man who had learned that mechanical devices in the Edgecombe household possessed their own opinions about how they ought to be operated. His pale eyes, sharp as winter sky, watched the pressure gauges with the attention of a man who had seen what happened when steam engines decided to express their dissatisfaction with their working conditions.

Sarah positioned herself at the far end of the table, her sketchbook open before her and her pen poised to capture whatever miracle or catastrophe was about to unfold. Her golden fur caught the firelight, and her dark eyes held the sort of anticipation that other young women reserved for balls or betrothals, though Sarah had long since decided that her mother's theological experiments were considerably more entertaining than anything society had to offer.

"Ready?" Lillian asked, though the question was clearly rhetorical. She had been ready for this moment since the day she had first heard a church organ and realized that if human hands could create such beauty, then human hands could certainly improve upon the Almighty's original design.

She began to pump the bellows with the steady rhythm of a woman who had spent years kneading bread and wringing necks and performing all the countless tasks that required both strength and patience. The leather chambers expanded and contracted with a sound like the breathing of some great beast, and the brass valves began to sing with the high, sweet note of air under pressure.

The skull's jaw opened with mechanical precision, and from its brass throat came a sound that was unlike anything that had ever been heard in the Highland hills. It was thin and high and pure, a note that seemed to exist in the space between music and mathematics, between the sacred and the profane. It was the sound of air given voice, of breath made manifest, of the very essence of song distilled to its most fundamental element.

The copper pots that hung from the ceiling began to vibrate in sympathy, their polished surfaces reflecting the candlelight in patterns that seemed to dance with the rhythm of the music. The fire in the hearth leaped higher, as if the flames themselves were trying to reach the note that hung in the air like a challenge to the very foundations of creation.

Sarah's pen flew across her sketchbook, capturing not just the physical reality of the scene but something of its spiritual significance as well. She drew her mother as a figure of mythic proportions, a woman who had decided that the universe needed improvement and was prepared to provide it personally. She drew Miles as a figure of quiet competence, a man who had seen enough of the world to know that miracles were usually the result of hard work and proper engineering. And she drew the skull as it truly was: not a symbol of death but a symbol of transformation, of the moment when the merely mortal reached toward the divine and found it within reach.

The note climbed higher, and the windows began to quiver like timid guests at a party where the conversation had turned to topics that made respectable people nervous. A fine rain of dust fell from the rafters, seasoning the stew that bubbled in its pot and adding its own commentary to the proceedings. The very stones of the manor seemed to hold their breath, waiting to see what would happen when human ambition met divine prerogative in the arena of acoustic engineering.

"Again," Lillian commanded, her voice carrying the authority of a woman who had found her calling and intended to pursue it with the single-minded determination of a Highland warrior. "Harder. Let it find its true pitch. God gave us larynges like fiddle cases and then forgot the bow."

Miles leaned into the bellows with the sort of enthusiasm that came from years of making the best of whatever situation he found himself in, whether that situation involved broken engines, broken roofs, or broken theological systems. His shoulders flexed beneath his patched vest, and the bellows responded with a surge of pressure that sent the note climbing toward heights that had previously been reserved for angels and opera singers.

The skull's voice climbed a notch, and the effect was immediate and dramatic. A soup tureen—family silver, inherited from an aunt who had believed in lace and silence and the sort of propriety that made strong men weep—began to shudder on its shelf. The vibrations traveled through the silver like a message written in a language that only metal could understand, and the tureen responded by developing a crack that ran down its spine like the line of a gentleman's composure under extreme duress.

The lid of the tureen leaped an inch into the air, hung there for a moment as if considering its options, and then fell back with a decisive pang that seemed to express the opinion of all proper crockery everywhere about the sort of music that made respectable dinnerware nervous.

Lillian clapped her hands together with a sound like thunder, a percussion that made even the hurricane outside hesitate in its assault on the manor's ancient stones. Her face shone with the sort of joy that other women reserved for the birth of grandchildren or the discovery of particularly scandalous gossip, but which Lillian reserved for moments when she had successfully challenged the fundamental assumptions of creation itself.

"Aha! Ye see?" she cried, her voice carrying the triumph of a woman who had just proven a point that had been three centuries in the making. "With naught but bone and brass we have unmannered God's crockery. Flesh is only clay, and I'll throw it on my own wheel. Miles, fetch the sheep's skull. The horns'll give us a trumpet's curl."

The sheep's skull was a thing of beauty in its own right, bleached white by Highland weather and Highland time until it resembled carved ivory. The horns curved back from the forehead in elegant spirals that suggested both strength and grace, and when Miles fitted it with a brass mouthpiece and connected it to its own set of bellows, it produced a sound that was deeper and richer than the human skull, a note that seemed to carry the memory of Highland meadows and Highland storms.

But one voice, however beautiful, was not a choir, and Lillian had not spent three centuries of family theological rebellion to be satisfied with a solo performance. She had grander ambitions, plans that would require more skulls, more brass, more bellows, and considerably more audacity than even the Edgecombe family was accustomed to displaying.

"We'll need more," she announced, surveying her prototype with the critical eye of a general planning a campaign. "Many more. A choir worthy of the name, worthy of the challenge we're laying before the Almighty Himself."

And so they planned their midnight expedition to the local churchyard, an adventure that would have scandalized the local rector and delighted the local gossips, had either group been privy to the details. But the Edgecombe family had always conducted their theological experiments with a certain discretion, not out of shame but out of a practical understanding that some conversations were best conducted in private, particularly when those conversations involved the improvement of divine creation through the application of human engineering.

They raided by night, because habit suggested that graveyards preferred the dark, and because the darkness provided a certain theatrical atmosphere that seemed appropriate to the magnitude of their undertaking. Lillian strode among the weathered stones with a lantern that cast dancing shadows across the carved names and dates, a shovel that gleamed like a sword in the moonlight, and an appetite for bone that would have made archaeologists weep with envy.

The churchyard of St. Columba's was a place of ancient dignity and recent neglect, where Highland warriors slept beside Highland merchants, where Presbyterian ministers lay in eternal proximity to Catholic rebels, and where the accumulated theological disagreements of three centuries had been resolved by the simple expedient of six feet of Highland soil. The stones themselves told stories of lives lived and deaths mourned, of hopes fulfilled and dreams deferred, of the endless human comedy that played out against the backdrop of Highland weather and Highland stubbornness.

Lillian moved among the graves with the confidence of a woman who had long since decided that death was merely another form of transformation, and that the dead, properly approached, might be persuaded to participate in projects that the living lacked the imagination to attempt. She read the names carved in stone with the sort of attention that other women gave to guest lists, evaluating each potential contributor to her choir with the critical eye of a conductor auditioning soloists.

"Here lies Horace Whitely, clerk," she announced, her voice carrying across the churchyard with the authority of a woman conducting a theological census. "Aye, and here lies the problem wi' creation—too many Horaces, too many Whiteleys, too many clerks. The Almighty's been recruitin' from the same pool for centuries, and it shows in the quality of the hymns."

She paused before a stone that bore the name of one Reginald Pemberton, Esquire, and snorted with derision. "Esquire, is it? Well, Reginald, ye'll be singin' soprano now, and we'll see how yer esquiring serves ye when ye're hittin' the high notes."

Miles kept a weather eye for the beadle and for unhelpfully living dogs, his pale eyes scanning the darkness with the sort of alertness that came from years of standing watch in places where vigilance was the difference between success and disaster. He had learned, through hard experience, that the best-laid plans could be undone by the appearance of an unexpected authority figure or an overly enthusiastic canine, and he had no intention of allowing Lillian's theological project to be derailed by such mundane considerations.

"Back home," he drawled, prying up a neat rectangle of turf with the sort of precision that spoke of considerable practice in the art of excavation, "we considered it poor hospitality to visit folks after internment. But then, y'all do hospitality different here in Scotland."

His voice carried the sort of dry humor that had sustained him through years of war and weather and the countless small disasters that seemed to follow in the wake of ambitious women with strong opinions about the nature of divine creation. He had learned to find amusement in situations that would have driven lesser men to drink or despair, and he approached the task of grave robbing with the same matter-of-fact competence that he brought to repairing roofs or mending engines.

Sarah knelt beside a fresh mound that bore a simple wooden cross and a handful of wilted flowers, her golden fur catching the moonlight as she traced the carved letters with a delicate forefinger. Her touch was gentle, reverent, as if she were greeting an old friend rather than disturbing the rest of a stranger.

"Mrs. Agnes Muir," she read aloud, her voice carrying the sort of quiet respect that the dead deserved, regardless of the purposes to which their mortal remains might be put. "I think she had a fine brow. She'd have loved a ribbon."

There was something in Sarah's voice that suggested she could see beyond the simple facts of death and burial to the person who had once inhabited the bones they sought. She had always possessed an artist's eye for the essential character that lay beneath the surface of things, and even in death, she could discern the qualities that had made each person unique.

"Put a ribbon on her," Lillian said, her voice carrying the sort of practical kindness that had always characterized her approach to both the living and the dead. "We'll put ribbons on all of 'em. We'll dress the dead to sing better than the living ever dared."

And so they worked through the night, gathering the raw materials for Lillian's choir with the sort of systematic thoroughness that the project demanded. They selected skulls with an eye for both acoustic properties and aesthetic appeal, choosing specimens that showed the sort of bone structure that would resonate properly when fitted with brass and bellows. They worked with the reverence of archaeologists and the efficiency of engineers, treating each skull not as a relic of mortality but as a component in a grand design that would challenge the very foundations of creation.

By dawn, they had assembled a collection that would have made anatomists weep with envy and theologians reach for stronger drink. Human skulls and animal skulls, each chosen for its unique acoustic properties and its potential contribution to the grand harmony that Lillian envisioned. Sheep skulls with their elegant horns, cow skulls with their broad resonating chambers, even the delicate skull of a cat that Sarah insisted would provide the perfect soprano voice for the more delicate passages.

They returned to the manor as the first light of dawn painted the Highland peaks in shades of rose and gold, their cart loaded with the components of a choir that would sing songs that had never been heard on earth or in heaven. They worked with the quiet satisfaction of craftsmen who had found the perfect materials for their art, and with the anticipation of revolutionaries who were about to challenge the established order of creation itself.

And so they built. In the workroom that had once served as a library, surrounded by books that had recorded three centuries of Edgecombe theological rebellion, they assembled their choir with the sort of attention to detail that the project demanded. Bone grew ribs of brass, carefully fitted and precisely calibrated to produce the exact resonance that each voice required. A rack of bellows became lungs in a choir stall, each one connected to its skull by a network of tubes and valves that would have impressed the finest engineers in Edinburgh.

Leather valves pulsed with mechanical precision, their rhythm as steady as a heartbeat and as reliable as Highland weather. Catgut lines ran like nerves through the assembly, carrying the breath that would give voice to the voiceless and song to the silent. Tin resonators shaped vowels that had never known a human palate, creating sounds that existed in the space between the earthly and the divine.

When they could not find an aperture suitable for their purposes, Lillian drilled one with the sort of precision that came from years of practical experience with both theology and carpentry, and named each new opening after a saint who had shown similar initiative in the face of divine indifference. She crowned skulls with filigree that caught the candlelight like captured starlight, and hung little bells inside like tongues that would ring with every note.

Sarah drew portraits of each skull as if it were a debutante preparing for presentation at court, capturing not just the physical reality of bone and brass but something of the personality that each voice would possess. She tied black ribbons on horns with the sort of care that other young women reserved for their own hair, and wove crushed violets through jaw hinges with an artist's eye for both beauty and symbolism.

"They are so lovely in their vacancy," she whispered, her voice carrying the sort of wonder that came from witnessing the transformation of the mundane into the miraculous. "So keen to be filled."

Miles, ever practical, focused on the mechanical aspects of the project with the sort of attention to detail that had kept engines running and roofs intact through years of Highland weather and Highland stubbornness. He sanded pieces of walnut until they gleamed like silk, fashioning throat-boxes that would channel the breath of the bellows into the voices of the choir. He screwed skulls onto their wooden foundations with the tenderness of a midwife delivering new life into the world.

"They're keen to be dust," he observed, his voice carrying the sort of philosophical acceptance that came from having seen enough of life to understand its essential impermanence. "Which is honest. But if we're gonna seduce dust, we might as well do it with polish."

The first full-bodied contralto woke at midnight on the third day of their labors, and its voice was a thing of beauty and terror that made the very stones of the manor tremble with anticipation. Lillian set her hand upon the brass ribs, felt the bellows drink deep of the Highland air, and then the thing cried out—one long, clean note that seemed to contain all the longing and all the defiance that had ever existed in the human heart.

The note rang through the manor like a bell, like a trumpet, like the voice of judgment itself. It made the pantry clap its hands in appreciation, sent a row of plates skittering merrily off their shelf to commit suicide upon the flagstones in a percussion of broken crockery and shattered propriety. The stew pot burst into bubbles though the fire had died hours ago, and the goose fat on the counter quivered like a sinner at the back pew during a particularly pointed sermon about the wages of sin.

"Glory be," Miles said, squinting at the ruin with the sort of practical affection that came from having witnessed many such disasters in the service of ambitious women with strong opinions about the nature of creation. "Reminds me o' the hog-calling back in Missouri, 'cept less ambitious with mud and more successful with crockery."

"Glory belongs to those who take it," Lillian replied, her voice carrying the sort of conviction that had sustained three centuries of Edgecombe theological rebellion. "Again! Louder! I want the Almighty to drop His spoon."

The contralto obliged, and on that note the storm outside slackened, as if the sky itself had taken pause to listen to this new voice that challenged the very foundations of creation. The wind died to a whisper, the rain gentled to a caress, and for a moment the Highland night was filled with nothing but the sound of a dead woman singing songs that had never been heard in heaven or on earth.

It was, Lillian reflected with considerable satisfaction, a very good beginning.

## Chapter III — Domestic Atrocities

By the following week, the manor had been transformed from a respectable Highland residence into something that resembled a cross between a conservatory, a workshop, and a theological battleground. The Choir had grown from a single experimental voice to a full ensemble of nine skulls, each one carefully selected for its acoustic properties and lovingly fitted with the brass and leather apparatus that would give it voice. They occupied the workroom like a congregation of the dead waiting for resurrection, their empty sockets turned toward the windows as if they could still see the Highland sky that had once looked down upon their living eyes.

There were six voices in the workroom and three in the breakfast nook, because Lillian had discovered that the acoustics of the breakfast nook were particularly suited to the higher registers, and she was not a woman to let architectural considerations interfere with artistic necessity. The breakfast nook, which had once been a place of quiet morning contemplation and civilized consumption of porridge and kippers, now resonated with the ethereal voices of the soprano section, their delicate tones floating through the morning air like the songs of angels who had decided to take up residence in a Highland manor.

They practiced scales while Sarah buttered bread, their voices rising and falling in perfect mathematical progression while she went about the mundane business of preparing breakfast. The contrast between the celestial and the domestic created a sort of cognitive dissonance that made even the most routine activities seem charged with significance. The simple act of spreading marmalade on toast became a ritual performed to the accompaniment of otherworldly music, and Sarah found herself moving with a sort of ceremonial precision that seemed appropriate to the magnitude of what was happening around her.

They tried harmonies while Miles patched the roof, their voices weaving together in complex patterns that seemed to follow laws of physics that had not yet been discovered by earthly science. Miles, perched on the ancient slate tiles with his tools and his patches and his stubborn determination to keep the Highland weather on the outside where it belonged, found himself working to the rhythm of their music, his hammer falling in time with their cadences, his movements becoming part of the grand composition that seemed to be writing itself around him.

They hummed while Lillian devoured pies as if sugar were a sacrament and pastry were a form of prayer. She ate with the sort of focused intensity that other women reserved for religious devotion, each bite a communion with the forces of creation and destruction that she was attempting to harness. The pies themselves seemed to respond to the music, their crusts growing more golden, their fillings more fragrant, as if the very act of being consumed in the presence of such transcendent sound elevated them from mere food to something approaching the divine.

The air of the house grew thick with sympathetic vibrations, a phenomenon that would have fascinated natural philosophers and terrified theologians in equal measure. The very molecules seemed to dance to the rhythm of the Choir's voices, creating a sort of atmospheric harmony that affected everything within the manor's ancient walls. It was as if the house itself had become a vast musical instrument, with every beam and stone and pane of glass contributing to the grand symphony that Lillian was conducting.

Custards curdled at a glance, their delicate proteins rearranging themselves in response to frequencies that had never before been heard in any earthly kitchen. The cream soured from fear, or perhaps from excitement—it was difficult to tell which, as the Choir's voices seemed to inspire both terror and ecstasy in equal measure. The mustard jar, upon hearing a particularly risqué glissando that would have made church organists blush and opera singers weep with envy, blew off its own cap with a sound like a small cannon, sending mustard seeds scattering across the kitchen floor like tiny yellow prayers.

Eggs went to weeping in their carton, their shells developing hairline cracks that leaked not yolk but something that looked suspiciously like tears. Whether they wept from sorrow or from joy was a matter of theological debate that Lillian was prepared to argue with anyone brave enough to challenge her interpretation of ovine emotional responses to transcendent music.

On Tuesday, in what would later be remembered as the Great Mustache Incident of 1847, Miles' mustache abandoned his lip and slithered, in a fit of moral outrage that would have impressed the most rigid Presbyterian minister, into a tumbler of porter, where it drowned with the sort of dignity that only facial hair can achieve when faced with circumstances that exceed its capacity for theological tolerance.

"Requiescat," Sarah intoned with the solemnity of a priest performing last rites, fishing the sodden mustache out of the porter and pinning it, wet as a drowned mouse and twice as pathetic, to the edge of her sketchbook. "We shall mount it on a tenor. It will serve as a cautionary tale about the dangers of excessive propriety in the face of divine inspiration."

"I'll grow another," Miles said with the sort of philosophical acceptance that came from years of dealing with the Edgecombe family's various theological experiments. "Hair knows a bad situation and chooses resurrection. It's got more sense than most folks when it comes to knowin' when to cut and run."

He stirred the porridge with the sort of careful attention that the task now required, as the porridge had set to a stubborn wobble that suggested it had developed ecclesiastical opinions about the proper way to conduct breakfast in a household that harbored a choir of the dead. The porridge quivered with each note, its surface rippling in patterns that resembled either musical notation or ancient runic script, depending on one's perspective and one's willingness to believe that breakfast foods could develop literacy.

"We need't talk about storage," Miles continued, his voice carrying the sort of practical concern that came from being the only member of the household who seemed to understand that even theological revolutions required attention to mundane details like food preservation and structural integrity. "The pantry's full of voices and the voices are eatin' the bread by singin' it to crumbs."

This was not metaphorical. The Choir's voices had developed what could only be described as an appetite, though whether they consumed food in any conventional sense was a matter that would have puzzled natural philosophers and delighted poets. The bread in the pantry had indeed been reduced to crumbs, not through any physical process but through some sort of acoustic digestion that left behind only the memory of wheat and the faint taste of yeast and theological rebellion.

"The pantry can learn to share," Lillian declared with the sort of imperial authority that brooked no argument from either the living or the dead. "Greatness requires sacrifice. Entire empires were fed to operas. Ask the Italians." She gestured toward the basses with a fork that dripped with plum jam, her voice carrying the conviction of a woman who had studied history and found it insufficiently ambitious. "Lower, my angels. I want the earth to feel misgivings."

The basses obliged with the sort of enthusiasm that the dead seemed to reserve for requests that involved making the living uncomfortable. Their voices dropped to frequencies that seemed to bypass the ears entirely and speak directly to the bones, to the very foundations of the manor, to the bedrock beneath the Highland soil. The effect was immediate and dramatic.

A bowl of pears, which had been sitting peacefully on the kitchen table and minding its own business in the way that fruit generally did, folded into itself with a sigh that sounded suspiciously like resignation and became a strange compote without anyone's consent or assistance. The transformation was complete and inexplicable, as if the pears had decided that their destiny lay not in being eaten in their natural state but in becoming something more complex, more musical, more suited to a household where the impossible had become routine.

The sugar tin rattled a little jig, its contents rearranging themselves in patterns that suggested either crystalline enthusiasm or simple molecular excitement at being part of something larger than the sum of its parts. The sound it made was like tiny bells, like distant laughter, like the whispered conversations of sugar crystals discussing the theological implications of their situation.

A mirror cracked, hesitated as if reconsidering its decision, and then, with the courage of glass that had decided to make a statement about the nature of reality, cracked again. The second crack intersected with the first at an angle that created a new reflection, reshaping Sarah's image into that of a girl with a crown she had not yet earned but which seemed to suit her nonetheless. The crown appeared to be made of light and shadow, of music and mathematics, of all the things that existed in the space between the earthly and the divine.

"They make everything ugly beautiful," Sarah breathed, her voice carrying the sort of wonder that came from witnessing the transformation of the mundane into the miraculous on a daily basis. "Or everything beautiful ugly. I cannot tell which I prefer."

She moved through the chaos with the sort of grace that came from having grown up in a household where the impossible was merely Tuesday's entertainment. Her sketchbook was always in her hand, her pen always ready to capture the latest miracle or catastrophe, her eyes always alert for the moment when the ordinary world revealed its extraordinary nature.

"They make expenses," Miles observed with the sort of practical wisdom that came from being responsible for maintaining a household where the laws of physics were treated as suggestions rather than requirements. He was tightening the bindings on a baritone whose jaw had developed a tendency to wander, a problem that was both mechanical and theological in nature. "Also, the dead want puddin'. They keep askin' me in the night for treacle. I ain't sayin' it's real, but I got spoon marks on my forearm from refusin'."

This was perhaps the most disturbing development yet. The Choir had begun to express preferences, to make requests, to demonstrate the sort of individual personality that suggested they were more than mere mechanical devices animated by bellows and brass. They had developed tastes, opinions, desires that extended beyond the simple act of making music. They wanted pudding, specifically treacle pudding, and they were apparently willing to use supernatural means to make their desires known.

Lillian seized a pudding basin with the sort of decisive action that had characterized three centuries of Edgecombe responses to supernatural requests. "Then we feed them," she declared, her voice carrying the authority of a woman who had never met a theological challenge she wasn't prepared to meet head-on. "If angels can eat manna, my Choir shall eat plum. Sing for your supper, dearies."

They did. The harmonics that emerged from their brass throats fluttered the curtains like invisible hands, made the very air visible in patterns of sound and light that danced through the room like living things. The curtains responded by developing a sort of rhythmic movement that suggested they were keeping time with music that existed on frequencies beyond human hearing. Sarah's pulse quickened in response, her heart finding the rhythm of the Choir's breathing, her very blood seeming to flow in time with their otherworldly music.

The chimney moaned with a sound that was part wind, part music, part theological complaint. It was the sort of sound that chimneys made when they were being asked to channel more than smoke, when they were being used as conduits for forces that belonged more properly in cathedrals than in Highland manors. The house itself expressed opinions in a language of creaks and shivers, its ancient timbers commenting on the proceedings with the sort of architectural criticism that only buildings of a certain age were qualified to provide.

Neighbors, who in calmer times might have objected to midnight music or theological experimentation or the general disruption of Highland propriety, said nothing. They could not be sure which syllables would shatter their crockery or spoil their bacon, and they had learned through bitter experience that the Edgecombe family's theological projects had a tendency to affect the entire neighborhood in ways that were both unpredictable and irreversible.

Mrs. MacReady, who lived in the cottage nearest the manor, had taken to sleeping with cotton wool in her ears and her best china wrapped in blankets. Mr. Sinclair, the blacksmith, had noticed that his horseshoes were ringing in harmony with whatever was happening up at the manor, and he had begun to charge extra for shoes that came with their own musical accompaniment. The rector had taken to conducting services in a whisper, afraid that any sound above a murmur might trigger sympathetic vibrations from the direction of Edgecombe Manor.

On Thursday, in what would later be remembered as the Day the Pig Achieved Enlightenment, the kitchen door burst inward with the sort of dramatic timing that suggested either divine intervention or very good theatrical instincts. Through the doorway wandered a pig, a large and philosophical-looking sow who moved with the sort of purposeful dignity that suggested she was participating in a ritual rather than simply seeking food.

The pig made eye contact with a tenor—specifically, the tenor that had been fashioned from the skull of one Hamish MacLeod, who had died of consumption but had possessed, according to local legend, a voice that could make angels weep—and fainted. Not gradually, not with the sort of slow collapse that might be attributed to natural causes, but instantly, dramatically, with the sort of theatrical timing that would have impressed opera singers and made theologians reach for stronger drink.

Miles dragged the unconscious pig out by the trotters, his movements careful and respectful, as if he were handling a religious relic rather than a farm animal that had succumbed to aesthetic overload. "Back home," he remarked with the sort of philosophical acceptance that came from years of dealing with the unexpected consequences of theological experimentation, "we had hogs that'd swoon for an apple. This one's got taste. Or fear. Them two are cousins, and they both run in the same family."

Sarah stood in the doorway, framed by the Highland morning light, and announced with the sort of calm proclamatory joy that other young women reserved for engagements or inheritances, "I would marry them, Mama. All of them. Each skull has its own poetry. I would be a Mrs. Choir."

The statement hung in the air like a challenge to the very foundations of conventional morality and practical theology. The idea of a young woman marrying a choir of the dead was the sort of concept that would have made bishops faint and made poets reach for their pens. It was romantic and horrifying and beautiful and blasphemous all at once, which made it perfectly suited to the Edgecombe family's approach to life, death, and the spaces in between.

"Make yer vows to the loudest," Lillian advised with the sort of practical wisdom that came from years of experience with both marriage and music. "If ye must be drowned, choose the sea that sings." She threw her head back in a gesture that was both admirable and indecent, and praised her voices with laughter that should have been a crime against nature and probably was a crime against several theological principles that had been established by men who had never heard the dead sing love songs to the living.

The laughter rang through the manor like a bell, like a trumpet, like the sound of joy itself given voice and set free to dance among the rafters. It was the sort of laughter that made the angels pause in their eternal singing to listen and to wonder if perhaps they had been approaching the whole business of celestial music from entirely the wrong direction.

And in that moment, as Lillian's laughter mingled with the voices of her Choir and the Highland wind sang its own ancient songs through the eaves of Edgecombe Manor, it seemed as though the very foundations of creation trembled with something that might have been fear, or might have been anticipation, or might have been the sort of divine excitement that came from witnessing something genuinely new under the sun.

The Choir sang on, their voices weaving together in harmonies that had never been heard in heaven or on earth, and the manor settled around them like a great stone embrace, holding them and their music and their impossible dreams safe from a world that was not yet ready to understand what they were trying to accomplish.

But ready or not, the world was about to find out.

## Chapter IV — The Blasphemous Recital

Lillian was not content with a household rebellion. It is the curse of remarkable women that the village must be involved, and it is the blessing of remarkable women that they possess the audacity to make involvement seem like privilege rather than imposition. She had spent three centuries of Edgecombe history learning that the difference between scandal and sacrament was largely a matter of presentation, and she was prepared to present her Choir to the world with all the ceremony that such a revelation deserved.

The preparations began at dawn on the Monday that would later be remembered as the Week of the Great Theological Disruption. Lillian stood in the center of the manor's great hall, a room that had not been used for entertaining since her great-grandmother had hosted a gathering of Highland witches in 1798, and surveyed the space with the calculating eye of a general planning a campaign that would either result in glorious victory or spectacular martyrdom.

"We shall need chairs," she announced to Miles, who was attempting to repair a window that had developed the habit of opening itself whenever the Choir practiced their higher registers. "Proper chairs. Not the kitchen stools. Not the garden furniture. Chairs that suggest we take our guests seriously, even if we intend to challenge their fundamental assumptions about the nature of reality."

Miles paused in his work, a putty knife in one hand and a philosophical expression on his face. "How many chairs we talkin' about, ma'am? Because the last time you invited the neighborhood to witness somethin' theological, half of 'em needed smellin' salts and the other half needed new undergarments."

"All of them," Lillian declared with the sort of imperial certainty that had characterized Edgecombe family decisions for generations. "Every soul within three miles. Every Presbyterian, every Methodist, every Catholic who thinks they're hiding their rosary beads behind their Protestant facade. Every skeptic, every believer, every person who has ever wondered what lies beyond the veil between this world and the next. They shall all bear witness to what we have accomplished."

Sarah looked up from her sketchbook, where she had been drawing detailed anatomical studies of the Choir's brass fittings with the sort of scientific precision that would have impressed medical illustrators and horrified theologians. "Shall I design invitations, Mama? Something elegant but ominous? Perhaps with a border of musical notation that spells out warnings in Latin?"

"Invitations suggest choice," Lillian replied, her voice carrying the authority of a woman who had never believed that theological revelations should be optional. "We shall send summons. Attendance is not a privilege to be granted but a duty to be fulfilled. Those who witness the birth of a new age in sacred music bear a responsibility to history that transcends their personal comfort or convenience."

She moved to the escritoire that had belonged to her grandmother, a piece of furniture that had witnessed the composition of letters that had started feuds, ended engagements, and occasionally resulted in duels fought at dawn in Highland meadows. The wood seemed to hum with anticipation as she drew forth paper that bore the Edgecombe crest—a badger rampant on a field of musical notation, a heraldic design that had puzzled genealogists for centuries.

The letters she composed were masterpieces of theological intimidation disguised as social courtesy. Each one was crafted with the precision of a legal document and the poetry of a psalm, written in her flowing hand that managed to make even the most mundane words seem charged with supernatural significance.

"A sacred recital," she wrote, the ink flowing like dark music across the cream-colored paper, "in the hall of Edgecombe Manor. The veil between Heaven and Earth shall be drawn aside, and voices that have been silent in death shall sing songs that were never heard in life. Attendance compulsory for the devout and the curious. Bring your hearts; leave your fainting couches. The Almighty Himself shall be in attendance, though whether as audience or participant remains to be determined."

Sarah, reading over her mother's shoulder with the sort of morbid fascination that had characterized her approach to life since early childhood, suggested with artistic enthusiasm, "Perhaps we should mention the refreshments? People are more likely to attend theological revelations if they know there will be pudding."

"Pudding is implied," Lillian replied, sealing the first letter with wax that seemed to smoke more than strictly necessary. "Those who require the promise of earthly sustenance to witness heavenly music are precisely the sort of people who most need to have their priorities rearranged by divine intervention."

Miles, who had been listening to this exchange while attempting to convince a door handle that it should remain attached to its door despite the Choir's tendency to make all metal objects vibrate sympathetically, added his own practical concerns. "Ma'am, with respect, the last time we had company, the teacups started singin' harmony and the sugar bowl developed opinions about the proper way to sweeten tea. If we're gonna have the whole neighborhood here, we might want to consider what's gonna happen to their personal effects when the Choir gets goin'."

"Their personal effects," Lillian declared with the sort of magnificent indifference to practical consequences that had made the Edgecombe family legendary throughout the Highlands, "will have the privilege of participating in a musical experience that transcends the mundane limitations of earthly craftsmanship. If their pocket watches begin keeping time to celestial rhythms, if their spectacles develop the ability to see into other dimensions, if their walking sticks start conducting invisible orchestras, they should consider themselves blessed rather than inconvenienced."

The letters were dispatched by Sarah's fair hand, each one tied with black ribbon that seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it, carried through the Highland countryside by a network of delivery that included the postman, the baker's boy, the rector's wife, and a series of local children who were paid in sweets and sworn to secrecy about the exact nature of their mission.

The responses began arriving within hours, carried by the same network that had distributed the summons, and they ranged from expressions of theological curiosity to declarations of moral outrage to simple statements of intent to attend armed with both prayer books and smelling salts.

Mrs. MacReady, the nearest neighbor, sent word that she would attend but requested permission to bring her own chair, as she had heard rumors about the Edgecombe furniture developing supernatural properties. Mr. Sinclair, the blacksmith, replied that he would be honored to witness whatever theological experiment the Edgecombe family had devised, and that he would bring his strongest horseshoes in case they were needed for protection against supernatural influences.

The rector, Dr. Hamish MacLeod—a man whose theological education had prepared him for many challenges but not for the possibility that one of his parishioners might achieve direct communication with the divine through the medium of reanimated human remains—sent a carefully worded response that managed to express both professional curiosity and personal terror in equal measure. He would attend, he wrote, in his capacity as a shepherd of souls, prepared to offer spiritual guidance should the proceedings venture into territory that exceeded the bounds of conventional Presbyterian doctrine.

The preparations intensified as the week progressed. The great hall was transformed from a dusty repository of family history into something that resembled a concert hall designed by someone who had never seen a concert hall but had heard detailed descriptions of cathedrals, opera houses, and possibly the throne room of Heaven itself.

Miles, working with the sort of determined craftsmanship that came from years of maintaining a household where the impossible was merely Tuesday's entertainment, arranged chairs in rows that followed the natural acoustics of the room. He had discovered, through careful experimentation involving the Choir's practice sessions and a series of increasingly sophisticated measurements, that sound behaved differently in the presence of the reanimated dead, following patterns that seemed to owe more to theology than to physics.

"The voices bounce off the walls different," he explained to Sarah as she helped him position the chairs with mathematical precision. "Regular sound, it follows the laws of nature. But these voices, they follow laws that ain't been written down yet. They curve around corners that ain't there, they echo off surfaces that don't exist, they harmonize with sounds that happened yesterday and sounds that ain't gonna happen till next week."

Sarah, who had been documenting these acoustic anomalies in her sketchbook with the sort of scientific enthusiasm that would have impressed natural philosophers and worried theologians, nodded with the sort of understanding that came from growing up in a household where the laws of physics were treated as suggestions rather than requirements. "Perhaps," she suggested with the sort of artistic insight that had characterized her approach to the impossible since childhood, "we should arrange the chairs to accommodate the sounds that aren't there yet. If the voices can echo off surfaces that don't exist, perhaps the audience should be positioned to hear harmonies that haven't been composed."

This led to a rearrangement of the seating that followed principles of acoustic engineering that would not be discovered by conventional science for another century. The chairs were positioned not just for optimal hearing of the sounds that would be produced, but for optimal reception of the sounds that would be implied, suggested, and harmonically generated by the interaction between the Choir's voices and the spiritual resonances of the audience.

The Choir themselves were prepared with the sort of ceremonial attention that other households reserved for wedding preparations or funeral arrangements. Each skull was polished until it gleamed like ivory, each brass fitting was buffed to a mirror shine, each leather bellows was oiled and tested until it breathed with the sort of mechanical precision that suggested divine inspiration rather than human craftsmanship.

Sarah, working with the sort of artistic dedication that had characterized her approach to the macabre since early childhood, designed costumes for the Choir that managed to be both respectful of their deceased status and appropriate for their role as performers in a theological revolution. She draped them with black silk that seemed to absorb light, adorned them with silver ornaments that caught and reflected the candlelight in patterns that resembled musical notation, and crowned each skull with a circlet of Highland heather that had been blessed by moonlight and morning dew.

"They must look the part," she explained to Miles as she adjusted the draping on a particularly distinguished baritone who had once been a Highland chieftain and still carried himself with the sort of dignity that death had not diminished. "They are not merely musical instruments. They are ambassadors from the realm of the dead, representatives of a world that exists beyond the veil of mortal understanding. Their appearance must reflect the magnitude of what they represent."

The day of the recital dawned with the sort of atmospheric drama that suggested either divine approval or supernatural warning, depending on one's theological perspective. The Highland sky was a tapestry of clouds that seemed to have been arranged by someone with a flair for theatrical lighting, with shafts of sunlight breaking through at angles that illuminated the manor like a stage set designed for the performance of miracles.

The guests began arriving at dusk, because dusk is when people most believe in their neighbors' capacity for the impossible, and because the Highland twilight provided the sort of atmospheric backdrop that made even the most skeptical observer willing to consider the possibility that the boundaries between the natural and supernatural might be more permeable than conventional wisdom suggested.

They came with umbrellas like black flowers and faces arranged for outrage, but also with the sort of barely concealed excitement that came from the knowledge that they were about to witness something that would either confirm their deepest fears about the Edgecombe family's theological experiments or provide them with stories that would entertain their grandchildren for generations to come.

Mrs. MacReady arrived first, carrying her own chair as promised and wearing her best black dress, the one she had worn to her husband's funeral and had been saving for occasions of comparable theological significance. She was accompanied by her sister, Mrs. Sinclair, who had traveled from the next village specifically to witness what she described as "the Edgecombe woman's latest assault on the natural order."

Mr. Sinclair, the blacksmith, arrived with his wife and three children, all of them wearing their Sunday best and carrying small bags that contained, according to local rumor, iron nails and salt, traditional protections against supernatural influences. He had brought his strongest horseshoes, as promised, and wore them openly on his belt like a gunslinger displaying his weapons.

The rector, Dr. MacLeod, arrived with his wife and his prayer book, both of which he clutched with the sort of desperate determination that suggested he was prepared to perform an exorcism if the proceedings ventured too far into territory that exceeded the bounds of Presbyterian doctrine. He had spent the week in prayer and theological research, preparing himself for the possibility that he might need to provide spiritual guidance to a congregation that had witnessed direct communication with the divine.

The village doctor, Dr. Fraser, arrived with his medical bag and a supply of smelling salts, having been warned by previous experience that Edgecombe family gatherings had a tendency to produce medical emergencies that ranged from simple fainting to more complex conditions that seemed to involve temporary displacement of the soul from the body.

They were joined by farmers and shopkeepers, widows and spinsters, children and grandparents, all of them drawn by the sort of morbid curiosity that made Highland communities legendary for their willingness to witness their neighbors' theological experiments, no matter how dangerous or blasphemous those experiments might prove to be.

Miles opened the door with the gravity of a sexton and the cheer of a man manning a firebreak, his newly grown mustache twitching with barely suppressed amusement at the sight of the assembled crowd. "Seats to the left," he announced with the sort of ceremonial authority that the occasion demanded. "Mind your glasses. Singin' may cause unexpected weepin', breakage, or opinions. Also, we got puddin' in the back, but I'd recommend waitin' till after the performance to partake, on account of the voices have been known to affect the digestion in ways that ain't been properly documented by medical science."

Sarah took tickets that no one had been asked to buy, a theatrical gesture that added to the sense of occasion while also providing her with an opportunity to study each guest with the sort of artistic attention that made them feel both welcomed and slightly unnerved. She tucked locks of hair behind her ear with a gesture that made old men think uncomfortable thoughts about their mortality and young men think uncomfortable thoughts about their theology.

"Do not stare," she advised a widow who stared at the sheep's horns that adorned one of the soprano skulls. "They are shy, and they have been known to respond to excessive attention by singing directly to the observer in frequencies that can cause temporary displacement of the soul from the body. We have smelling salts available, but prevention is preferable to treatment."

The great hall filled with the sort of expectant tension that preceded either religious revivals or public executions, the two events sharing a common atmosphere of anticipation mixed with the possibility of witnessing something that would fundamentally alter one's understanding of the nature of reality. The pews were dining chairs arranged with mathematical precision; the nave was a corridor that had been transformed into an aisle worthy of a cathedral; the altar was a long table heaped with puddings in shades of sinful brown, their surfaces gleaming with glazes that seemed to reflect more light than they received.

The Choir waited in the east recess, nine skulls high, bells trembling in their throats like the hearts of nervous angels, brass ribs glinting with the last of the day's light filtered through windows that had been cleaned until they were transparent as air. Candles had been arranged throughout the hall with the sort of ceremonial precision that suggested either religious devotion or theatrical expertise, their flames dancing in patterns that seemed to respond to currents of air that existed only in the presence of the supernatural. Lillian entered like a scandal made flesh, like a theological argument given human form and dressed in silk that had been cut by someone who understood that clothing could be both armor and advertisement. She wore black silk that took the candlelight hostage and held it prisoner, reflecting it back in patterns that seemed to spell out words in languages that had been forgotten before the first stone of the manor had been laid. Her hair was pinned high with a meat fork that gleamed like silver, like steel, like the sort of implement that might be used to carve reality itself into new and more interesting shapes.

Her mouth was butter and command, sweet and imperious, curved in a smile that suggested she knew secrets that would make angels weep and devils applaud. Her eyes held the sort of light that came from looking directly into the face of the impossible and finding it beautiful rather than terrifying.

"Neighbors," she said, the word rolling off her tongue like honey mixed with acid, sweet and corrosive in equal measure, "we gather to show the Almighty what He should have done, had He any vanity at all. Tonight, you shall hear voices that have been silent in death sing songs that were never heard in life. You shall witness the resurrection not of the flesh, but of the spirit, not of the body, but of the soul's capacity for music that transcends the boundaries between this world and the next."

She gestured toward the Choir with the sort of theatrical flourish that would have impressed opera singers and made theologians reach for stronger drink. "These are not mere instruments. They are ambassadors from the realm of the dead, representatives of a world that exists beyond the veil of mortal understanding. They have returned not to haunt us, but to teach us, not to frighten us, but to show us what lies beyond the narrow confines of earthly music."

The audience sat in the sort of stunned silence that preceded either religious revelation or mass hysteria, their faces reflecting a mixture of fascination and terror that suggested they were beginning to understand that they were about to witness something that would fundamentally alter their understanding of the nature of reality.

And then, with the sort of perfect timing that suggested either divine inspiration or years of theatrical experience, Lillian raised her hand, and the Choir began to sing.

The first note emerged from the soprano section like a silver thread being drawn from the very fabric of creation, pure and clean and impossibly beautiful.
