## The Velvet Room

### A Lillianverse Dramaverse/Kinkyverse Story  
*(in the blended styles of R.J. Breakwater and E.R. Marsh)*

---

## Scene One – The Routine

By nine o’clock, the tower had swallowed the city light.

From the pavement, it was a sheet of glass and polished stone rising into the grey London sky. From the twenty-ninth floor, it was a different kind of weather system: air-conditioning set a degree too cold, filtered coffee and printer toner, the low murmur of voices on calls with other time zones.

Miles Ferguson’s world was the stretch of corridor from the lift lobby to Lillian Edgecombe’s office door.

He stood when the lift dinged, straightened his navy tie with one paw, and opened the frosted glass door a second before she reached it. Timing was part of the job. So was knowing which days she wanted words and which days she only wanted efficiency.

Today, she looked like she wanted nothing at all.

Lillian Edgecombe, CEO, badger, forty-two. Tailored black suit that fit her broad, solid frame like armour; white shirt, closed all the way up; hair slicked back. Her expression was as blank and polished as the glass walls.

“Good morning,” he said, voice even.

She passed him a leather folio without looking up. “Board pack printed? Annotated?”

“On your desk,” he said. “Colour-coded tabs. Finance in blue, legal in red, the bit about Kasper’s merger proposal in something approaching blood.”

One corner of her mouth moved—almost a smile, almost not.

“Calendar?” she asked.

He recited: “Nine-thirty prep with Dahlia. Eleven with Kasper’s people. One o’clock with HR. Three with comms. Six o’clock exit, if the gods of capital allow.”

“They never do,” she said, but her voice had warmed half a degree. She strode past him into her office, heels silent on the thick carpet.

His desk sat just outside, a small island of order: in-tray, out-tray, two monitors, a mug with *World’s Most Overqualified Secretary* printed on it in peeling letters. He’d bought it himself, on a day when the absurdity of his CV—degree, engineering licence, a whole other life—had made him want to laugh rather than scream.

He sat, fingers already moving over the keyboard, and resumed the work of making her day run like clockwork. Emails, calls, small fires.

The routine had its own rhythm. He liked that. Liked the click and hum of it, the way the office floor was a kind of drum kit—phones ringing on the beat, printers coughing off it, the hiss of the coffee machine like cymbals. Out beyond his desk, London moved; in here, everything moved because Lillian said so, and because he made sure she never had to say it twice.

At nine-twenty-seven, she called his name.

“Miles.”

“Yes, ma’am?”

He stepped into her office with the notepad that lived in his paw like a second skin. Her office was glass on three sides, city on two, and one wall of dark wood broken only by a single door. That door, to a small inner room with no windows, was matte black. People called it the Velvet Room, under their breath. They thought it was a joke about money—about deals done in softer shadows.

Lillian was at her desk, scrolling through a document, jaw tight.

“Dahlia sent through edits to the board report,” she said. “They are… enthusiastic.”

“That’s one word,” he said.

She held out the tablet. He stepped closer, took it from her, their fingers brushing for the briefest moment.

Static. Or something like it. Heat, a half-second late.

He heard it in the beat of the office outside. A stutter, a syncopation. His paw closed around glass and metal. Her paw withdrew more carefully than it needed to.

Just skin, he told himself. Just fur and the usual electrical storm that came with living in a body. Nothing.

“Schedule me ten minutes with her before the prep,” Lillian said. Her voice was as level as ever, but he saw the small give in her shoulders as she leaned back. “And get rid of slide fourteen. If the board sees that projection, I will spend the entire morning explaining basic arithmetic.”

“On it,” he said.

The moment was gone. The routine flowed on.

But as he left her office, closing the glass door behind him, he caught his reflection layered over hers: tall shepherd, compact badger, the thin barrier of air and propriety between them. For the first time in a long time, he was sharply, inconveniently aware of it.

## Scene Two – The Crack

The building at night was a different creature.

Most of the floor lights were off, leaving pools of brightness in the open-plan areas and corridors, like islands in a dark sea. The city outside the glass had inverted; now London’s lights were brighter than the offices’, flickering in the river and the windows of flats.

Miles checked the time—twenty-three-seventeen—and the email queue—terrifying—and told himself he would leave after he cleared the last three urgent messages.

Lillian was still in her office.

He could see her silhouette through the frosted glass: broad shoulders curved over the desk, light from the monitor washing her fur in a pale glow. Earlier, he had watched her dismiss the rest of the floor. “Go home,” she’d said, not unkindly, but with an edge that discouraged argument. Dahlia had hesitated, then left. Only Miles had hovered.

“I’ll stay,” he’d said. “In case you need anything.”

She’d looked at him for a long second, something unreadable in her gaze. “Suit yourself,” she’d said, and closed the door.

Now, an hour later, he heard a muted thump.

Then another.

He was halfway to his feet before his brain caught up. The rational options arrived in order: she’d dropped a file; she was rearranging something; it wasn’t his business.

He knocked anyway.

“Come in,” came the muffled reply.

He opened the door to find chaos, by Lillian’s standards: a spilled stack of papers on the floor, a coffee cup rolled to the edge of the rug, and Lillian herself crouched awkwardly, one knee cracking audibly as she reached for a runaway report.

“Don’t—” she began, but he was already there, picking up the cup before it completed its slow-motion suicide off the table. The contents were mostly gone, a brown stain spreading on the carpet like a tactical error.

She hissed softly through her teeth.

“I’ll call facilities,” he said. “They can get someone—”

“No,” she said, too quickly. “No. It’s late. They’ll log it, it’ll be a whole thing with health and safety and budgets and—” She stopped, slightly breathless. It occurred to him that he had never heard her ramble before.

He crouched beside her, their shoulders almost touching.

Up close, she looked less like an icon carved in granite and more like a mammal who had stayed at the office far past the point of sanity. There were faint smudges beneath her eyes where her make-up had worn away. A strand of hair had escaped its slicked-back prison and curled damply at her temple. Her shirt collar was unbuttoned by one notch; he could see the strong line of her throat, the catch of her swallow.

There was a small scar at the base of her left paw, pale against the dark fur. The kind of mark you’d get from a childhood accident, or hot oil, or smashing a mug out of sheer rage.

He reached for a sheaf of papers. Their paws brushed again. This time neither of them moved away immediately.

He could feel her body heat through the fine wool of her suit. His own pulse, too fast for the amount of exertion involved in picking up loose paper.

“You should have gone home,” she said, voice lowered.

“Occupational hazard,” he said. “My job description does include ‘dramatic late-night coffee rescue’ in the fine print.”

She huffed out a small, involuntary laugh. It loosened something in the room.

They gathered the papers in silence. When they stood, she swayed a fraction. He steadied her with a paw at her elbow, and suddenly she was very close.

Her perfume was subtle, expensive, and almost gone, leaving something more personal beneath it: clean fur, coffee, the faint metallic tang of stress.

Her eyes met his.

For one suspended second, the office receded; the glass walls, the polished desk, the emails waiting in his inbox. There was just the two of them, the space between their bodies, the shared knowledge that this—this—was not in any corporate policy manual.

She stepped back first.

“Thank you,” she said. Her voice had its usual weight again, but the edge was softer, blurred. “You can go home, Miles. I’ll… manage.”

He wanted to say, You don’t have to manage alone. He wanted to say, Let me stay. He wanted to say nothing and sink to his knees in front of her desk and rest his head against her thigh like a supplicant, like a worshipper, like a man finally admitting what he wanted.

He nodded instead.

“Good night, ma’am,” he said.

As he left, he heard the soft thud of her sitting heavily in her chair. Later, in his small flat with its second-hand furniture and humming radiator, he lay awake with the city sounds filtering through the thin walls, replaying the moment when her weight had leaned almost imperceptibly into his touch.

Every routine has a crack line. He could feel this one, running through his carefully constructed professionalism like a fault beneath the glass tower.

## Scene Three – The Test

The next morning, the tower was back to its usual hum. People chatted about commutes and show recommendations at the coffee machine. An all-staff email announced a new wellness initiative with bright icons and vague promises.

Miles pretended his body wasn’t on a half-second delay with his thoughts. He’d slept badly; his dreams had been full of glass and paper and the sound of her voice saying his name in a tone he’d never heard before.

He arranged her day, as always. He briefed her before the prep with Dahlia, as always. He kept his eyes on the notepad, carefully away from the scar at her paw, the open notch of her collar.

He was halfway through listing the expected questions Kasper’s team would ask when she interrupted.

“Miles.”

“Yes, ma’am?”

She set down the tablet. Her gaze was very steady.

“Reschedule my three o’clock with comms,” she said. “Block that hour out as private.”

“Of course,” he said. His pen moved automatically. “And put a note in your calendar?”

“No note,” she said. “Just… private.”

He looked up, then, unable not to.

“And,” she added, almost as an afterthought, “clear the conference room. The small one. Close the blinds. I’ll need it after the HR meeting. You’ll be there.”

His heart misfired.

“Me?”

“Yes,” she said. “We need to go through the pending contracts. It’ll be easier without interruptions.”

It was entirely professional. Completely plausible. The sort of thing they had done a hundred times.

“Of course,” he said again, but his voice came out a touch lower than usual.

“Three o’clock sharp,” she said, and went back to her screen.

At two fifty-eight, he was in the small conference room on the interior side of the floor, the one without a view. The blinds were indeed closed. The only light was a soft, indirect glow from the ceiling and the blue-green of the monitor on the wall.

He had arranged the contracts in a neat stack. He’d brought two pens, two glasses of water, a jug. His paws felt damp against the glass.

The door opened at exactly three.

She stepped in, closed it carefully behind her, and turned the lock.

The click was a small sound. It landed in his chest like a dropped stone.

“Sit,” she said, and for a moment his body almost obeyed on the wrong axis. Kneel, something in him translated, delayed. He pulled the chair out instead and sat like a sensible person.

She remained standing at the head of the table, paws flat on the smooth surface.

“We have fifty-seven minutes,” she said. “HR requires I attend the last three, to reassure them that the company’s leadership takes the new relationship policy seriously.”

“Do we?” he asked before he could stop himself. “Take it seriously?”

Her eyes flickered. It might have been amusement, or warning, or both.

“We will,” she said. “Now. Contracts. Start with Kasper’s.”

He slid the first document across. As he briefed her on the key clauses, she paced slowly along the length of the table, a metronome in heels. The space felt too small for her; it always did, as though she’d outgrown the room.

She stopped behind his chair.

He could feel her there, without turning: heat, presence, gravity.

“Read clause seventeen,” she said. “Out loud.”

He did. His voice sounded steady, even. He wasn’t sure who he was fooling.

Halfway through, she laid a paw on his shoulder.

Just weight. Just touch. Nothing his HR training could complain about, not definitively.

His tongue stumbled over a word. He corrected himself. Her paw didn’t move.

“Again,” she said quietly. “Slower.”

He read the clause again, savoury and sour on his tongue. She listened. He could feel the rise and fall of her breathing, the subtle increase in the pressure of her paw when he hit certain words—penalty, breach, termination.

The air in the room thickened.

“Good,” she said when he finished. Her paw squeezed once, utterly deliberate. “You follow instructions well.”

Heat washed down his spine. Relief, humiliation, hunger.

He could have shrugged her off. Could have stepped away, made a joke, broken the spell. Instead, he stayed very still and let her hand rest there, let himself be exactly what she called him: obedient.

It was nothing. It was everything.

She moved to the other side of the table, resumed her seat as though nothing had shifted.

They went through the rest of the contracts like any normal CEO and secretary team: efficient, brisk, precise. When the hour was up, she gathered the papers, stacked them, and unlocked the door.

“Thank you, Miles,” she said. “That will be all for now.”

He walked back to his desk on legs that felt faintly unreal. The floor hummed around him. Emails pinged, phones rang, someone laughed too loudly in the kitchenette.

He sat, opened his inbox, and tried very hard not to think about the way her paw had held him in place.

## Scene Four – The Dinner

Kasper always made an entrance.

He arrived at the restaurant like a man stepping onto a stage he owned: coat thrown back, cufflinks catching the light, smile sharp enough to cut glass. Fox, mid-forties, expensively lean. He kissed Lillian’s cheek in a way that managed to be both practiced and intrusive.

“Still conquering empires, Edgecombe?” he said.

“Somebody has to,” she replied. The words were dry as gin.

Miles watched from half a step behind her shoulder, expression politely blank. He had a pad and pen in his paw, a jug of water within reach, and a growing urge to take the jug and pour it over Kasper’s slicked-back head.

The private room was all velvet and low lighting, a different kind of Velvet Room. The kind corporate clients booked for deals and discreet affairs. London lay beyond the tinted windows; inside, everything was candlelight and curated intimacy.

Dahlia was there, sharp in emerald silk, fingers already on her phone under the table. Two of Kasper’s lieutenants occupied the other seats, spreading charm and menace in equal measure.

“And this must be the famous Miles,” Kasper said, finally deigning to look at him. “The miracle worker. I hear you keep Lillian’s world turning.”

“I keep the calendar from imploding,” Miles said. “Beyond that, I make no promises.”

Kasper laughed, too loud. “Modest. I like him.”

Lillian’s paw brushed the back of Miles’ chair as she sat. A small, proprietary touch. Anyone else might have missed it. Kasper’s eyes flicked down, then up.

“Well,” Kasper said, settling in. “To new partnerships.”

They toasted. They talked numbers, synergies, market share. Miles poured wine, fetched files from his bag, leaned in to correct a figure when Kasper deliberately inflated it to test the room.

Through it all, Lillian was flawless: charming, incisive, every inch the CEO. The board would have approved. The City would have approved. Miles, who had known her for three years and watched her wear that armour every day, saw the strain at the seams.

He also saw the way Kasper’s gaze lingered on her throat, on the strong curve of her shoulder where the suit jacket framed her body.

At one point, Dahlia leaned in too close to Lillian under the pretence of pointing something out on a chart, her perfume a sharp citrus note in the air. Lillian didn’t move away. She held herself perfectly still, letting Dahlia’s proximity slide off her like rain off glass.

Miles felt something tighten under his ribs.

He knew the story, in outline. Everyone did. Lillian and Kasper, once. Before the company, before the tower. Before she built herself into steel and glass. It was all gossip and half-truths, but the look that passed between them when talk turned to risk told him there was more there than any rumour.

“You’ve gone soft,” Kasper said at one point, smiling as he swirled his wine. “All this talk of impact and culture and staff wellbeing. The Lillian I knew would have cut three departments to make that margin.”

“The Lillian you knew,” she said, “hadn’t watched half a generation burn out on the altar of shareholder value yet.”

“Ah,” he said. “We’re doing ethics now. How novel.”

Miles saw the small flare of anger in her eyes, the way her paw tightened around her knife. He poured more water into her glass. When her free paw brushed his as he set it down, the contact was brief but deliberate.

Mine, he heard in it. Not for you.

Later, when the deal points had been agreed in principle and the dessert plates cleared, Kasper rose.

“Walk me out?” he said to Lillian.

She hesitated. For a moment, Miles thought she would say yes. The old patterns were there, muscle memory.

Then she glanced at him.

“Miles will order us a car,” she said. “We have another meeting afterwards.”

“Do you?” Kasper asked, amused. His gaze slid between them, taking in the open collar of Miles’ shirt, the faint flush on Lillian’s cheeks. “How very conscientious.”

“Goodnight, Kasper,” she said. “We’ll be in touch.”

When he finally left, trailing charm and cologne into the corridor, the room felt a degree cooler.

Dahlia lingered, eyes sharp.

“Do you need me for the next thing?” she asked. “I can stay.”

Lillian shook her head. “Go home. Sleep. Dream up ways to make Kasper look reasonable to the board tomorrow.”

Dahlia smirked, gathered her things, and left.

The door clicked shut behind her.

Silence rushed in. The city’s pulse beat faintly through the glass.

Lillian exhaled, long and slow. The sound scraped at the edges.

“You didn’t have to do that,” Miles said quietly. “Use me as your shield.”

She turned to look at him. Her eyes were tired, the eyeliner smudged just enough to make her look momentarily younger, less invincible.

“But you were there,” she said. “And you are very good at your job.”

Something in him tightened at the flatness of her tone.

“What exactly is my job, Lillian?” he asked. He rarely used her first name. It tasted too intimate, like stolen dessert.

She blinked, slow. “Tonight?” she said. “To make sure I don’t do something I regret with a man who already took more than he had any right to.”

“You could have just said you didn’t want to walk him out,” Miles said. “You don’t need me as an excuse.”

“Don’t I?” she asked.

The question hung between them, heavier than any file.

He could hear the music from the restaurant outside the door. A jazz standard, saxophone working through an old melody. His paws felt too big for his body.

“I ordered you a car,” he said. “It’ll be outside in five minutes.”

“Come with me,” she said. “We need to talk.”

He hadn’t planned on that. He’d thought: send her home, go back to his flat, stare at the ceiling and dissect the evening beat by beat. Instead, he was in the back of a car with her, London’s lights streaking past, her knee an inch from his.

She lived high, of course. Penthouse, river view, art on the walls that looked like it cost more than his annual salary. He followed her into an apartment that was all clean lines and deep colours, a domestic echo of her office but softer at the edges.

“Drink?” she asked, toeing off her heels by the door. She frowned when her left paw flexed. “Apparently I am not twenty-five anymore.”

“You never were,” he said, then flushed. “I mean. You didn’t let yourself be.”

She poured whisky into two heavy glasses and handed him one. Their fingers brushed. Heat again. Electricity. A line that had already been crossed, and crossed, and still somehow held.

“Sit,” she said.

This time, he did.

## Scene Five – The First Kiss

Her sofa was low and firm, upholstered in dark green velvet. Of course it was velvet. He sat at one end, glass cradled between his paws. She sat at the other, shoes off, suit jacket undone.

Without the jacket, she looked… smaller, somehow. Or just more three-dimensional. Solid badger shoulders, yes, but also the curve of her waist, the way her shirt pulled slightly across her chest when she breathed.

She took a sip of whisky, then stared at the glass as if it might have answers engraved on the bottom.

“You have questions,” she said.

He swallowed. “Several.”

“Ask one,” she said.

He thought of all the things he could ask. Why him. Why now. What exactly had happened in that conference room, beyond an HR violation so subtle it might as well have been jazz.

“Are you all right?” he asked instead.

Her mouth twitched.

“That’s a very dull question, Miles.”

“It’s the one I’ve been sitting on all day,” he said. “Every time you walked into a room. Every time you smiled at Kasper like you didn’t want to claw his face off. So. Are you all right?”

She stared at him for a long moment. He let the silence stretch. It felt like waiting for the downbeat in a song he didn’t know yet.

“I am very good at my job,” she said at last. “I am less good at being a person in front of men who have known me longer than my current job title.”

“That’s not an answer,” he said gently.

Her eyes narrowed, but not in anger.

“You are irritating,” she said. “Has anyone told you that?”

“Regularly,” he said. “Usually right before they ask me for a miracle.”

She set her glass down with more force than necessary.

“You were not wrong,” she said. “About not needing you as an excuse. I could have told Kasper to go to hell on my own. I have, in the past. But tonight it was… easier to have you there. To hold onto the idea that someone in the room was on my side without wanting something from me I wasn’t prepared to give.”

“I want something from you,” he said, before his brain could apply the brakes.

Her gaze snapped to his.

“Do you,” she said. Flat. Dangerous.

He could have backed off. Laughed. Said he wanted a pay rise, or fewer twelve-hour days.

He put his glass down carefully.

“I want you to stop pretending that last night didn’t happen,” he said. “That today didn’t happen. That whatever this is between us is just you being a demanding boss and me being a good dog who fetches.”

The words hung there, naked and raw.

Her pupils widened. Badgers didn’t blush the way shepherds did, but he saw something flare under her fur, heat rising.

“You are my employee,” she said. “My secretary.”

“Yes,” he said. “And?”

“And there are policies,” she said. “Power dynamics. Gender politics. HR presentations with very expensive slide decks about why this exact thing is a bad idea.”

“This exact thing being…?” he asked.

She glared at him. “Don’t make me say it,” she said.

He laughed, short and sharp. “You can command a boardroom,” he said. “You can walk into a meeting with Kasper and make him sweat without raising your voice. But you can’t say you want to—”

He didn’t finish the sentence. He didn’t have to.

He moved before he thought about it too much, crossing the small distance between them on the sofa in a single, reckless shift. His paw came up to her jaw, thumb brushing the line where her mask of composure sat.

“May I?” he asked. Quietly. Carefully.

Her breath hitched.

“You are very presumptuous,” she said.

“That’s not a no,” he said.

A long pause. The sound of traffic outside; the faint hum of the building; his own heart beating loud enough to count time.

“Yes,” she said.

He kissed her.

It wasn’t the way he’d imagined it in the dark moments between sleep and waking, over the past months, when his mind had wandered where it shouldn’t. It was slower. He was careful, conscious of every point of contact: his paw at her jaw, his other hand hovering by her shoulder in case she pushed him away; the plush give of her mouth, the surprising softness against his.

She tasted of whisky and coffee and something that was just… her. Years of control, a whole life pressed into this one point of surrender.

For a second she stayed very still, as if cataloguing the sensation. Then she kissed him back.

Her paw came up to the back of his neck, claws scratching lightly through his fur. She pulled him closer with a strength that made something in his chest light up and sink at the same time. He let her, let himself fold into the space where her body met his, where the sharp lines of her suit gave way to warmth.

The kiss deepened. The air thinned. The room narrowed to the rhythm of their breathing.

When they broke apart, both of them were breathing harder than long days at the office strictly warranted.

Her eyes searched his face, sharp even through the blur of desire.

“That,” she said, voice rough, “was a very foolish thing to do.”

“I know,” he said.

“We cannot do this,” she said.

“I know,” he said again.

He didn’t move away.

Her paw tightened at his neck. Her gaze dropped briefly to his mouth, then back up.

“And yet,” she murmured.

He kissed her again.

The second time was less careful. More desperate. Years of restraining himself broke open in a rush of heat and wanting. His paws found her shoulders, the line of her back, the solid weight of her. She made a sound low in her throat that he would have replayed on loop if he could have.

They didn’t go further that night. There were lines neither of them was ready to cross. Eventually, she pushed him away gently, paws on his chest.

“Go home,” she said, voice unsteady. “Before we do something that cannot be undone.”

He stood, limbs shaking, and looked down at her: shirt slightly askew, lipstick smudged, eyes dark with something that wasn’t just stress.

“Lillian,” he said. “This is already something that can’t be undone.”

She shut her eyes briefly, like a woman bracing for impact.

“Go,” she said.

He went.

On the night bus home, pressed between other commuters and the damp ghosts of their days, he watched his reflection in the window: tall shepherd, tired eyes, mouth bruised by a woman who ruled a tower.

He felt like he had stepped off the familiar score of his life into improvisation. Dangerous, intoxicating, no sheet music.

## Scene Six – The Fallout

The next morning, the tower was colder.

Or maybe it was just him.

Lillian arrived at nine sharp. Her suit was perfect, her hair precise, her expression carved out of stone. She walked past his desk without a glance.

“Good morning, Ms Edgecombe,” he said.

“Miles,” she said, and if there was anything in that single word beyond acknowledgement, he couldn’t hear it.

The day unfolded in a series of small, calculated absences.

She sent instructions by email instead of calling him in. She kept her office door closed. She allowed Dahlia into meetings she would usually reserve for him. At one point, he watched through the glass as she stood at the window with Gregory from HR, arms folded, listening as he gestured in that way that meant “policy” and “risk” and “optics.”

Gregory came to see Miles afterwards.

“Got a minute?” he asked, hovering by the desk with a careful smile.

“Of course,” Miles said.

They took a meeting room. Gregory shut the door, didn’t lock it.

“Nothing to worry about,” Gregory said, which was, of course, how he always started conversations in which there was something to worry about. “We’re just having routine check-ins with people in senior staff support roles. Given the new guidelines.”

“You mean the no-more-secret-affairs-in-the-supply-cupboard guidelines,” Miles said. “Very catchy.”

Gregory’s smile tightened. “We both know those things happen,” he said. “The board is… keen to mitigate reputational risk.”

“Of course,” Miles said. His throat felt dry. “How can I help?”

“Just a few questions,” Gregory said, consulting his tablet. “You work closely with Ms Edgecombe.”

“Yes.”

“How would you characterise your relationship?”

He thought of last night’s velvet sofa. Of her mouth on his, her paw at his neck. Of the way she hadn’t looked at him this morning.

“Professional,” he said. The word scraped his throat on the way out.

Gregory studied him for a moment.

“You’ve been in the role three years,” he said. “Any concerns? Feelings of… blurred boundaries?”

“We all drink too much coffee and stay too late,” Miles said. “If that’s what you mean, then yes, the boundaries between working and living are absolutely obliterated.”

Gregory didn’t laugh.

“You know you can come to me,” he said, “if anything feels… uncomfortable.”

Miles smiled. “If Ms Edgecombe ever tried to coerce me into anything untoward,” he said, “I would have very strong opinions about it, and I would not hesitate to share them.”

That, at least, was true.

Gregory nodded, made a note, and left him with a pamphlet about workplace wellbeing that he threw into the recycling as soon as the door closed.

When he returned to his desk, there was a new email from Lillian. One line.

> Please send me the updated Kasper contract draft by five. – L

No greeting. No thanks. No may I see you in my office.

He worked. He was good at that. Numbers and clauses and calendars bent under his paws. The day passed in a blur. The crack in the routine widened into a gulf.

In the quiet between meetings, the city’s weather shifted. Clouds gathered. By late afternoon, rain streaked the glass in thin silver lines.

At sixteen fifty-eight, he sent the contract.

At seventeen-twelve, she called.

“Miles.”

Her voice on the internal line. It hit something behind his ribs.

“Yes, ma’am?”

“My office,” she said. “Now.”

He went.

She was at the window when he entered, paws braced on the sill, looking down at the city as if it were a problem set she could solve if she stared hard enough.

He closed the door. The sound was loud in the quiet room.

“You’ve been avoiding me,” he said. No point in pretending otherwise.

She turned. Her face was carefully composed, but he could see the hairline fractures: tightness at the corners of her mouth, the too-bright glint in her eyes.

“I’ve been doing my job,” she said.

“So have I,” he said. “That doesn’t answer the question.”

She inhaled, exhaled. Badger lungs, built for long work.

“Last night was a mistake,” she said. “An indulgence I cannot afford.”

Something in him recoiled. “An indulgence,” he repeated.

“You know what I mean,” she said. “It was… a lapse. You were kind. I was tired. Kasper has a way of scraping old wounds raw. I let the line blur. It won’t happen again.”

He laughed, then, because if he didn’t, he might shout.

“You kissed me back,” he said. “That wasn’t weakness. That was a choice.”

“A foolish choice,” she said.

“Maybe,” he said. “But it was real.”

She stepped closer, eyes flashing.

“Real doesn’t matter,” she said. “Real doesn’t keep HR off my back. Real doesn’t protect you if the board decides I’ve abused my position and you’re some poor underling I’ve preyed on. Real doesn’t—”

She broke off, jaw clenched.

“I built this,” she said, gesturing vaguely at the office, the tower, the city beyond. “Out of nothing. Out of weekends and blood and missed birthdays. Out of not letting men like Kasper define me. I will not see it all undone because I couldn’t keep my hands off my secretary.”

The word landed between them like a slap.

He flinched.

“Is that what you think this is?” he asked, quietly. “You taking advantage of me?”

She opened her mouth, then shut it. For once, Lillian Edgecombe, queen of the sharp retort, had no ready answer.

He took a breath. Let it out. Let the hurt edge his words without overwhelming them.

“I am not a child,” he said. “I am not an intern. I’m forty-two years old. I’ve had more jobs than some of your board members. I chose this role. I chose to stay. I chose to kiss you. I would choose it again.”

“Then you are more of a fool than I thought,” she said.

“Maybe,” he said. “But I’m not your victim.”

Silence. Rain tracked down the glass behind her.

“We can’t keep pretending we’re just boss and secretary,” he said. “Not after last night. Not after the conference room. Not after…” He gestured vaguely at the air between them, where all the unsaid things still glowed.

“Then we stop,” she said. “We put it back in the box. We lock the door. We behave like adults with a mutual interest in not destroying both our careers.”

“Can you?” he asked.

She didn’t answer.

He stepped closer, close enough to see the small scar on her paw again, the one he’d noticed on the floor with the spilled coffee.

“Lillian,” he said. “Do you want me?”

Her jaw tightened. Her eyes closed for a heartbeat. When she opened them, they were bright with something that looked very much like fear.

“Yes,” she said. “And that is exactly the problem.”

## Scene Seven – The Submission

The storm broke fully after dark.

Lightning stitched the sky over the Thames, white veins flashing against the heavy clouds. Rain hammered the glass, loud enough that most of the staff on the upper floors decided to call it a night rather than risk flooded train lines.

Miles stayed.

He told himself it was to clear his inbox. To get ahead on the Kasper integration plan. To avoid being alone with his thoughts in his flat while the sky tried to tear itself apart.

He made it until nine-thirty before he admitted the other reason.

Lillian’s office light was still on.

He could see her silhouette again: shoulders slumped, paws on the desk, head bowed. For once, she hadn’t closed the blinds. The storm lit her from behind in jagged bursts.

He knocked.

“Come,” she said.

He opened the door.

Her jacket was off, draped over the back of the chair. Her shirt sleeves were rolled up, exposing forearms crossed with faint lines from years of carrying files, lifting boxes, living in a solid body in a world that preferred its CEOs sleek and interchangeable.

She looked up as he stepped in. Her eyes were tired, but clearer than they had been that afternoon.

“You’re still here,” she said.

“So are you,” he said.

A low rumble of thunder rolled across the city.

“I thought about going home,” she said. “But home is just another glass box at the top of a building someone else owns. At least here, I signed the lease.”

He smiled, small. “You could go somewhere that’s not made of glass,” he said. “We could get on a train. Go to the seaside. Buy terrible chips and watch the water until all this feels less… large.”

“In this weather?” she asked.

“In any weather,” he said.

She looked at him for a long, weighing moment.

“Close the door,” she said.

The words landed differently this time. He obeyed, paw on the handle heavier than it should have been. The soft click of the latch sounded final.

“Lock it,” she added.

He did.

When he turned back, she was standing in front of the matte black door in the wooden wall. The Velvet Room. The inner office he’d almost never seen inside.

“Come here,” she said.

His pulse picked up. He crossed the room, each step an act of intention.

Up close, the door looked the way its nickname promised: the wood stained so dark it was almost black, the handle a brushed metal curve that fit the paw like something made to be gripped tightly.

She opened it.

Inside, the room was smaller than he’d imagined, and warmer. No windows. The walls were lined in deep blue velvet panels, absorbing sound and light. A low sofa ran along one wall, covered in the same fabric. A single lamp cast a golden pool over a small table. It felt less like a corporate office and more like the inside of a jewellery box. Or a confession booth.

He stepped in. The door closed behind them with a soft thud.

The quiet was almost total. The storm outside became a distant bass line.

“This is where I come when I need to think,” she said. “Or when the board is being particularly obtuse. Or when Kasper used to—” She cut herself off. “Used to.”

He glanced at her. “You brought him in here?”

“Once,” she said. “Twice. Enough.”

Jealousy flared, hot and pointless. He let it pass. The room, as it was now, was not about Kasper. It was about the fact that she had chosen to bring him here.

“Why am I here?” he asked, voice low.

She took a breath.

“Because I am tired of pretending I don’t know what I want,” she said. “And because if I’m going to do something this reckless, I’d rather do it somewhere I control.”

The words sent a shiver down his spine.

Control. The thing she wore like a second skin. The thing he’d secretly imagined giving her, in a more intimate register, long before he’d had the courage to name it even to himself.

“Miles,” she said. “What I am going to ask for, I can only ask once. If you say no, we walk out of here, we write new job descriptions, Gregory never has to raise an eyebrow over us, and you find another CEO who appreciates your particular talents and lets you go home at a reasonable hour.”

“And if I say yes?” he asked.

“Then I am no longer just your boss,” she said. “And you are no longer just my secretary. And we will both have to live with what that means.”

He swallowed.

“Then ask,” he said.

She stepped closer. Close enough that he could see the rise and fall of her breathing, the way her pupils dilated in the low light.

“Kneel,” she said.

The word was simple. Unadorned. It hit him like a chord he’d been carrying around, unresolved, for years.

His knees bent.

He lowered himself onto the soft carpet without hesitation. His body knew the movement, even if his brain scrambled to catch up. There was no pain; his joints were still good. He’d kept himself in decent shape, long commutes and longer days notwithstanding. He was suddenly, profoundly grateful for every stretch he’d ever forced himself to do in silent flats at midnight.

From this angle, she loomed. A silhouette against the velvet walls: broad, solid, powerful. Her eyes looked darker, framed by the fall of her hair, loosened at the temples.

“How does that feel?” she asked. Her voice was softer now, but no less commanding.

He exhaled slowly.

“Right,” he said. “Scary. Exposed. But… right.”

“You understand what you are giving me, if you stay there,” she said. “Not just your body. Your attention. Your… compliance.”

He nodded.

“Words, Miles,” she said. “I need to hear them.”

“Yes,” he said. “I understand.”

She studied him. The weight of her gaze felt like a paw on the back of his neck.

“Do you trust me?” she asked.

He thought of her at the window, holding the company’s future in her head. Of her in the restaurant, using him as a shield against an ex who would have swallowed her whole if she let him. Of her on the floor of her office, picking up spilled papers with cracked knees and stubborn dignity.

“Yes,” he said. The word cost nothing and everything. “I do.”

Her shoulders relaxed a fraction.

“Then this is how it will be,” she said. “In here, when the door is closed and we have both agreed, I am not just your CEO. I am something else. And you—” She reached down, tipped his chin up with one paw. “You are mine. Not as property. As choice.”

His throat felt tight. “Yes,” he whispered.

“If I ask you to stop, you stop,” she said. “If you ask me to stop, I stop. If I ever use what you give me here against you out there—” She jerked her head toward the outer office. “You walk. You tell Gregory everything. You burn me to the ground. Do you understand?”

He almost laughed at the fierceness of it.

“Yes,” he said. “Lillian.”

Her mouth curved. Not quite a smile. Something softer, more dangerous.

“When you kneel for me,” she said, “you can call me ‘ma’am.’”

Heat flooded him. His body reacted, predictable, embarrassingly enthusiastic. He shifted, grateful that the cut of his trousers gave him some cover even in this intimate position.

“Yes, ma’am,” he said.

The word fit his mouth like it had been waiting there. It landed in the room like a dropped match.

She exhaled slowly, as if she’d been holding her breath for years.

“Good,” she said. “Very good.”

She moved closer, until her thighs were inches from his face. She laid one paw on his head, fingers threading through the fur between his ears, and for the first time in a very long time, he felt… safe. Even on his knees. Especially on his knees.

They didn’t rush into sex. Not that night. It would have been easy to let the tension snap into something purely physical, to let hands and mouths do the clumsy work of trying to resolve everything at once. Instead, they talked.

He told her about the first time he’d realised he liked being told what to do by someone he respected. How humiliating it had felt, and how good. How long he’d hidden it, because men like him weren’t supposed to enjoy surrender.

She told him about Kasper, and the way he’d tried to own her under the guise of partnership. About the boss before that, who’d taken credit for her work and then assumed her loyalty came with a side of access to her body.

“If I’m going to take control,” she said, “it’s going to be on my terms. Not theirs.”

“And mine,” he said from the floor. “It has to be on mine too.”

She inclined her head. “And yours,” she said. “We write this script together.”

Lightning flashed again, painting the velvet walls in stark white for a heartbeat. When it faded, the room felt even more separate from the rest of the world.

Eventually, she told him to stand.

His legs tingled as the blood rushed back. She steadied him with both paws at his arms. They stood close, breath mingling.

“We stop here tonight,” she said. “We sleep alone. We go home. Tomorrow, we face the tower.”

He nodded. A small ache bloomed in his chest at the thought of going back out there, to the glass and the emails and Gregory’s carefully phrased questions. But under it was something steadier: a line of steel he hadn’t known he carried.

She had asked, and he had chosen. That mattered.

“Yes, ma’am,” he said.

Her paw slid up to his cheek. She kissed him once, slow and deep, then stepped back.

“Go home, Miles,” she said.

He did. This time, when he lay in his flat, listening to the storm and the distant sirens and the neighbour’s television, he felt less like a man falling and more like one who had finally stepped onto solid ground, even if it was on his knees.

## Scene Eight – The Choice

The storm washed the city clean overnight, or as clean as London ever got. By morning, the sky was a washed-out blue, the streets streaked with yesterday’s leaf mulch and last night’s regrets.

In the tower, the floors hummed with gossip about flooded train lines and delayed deliveries. Nobody talked about what had happened on the twenty-ninth floor, in the small room behind the matte black door. That was just as well.

Lillian called a nine a.m. leadership meeting.

Miles watched from his usual seat in the corner as she stood at the head of the table and outlined the next phase of the Kasper integration. Her voice was steady, her slides sharp. She was, to all external appearances, the same CEO she’d been the week before.

Only he knew that when she said “control,” a small muscle in her jaw jumped.

Afterwards, she asked him to stay.

“Close the door,” she said. Not the inner door. Just the meeting room door.

He did.

“I have three options,” she said without preamble. “One: we ignore last night. We pretend it never happened. We both die slowly of frustration and unspoken tension and occasional erotic dreams and carry on as if the only thing between us is a table full of board papers.”

“Tempting,” he said dryly.

“Two,” she continued, “we… pursue this in secret. We sneak around. We lie to Gregory. We hope nobody notices when I look at you for a beat too long in a quarterly review. Eventually someone does, it blows up, and we both end up giving statements to a lawyer while the tabloids try to decide if I am a predatory cougar or a tragic romantic heroine.”

He winced. “Less tempting.”

“Three,” she said, and here her voice shifted, softened, “we admit something is happening. We change the structure. You stop being my direct report. We put distance between our names on the org chart even if there isn’t any between our bodies. We talk to Gregory before he talks to us.”

His stomach flipped.

“You’d do that?” he asked. “Move me?”

“It would be a loss,” she said frankly. “You are very good at what you do. Replacing you would be… inconvenient.”

“Flattery,” he said, “will get you everywhere.”

“I am not finished,” she said.

He shut up.

“It would also be honest,” she said. “If I am going to ask you to kneel for me in private, I cannot pretend you are just my secretary in public. That’s the kind of hypocrisy I left behind with Kasper.”

He thought of his desk, of the rhythm of his days. Of the way the tower felt with him at its centre, in that strip of corridor outside her door. He thought of the Velvet Room, and the way his body had answered her command without hesitation.

“What would I do?” he asked.

“Operations,” she said. “Officially. You’re already half doing that work. You keep this place running. We may as well pay you accordingly and give you a title that makes Gregory feel better about our… extracurriculars.”

“And unofficially?” he asked.

Her eyes darkened.

“Unofficially,” she said, “you are mine when we are alone. When we are not, you are a respected member of the leadership team whose opinions I happen to value more than most.”

Heat flushed through him at the word “mine” spoken in that cool, matter-of-fact tone. It shouldn’t have worked. It did.

“You realise,” he said slowly, “that option three still involves talking to HR about a relationship between the CEO and a senior staff member.”

“Yes,” she said. “The difference is that in option three, we tell them ourselves. We define the terms. We don’t let them write the story for us.”

He thought of E.R. Marsh’s world, though he didn’t know her name: systems that chewed people up, institutions that pretended to care. He thought of the horror stories whispered at office Christmas parties about women who’d slept with their bosses and men who’d been quietly reassigned when they complained.

“You know they’ll assume you coerced me,” he said. “Because you’re the woman. Because you’re the boss.”

“They assumed that about Kasper and me,” she said. “They were wrong then. They’d be wrong now. The difference is, this time, I am not going to let their assumptions define what we are.”

“And what are we?” he asked, because he had to hear it.

She met his gaze head-on.

“Two adults,” she said. “With complicated desires and slightly better-than-average communication skills. Who are going to need to be very boring on paper so we can be as reckless as we like behind closed doors.”

He laughed, startled and genuine.

“That’s the most romantic thing you’ve ever said to me,” he said.

“Set the meeting with Gregory,” she said. “This afternoon. Tell him we have something to disclose.”

“Yes, ma’am,” he said automatically, then winced.

Her mouth quirked.

“Perhaps,” she said, “save that for the Velvet Room.”

“Understood,” he said. “Lillian.”

## Scene Nine – The Decision

Gregory was, to his credit, more professional than Miles had expected when your boss and your boss’s boss turned up in his office together and said, almost in unison, “We’d like to talk about the relationship policy.”

He listened. He took notes. He asked careful questions.

“So,” he said at one point, looking from Lillian to Miles and back again. “You are disclosing a personal relationship.”

“Yes,” Lillian said. Her voice did not waver.

“And you understand why the company has concerns about relationships where there is a direct reporting line.”

“Yes,” she said again.

“What steps are you proposing to mitigate that?”

Miles laid out the plan they’d sketched between scenes of institutional horror: his move to a Director of Operations role, reporting to the COO instead of to Lillian; the recruitment of a new executive assistant; a clear statement of boundaries to their teams.

Gregory nodded, made more notes.

“This is… unusually proactive,” he said. “I appreciate you coming to me rather than the other way around.”

“We both have too much to lose by handling this badly,” Lillian said. “And I have no interest in being the case study in next year’s training slides.”

Gregory almost smiled at that.

“There will be paperwork,” he said. “Disclosures. Conflict-of-interest forms. A need for transparency if, in future, performance or promotion decisions are involved.”

“We understand,” Lillian said.

He hesitated.

“Off the record,” he said, “I feel obliged to ask: Miles, do you feel any pressure here? Any sense that your position is contingent on… compliance?”

Miles thought of the Velvet Room. Of kneeling by choice. Of the way she had told him, fierce and clear, that if she ever used what he gave her there against him out here, he was to burn her down.

“I feel more respected,” he said slowly, “and more seen, in this relationship than I have in any I’ve had before. Professionally and otherwise.”

Gregory studied him for a moment, then nodded.

“Very well,” he said. “We’ll proceed with the structural changes. I’ll need you both to sign some forms. And, obviously, discretion would be appreciated.”

“Discretion is one of our core competencies,” Lillian said.

When they left Gregory’s office, closing the door on the smell of cheap carpet cleaner and laminated posters about resilience, something in Miles’ chest loosened.

The worst of the institutional horror had been faced. Not defeated—the systems would grind on, the policies would evolve, the risks would remain—but navigated on their terms.

Outside, the sky had settled into a pearly overcast. London looked almost gentle.

“Well,” Lillian said. “That was… not pleasant.”

“But survivable,” he said.

“For now,” she said.

They walked back to her office in silence. At his desk—the soon-to-be-former desk—he paused.

“You know this feels a bit like being fired and promoted at the same time,” he said.

She stopped. Turned.

“I know,” she said. “If you decide you hate ops, if you decide this was all a terrible mistake, you can walk. I won’t hold you.”

“You might try,” he said lightly.

Her eyes softened. “Not out there,” she said. “Not in ways that matter.”

He nodded.

“Velvet Room tonight?” he asked, heart kicking a little at his own boldness.

She considered.

“Nine,” she said. “If you’re still willing.”

His answer came easily.

“Yes,” he said. “Always.”

## Scene Ten – The Velvet Room

At nine o’clock, the tower was mostly dark again. Cleaning staff moved through the floors, ghosts with vacuum cleaners and mirrored carts. The lifts chimed softly in the distance.

On the twenty-ninth floor, the light above Lillian’s office door glowed.

Miles’ desk was already half packed into boxes. His monitors were gone, reassigned to a junior assistant who would learn, in time, the rhythm of Lillian’s days. His mug sat alone, *World’s Most Overqualified Secretary* staring up at the fluorescent lights.

He picked it up and carried it with him into her office.

She stood by the inner door, jacket off, shirt sleeves rolled, hair down for once, falling in soft waves around her face. She looked less like a corporate promotional photo and more like the woman he’d seen on her sofa, laughing with whisky on her breath.

“You kept it,” she said, nodding at the mug.

“Reminder,” he said. “Of where I started.”

She opened the Velvet Room door.

Inside, the lamp cast its familiar pool of gold. The velvet walls soaked up the world.

He stepped in. She followed, closing the door behind them.

“Any regrets?” she asked quietly.

He thought of the paperwork they’d signed. The way some colleagues had looked at him strangely that afternoon when the email announcing his promotion went out. The knowledge that office gossip would fill the gaps in that bland corporate paragraph with whatever story entertained them the most.

He thought of kneeling on the velvet carpet, of her paw in his fur, of the way his body had answered a truth he’d spent years trying to bury.

“None that matter,” he said.

Her shoulders loosened.

“Then kneel,” she said.

He did. The movement came more easily this time, not because the weight of it was less, but because he knew where it landed in him.

She stepped closer, between his knees. Her paw rested on his head, firm and warm.

“You are mine,” she said. “Here. By choice. Say it.”

He looked up at her, at the badger who had clawed her way to the top of a glass tower and still found room in her life for the mess of desire.

“I am yours,” he said. “By choice.”

Her mouth softened into something that might, in a more sentimental story, be called a smile.

“Good,” she murmured.

She tipped his head forward, guided him to rest his forehead lightly against her thigh. The velvet of her trousers was cool under his fur; the warmth of her body radiated through.

Out in the city, the river moved under bridges. Trains rattled. People stumbled out of bars and into cabs, into bad decisions and good ones.

In the Velvet Room, power and pleasure sat down together in the same small space and, for once, did not devour each other.

They didn’t have all the answers. There would be more paperwork, more compromises, more storms. The tower would continue to hum. The systems would continue to demand more than they gave.

But here, on the soft carpet under the dark walls, a badger and a shepherd wrote their own clause into the contract: that the things they did to each other in private would be acts of care as much as desire; that vulnerability would not be a weapon; that neither of them would ever again let someone else tell their story for them.

“Ma’am?” he said softly.

“Yes, Miles?”

“Thank you,” he said.

She ran her paw through his fur, slow and sure.

“You’re welcome,” she said. “Now. Let me take it from here.”

Outside the velvet walls, the tower lights burned on. Inside, the scene landed not with a neat closure but with a held note, humming under the skin, promising that this was not the end of anything.

It was, at last, a beginning.
