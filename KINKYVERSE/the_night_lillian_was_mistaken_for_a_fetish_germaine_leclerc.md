## Chapter I — The Invitation Without Instructions

The envelope arrived on a Tuesday, which was already suspicious.

Tuesdays, in Lillian Edgecombe’s life, were for tutorials, committee emails, and the particular flavour of exhaustion that came from encouraging twenty-year-olds to care about phrasing and then begging the photocopier not to eat their scores. Tuesdays were for other people’s expectations, neatly stapled.

This envelope did not believe in staples.

It lay on the kitchen table between an electricity bill and a flyer for “Authentic Neolithic Bread (Now With More Gluten)”. Its paper was thick, cream, faintly textured under her pads. The handwriting on the front was precise and ornamental, the kind that made ballpoint pens feel ashamed.

It knew her name.

Not “Dr L. Edgecombe” in cramped officialese, or “Professor Badger” in the affectionate graffiti of students who thought she could not see the back of the rehearsal-room door. Not even “Mrs Ferguson”, which belonged to certain very particular contexts involving tax, shared ownership, and Miles’s habit of leaving his socks in poetic drifts.

Just:

> LILLIAN EDGECOOMBE  
> (attendance presumed)

Her whiskers twitched.

“Presumed,” she muttered, easing herself into the chair. The fur along her shoulders prickled in a way that suggested attention, or static, or both.

The kettle hummed on the hob. The kitchen smelled of last night’s curry, today’s coffee, and the faint, comforting clay-dust of her pottery scattered across the windowsill in cracked, beloved mugs.

She slit the envelope with the back of a claw.

The card inside was black, the lettering silver. It was the kind of invitation that could only have been designed by someone who believed in words like “curated” and “discreet” and “experience” with unnecessary capital letters.

> You are cordially invited to an evening of Cultural Consideration and Experimental Etiquette.  
>  
> Location: To be disclosed upon acceptance.  
> Time: After dark.  
> Dress code: Comfortable, honest.  
>  
> The organisers are honoured by your presumed presence.  
>  
> No further instructions will be provided.  
>  
> — The Committee

There was an embossed symbol in the corner: not a logo she recognised, just a stylised circle with three short lines radiating from it, like a simplified sun or an abstract paw print.

Lillian read the card twice, then a third time.

She had lived long enough in this city, and in her own body, to know the difference between an invitation and a summons. This was neither. It was a… supposition. An assumption made into stationery.

“Cultural consideration,” she said to the card. “Experimental etiquette. That’s not ominous at all.”

She turned it over. The back was blank, except for a line of tiny print along the bottom.

> No photography. No recordings. No posting. Presence only.

Her fur bristled, pleasantly.

It was not that she mistrusted cameras. Cameras had, at various points, caught her in flattering light, unflattering angles, and once in a deeply compromising position involving a marching band, an inflatable swan, and three pints of lager. Cameras were, like students, only as dangerous as the stories you allowed them to tell.

But an event that explicitly refused to be documented suggested intent. Suggested that whatever happened would be held in nerves and memory, not on someone’s curated feed.

“You’re thinking about it,” Miles said from the doorway.

She startled, then relaxed as her husband padded into the kitchen, still in his dressing gown, ears half-cocked, fur ruffled from sleep. He was a German Shepherd in the comfortable prime of his forties, all solid lines and competence, the kind of man who could fix a leaky tap, hold an entire band together with a look, and remember everyone’s tea order.

“I am reading,” she corrected. “Thinking is a side effect.”

He leaned down, kissed the top of her head, and picked up the flyer for Neolithic bread.

“Please tell me the electricity bill is less terrifying than this,” he said.

“It is marginally more honest,” she replied.

He glanced at the black card.

“Another concert?” he asked. “A gig? Secret society of middle-aged bandleaders?”

She considered, for a dangerous moment, handing it to him and asking what he saw. Whether the invitation read differently to someone who had not spent most of their adult life being viewed, evaluated, and occasionally fetishised in auditoriums.

“Just an event,” she said lightly. “Not work. Cultural… something.”

Miles’s eyes warmed. “You should go,” he said. “When was the last time you went to a thing that wasn’t your thing?”

“I go to students’ recitals,” she protested.

“Those are your thing,” he said. “You built half of them.”

He put the Neolithic flyer back down and squeezed her shoulder.

“You spend all week holding space for other people’s emotions,” he said. “Maybe let someone hold a room for you for once.”

It was a kind remark. It also made her feel, briefly, like a piece of furniture.

She flicked his paw away, gently.

“Fine,” she said. “I will go. If it turns out to be a pyramid scheme for artisanal sauerkraut, I am blaming you.”

He grinned, sharp and fond.

“Deal,” he said.

She did not tell him, not yet, about the line that had snagged her attention: no further instructions provided. All that deliberate vagueness. All that assumption.

She wrote her acceptance in the only space the card allowed: a small rectangle on the back that darkened when she touched it, like paper flushing.

The circle with its three lines pulsed, faintly, as though satisfied.

An address appeared in silver ink where there had been none: a street she dimly recognised from half-remembered post-show wanderings, a time tomorrow night, a note.

> No need to knock. We will know when you arrive.

“Of course you will,” she said to the card. “Everyone always does.”

She tucked the invitation into her bag, between a marked-up score and Sarah’s latest scribbled postcard (“Mum, send pictures of cups, people here don’t understand crockery”). The card sat there like a small, self-satisfied secret.

For the rest of the day, as she taught and negotiated and walked home past students practicing scales in open windows, she felt its presence against her hip: a quiet, insistent weight.

Attendance presumed.

She bristled at the presumption. She went anyway.

## Chapter II — A Room That Waits

The building did not look like a place where anything interesting happened.

It was a converted warehouse in the part of the city that had once been all factories and now aspired to be all galleries. By day, Lillian knew, this stretch of street was a mixture of start-ups, yoga studios, and the kind of cafes that took hours to make coffee and called it ritual.

By night, under a low, honest drizzle, it was mostly wet brick, sodium light, and the smell of old rain.

She had dressed as the card instructed: comfortable, honest.

For Lillian, this meant a long dress in deep plum that skimmed her curves without apologising for them, a black cardigan that could be shrugged off if she grew too warm, and boots she could stand in for hours without resenting. Her fur was brushed, her hair pinned up in a way that looked casual and had taken three attempts. She wore minimal jewellery: wedding band, simple silver earrings, a bracelet Sarah had made her out of kiln-fired beads that looked like small planets.

She looked, she thought, like herself in concert-hall mode: solid, composed, a little theatrical. A badger who had made her peace with taking up space.

The door was unmarked. No sign, no buzzer, just a single black handle and a brass plate polished to anonymity.

As she approached, the lock clicked.

“Of course,” she murmured.

The door opened onto a staircase lit from somewhere above. The air smelled of polished wood, candle wax, and a faint undertone of something human: sweat, perfume, nerves.

She climbed.

At the top of the stairs, the room waited.

It was not large. Taller than it was wide, with exposed beams and walls painted a soft, ambiguous grey. Candles burned in niches along the walls, their flames steady, their wax catching in small, controlled drips. A few lamps added a warmer glow, leaving pockets of shadow where people could pretend not to be seen while absolutely being seen.

People clustered in loose groups. Some sat on low sofas, others stood, drinks in hand. There was no bar, only a side table with bottles of water, wine, and something amber. No music played. No one spoke loudly.

They noticed her.

It was not the stare of a crowd presented with a performer. That, she knew intimately: the forward lean, the quieting, the collective breath that sharpened as she walked onto a stage.

This attention was different, softer but more particular.

Conversations thinned as she stepped fully into the room. Heads turned, then politely turned away, the way people did when trying to pretend they had not just been talking about you.

She felt, in the arch of her back and the set of her shoulders, the way bodies reoriented themselves around hers. People shifted slightly, leaving a clear path from the entrance to the centre of the room. A space on the main sofa opened as if by accident: a gap exactly wide enough for one large badger to sink into.

“Good evening,” said a voice at her left.

A woman stood there, slender, with neat dark hair and glasses that caught the candlelight. Her suit was sharp, charcoal; her shirt, pale. She wore no name tag, but her stance suggested someone accustomed to hosting.

“Lillian Edgecombe,” the woman said, as if confirming an identity that had been expected, not discovered. “We’re so glad you made it.”

“You had my presence presumed,” Lillian replied. “It seemed rude to disappoint.”

The woman’s mouth curved.

“I’m Amira,” she said. “I’m one of the organisers. The Committee, if we’re being pompous.”

“And if we’re being honest?” Lillian asked.

“Chaos with spreadsheets,” Amira said briskly. “Can I get you something to drink? Water? Wine? Something stronger? We don’t do labels here, but we do hydration.”

“Water, to start with,” Lillian said. “It’s been a long day of convincing strangers that crotchets matter.”

“They do,” Amira said with the conviction of someone who had once lost hours to a metronome. “Make yourself at home. You are—”

She paused, searching for a word, and chose one that made Lillian’s fur prickle.

“—our axis tonight.”

She moved away without elaboration.

Axis.

Lillian held the word in her mouth like a lozenge, letting it dissolve.

She took a glass of water from the side table. The carafe was heavy crystal; the water tasted faintly of lemon and something floral. She sipped, aware of eyes skimming over her and away again, like bats avoiding a lighthouse.

No one approached with the bright, brittle eagerness of networking. No one shoved a business card into her paw or asked what she did. Instead, people adjusted: drew their feet in to give her space to pass, softened their voices, shifted their bodies so that if she chose to join a group, there would be a natural place for her.

The room had an etiquette. She simply did not know the rules yet.

She stood for a moment, letting her senses adjust.

Soft fabrics brushed ankles. Glass clinked gently. Someone laughed, then smothered it. The air was warm, working its way under her cardigan. Her body, attuned to atmospheres after years of concert halls and rehearsal rooms, hummed.

She sat on the sofa.

The cushions were deep, the fabric dark, soft as sin. People near her modulated their posture almost imperceptibly: straightening, turning slightly towards her, crossing their legs in ways that made them smaller, more contained.

She sipped her water and realised, with a slow, amused horror, that the room was orienting itself as though she were not merely a guest, but a… focal point.

A presence.

An axis.

She had not been given a role. They had assumed one for her.

The question was: which?

## Chapter III — Incorrect Assumptions, Carefully Held

She did not understand, at first, what she was being mistaken for.

There are many things one can be in an unfamiliar room. Lillian had, over the years, been variously presumed to be the singer, the manager, the patron, the chaperone, the problem, the solution. People read bodies quickly and badly; they hung roles on shoulders like coats that did not quite fit.

Here, the reading was precise, but the text was unfamiliar.

A man with carefully tousled hair and an expensive uncertainty in his posture approached with two glasses of wine. He offered one to her without quite meeting her eyes.

“We’re honoured you came,” he said, the words careful, rehearsed.

“You’re very kind,” she replied. “You are…?”

“One of the committee,” he said. “Call me Theo. You don’t have to— I mean, if we’re using first names— We thought you might prefer—”

He floundered.

Lillian rescued him, because it was in her nature and her profession to prevent unnecessary embarrassment.

“Theo is fine,” she said. “What exactly is this evening?”

He brightened with the relief of being allowed to recite something.

“An experiment in decorum,” he said. “A space for adults to consider how we treat one another when we are not pretending not to want things.”

It was a sentence full of negatives, which made her teeth itch.

“And you invited me because…?”

Theo blushed, the flush visible even under his fur.

“Because you… exemplify something,” he said. “We’ve seen the way you hold a room. The way people slow down around you. The way you… embody a certain…”

His hands made a vague, circular gesture that encompassed her shoulders, chest, hips, boots, the curve of her belly, the line of her jaw.

“Gravitas,” he finished, hopefully.

“I have a doctorate,” she said dryly. “Gravity comes with the paperwork.”

He laughed, a little too loud, then caught himself and dialled the volume back.

“Not just that,” he said. “People respond to you. You say ‘take a breath’ and they do. You say ‘wait’ and they wait. We wanted to see what would happen if we built an evening where… that was the point.”

The point.

She felt, suddenly, acutely, as if she had stepped onto a stage without knowing her lines.

“You’ve built an evening around people… behaving as though I—”

She stopped. There were several nouns available: host, priestess, conductor. None of them fit quite right.

Theo rescued her, perhaps without meaning to.

“Around the idea that your presence changes the room,” he said. “And that this can be… kind. Structured. Consensual.”

The word “consensual” sat between them, heavy and deliberate.

Lillian’s body reacted before her mind did.

The dress slid against her fur in a way that reminded her, inconveniently, of other evenings in other rooms, where consent had been negotiated with words and looks and the tap of a paw on a knee.

Experimental etiquette. Presence only.

Someone, somewhere, had looked at her—at the way she moved through parties and rehearsals, at the way students and bandmates and strangers at bars reacted to her—and decided she represented something enough to be invited as its living emblem.

It was flattering. It was absurd. It was dangerous.

“Does everyone here know who I am?” she asked.

Theo hesitated.

“No,” he said. “Not by name. Some know your work. Some know you from… other circles. Most only know that tonight, they are practicing paying attention to someone who will not perform for them unless she chooses to. That’s the… agreement.”

Agreement. Committee. Axis.

In another universe—one with labels and contracts and safe words printed on laminated cards—this would have been a declared scene. Here, it was an art event.

“And if I simply sit here and drink my water and talk about the weather?” she asked.

Theo’s gaze flicked to her hands, her throat, the span of her shoulders. His voice, when he answered, was sincere.

“Then that is what we will learn from,” he said.

He bowed a little—not deeply, not theatrically, just enough to acknowledge something—and withdrew.

Around her, the room adjusted again.

Conversations flowed, but with a strange, careful modulation. People did not crowd her. They arranged themselves at respectful distances, close enough to be influenced, far enough not to intrude. When she laughed at something, ripples of amusement travelled outward, as though her reaction had set a tempo.

It dawned on her, slowly, that they had mistaken her not for a generic category, not for “a dominatrix” or “a goddess” or any of the other tired archetypes that tended to be hung on large, self-possessed women.

They had mistaken her for the idea that her gravity was their kink.

## Chapter IV — The Decision Not to Correct

There are misunderstandings that must be corrected: mistaken identities in hospitals; wrong notes in exams; the moment where someone thinks “no, thank you” means “try harder”.

And then there are misunderstandings that have been crafted with such hopeful care that to puncture them feels like cruelty for its own sake.

Lillian sat on the sofa and considered the ethical implications of being mistaken for a fetish.

If she told them they were wrong—stood up, clinked her glass, announced that she was simply a tired music professor who occasionally modelled for art students and did not, in fact, exist to rearrange their nervous systems—they would be… what? Embarrassed. Disappointed. Relieved? Possibly. Some would slink away, nursing wounded fantasies. Others would insist that no, no, the point was the conversation, really.

If she said nothing, if she allowed the evening to unfold according to their careful, unspoken script, she would be consenting to participate in a shared theatre where she had not written the part.

Consent, she reminded herself, was the spine of all good enchantments. And of the better bad ones.

She took another sip of water and watched the room.

A couple near the window were discussing something in low tones. Every so often, one of them would glance over at Lillian and adjust their own posture: sitting up straighter when she did, leaning back when she shifted. A kind of sympathetic politeness.

At the side table, a trio debated whether to open another bottle. One of them made a small, querying gesture in her direction, as if asking her implicit permission to add more alcohol to the air. She gave the slightest nod. They relaxed, uncorked, poured.

The rules here were odd, but they were not malignant.

No one touched her without asking. No one intruded on her space. No one tried to make her perform. They were holding her with an exaggerated care that was, if anything, more respectful than most parties she had attended where people knew precisely who she was and felt entitled to her attention because of it.

The fetish, such as it was, seemed to be about how they treated her, not what they wanted to do to her.

And she—traitorous, curious creature that she was—wanted to see what would happen if she leaned into it.

Not into sex. Not into drama. Into gravity.

Into the slow, deliberate way she knew how to inhabit her own body when she was not apologising for its size or its history.

She thought of Miles, of the way he looked at her when she stood in the kitchen in nothing but an old shirt, making tea. Of the way he respected the rhythms of her moods, the days she needed quiet, the evenings she wanted to be held down by his weight and his paws and his calm voice saying “breathe”.

She would not betray that. She did not need to. There were kinds of intimacy that did not trespass on that territory at all.

“Observation,” she murmured to herself. “Not intervention.”

She chose, deliberately, not to correct the room’s assumption.

Instead, she decided to become a scientist of it.

She sat a little more comfortably. Let her shoulders ease. Let her belly rest against the fabric of her dress without tensing. Allowed herself to be fully present in her own skin, the way she had in rehearsal rooms when showing students how to stand, how to breathe.

The effect was immediate.

Conversations shifted key.

People who had been standing on the balls of their feet settled their weight into their heels. Laughter slowed, deepened. Voices dropped half a register. The whole room exhaled, as if the signal from her body had given them permission to stop performing and simply be.

Her fur tingled.

She was not doing anything.

And yet.

Someone passed, close enough that she could smell their perfume—a warm, spicy scent undercut with something sharp. Their arm brushed the back of the sofa and they flinched, almost, then relaxed, as if the proximity to her space had briefly overwhelmed them.

The power was unsettling. It was also familiar.

She had felt most of this before on stage, in rehearsal, even in seminars. The difference was that here, the focus was on the space between, not on any explicit output.

That space, tonight, was charged.

Her body knew it. Her thighs pressed together, without her quite deciding to do so. Her breath deepened.

She allowed herself a small, private smile.

If they wanted to treat her as an axis, she would see how far the room would turn.

## Chapter V — Small Experiments in Gravity

The first experiment was a pause.

Not the performative kind, the sort she used on stage to juice a phrase, or in lectures to let a point sink in. This was a simple, gratuitous pause: she put her glass down, folded her paws in her lap, and did nothing at all for the span of three full breaths.

Around her, the room slowed.

The couple near the window stopped mid-sentence. One of them blinked, glanced over, then, apparently seeing that Lillian was simply… paused, softened their own shoulders. The other followed suit. Their argument, whatever it had been, dropped in pitch.

Someone at the drinks table reached for a bottle, hesitated, withdrew their hand, then, after a heartbeat, poured discreetly, as if not wishing to break something delicate.

The pause spread, subtle as smoke.

Lillian breathed through it, feeling the way her lungs expanded against her dress, the movement of air over her tongue. Her body thrummed with a low, steady arousal that had very little to do with sex and everything to do with being the lever that moved the room’s tempo.

She released the pause.

Picked up her glass. Shifted slightly. Tilted her head to listen to a nearby conversation about nineteenth-century etiquette manuals and their passive-aggressive footnotes.

The room resumed, relieving itself with small bursts of chatter, laughter, the clink of glass.

The second experiment involved accepting a compliment.

A woman with close-cropped hair and elaborate earrings approached, moving with the careful confidence of someone who had thought very hard about how not to appear threatening and had not quite noticed that she herself was a little intimidated.

“I love your dress,” she said. “And the way you— I mean, you look… beautiful.”

Ordinarily, Lillian would have deflected. Years of being looked at had given her a reflex for that: a joke, a compliment in return, a little verbal shrug that said “it’s nothing” to protect both of them from the rawness of unqualified appreciation.

Tonight, she did not.

She met the woman’s eyes.

“Thank you,” she said. Nothing else. No protest, no devaluation.

The woman’s pupils dilated.

“You’re welcome,” she said, voice a little breathless.

She stepped back as if she had survived something. Her friends, hovering nearby, eyed Lillian with a mixture of gratitude and awe usually reserved for structural engineers and therapists.

The third experiment was territorial.

Amira brought over a plate of small, elaborate snacks—crackers topped with things that looked like microgreens and hopes—and set it on the low table in front of the sofa.

“If the Committee forgets to feed the axis,” she said under her breath, “we will have to rebrand.”

“We could call it a cautionary tale,” Lillian replied.

Amira’s mouth twitched.

“You’re doing very well,” she said quietly. “You haven’t run away yet.”

“I am still deciding whether that’s professionalism or stubbornness,” Lillian said.

Amira’s gaze brushed her face, her throat, the line of her collarbone above the neckline of the dress, then slid away with the careful courtesy of someone not presuming more than the room allowed.

“You’re allowed to enjoy it,” she said.

Lillian did not answer. She picked up a cracker instead, inspected it, and then, decisively, set the plate closer to her, occupying the table space.

It was a small movement, but symbolic: claim. This is within my orbit.

Several people visibly recalibrated their routes through the room to avoid the edge of the table, as if the plate now had a gravitational field.

Her experiments grew bolder.

When a conversation at the far end of the room grew a little too fast, too brittle, she raised an eyebrow in its general direction. The laughter there softened, slowed, turned from edge to warmth.

When someone spilled a few drops of wine, froze, and looked mortified, she caught their eye and gave a minute shrug: accidents happen. They exhaled and smiled, embarrassed but not crushed.

At one point, a young man whose shirt was more ambition than fabric paused near her, as if to ask a question, then seemed to think better of it and sank instead onto a cushion on the floor, back to the sofa. His shoulders relaxed as he let himself rest against the furniture she occupied.

She could feel the tension of his back through the fabric. Could feel, too, the way he consciously did not lean too far, did not touch her without permission. The restraint was its own kind of touch.

Her skin buzzed.

This was the soft erotica of the evening: not bodies in beds, but bodies in orbits. The charge lay in restraint, in permission, in the mutual acknowledgement that she could alter the room’s atmosphere with a breath and they were choosing to let her.

It was not, she thought, so different from her classroom, or the bandstand. The difference was that here, the students were desire, and the curriculum was how to be around it without flailing.

She found herself smiling, genuinely.

Perhaps being mistaken for a fetish was not an insult. Perhaps it was, in its strange, baroque way, a compliment: an acknowledgement that the way she inhabited her own weight and authority could be, for some, quietly thrilling.

She felt no obligation to fulfil that thrill.

But she was not above using it as data.

## Chapter VI — The Fetish Debated (Without Her)

It was in the cloakroom that she overheard them.

The coat rack, like everything else in the building, seemed to understand choreography. Coats arranged themselves in gradients of colour and texture, heavy winter garments at one end, lighter jackets at the other, as if the room had a theory about weather as well as etiquette.

Lillian had come in search of a brief moment of unthemed air. The main room’s atmosphere, thick with mutual consideration, had started to press against her skin like a second, scented fur. She wanted, for a minute, to stand between other people’s coats and remember that outside this warehouse the world did not orbit her unless she made it.

Voices drifted from the other side of the rack.

“She’s nothing like I expected,” said one, low, familiar with disappointment and wonder.

“Good,” replied another, sharper. “Expectations are how people get hurt.”

Lillian stilled.

“I just—” The first voice belonged, she realised, to the young man with the ambitious shirt. “From the way people talked about her, I thought she’d be more… theatrical. More command-y.”

“You wanted a cartoon,” the second voice said. “Instead you got an adult.”

“I know,” he said quickly. “I don’t mean— She’s incredible. It’s just… she doesn’t do anything.”

Lillian fought the urge to step out and demonstrate exactly how much doing went into not doing anything.

The second voice—a woman, older, bearing the dry humour of someone who had been to too many workshops—snorted.

“She sits in a room full of people who desperately want her attention,” she said, “and refuses to be hurried, flattered, or drafted into their stories. That’s not nothing. That’s the entire point.”

“But the way they talk about her online,” he protested. “The stories. All those posts about how she… stabilises people. How she makes them feel seen. I thought there’d be more— I don’t know. Kneeling.”

“You can kneel in your own time,” the woman said briskly. “Tonight is practice. For you. Not for her.”

Online.

Lillian’s stomach did a slow, cold turn.

There were, she knew, spaces on the internet where people discussed her. Not her music, particularly, or her teaching, but her. Photos from gigs, from conferences, from public events: screenshots of her lectures, candid shots from art shows, a blurry picture of her laughing at a bar. Threads filled with comments about her size, her fur, her voice, her age, her “vibes”.

She did not, as a general rule, go looking for them.

The idea that those spaces had coalesced into enough of a mythology that someone had expected her to walk into this room and start issuing commands made her want to blow her nose very loudly and ruin the mood.

The woman went on.

“You wanted a fetish,” she said. “A neat, named thing you could plug into. ‘Oh, she’s a [insert label here]. I know what to do with that.’”

“Isn’t that what tonight is?” he asked, plaintive.

“Tonight,” she said, “is about not naming it. About seeing who you are around someone who doesn’t give you a script.”

He made a small, wounded noise.

“So she’s not—”

“She is exactly what they said she was,” the woman cut in. “She’s just more severe than the porn version. Which is why I brought you. You need to learn the difference between being held and being catered to.”

“That’s harsh,” he muttered.

“It’s accurate,” she said. “Also, if you think she’s doing nothing, you haven’t watched your own shoulders in the last hour.”

There was a pause. Lillian could almost hear him checking his own posture.

“Fine,” he said, grudging. “So… what is she, then, if she’s not—”

He groped for a label, picked one he apparently dared not say aloud.

The woman saved him the trouble of embarrassing himself.

“She’s a person,” she said. “A married forty-two-year-old badger who teaches, plays, makes pots, and has chosen to sit in a room while you and I and everyone else try not to make her our homework. We’re lucky she’s humouring us.”

“You’re very protective of her,” he observed.

“I’m protective of anyone people talk about like a product,” she said. “Go back in there. Watch what happens when she says ‘no’ with a look. Learn something.”

Their footsteps retreated.

Lillian let out the breath she had been holding.

The temptation to march back into the main room and declare an emergency seminar on fetishisation and hospitality was strong. She could see the layout in her mind: chairs in a circle, bullet points on a flipchart, diagrams of attention flows. PowerPoint, if the building would tolerate it.

Instead, she adjusted her cardigan, smoothed a paw down the front of her dress, and looked at herself in the small mirror by the door.

Her reflection looked back steadily. A badger, solid and soft and sharp at once, with laugh lines and frown lines and the kind of presence you could hang a room on.

The fetish, she realised, was less about her body than about her refusal to hurry anyone out of theirs.

They had come here to practice wanting things slowly, respectfully, in a space held by someone who knew how to say “enough”.

She could give them that.

Not as a performance. As a side effect of being herself.

She smiled, very slightly, at the mirror.

“Fine,” she said. “Let’s go back to class.”

## Chapter VII — Holding the Room

When she re-entered, the room felt like a held breath.

Not a panicked one. The kind you take before stepping into cold water. Anticipation and fear and thrill, all braided.

She walked back to the sofa at an unhurried pace, feeling every step: the weight of her boots, the swish of her dress, the way the floorboards creaked in acknowledgement. People shifted to accommodate her path, the air parting around her like water around a hull.

She sat.

The cushions accepted her. The sofa’s frame made a small, content sigh. Or perhaps that was the young man in the ambitious shirt, whose eyes darted briefly to hers and then away, as if remembering the admonition he had just received in the cloakroom.

She decided to speak.

“You are all very tense,” she observed.

Her voice was not loud. It didn’t need to be. The acoustics were arranged for this kind of intimacy; the building, perhaps, had been built by people who understood that confession and instruction were cousins.

Conversations stuttered, then quietened to a level where her words could sit comfortably on top of them.

Theo, across the room, looked as if he had been waiting for this moment and was trying very hard not to wring his paws.

Lillian took pity on them all.

“I was not given a job description for tonight,” she said. “Which is, frankly, poor management. But as far as I can tell, you have assembled here to practice being around desire without flinging yourselves at it like badly aimed confetti.”

A ripple of laughter, nervous and grateful, ran through the room.

She went on.

“So here is my suggestion. You may consider it a rule, or a spell, or just something to do with your hands.”

She held up her own, palms open.

“You will not move faster than my breathing,” she said.

A murmur. Someone choked softly. Theo half-sat, half-collapsed into a chair.

“If you find yourself wanting to rush over, to ask, to impress, to confess, to perform,” she continued, “you will check my shoulders first. If they are relaxed, you may approach—slowly. If they are tight, you will stay where you are and talk to the person next to you about something delightfully boring until the feeling passes.”

Laughter again, more genuine this time.

“I am not here to be your project,” she added. “I am certainly not here to be your fantasy made flesh. I am here to see what happens when an entire room of adults chooses not to chase the thing they want most for ten seconds longer than they think they can bear.”

She let that sit.

Ten seconds can be a long time.

“You may, if you wish,” she said, “consider that restraint itself a kind of… play.”

Now the laughter had an edge to it: recognising, aroused, slightly horrified with itself.

Someone near the drinks table muttered, feebly, “I didn’t sign up for introspection.”

“You did,” Lillian said, without looking. “You just didn’t read the terms and conditions in the small print of your own fantasies.”

A tiny, involuntary whimper from the ambitious shirt.

She ignored it.

“I am going to sit here,” she said. “I am going to drink my water. I am going to have conversations. You are going to notice what happens in your bodies when you want to hurry and choose not to.”

She smiled, slow, not unkind.

“If that happens to be erotic for you, congratulations. You have discovered that patience is horny.”

The room exhaled.

The charge shifted, from a diffuse static to something more grounded.

They obeyed her, of course. Of course they did.

People moved more deliberately. Hands unclenched. Breaths elongated. When someone did approach—Amira, to ask if she needed more water; the woman with the close-cropped hair, to tell her a story about a disastrous gallery opening—they did so with awareness, checking her shoulders, her jaw, the set of her mouth.

She rewarded them with attention.

When someone obeyed the “rule” visibly—stopping themselves from asking for something, turning instead to the person beside them—she caught their eye and nodded once. A small blessing.

It was absurd, of course. It was also, in its own quiet way, profoundly arousing.

Control, after all, was not about barking orders. It was about being the reference point.

She felt the room steady around her, like a ship finding its true north.

Her own body responded: heat low in her belly, a pleasurable ache in her thighs from holding herself with deliberate poise. Her fur felt too tight, her dress both a barrier and a reminder.

She thought of Miles, of nights when he had asked “do you want me to stop?” and she had said “no” and meant “never”, and of how much of their joy came from the shared delight in boundaries observed and occasionally, consensually, nudged.

She thought of the stories online, the ones she did not read, and hoped—not entirely charitably—that at least some of them would now have to incorporate the idea that her fetish, if she had one, was watching other people learn to slow down.

Time blurred.

When she next checked the clock above the door, an hour had passed.

The room was less tense, more… saturated. People sat on the floor near her, backs to the sofa, not touching but sharing her orbit. Others leaned against walls, talking in low voices, hands moving less, eyes more open.

No one asked her to do anything more.

She realised, with a sudden clarity that made her throat tight, that she had been bracing all evening for the moment when the demand would come: be more. Be more explicit. Be more generous with your body, your history, your hurt.

It did not.

The discipline of the room was, startlingly, real.

She had been mistaken for a fetish, yes. But the fetish, it turned out, was not her body. It was the possibility of being around a woman who did not have to explain herself and was being left, remarkably, almost entirely alone.

She sat in the middle of their hunger and did not feed it, and that was the feast.

## Chapter VIII — Leaving Early

The decision to leave was, like most good exits, made three seconds before it needed to be.

Lillian felt it first in her knees: a particular ache that said “if you stay another hour, you will have to unfold yourself like origami and you will resent everyone, including the cushions.” Then in her head: a slight fuzziness at the edges of her attention, the sign that she was nearing the end of her capacity to hold space without leaking.

The room, too, had begun to change.

Conversations that had deepened into honest territory—fears, desires, griefs—were starting to circle back, repeat themselves. Laughter had a frayed quality. The thrill of restraint was on the verge of turning into the crankiness of children kept well-behaved for too long.

She did not want to be here when the experiment tipped into either confession or farce.

She stood.

The effect was immediate.

Bodies straightened. Voices dipped. The room oriented itself like iron filings around a magnet.

She could, at that moment, have said anything. Could have offered a benediction, or a reprimand, or an invitation to continue elsewhere, to step into some other configuration of the night.

Instead, she simply said:

“Thank you for your company.”

It was astonishing how erotic gratitude could sound when addressed to a room that had spent hours tiptoeing around the edges of its own desire.

Amira materialised at her elbow.

“Leaving already?” she asked, voice gentle.

“Already?” Lillian echoed. “I’ve been here for hours. I have a husband who will start worrying, a cat who will demand to know why I smell of strangers, and emails breeding in my inbox as we speak.”

“Time moves strangely in here,” Amira said. “People forget that the rest of their lives are not in suspended animation.”

She hesitated.

“You’ve given us more than we had any right to ask,” she added. “Even if all you think you did was sit.”

Lillian considered correcting her. Telling her that she had, in fact, done quite a lot. That doing nothing on purpose was labour, that being the axis of a room was work.

Then she remembered the hour she had just spent teaching strangers to breathe.

“You gave me decent snacks,” she said instead. “We’ll call it even.”

Theo appeared, looking wrung out and luminous.

“Thank you,” he said, with the solemnity of someone thanking a surgeon. “For… all of it.”

“You’re welcome,” she replied. “Try not to make a religion out of me in absentia.”

He winced, self-aware enough to take the hit.

“We’ll… debrief,” he said. “Adjust. This is a learning process.”

“Good,” she said. “If I hear about anyone here starting a cult of the Slow Badger, I will come back with placards.”

“Slow Badger,” someone near the door repeated under their breath, delighted.

She rolled her eyes, but affectionately. Names had power. They also had comedy.

At the threshold, she turned.

The room watched her, not in worship, but in something like gratitude. Faces open, bodies less rigid than when she had arrived. The tension in the air had settled into something warmer, more human. The fetish, unnamed, unconsummated, hung in the air like a story mid-sentence.

“Go home,” she said. “Drink water. Sleep. Try being this patient with yourselves tomorrow.”

It was not an order. It landed like one anyway.

As she descended the stairs, several people murmured thank you, not to her face but to the air she had just vacated, as if the room itself had done them a kindness.

Outside, the rain had stopped. The street smelled of wet tarmac and the city’s particular bouquet of takeaway, diesel, and distant, illicit smoke.

She pulled her cardigan tighter and walked, the night folding around her like a cape.

Her body buzzed.

She was aroused, she admitted, but not in the simple, solvable way of lust that could be addressed with a hand or a partner. This was a deeper, stranger stimulation: the titillation of having been seen and mis-seen, of having stepped into a projection and rearranged it without dismantling it entirely.

She had not kissed anyone. No one had touched more than the fabric of her sleeve or the air around her paws. And yet the evening hummed under her skin like a long, sustained note.

At home, the lights were on.

Miles met her at the door in track bottoms and a T-shirt, hair damp from a shower, expression balancing worry and welcome.

“You’re back,” he said, as if she had only been out for bread.

“I am,” she said. “Your wife has apparently become a concept while you were doing the washing up.”

He sniffed her, theatrically.

“You smell of candles and anxiety,” he said. “How was it?”

She considered.

“Strange,” she said. “Interesting. Respectful. Possibly the most elaborate group project in ‘how to sit near a woman without being an idiot’ I’ve ever seen.”

He grinned.

“Did you enjoy it?”

She thought of the pauses, the experiments, the overheard debate in the cloakroom.

“Yes,” she said. “In the same way I enjoy conducting a very good ensemble that’s just on the edge of getting it.”

He kissed her, slow.

“That’s your favourite edge,” he murmured.

Her body, which had been humming at a polite background frequency all evening, surged.

Later, in the quiet of their bedroom, with the ordinary intimacy of shared sheets and familiar paws and the explicit, spoken consent of twenty years, she let the arousal find its obvious outlet.

She did not tell him everything.

Not because she needed to keep secrets, but because some experiences were like certain chords: diminished, if resolved too quickly in words.

She told him the funny parts. The earnestness of Theo. The phrase “axis”. The moment someone had visibly recommitted to not flinging themselves at her.

He listened, attentive, amused.

“You’re going to change them,” he said. “Those people. They’ll go home and be different with each other.”

“They’ll go home and start seventeen different group chats about whether they did it right,” she said.

“That too,” he allowed.

She fell asleep with his hand warm on her hip, feeling, for the first time in a long time, oddly honoured by being misread.

## Chapter IX — Aftermath Without Revelation

In the weeks that followed, the night receded in her memory not as an event but as a shift.

The city did not change, not in any cinematic way. The bins still overflowed. The buses still ran late. Her students still turned up to tutorials having only underlined the bits of the score they liked.

But small things were different around the edges.

At a gallery opening a fortnight later, she noticed the same woman with the close-cropped hair and elaborate earrings. Their eyes met across a crowded room. The woman’s shoulders, instead of tightening in the usual “oh god, that’s her” reflex, dropped a fraction. She did not rush over. She finished her conversation, then approached at a pace that would have made a health-and-safety officer proud.

“Good evening,” she said. “No axes tonight?”

“Only the invisible kind,” Lillian replied. “How are your shoulders?”

The woman laughed, genuine.

“Less eager,” she said. “More… accountable.”

At a friend’s birthday party, she overheard a fragment of conversation that made her ears twitch.

“No, I asked myself ‘would the Slow Badger approve?’ and then I slowed down,” someone said, half-joking, half-serious.

She did not turn to see who it was. She did not want to put a face to the nickname. It was enough to know that the story had already shed her real name and grown teeth of its own.

She felt no desire to correct it.

In staff meetings, she found herself deploying the evening’s lessons on a smaller, more mundane scale.

When a colleague began steamrolling a shy PhD student, she cleared her throat and inhaled, slowly, visibly. The impatient colleague, caught in the gravitational field of her deliberate breath, faltered long enough for the student to speak.

In rehearsal, when the band rushed the tempo of a piece, she stopped them, closed her eyes, and let the silence stretch until someone giggled nervously, then relaxed. They played better after that. Not because of fear. Because of consideration.

In her own body, the night had left a residue.

She was more aware, now, of the way people looked at her. Not in the vague, general way of “being noticed”, but in specific currents: admiration, discomfort, desire, curiosity. She noticed when they caught themselves, when they slowed, when they tried not to make her the answer to questions they had not yet phrased.

Sometimes she chose to encourage that restraint with a small nod, a tiny smile. Sometimes she walked away, because being a fetish, even a well-mannered one, was tiring.

She did not seek out another invitation.

If the Committee wanted her again, they could ask. Politely. With clearer job descriptions this time.

She suspected they would, at some point. People who discovered a functional ritual rarely resisted the temptation to repeat it. Perhaps they would adjust. Perhaps they would find other axes, other projections to test.

She did not intend to make a habit of being misread on purpose.

But she also did not regret having spent one night as a living exercise in experimental etiquette.

One evening, months later, Sarah came round for dinner, trailing paint on her jeans and opinions in her wake.

They ate, they argued amiably about a new exhibition, they traded gossip about mutual acquaintances. At some point, Sarah squinted at her over her glass of wine.

“You’re different,” she said. “In a… smug way.”

“I am forty-two,” Lillian said. “I am allowed to be smug about having survived my twenties.”

“No, it’s very specific,” Sarah insisted. “You’re… calmer. Like you know a secret about people that makes them less annoying to herd.”

Lillian considered lying.

“I went to a thing,” she said instead. “An… experiment. People were very strange around me on purpose for an evening. It was oddly educational.”

Sarah’s eyes gleamed.

“Kinky strange?” she asked. “Or art strange?”

“Both,” Lillian said. “And neither. It was about how we behave when we think someone is watching.”

Sarah snorted.

“Mum, people always think you’re watching,” she said. “That’s half your brand.”

“I do not have a brand,” Lillian protested.

“Tell that to the Slow Badger discourse,” Sarah said casually, and took a sip.

Lillian choked on her wine.

“You know about that,” she wheezed.

“I am very online,” Sarah said serenely. “Don’t worry. I only read the posts that are funny or respectful. The rest I block, report, and assign a symbolic detention in my head.”

“You are not to engage,” Lillian said, maternal authority kicking in on reflex.

“I’m not,” Sarah said. “It’s just… sweet, in a weird way. People teaching each other how not to be creepy about you.”

Lillian’s throat tightened.

“Sometimes,” she said slowly, “being mistaken for a fetish is just people trying to find a language for what they wish they could be with each other.”

Sarah considered this, brow furrowing.

“So you’re like… a test pattern,” she said. “If they can be decent about you, there’s hope for the rest of us.”

“I refuse to be anyone’s calibration chart,” Lillian said, but her heart was not in the protest.

After Sarah left, after the dishes were done and the house had settled into the comfortable hush of shared domesticity, Lillian sat at her Yamaha in the studio.

She placed her paws on the black keys and played a slow, stubborn pattern that refused to resolve.

It was not a piece yet. Just a feeling.

In the pauses between phrases, she heard echoes: the held breath of the room, the careful laughter, the murmured thank yous at the door.

She did not write it down.

Not everything needed to be formalised into repertoire or ritual. Some things could remain what they were: a night in a warehouse, a room full of people learning to want slowly, a badger choosing not to correct them.

She smiled to herself.

The next time someone mistook her for a fetish, she thought, she might simply ask them which one.

Not to shame them. To make them say it out loud. To see if, in hearing themselves, they learned to be kinder with their desires.

Until then, she would keep doing what she had always done: taking up space, saying no when needed, letting yes be deliberate.

The night had not changed who she was.

It had only reminded her, vividly, how powerfully other people’s fantasies bent around her shape—and how much gentler the world could be if those fantasies included, at their core, a respect for her silence.

She played another unresolved chord and let it hang.

For once, she did not feel any urgency to complete it.
