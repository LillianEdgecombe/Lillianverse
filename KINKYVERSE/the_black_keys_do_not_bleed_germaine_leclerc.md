## The Black Keys Do Not Bleed

### A Lillianverse Soft-Erotica Novella  
*(in a Germaine Leclerc voice; Primeverse Lillian in a very peculiar salon)*

---

## Chapter I — The Invitation That Knows Her Name

The envelope arrived between a takeaway menu and a charity appeal, which was how it slipped past Miles.

Lillian found it later, when the evening had cooled and the dishes had been dried and stacked and Sarah’s latest message (“send cat pictures, mother, my studio is a concrete tomb”) had been answered with three photos of mugs instead, because the actual cat was asleep on Miles’s chest upstairs and she did not have the heart to move either of them.

It lay on the hallway table like an accusation: heavy, cream, its paper faintly textured under her pads. Her name was written in a hand that did not quite belong to any century she recognised.

Not “Dr Lillian Edgecombe” or “Professor L. Edgecombe”, the hydra-headed titles that congealed around her at the university. Not “Lilli”, which meant the letter belonged to someone who did not know her at all. Not even “Mrs Ferguson”, which would have sent her back down the corridor in search of Miles with amused suspicion.

Just:

> LILLIAN  
> (and the rest of you)

Her whiskers twitched.

“The rest of me?” she murmured, turning the envelope between her paws. The paper smelled faintly of incense and a colder, metallic tang she associated with concert halls and hospitals: polished brass, polished bone.

She slit it open with the back of one claw, careful not to tear what might be, at worst, a bill written by a dramatist.

The card inside was thick enough to be self-confident. Black ink, serifed letters, the kind of typesetting that suggested someone had pointedly refused to use a computer.

> You are invited to a period of residency and curation at the Salon of the Unfaltering Ear.  
>  
> You are requested in your **presence** rather than your résumé.  
>  
> Duration: Nine nights and such days as insist.  
>  
> Location: Disclosed upon acceptance.  
>  
> Compensation: Irrelevant. You will not emerge ruined. Improvement is neither promised nor required. Rearrangement is inevitable.  
>  
> We await your reply.  
>  
> — Management

There was no contact number. No email address. Just a line at the bottom, in smaller print, that read:

> Please do not forward this invitation. It already knows where it is going.

Lillian read it twice. Then a third time, because the word “presence” had hooked somewhere deep in her chest and would not come loose.

Presence, not credentials. Not the usual list: Music professor, forty-two, badger, curvy in a way that made the cheaper end of academia nervous and the life drawing department delighted. Band leader of the kind of ensemble that would be described as “eclectic” by reviewers who were afraid of saying “too much fun”. Potter of lopsided mugs and bowls that nevertheless sat comfortably in the paw. Occasional art model. Married, domesticated in that feral, negotiated way that meant there were equal numbers of Miles’s shirts and her dresses on the chair in the bedroom.

Presence: the part of her that filled a room when she stepped into it, the part she had learned to wield gently as a kindness and sharply as a defence.

She flipped the card over. The reverse was blank, except for a faint watermark: a grand piano in white, its lid open, a black spill of keys inside. The black keys were elongated in the design, gleaming like lacquered teeth.

“Well,” she said to the empty hallway. “That’s not ominous at all.”

The radiator hissed quietly, unimpressed.

She should have shown Miles. Of course she should. They shared everything: bills, playlists, trauma, the ongoing question of whether Sarah was eating actual vegetables or just different forms of potatoes.

Instead, she slipped the card back into its envelope and put it on her pottery shelf in the studio, between a cracked vase and a mug that read “World’s Okayest Tenor Saxophonist” in smudged gold leaf.

She told herself she would mention it over tea, when the kettle boiled and the kitchen smelled of steam and toast and the ordinary agreed to continue being itself.

She did not mention it.

That night, after Miles had fallen asleep with a book on his chest and one paw resting, possessive and unconscious, on the swell of her hip, she lay awake and thought of the phrase “you will not emerge ruined”.

It was not, strictly speaking, reassuring.

The next morning, she found a second card, smaller, tucked under the first.

> We note your acceptance.  
>  
> Your Yamaha is expected.  
>  
> — M.

Lillian stared at it for a long time. The ink was still fresh, as if the card had written itself in the night and then waited, politely, to be discovered.

“I do not recall—” she began, then stopped, because some secret part of her had accepted the moment she saw the word *presence*.

The rest of her caught up.

She dressed in jeans and a plain black shirt, circled her studio once, touching objects in passing: the kiln, the easel, the Yamaha upright in the corner with its familiar scratches and the faint, stubborn smell of old wood and older chord progressions. The piano’s black keys shone under her claws, worn smooth by years of practice and grief and small, private joys.

“We’ve been invited,” she told it. “Apparently you’re expected.”

The Yamaha, being an instrument and not a person, declined to respond. Nevertheless, as she closed the key cover, she felt an entirely ridiculous sense of assent, as if the piano, too, had read the card in the night and concluded that inevitability was as good a reason as any to travel.

She left a note on the kitchen table for Miles.

> Love,  
> Have been asked to help with a private salon residency. Nine days. Probable nonsense, possible art. Taking the Yamaha. Don’t worry; this is not a midlife crisis, it is a midlife comma.  
>  
> Back soon.  
> — L.

She did not write that she had already been accepted without replying. She did not mention the promise of rearrangement. She did not mention that the envelope had addressed itself to the rest of her.

Down on the street, a car waited: sleek, black, the kind of vehicle that made the neighbours’ curtains twitch. The driver held a sign that simply read “LILLIAN (and entourage)”.

She hesitated for four heartbeats—a small, ritual resistance—and then, because the day was grey and her bones hummed with restlessness, she opened the door and stepped into a story that had, apparently, already memorised her.

## Chapter II — The Salon That Listens

The city peeled away in layers of brick and concrete and bureaucratic signage.

Lillian watched from the back seat as familiar streets thinned into industrial estates, as cafes gave way to wholesalers, as the tight, comforting geometry of terraced houses dissolved into wider, more uncertain spaces: warehouses, skeletal cranes, an abandoned toy factory whose painted clown sign had long since cried away its colours.

The car turned down a road that did not exist on her mental map, which annoyed her. She had lived here for years. She taught students who came from every corner of this city. She prided herself on knowing where things were: rehearsal rooms and cheap takeaways, quiet corners of parks where one could rescue a song half-suffocated by marking.

This street was not one of them.

It was narrow but impeccably paved, lined with buildings that had been something else before they were this. She caught glimpses: the bones of a cinema behind a blank façade, the suggestion of a church steeple amputated to make way for flats. The car eased to a stop before a door that had once belonged to a bank and now belonged, at least in brass lettering, to:

> THE SALON OF THE UNFALTERING EAR  
> EST. 1891 / 1974 / REINSTATED WHEN NECESSARY

The dates did not agree with one another. The brass shone without a speck of tarnish. The stone around it bore faint soot marks in the shapes of hands.

The driver—a young fox with a tie knotted so precisely it hurt to look at—opened her door and offered a paw.

“Professor Edgecombe,” he said.

“Just Lillian,” she replied automatically. Titles itched in certain rooms.

“Not here,” he said, with a smile that did not quite reach his eyes. “Here you are precisely as named.”

Her whiskers prickled.

The foyer smelled of polish and old money, but something had been layered over it: incense, perhaps, or the trace of musicians indulging their nerves with cigarettes by the back door. The floor was tiled in black and white, the pattern so intricate it seemed to move if she looked at it too long. A staircase climbed the far wall, its banister carved with scrolling vines and tiny, watchful faces.

At the top of the stairs, double doors stood open. Beyond them, a soft darkness pulsed with muffled acoustics.

“Your Yamaha is being brought up from the van,” the fox said. “She did not enjoy the ramp.”

“She never does,” Lillian murmured, and was secretly relieved that the piano had been recognised as feminine. Instruments had their own stubborn genders. The Yamaha sulked, demanded attention, and bore everything with a kind of chipped dignity that reminded Lillian uncomfortably of herself at nineteen.

“Management is delighted you accepted,” the fox said. “We have had… a long vacancy.”

The way he said “vacancy” made it sound like a wound.

He escorted her up the stairs. The air thickened as they climbed, not unpleasantly, but like a room that had decided to listen very carefully. The sound of her footsteps dulled. Her breathing seemed louder than it should be. Her fur prickled with static, raised not by fear but by attention.

The Salon opened around her like a mouth.

Velvet swallowed the walls: deep red, almost black, gathered in heavy drapes between tall windows. Mirrors hung in gilt frames, but their glass was slightly clouded; her reflection wavered, never perfectly aligned, as if each mirror remembered a different version of her and could not agree on which to display.

A grand piano dominated the centre of the room. Not a Yamaha, not anything so modest. This was a beast of an instrument: glossy, black, its lid propped open to reveal strings like taut nerves. Its bench sat waiting, positioned as though for an audience of invisible judges.

Chairs lined the walls—some upholstered, some bare wood, one throne-like monstrosity with carved lions’ paws and cushions that had seen things. Tables held decanters of water, of something amber, of something almost colourless and faintly viscous. Sheet music lay in loose stacks, notation precise and anonymous.

The Salon listened.

That was nonsense, of course. Rooms did not listen. Rooms had properties, acoustics, a way of being that shaped sound and heat and light. She had lectured on it: the way a hall’s architecture could cradle a cello or murder a violin, how certain frequencies clung to corners.

But as she stood in the centre of the carpet, the room’s silence tilted toward her.

“You are very quiet,” she said aloud.

Her voice did not echo. It sank, gently, into the velvet. The mirrors shivered. Somewhere in the walls, a faint, almost inaudible hum adjusted itself.

The door closed behind her with a soft, final click.

“You have the advantage,” she added, because politeness cost nothing. “You know my name.”

One of the mirrors—an oval one by the left-hand window—caught her eye. Her reflection there was as she had been that morning: hair hastily pinned up, fur brushed but not fussed, jeans that had seen better dye jobs, the familiar curve of her belly outlined by the black shirt.

The mirror to its right showed her as she had been ten years ago: a little slimmer, perhaps, but also more gaunt, tiredness pooled under her eyes, grief still raw in the set of her shoulders. A blur of music stands behind her.

The third mirror offered a version of her she had not yet met: silver streaking her hair more boldly, a softer jawline, a dress that belonged to no item currently in her wardrobe. Her eyes were calm, speculative. There was a ring on her finger that was not her wedding band—a thin band of black metal, as if someone had decided that mourning jewellery could be charming.

Her heart did a small, unhelpful thing in her chest.

“Clever,” she said. “Manipulative.”

A voice, mild and dry, spoke from the corner.

“We find that honesty is an efficient form of manipulation.”

Lillian turned.

A woman stood by the bar: tall, angular, her species difficult to pinpoint at a glance. There was something feline in the tilt of her head, something vulpine in the set of her mouth. Her suit was impeccable, charcoal grey; her shirt, blood-red silk. A name tag gleamed at her lapel.

> M. — MANAGEMENT

“You must be M,” Lillian said.

“I must,” M agreed. “Otherwise the stationery would be exhausting.”

Her smile was small and sharp. Her eyes—amber, decidedly non-human—flicked over Lillian with the practised efficiency of someone taking inventory.

“Thank you for bringing the Yamaha,” M said. “The Salon appreciates personal history. It prefers it to professional reputation. Professional reputation is very noisy.”

“And personal history isn’t?” Lillian asked.

“Personal history,” M said, pouring something clear into a glass, “is at least specific.”

She offered Lillian the drink. It smelled of juniper, and something else: pencil shavings, perhaps, or the inside of a piano.

Lillian hesitated, then took a cautious sip. The gin—if that’s what it was—burned pleasantly. The something else slid down after it, cool and abstract, as if she had just swallowed a metaphor.

“Your role,” M said, “is to inhabit. Curate. Allow the Salon to learn you. In return, it will permit you to learn it. Within limits.” She gestured to the mirrors. “It is an old contract. We have updated the fonts.”

“And what happens to me?” Lillian asked. “After the loitering and the artistic mutual stalking?”

M’s expression flickered. For a moment, the amused professionalism dropped, and Lillian glimpsed something like genuine concern.

“You will not emerge ruined,” M said softly. “We no longer… do that.”

“Rearranged?” Lillian pressed.

“Inevitable,” M replied. “But you are already rearranging yourself all the time, are you not? This is merely… formalised.”

The Salon hummed, almost approving.

Lillian finished her drink and set the glass down with a small, decisive clink.

“Very well,” she said to the room, to M, to the three versions of herself in the mirrors. “Let’s see what you want from me.”

The grand piano, glossy and monstrous, waited.

In the corner, her Yamaha upright sat on its wheeled platform, newly arrived, looking as out of place as a well-loved mug in a museum display case. Its black keys, dulled with age and touch, held their own quiet, stubborn shine.

The Salon exhaled, softly. The residency had begun.

## Chapter III — The Yamaha Among Relics

They brought the Yamaha in like a patient and parked her beside the grand.

Lillian supervised, arms folded, as two porters negotiated the narrow doorway. The instrument protested in the language of squeaking wheels and muted thumps, its weight asserting itself with every step.

“Careful,” she said, aware that she was speaking to both the men and the piano. “She’s been with me longer than anyone else apart from Miles.”

“And does she mind being second?” M asked from the doorway.

“She sulks,” Lillian said. “Then plays better than ever, to prove a point.”

The grand piano glistened under the chandelier, its black lacquer flawless. The Yamaha, by contrast, bore the marks of a life actually lived: a scratch from the time the moving company underestimated a staircase, a cigarette burn from a long-ago bandmate, a faint ring where someone had left a glass during a party and never confessed.

Side by side, they looked like a queen and her scandalous older cousin.

“She clashes,” M observed.

“Good,” Lillian said.

The porters left. The door closed. The room took possession of the new arrival like water accepting a stone.

Lillian sat at the grand first, because that was what was expected. The bench was perfectly positioned. The keys were immaculate, their ivory and ebony polished to an almost obscene gloss. When she placed her paws on them, they felt… generic. Perfect, but in the way of hotel sheets: ironed, anonymous, smelling faintly of nothing.

She played a few scales, then one of the pieces she used when testing the temper of a new instrument. The sound that came out was big—of course it was. The Salon had been built to flatter this kind of instrument, to let it bloom. Notes rose, hung, swelled. The walls drank them in like old wine.

It was technically flawless. It was emotionally hollow.

Her fur crawled.

“You like this one?” she asked the room.

The mirror to her left showed her as she was: serious, intent. The mirror to her right blurred, as if it had turned its head away.

She stopped mid-phrase.

“No,” she said. “I don’t either.”

The silence that followed was not hostile. It was… expectant.

She crossed to the Yamaha.

Sitting at that battered, beloved instrument felt like climbing back into her own skin after a costume party. The bench creaked familiarly. The keys welcomed her paws with their small imperfections, the tiny deviations she could have recognised blindfolded.

She played the same test piece.

It sounded wrong.

Not in the charming way an old instrument might be out of tune with itself, or in need of a sensitive technician. The notes emerged muted, resistant, as if the Yamaha were playing through layers of cloth. Dynamics flattened. The bass, which usually had a warm, growling presence, sounded thin.

The Salon’s velvet walls absorbed the sound and did nothing with it.

Lillian frowned and pressed harder. The piano responded with a kind of offended wheeze.

She stopped, hands resting on the keys.

“Excuse me,” she said—to the piano, to the room, to the history of every space that had ever tried to force a performance out of her before she was ready. “What exactly is going on?”

One of the overhead lights flickered, then steadied. A draught slipped along the floorboards, coiling around her ankles.

M appeared beside the bar, as if she had always been there and Lillian had only just noticed.

“The Salon,” she said, “is withholding response.”

“Is that in the contract?” Lillian demanded.

“The contract,” M said, “assumes that artists will attempt to seduce the room with their usual tricks. The Salon has… learned. It does not wish to encourage a merely aesthetic surrender.”

Lillian’s hackles rose.

“Merely aesthetic,” she repeated. “You do realise that’s my job description.”

“Your job description,” M said, “is a costume. The question is what you look like without it.”

Lillian’s paw tightened on the edge of the keyboard. She felt suddenly, acutely naked, despite her jeans and shirt.

She thought of every concert hall she had ever played in, every classroom, every bar with a sticky floor and a piano that had seen better decades. Places where the room wanted something from her: flash, virtuosity, a respectable grief they could applaud and then go home from.

The Salon wanted something else. Or wanted nothing until she found whatever it was, which was worse.

“You brought me here,” she said.

“We extended an invitation,” M corrected. “You accepted. That distinction matters. Consent is the spine of all good enchantments.”

The comment should not have been erotic. It lodged, nonetheless, somewhere low and molten in Lillian’s belly. The Salon’s attention tightened, like hands resting lightly on her shoulders.

She inhaled, slow. The air smelled of dust and polish and a faint undernote of candle wax. It smelled, intimately, of expectation.

“Fine,” she said. “If you won’t play along, neither will I.”

She closed the Yamaha’s lid with unnecessary care and stood.

“I’ll unpack,” she told M. “And then I’d like to see where you keep the ghosts.”

“We prefer the term ‘precedents’,” M said. “But of course.”

As Lillian left the room, she felt the eyes of the mirrors on her back like fingertips: not touching, not yet, but measuring the distance.

## Chapter IV — Music That Refuses Consolation

Her assigned rooms were an echo of the Salon, scaled down for private consumption.

The bedroom held a bed large enough to fend off nightmares, if size were any protection. Heavy curtains muffled the window. A wardrobe stood open, filled with hangers that seemed smugly certain they would be used. Someone had unpacked her suitcase: her dresses, her jeans, her favourite soft cardigan in dark teal. Her underwear drawer was precisely arranged, which made her feel simultaneously flattered and violated.

“I could have done that myself,” she told the air.

The air did not apologise, but a drawer slid shut more gently, as if in belated discretion.

There was a small sitting room with a chaise longue upholstered in something that looked like velvet and felt like sin, and a bathroom whose tiles were black and white and older than her grandparents. The bath had claws. So did she. This seemed fair.

That first night, she did not sleep much.

She lay in the enormous bed and listened to the building breathe: pipes ticking, wind worrying the windows, distant murmur of conversations she could not quite hear. Somewhere, a piano played a few careful, exploratory notes—a scale abandoned halfway through, like a thought interrupted.

Her body, traitorous and experienced, recognised the signs of ritual space. The Salon was not just a room; it was an arrangement: invitations, clothing, mirrors, a grand piano, a history of women who had been asked to pour themselves out in the name of art while the walls took notes.

There was heat in that recognition, as well as anger. A small, steady pulse between her thighs, a prickle of awareness along her skin, as if the very idea of being watched, measured, expected to bare herself (emotionally, musically, perhaps in other ways if she was not careful) had flipped some switch she had spent years learning to respect.

She turned onto her side, gripped the pillow, and breathed through it.

Consent is the spine of all good enchantments, M had said.

“And bad ones,” Lillian whispered.

By morning, resolve replaced most of the lust, which was perhaps the most middle-aged thing that had ever happened to her.

She dressed in a long black dress that skimmed her curves without apologising for them, a cardigan that could be shrugged off if the room grew too warm, boots that made sensible noises on the staircase.

Back in the Salon, the grand waited, smug. The Yamaha sulked.

“Right,” she said to both of them. “Here’s the deal. I am not here to be improved. I am certainly not here to have my grief prettied up for consumption. If I am going to play for you, you need to understand what that costs.”

She sat at the Yamaha.

She did not begin with repertoire. She began with scales.

Major, minor, modes, slow enough that each note had time to show its face. Her paws moved with practised economy. Her shoulders loosened. The familiar patterns grounded her body even as the room did its best to destabilise her sense of self.

The sound was still wrong: flattened, thinned, as if the Salon were filtering out everything but pitch.

She changed approach.

Instead of trying to bleed into the music, she played dry, almost mechanical exercises: Hanon, Czerny, the kind of finger-twisting drudgery she inflicted on first-year students with the fond cruelty of someone who knew how much it helped later.

Her mind wandered.

To the letter she had not fully explained to Miles. To the way Sarah would roll her eyes and say “of course it’s another cursed building, how else do you spend your holidays”. To the fact that the Salon had history she did not yet know, and women’s names it had written and erased.

She tried, once, to aim directly at the grief she had brought with her: the old, stubborn ache of the child she had not had, the way her body had carried possibility and loss within the same skin. She laid her paws on the keys and reached for that wound the way she had in safer rooms, in private, where she could turn pain into music and music into something more bearable.

The notes emerged clean and empty.

The Salon did not care about grief offered as performance.

“You’re withholding,” she told it. “Fine. So can I.”

She stopped playing elegiac nonsense and let her hands wander into something else entirely: a jagged, stubborn little pattern she did not yet recognise. Short phrases that refused to resolve. Harmonies that rubbed against one another like teeth.

Her shoulders burned. Sweat prickled under her fur. The air around her seemed to thicken, awareness pressing in.

The more she refused to deliver a tidy catharsis, the more the room leaned closer.

“Oh,” she breathed, unexpected amusement and arousal sparking together. “You like refusal.”

The chandelier above her tinkled faintly, though no breeze moved it.

She kept playing.

Time lost its edges. Her back ached. Her thighs complained. The narrowness of the bench, the pressure of her dress against her waist, the friction of fur against fabric—all of it blurred into a single, continuous sensation, as though she were being sanded, gently and insistently, down to whatever raw nerve lay beneath habit.

When she finally stopped, the silence that followed was thick.

The Yamaha’s tone was still wrong. But in the last bars, something had shifted—a resonance she could almost feel in her teeth.

M appeared beside the piano, as if conjured by the absence of sound.

“Better,” she said.

“That was not better,” Lillian replied. “That was a sulk with time signatures.”

“The Salon,” M said, “does not require you to be beautiful. It requires you to be exact. Consolation is a hospitality we no longer offer.”

Lillian’s paws, resting in her lap, curled slightly.

“Fine,” she said again. “Then we are agreed.”

She would not give the room what it expected. She would not strip herself for its benefit, not in the way the mirrors hinted at. If this was to be an intimacy, it would be on her terms: slow, negotiated, with safewords written in key signatures.

The knowledge sat low and hot in her body, its edges softened by a weird tenderness. It was possible, she thought, to be undone by a room and still be held by it.

That, she suspected, was where the real danger lay.

## Chapter V — The History of Adoration

On the second day, M brought her paperwork.

“I thought you said the contract was old,” Lillian said, accepting the sheaf of documents with a sigh.

“Traditions evolve,” M replied. “Liability does not.”

They sat at a small table in the corner, under the eye of a portrait that might once have been a woman and was now mostly frame and attitude. The papers smelled faintly of toner and sandalwood.

The top page bore a title in ornate script.

> THE SALON OF THE UNFALTERING EAR  
> POLICY ON RESIDENCY, ADORATION, AND AFTERCARE

“I refuse,” Lillian said, “to sign anything that uses ‘aftercare’ in a non-specific sense.”

M’s mouth twitched.

“Read first,” she suggested. “Object later.”

Lillian read.

The language was, perversely, reassuring. It listed conditions with bureaucratic precision: the Salon’s responsibility to provide food, shelter, privacy; the resident’s right to refuse any performance, audience, or ritual; the explicit prohibition of physical coercion or unsanctioned magic.

Buried in the middle, like a splinter, was a clause:

> The Salon may, with the resident’s ongoing consent, facilitate encounters between the resident and her own narrative excesses (hereinafter “adoration”). Such encounters shall not be recorded without permission. The resident may terminate any encounter at any time by ceasing to perform.

“That is vague,” Lillian said.

“It is honest,” M countered. “We engineered it after the last disaster.”

Lillian looked up sharply.

“Disaster?”

M’s gaze slid to the portrait above them.

“The Salon,” she said, “was designed by men with good intentions and bad habits. It was built to exalt women’s art while quietly extracting their surplus—grief, desire, intensity—to display for others. They called it inspiration. The women called it being eaten alive.”

The portrait’s painted eyes glittered.

“We changed the policy,” M continued. “We did not destroy the building. Architecture remembers. It has appetites.”

Lillian thought of the mirrors, of the way the room listened when she held herself back.

“So I am here to feed it?” she asked.

“You are here,” M said, “to negotiate with it. You are good at that. Your students survive your classes. Your band still adores you, even when you make them rehearse the coda eight times. You have spent your life learning how to give enough and no more.”

The compliment landed like a hand on her throat: gentle, controlling.

“And what do I get?” she asked, because it was safer than saying what she wanted.

M’s gaze softened.

“You get to decide what your grief is for,” she said.

The words hit harder than any flattery.

Lillian signed.

Later, alone, she explored the Salon’s paper intestines.

In a small archive room off the main hall, she found boxes of programmes and newspaper clippings, invitations yellowed with age. Names repeated: pianists, singers, poets, all women, their portraits staring out from sepia-toned posters with the faintly dazed look of people who had been promised everything and given something else.

Her paws brushed a pamphlet from 1923.

> LADIES’ EVENING OF EXALTATION  
> FEATURING MISS E. HARROW (PIANO)  
> AND OTHER VESSELS

“Vessels,” she muttered. “Charming.”

Marginalia scarred the edges of the programme in a different hand: “Not a vessel. A person. Idiots.”

Another pamphlet, later, bearing the Salon’s logo and an updated aesthetic: minimalist lines, all lowercase, the kind of design that pretended not to be hungry.

The copy read:

> The Salon of the Unfaltering Ear believes in giving women artists a space to fully express their emotions, without judgement.

In the margin, someone had written: “Without control, more like.”

Lillian’s fur bristled.

She found, tucked between two policy drafts, a letter.

> To whom it may concern,  
>  
> Your Salon is an exquisite parasite. You dress consumption as worship and wonder why our teeth ache. You will not have me again.  
>  
> — A former resident

No name. No date. The paper smelled faintly of smoke.

Lillian folded the letter and slid it into her pocket.

Back in the main room, the mirrors caught her reflection: flushed, angry, oddly alive.

“You’re not getting adoration,” she told them. “You’re getting an audit.”

Her body, traitorous, thrummed at the thought of being studied so closely, even if she intended to study back.

She sat at the Yamaha and played nothing for a very long time, her paws resting on the keys like a promise withheld.

The Salon hummed, frustrated and fascinated.

## Chapter VI — Mirrors of Weight and Time

On the third night, the mirrors changed the rules.

Lillian woke with the particular stiffness that came from having fallen asleep in an armchair: neck aching, lower back muttering, all of her aware that she was no longer twenty-five and capable of passing out anywhere.

She had drifted off in the sitting room, fully dressed, after a day of deliberate refusals. She had played scales that went nowhere, fragments of melodies that refused to budge, chord progressions interrupted just as they promised resolution.

The Salon had grown restless. Lights flickered in the corners. The grand’s lid vibrated, slamming shut once in what she could only interpret as a tantrum.

She had smiled, satisfied, and then exhaustion had folded her up like a score.

Now, her mouth tasted of old coffee and defiance. The room was dim, lit only by the streetlamp’s spill through the curtains.

Something was wrong.

She felt it before she saw it: a pressure behind her eyes, a sense of being watched more intimately than the usual, omnipresent attentiveness of the building.

The mirror over the mantelpiece, which had previously behaved itself, was no longer reflecting the room as it was.

Lillian pushed herself upright, joints protesting, and faced it.

It showed her. That was a start.

But not as she was.

In the glass, she sat in the same chair, in the same black dress. Her fur was mussed from sleep. Her eyes were dark. Her body was heavier, not with weight exactly, but with something else: time, perhaps, or knowledge. Lines had deepened at the corners of her mouth in the way they did when she spent a long day listening to students who wanted the world and were already afraid of it.

Her mirrored self’s hands were clasped, white-knuckled, in her lap. A ring glittered on her right paw: black metal, thin, set with a stone the colour of dried blood.

Lillian looked at her own right hand. No ring.

“Options,” she said aloud, because naming them helped: “One, I am asleep. Two, I am hallucinating, in which case the NHS is going to have a field day. Three, the Salon is upping the stakes.”

The mirrored Lillian lifted her gaze.

Her eyes were softer than the real Lillian felt, but there was a sharpness under the softness, like glass under velvet.

When she spoke, her voice arrived with a slight delay, as if coming from a different recording.

“Four,” the reflection said. “You have finally reached the part where you admit that the past and the future are both already in the room.”

“Charming,” Lillian said. “And you are?”

“One of the rest of you,” the mirror replied.

Her tone was familiar: arch, weary, unwilling to let go of humour even when it hurt. Lillian felt a twist of recognition.

“What did they take from you?” she asked.

The mirrored Lillian considered.

“They didn’t take,” she said. “They invited. I offered. That is the unpleasant part. Consent is such a useful word.”

Her gaze dropped to the ring.

“I gave them my grief,” she said. “All of it, in one engineered, perfect performance. I was glorious. It was obscene. They adored me. And afterwards it was… tidy. Manageable. I could speak of it without choking. People said I was inspiring, which is the polite word for ‘you bled appropriately in public’.”

Lillian’s stomach knotted.

She knew that performance. She had seen women do it onstage, in lectures, in support groups: turning trauma into narrative, narrative into spectacle, spectacle into something audiences could congratulate themselves for witnessing.

She had done versions of it herself, smaller, in rehearsals and conversations and once in a cheap bathroom with a stranger who smelled of gin and sympathy.

“And then?” she asked.

“And then,” the reflection said, “the grief I had not named, the parts I did not offer, grew teeth.”

The mirror showed, for a moment, a flicker: the Salon as it had been then. Crowds, all eyes and teeth. The grand piano devouring sound. Women’s hands shaking on keys, on microphones, on the arms of chairs.

M, younger, standing at the back with a stack of policy documents and a face like someone who had realised too late which side of the contract she was on.

“They do not do that any more,” the reflected Lillian said. “But the building remembers the path.”

In the real room, Lillian’s body reacted the way any body did, confronted with danger and desire braided together: her heart sped; heat flooded low and insistent; her skin felt too tight.

The thought of being watched, of being demanded from, of offering herself in some ritual of controlled surrender, was terrifying. It also made her thighs press together.

“You’re not helping,” she told the mirror.

“I am,” the reflection said. “I am what you decided you did not want to become. Negative space is still a guide.”

“And if I fail?” Lillian asked, too quickly. “If I give them the performance?”

The mirrored Lillian smiled, small and brutal.

“Then you will still be you,” she said. “Just rearranged. But you did not drag that obstinate Yamaha up three flights of metaphysical stairs to do what they trained you for. You came here to find a way to play that does not bleed on command.”

The ring on her paw gleamed. The stone was the colour of dried blood, yes—but under the lamplight, it held a vein of something else. Not redemption. Not consolation. Something like iron resolve.

“You could wear this,” the reflection said softly. “You could let them crown you their tragic matron saint. Or—”

She lifted her hand. The ring slid off and dropped, chiming, against the glass.

“—you could refuse the jewellery.”

The ring hit the inside of the mirror’s frame and vanished.

On the mantelpiece, in the real room, something clinked.

Lillian looked down.

A ring lay on the wood: black metal, thin, set with a stone the precise shade of congealed stage blood.

Her breath caught.

“Cute,” she said to the room. “Very theatrical.”

The ring was warm when she picked it up, as if it had just been removed from a finger. It fit her right paw perfectly, of course. Enchantments had no respect for sensible variations in ring size.

She held it, feeling its weight.

She did not put it on.

Instead she slipped it into her pocket, next to the letter from the unnamed former resident.

In the mirror, the older Lillian nodded.

“Good,” she said. “Now go back to the Yamaha.”

“Bossy,” Lillian muttered.

“You like them bossy,” the reflection said. “Don’t pretend you don’t.”

Heat flared embarrassingly in Lillian’s face, because there were entire sections of her marriage she did not intend to discuss with her own potential future.

“Enough,” she said.

The mirror fogged, then cleared. Her reflection was ordinary again: rumpled, tired, very much herself, with no prophetic jewellery.

The Salon’s attention hummed around her like bees.

She stood, straightened her dress, and went back downstairs.

The grand gleamed. The Yamaha waited, sullen and hopeful.

Lillian sat, placed her paws on the keys, and chose, very carefully, not to give the room anything it could frame.

## Chapter VII — Refusal as Composition

The piece began as a list of things she would not do.

She would not write a tidy elegy for the child she did not have.

She would not dramatise the years of appointments and scans and quietly humiliating conversations with doctors who referred to her body as a problem set.

She would not turn her love for Miles into a martyr’s anthem, turning his gentle, practical care into yet another prop in a tragedy.

She would not offer the Salon a climax.

Instead, she wrote around the absences.

Pauses, first: spaces large enough to walk through, the kind of silences that made audiences shuffle in their seats because they did not trust that nothing was happening.

She strung them together with small, stubborn motifs. A handful of notes that refused to resolve, that circled the same question without answering it. Harmonies that edged close to something lush and then backed away.

Her body hurt from the concentration. Her shoulders knotted. Her wrists ached. She found herself breathing with the phrases, shallow at first, then deeper, as if the composition were a form of physiotherapy for parts of herself she had immobilised.

M watched from the corner, silent.

Occasionally, the fox driver appeared with tea. Once, a cat in a waistcoat slipped through with a plate of sandwiches and the kind of glance that said “I have seen residencies devour people alive; I hope you are different.”

The Salon responded not with applause but with alterations.

The lights dimmed when she approached a cliché. The air grew cooler when she tried to slip into comfortable patterns. Once, when she nearly, *nearly* allowed a soaring melody to carry her into catharsis, the bench leg wobbled, and she stopped with a swear and a laugh.

“You are insufferable,” she told the room. “And right.”

That admission was its own kind of surrender.

In the evenings, she wrote the piece down, slowly, in a notebook the Salon had provided: heavy paper, faint lines, a cover embossed with the same piano watermark as the invitations. Her handwriting, cramped from years of marking, stretched under the weight of this new task.

She titled the piece “Black Keys,” as a private joke. The black keys on the Yamaha were where the melodies hid when she was improvising, the half-steps and slips that made things interesting. They were also the keys most likely to stick under a careless paw.

The Salon seemed to approve.

On the fourth night, M intercepted her on the stairs.

“You are starving it,” she said, not entirely displeased.

“I am educating it,” Lillian replied. “There’s a difference.”

M’s gaze dipped, briefly, to Lillian’s throat. Lillian was very aware of the pulse there.

“It is accustomed to banquets,” M said. “You are feeding it something closer to… communion.”

The word shivered through Lillian, conjuring old Catholic childhood echoes: incense, Latin she did not fully understand, bodies kneeling in unison. The idea of communion as shared consumption, shared vulnerability.

“Careful,” she said. “You’ll make me think you’ve done this before.”

M’s smile thinned.

“I have done it badly,” she said. “I am attempting to do it better.”

The admission was a small, precise stroke of intimacy, more potent than any touch.

Lillian wanted, irrationally, to take her hand. To say: I know what it is to be complicit and then try to repair it. I know what it is to be the good girl who handed the knife and then realised it was not only cutting the enemy.

Instead she inclined her head, formal.

“Then let’s both do it properly,” she said.

Back at the Yamaha, she played a sequence of chords that felt, unmistakably, like the bruise of wanting someone she was not supposed to want.

The Salon hummed, delighted.

She did not reward it with anything more explicit. Not yet.

At night, alone in her enormous bed, her body refused to let the arousal dissipate neatly. She lay in the dark, the sheets cool against her fur, and replayed the day: M’s gaze, the weight of the room’s attention, the thrill of holding back.

Her hand slid, inevitably, between her thighs.

It would have been easy to imagine an audience. The Salon, watching. The mirrors, reflecting. M, standing at the foot of the bed, arms folded, expression half appraisal, half concern.

She refused even that.

When she brought herself off, quietly, biting the heel of her paw to keep from making a sound, she imagined only one witness: herself, years from now, unashamed and unframed, affirming that this, too, was part of the composition.

Afterwards, her body softened. Her mind, perversely, sharpened.

Refusal, she thought drowsily, is still a form of engagement. The question is who writes the rules.

The Salon slept uneasily around her.

## Chapter VIII — The Salon Rewritten

The performance, when it came, was not announced.

There were no posters, no programmes. No marketing emails with breathless copy about “an evening of raw emotion”.

Lillian came down to the Salon on the eighth night to find chairs arranged in a semi-circle. Candles burned in wall sconces, their flames steady despite the lack of breeze. The air was thick with expectation and something like incense, though she could not see any burning.

A small audience waited.

They were not celebrities, or critics, or donors. They were… staff. The fox driver, still in his immaculate tie. The waistcoated cat from the kitchen. A cleaner in overalls, hands rough with work. A woman in overlarge sunglasses and a scarf who might have been a former resident, or a ghost, or both.

M stood near the back, arms folded, eyes on Lillian’s face.

“No one asked me if I was ready,” Lillian said, paws damp, heart pacing.

“We assumed you would tell us if you weren’t,” M replied. “Will you?”

Lillian inhaled. The question, so simple, so dangerous, slid under her skin.

Was she ready?

No, of course not. She would never be ready, not in the way performance demanded: perfectly rehearsed, entirely certain, prepared to bleed in neat arcs.

But the piece was written, in the only sense that mattered. Her body knew it. Her hands itched for the keys.

“I will play,” she said.

The audience did not applaud. They settled. They listened.

Lillian sat at the Yamaha.

The grand loomed at her side, amused and slightly offended. She ignored it.

She placed her paws on the keys and began.

The first notes were almost inaudible: a simple, hesitant motif, a handful of black-key intervals that seemed to be searching for a home. The Salon’s acoustics caught them, held them close, offered no embellishment.

She played the pauses as if they were notes.

Silences stretched, deliberate, threaded through the phrases. In each, she felt the audience’s attention sharpen, then begin to waver, then steady as they understood that the nothingness was intentional.

Images flickered behind her eyes, unbidden: hospital corridors, the smudge of ultrasound scans, the strange, humiliating intimacy of being told by strangers what her body could not do. She did not put them into the music as alone, isolated griefs to be admired. She let them be part of the texture, like dissonant chords buried in the harmony.

Her thighs tensed. The weight of her body on the bench, the press of her dress against her belly, the way her breasts moved with each breath—all of it was part of the performance, though no one touched her. Though no one would.

This was the striptease she had agreed to: not of skin, but of self-construction.

She refused the high note.

Twice, the piece approached the kind of soaring, cathartic resolution that would have made audiences weep on command. Twice, she turned aside at the last moment, letting the melody fall into a lower, quieter place.

The Salon shuddered. Literally.

The velvet on the walls rippled. The chandelier’s crystals chimed. A hairline crack appeared in one of the mirrors, running right through a version of her that had once been thinner, prettier, more obviously marketable.

She played on.

At some point, the room stopped being a predator and became a collaborator.

She felt it: the way the acoustics adjusted, offering certain notes more space, absorbing others. Not forcing. Responding. M’s presence at the back of the room, steady as a metronome. The fox’s breath catching at a particularly stubborn unresolved chord.

Lillian reached the final section without quite knowing how.

Here, the temptation to tie everything up was strongest. To give them a last, lingering, consoling motif. To say: it hurt, but look how beautifully we can package it now.

She did not.

The last bars walked a narrow ridge between major and minor, between sorrow and something else that refused to be named. The final chord was neither triumphant nor tragic. It simply… was.

She let it ring.

The sound faded.

Silence unfolded, immense and awkward and sacred.

No one moved.

Then the cleaner, who had seen more mess than any of them, nodded once. The fox’s shoulders slumped, as if a burden he had not known he carried had slid off. The woman in sunglasses took off her glasses, very slowly, revealing eyes rimmed in old, old red.

M exhaled.

“Thank you,” she said.

It was not applause. It was better.

The Salon’s architecture held steady—and then shifted.

Lillian felt it in her bones: walls relaxing, mirrors un-clenching. The oppressive attentiveness eased. The room became just that: a room. A beautiful one, yes, with excellent acoustics and unsettling décor, but not a hungry ritual engine.

The black keys under her paws felt warm and ordinary. They did not bleed. Neither did she.

She stood.

Her knees shook. Her body hummed. There was sweat under her fur, the faint ache of arousal unresolved and unimportant.

M approached.

“We are done,” she said. It sounded like a benediction.

“We are different,” Lillian corrected.

M’s lips curved.

“Yes,” she said. “That, too.”

In the corner, the grand piano sulked. The Yamaha, finally, sounded like herself again.

## Chapter IX — After the Sound Clears

The Salon did not expel her.

There was no dramatic ritual of dismissal, no sudden return to normality. The building simply… stood, unthreatening. The mirrors, when she passed them, showed only her current self: tired, satisfied, still solid.

On the final morning, she woke to find her suitcase packed and a note on the dresser.

> Your residency has concluded.  
>  
> You may return at any time as a guest, not as an offering.  
>  
> — Management

The ring lay beside the note.

She picked it up.

It was cool now, ordinary metal. The stone, once the colour of dried blood, seemed darker, as if it had remembered something and put it away.

She slipped it onto her right paw.

Not as a badge of martyrdom, but as a reminder: she had chosen what to give. She could choose again.

Downstairs, the Yamaha waited on its trolley, strapped and ready, looking grumpy about being moved and yet faintly smug about having outlasted a grand.

M met her at the door.

“Will you tell your husband where you’ve been?” she asked.

Lillian snorted.

“He’s been living with the ghost of this place in our house for years,” she said. “It’s only fair to introduce him to the original.”

M’s gaze softened.

“Come back,” she said. “If you wish. We have other rituals to rewrite.”

“Maybe,” Lillian replied. “I have a band that will sulk if I abandon them. Students who need to be told their feelings are valid but their fingering is not. A daughter who will demand every sordid detail and then pretend she’s too cool to care.”

“Sordid?” M echoed, eyebrow arching.

Lillian smiled, slow.

“I will edit,” she said.

The fox driver opened the car door.

As they pulled away, Lillian looked back.

The brass sign on the door caught the light.

> THE SALON OF THE UNFALTERING EAR  
> EST. WHENEVER WE LEARN BETTER

Someone had scratched the last line in with a key.

The city folded itself back around her. Streets she recognised reassembled. Traffic resumed its indifferent symphony.

Home smelled of coffee and dog and kiln dust.

Miles stood in the doorway, arms crossed, a dish towel in one paw, an expression mixing relief and “we are going to have a conversation”.

“You’re back,” he said, as if she had merely popped out for milk.

“I am,” she said. “The building did not eat me. It just rearranged a few cupboards.”

He stepped forward and wrapped his arms around her, pulling her in. Her body melted against his, that familiar, beloved solidity grounding her more than any spell.

“Good,” he murmured into her fur. “I like your cupboards.”

She laughed into his shoulder, the sound bubbling up from somewhere unknotted.

Later, when the Yamaha was eased back into its corner of the studio, she sat at it and played.

The keys felt right under her paws. The black keys did not cling or resist. They simply waited, as they always had, for whatever she chose to do with them.

She played a few bars of “Black Keys,” testing the way it lived here, in this smaller, messier room with its chipped mugs and drying pots and bills pinned to the wall.

The notes sounded honest.

Grief was still there, of course. It would always be part of the chord structure of her life. But it no longer demanded spectacle.

The ring on her paw caught the afternoon light, its stone a small, dark constant.

She stopped playing and rested her hands on the keys.

In the silence that followed, she heard it—the slightest echo of the Salon, very far away, no longer hungry. A room ventilated after years of incense, windows finally opened.

“All right,” she said, to the Yamaha, to the absent building, to the rest of herself. “We’re not bleeding today. We’re just playing.”

She began again, and this time, the sound did not ask to be anything but itself.
