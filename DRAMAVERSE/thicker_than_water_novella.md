# THICKER THAN WATER
*A Political Drama*

---

## Chapter 1: Rain and Revelations

The rain fell in sheets across the cemetery, each drop a small percussion against the black umbrellas that dotted the hillside like mushrooms after a storm. Lillian Edgecombe stood apart from the cluster of mourners, her crimson lipstick a defiant slash of color against the monochrome tableau. Ten years. Ten years since she'd spoken to any of them, and now here she was, watching them lower Marcus into the ground.

*Marcus, who used to steal her Halloween candy and hide it in his sock drawer. Marcus, who taught her to tie her shoes when she was five. Marcus, who called her a fat disappointment at Christmas dinner in front of the entire family and never apologized.*

The thoughts came in waves, as they always did when she tried to focus on one thing—the way memory and present moment collapsed into each other like waves against a seawall. She was thinking about the way her brother's face had looked in the casket, peaceful in a way he'd never been in life.

The funeral director's voice droned on, something about ashes to ashes, but Lillian's attention was caught by the corporate contingent standing near the front. Board members from Edgecombe Holdings, their faces appropriately solemn, their suits expensive enough to feed a family for months. She recognized most of them from the business pages—vultures in Armani, circling the corpse of her family's empire.

Miles Ferguson stood beside her, his German Shepherd features sharp and alert even in grief. At six-foot-four, he towered over her compact badger frame, but he'd learned long ago to match her pace rather than expect her to match his. His hand found hers, warm and steady, an anchor in the storm of her thoughts.

"You don't have to do this," he murmured, his voice barely audible above the rain.

"Do what?" she whispered back, though she knew exactly what he meant.

"Whatever you're planning. I can see it in your eyes."

She squeezed his hand but didn't answer. How could she explain that she wasn't planning anything yet? That the wheels were only beginning to turn, like the first tumbler falling in a lock she'd been trying to pick for a decade?

The service ended with the mechanical precision of corporate efficiency. People began to disperse, shaking hands and murmuring condolences that sounded more like business negotiations. Lillian watched them go, cataloging faces, noting who spoke to whom, filing away information with the methodical attention to detail that had made her such a formidable investigative journalist before she'd burned out and retreated to her quiet life with Miles.

"Lillian."

She turned to find her sister Victoria approaching, her heels clicking against the wet pavement with military precision. Victoria had always been the efficient one, the one who made the trains run on time and the quarterly reports balance to the penny. Her black suit was perfectly tailored, her blonde hair pulled back in a chignon that hadn't moved despite the wind and rain.

"Victoria." Lillian's voice was neutral, professional. The voice she used with sources who might or might not be lying to her.

"I'm sorry for your loss," Victoria said, and for a moment, Lillian almost believed she meant it. "Marcus spoke of you often."

"Did he?" Lillian raised an eyebrow. "That's interesting, considering he hadn't spoken *to* me in a decade."

Victoria's smile was thin as paper. "Family is complicated."

"Yes, it is."

They stood in silence for a moment, the rain drumming against their umbrellas. Around them, the cemetery was emptying, leaving only the groundskeepers and the lingering scent of expensive flowers.

"The will reading is tomorrow," Victoria said finally. "Two o'clock. Pemberton, Hale & Associates."

"I know."

"You're planning to attend?"

Lillian studied her sister's face, looking for tells the way she used to study interview subjects. The slight tightening around the eyes, the way her fingers gripped the umbrella handle just a little too tightly. Victoria was nervous. Interesting.

"I wouldn't miss it," Lillian said.

Victoria nodded curtly and turned to go, then paused. "Lillian? Whatever Marcus left you... it would be easier for everyone if you just took the money and walked away."

"Easier for whom?"

But Victoria was already walking away, her heels clicking a staccato rhythm against the wet pavement. Lillian watched her go, filing away another piece of information in the growing puzzle.

Miles appeared at her elbow, having given her space for the family reunion. "How did that go?"

"Like a chess match where I don't know all the rules yet," Lillian said. She looked around the cemetery one more time, taking in the Gothic architecture of the mausoleums, the way the city skyline loomed in the distance—all glass and steel and neon bleeding through the rain. "But I'm learning."

## Chapter 2: The Reading

The law offices of Pemberton, Hale & Associates occupied three floors of a glass tower that scraped the belly of the Seattle sky. Lillian had always hated buildings like this—all sharp angles and reflective surfaces, designed to make you feel small and insignificant. The kind of architecture that said *we have money and you don't* in a language everyone understood.

She sat in the mahogany-paneled conference room, her hands folded in her lap, watching her siblings file in like actors taking their places for the final act of a play she'd never auditioned for. Victoria, of course, arrived precisely on time, her efficiency a weapon she wielded with surgical precision. Then came Reginald, the youngest, his designer clothes and carefully tousled hair marking him as the family's designated hedonist. And finally, Charlotte, the middle child, who'd built her political career on a foundation of manufactured empathy and focus-grouped compassion.

*Each sibling represents a moral extreme*, Lillian thought, remembering something she'd read about dramatic structure. Victoria was ruthless efficiency incarnate. Charlotte was populist empathy twisted into manipulation. Reginald was indifferent decadence, floating through life on a cloud of inherited privilege.

And what was she? The prodigal daughter, returned to claim... what, exactly?

The lawyer, a thin man named Pemberton who looked like he'd been pressed between the pages of a law book, cleared his throat and began to read. Most of the will was predictable—charitable donations, bequests to longtime employees, the usual tax-avoidance strategies of the wealthy. But then came the surprise.

"To my sister Lillian Edgecombe Ferguson, I leave my controlling interest in Edgecombe Holdings, consisting of fifty-one percent of all outstanding shares, along with the associated voting rights and board positions."

The silence that followed was so complete that Lillian could hear the rain against the windows forty floors up. She felt the words settle into her consciousness like stones dropping into still water, sending ripples through everything she thought she knew about her family, her brother, her place in the world.

*Fifty-one percent.* Controlling interest. After ten years of exile, Marcus had handed her the keys to the kingdom.

Victoria was the first to speak, her voice tight with controlled fury. "There must be some mistake."

"I'm afraid not," Pemberton said, consulting his papers. "Mr. Edgecombe was quite specific. He also left a sealed letter for Ms. Ferguson."

He handed her an envelope, heavy cream paper with her name written in Marcus's familiar scrawl. She tucked it into her purse without opening it, aware that every eye in the room was on her.

"Well," Charlotte said, her politician's smile never wavering, "this is certainly unexpected. But I'm sure Lillian will do what's best for the family. Won't you, dear?"

There it was—the assumption that she would sell, that she would take whatever buyout they offered and disappear back into obscurity. The assumption that she was still the same broken woman who'd fled the family ten years ago, tail between her legs, after the Christmas dinner incident.

But she wasn't that woman anymore. She'd spent a decade learning to trust her own instincts, to follow evidence wherever it led, to ask the hard questions that made people uncomfortable. She'd learned from the best—Connelly's methodical approach to investigation, Woolf's understanding of the human psyche, Otomo's appreciation for the way power structures shaped the world around them.

"Actually," she said, her voice cutting through the tension like a blade, "I think I'll attend the next board meeting."

The reaction was immediate and electric. Victoria's face went white. Charlotte's smile faltered for just a moment before snapping back into place. Reginald actually laughed, a sound like breaking glass.

"You can't be serious," Victoria said.

"Why not? It's my company now, isn't it? At least, the controlling interest is."

"You don't know anything about running a business," Charlotte said, her voice taking on the patronizing tone she used with voters who asked inconvenient questions.

"No," Lillian agreed. "But I know how to ask questions. And I know how to follow the money."

She stood up, smoothing down her skirt, aware that she was probably burning bridges she might need later. But sometimes you had to burn bridges to keep your enemies from following you across them.

"When's the next board meeting?" she asked Pemberton.

"Friday at ten," he said, consulting his calendar. "But Ms. Ferguson, perhaps you should take some time to consider—"

"Friday at ten," she repeated. "I'll be there."

She walked out of the conference room without looking back, leaving her siblings to stew in their own shock and fury. In the elevator, she finally allowed herself to breathe, to feel the full weight of what had just happened.

*What the hell have you gotten yourself into, Lillian?*

But even as the thought crossed her mind, she felt something she hadn't experienced in years: the thrill of the hunt. The electric anticipation of a story that was bigger than anyone realized, with secrets buried so deep that it would take everything she had to dig them up.

She pulled out her phone and called Miles.

"How did it go?" he asked.

"I just inherited a controlling stake in a multimillion-dollar corporation," she said.

There was a pause. "And?"

"And I think I'm about to find out why my brother really died."

## Chapter 3: The Weight of Water

Miles was waiting for her in the parking garage, leaning against their ten-year-old Honda Civic like a sentinel. The contrast between him and the gleaming BMWs and Mercedes surrounding them was stark—he belonged to a different world, one where value was measured in character rather than quarterly earnings.

"You look like you've seen a ghost," he said as she approached.

"Maybe I have." She handed him Marcus's letter, still unopened. "I can't... would you?"

He studied her face for a moment, then carefully opened the envelope. His expression grew more serious as he read, and when he finished, he looked up at her with something that might have been concern or might have been fear.

"What does it say?"

"You should read it yourself."

She took the letter with hands that were steadier than she felt:

*Lillian,*

*If you're reading this, then I'm dead, and you're probably wondering why I left you the company. The truth is, I've been wondering the same thing for months. Maybe it's guilt. Maybe it's because you're the only one of us who ever had the courage to walk away.*

*But I think it's really because you're the only one who might be able to fix what we've broken.*

*The company isn't what it appears to be. It never was. Dad built it on foundations that were rotten from the start, and we've all been complicit in keeping the rot hidden. Victoria knows. Charlotte knows. Even Reginald knows, though he pretends not to care.*

*I tried to make it right. I really did. But they wouldn't let me. And now I think they're going to kill me for trying.*

*If something happens to me, look into the Cascade Project. Start with the shipping manifests from 2018. Follow the money, Lillian. You always were good at that.*

*I'm sorry for what I said at Christmas. I'm sorry for a lot of things. But mostly I'm sorry that it took dying to find the courage to tell you the truth.*

*Your brother,*
*Marcus*

*P.S. - Don't trust anyone on the board. Not even the ones who seem friendly.*

Lillian read the letter twice, her journalist's mind automatically cataloging the important details. The Cascade Project. Shipping manifests from 2018. The implication that Marcus's death might not have been the heart attack the newspapers reported.

"Jesus," she whispered.

"What are you going to do?" Miles asked.

She looked up at him, this man who'd stood by her through everything—the breakdown, the career change, the long years of rebuilding herself from the ground up. He was asking what she was going to do, not telling her what she should do. It was one of the reasons she loved him.

"I'm going to that board meeting," she said. "And then I'm going to find out what the Cascade Project is."

"This could be dangerous, Lil. If Marcus was right, if they really—"

"Then they need to be stopped." She folded the letter carefully and put it back in her purse. "I spent ten years running away from this family. Maybe it's time I ran toward something instead."

They drove home in silence, through the rain-slicked streets of Seattle. The city looked different to her now, like a film negative where all the shadows had been reversed. Every corporate logo, every glass tower, every billboard advertising the latest consumer necessity—it all seemed connected to something larger and more sinister than she'd ever imagined.

Their house was a small craftsman bungalow in Wallingford, the kind of place where neighbors still talked to each other and kids rode bikes in the street. It had been their refuge for the past five years, a place where they could pretend that the world outside was simple and good and manageable.

But as they pulled into the driveway, Lillian realized that refuge was an illusion. The world had found her anyway, in the form of a dead brother's confession and a corporate empire built on secrets.

"I need to make some calls," she said as they walked up the front steps.

"To whom?"

"Old contacts. People who might know something about Edgecombe Holdings that isn't in the annual reports."

Miles stopped at the front door, his key halfway to the lock. "Lillian, are you sure about this? Once you start pulling on these threads..."

"The whole thing might unravel," she finished. "I know."

"And you're okay with that?"

She thought about Marcus, lying in his casket with that peaceful expression. She thought about Victoria's nervous grip on her umbrella handle. She thought about Charlotte's manufactured smile and Reginald's bitter laughter.

"I'm more than okay with it," she said. "I'm looking forward to it."

## Chapter 4: The Architecture of Power

The Edgecombe Holdings building rose from the Seattle waterfront like a glass and steel monument to capitalism, its forty-seven floors reflecting the gray sky and churning waters of Elliott Bay. Lillian stood on the sidewalk across the street, studying the building with the same methodical attention she'd once brought to crime scenes and political scandals.

The building seemed to pulse with its own malevolent energy, the windows reflecting not just light but the ambitions and corruptions of everyone inside. It was the kind of architecture that made statements about power—who had it, who didn't, and what they were willing to do to keep it.

She'd spent the past three days preparing for this moment. Phone calls to old sources, late nights poring over public records, hours spent in the basement of the Seattle Public Library digging through newspaper archives. The picture that emerged was troubling but not yet complete—a family business that had grown too fast, expanded into too many markets, made too many deals with people whose names appeared in the shadows of other, darker stories.

The Cascade Project remained a mystery. No public records, no mention in any of the corporate filings she could access. But she'd found traces—a shell company registered in Delaware, a series of wire transfers to accounts in the Cayman Islands, a pattern of shipping routes that made no economic sense unless you were trying to hide something.

"You ready for this?" Miles asked. He'd insisted on driving her, though they both knew he couldn't go up to the boardroom with her.

"As ready as I'll ever be."

She kissed him goodbye and crossed the street, her heels clicking against the wet pavement with a rhythm that reminded her of Victoria's military precision. But where Victoria's walk spoke of control and efficiency, Lillian's spoke of something else—determination, maybe, or the kind of focused anger that could move mountains if properly channeled.

The lobby was all marble and chrome, designed to intimidate. A security guard checked her ID against a list, his expression neutral but his eyes curious. She wondered how much he knew about the woman who'd just inherited control of the company, whether the gossip had already started flowing through the building's veins like poison in a bloodstream.

The elevator ride to the forty-second floor felt like ascending through layers of atmosphere, each floor representing another level of power and privilege. By the time the doors opened, she felt like she was entering a different world entirely—one where the air was thinner and the stakes were higher and the rules were written by people who'd never had to worry about paying rent or buying groceries.

The boardroom was exactly what she'd expected—a long mahogany table surrounded by leather chairs, floor-to-ceiling windows offering a panoramic view of the city below. The other board members were already seated, their faces carefully neutral as she entered. She recognized most of them from her research: Harold Pemberton Jr., son of the lawyer and heir to his own legal dynasty; Margaret Chen, former state senator and current lobbyist; David Kowalski, whose construction company had built half the office towers in downtown Seattle.

And at the head of the table, Victoria, her blonde hair pulled back in the same severe chignon she'd worn to the funeral. She looked up as Lillian entered, her smile sharp enough to cut glass.

"Lillian. How good of you to join us."

"Wouldn't miss it," Lillian replied, taking the empty chair at the opposite end of the table. The symbolism wasn't lost on anyone—two sisters facing each other across a battlefield of polished wood and corporate ambition.

The meeting began with the usual corporate theater—quarterly reports, budget projections, discussions of market conditions that sounded more like incantations than actual business. Lillian listened with half her attention, using the other half to study the faces around the table, looking for tells and weaknesses and the kind of micro-expressions that revealed more than words ever could.

Everyone was performing their roles, saying the right things while thinking something entirely different. Like suspects in an interrogation room, all trying to look innocent while hiding their own secrets.

"Now," Victoria said, consulting her agenda, "we need to discuss the transition of leadership following Marcus's unfortunate passing."

"What's to discuss?" Lillian asked. "I own fifty-one percent of the company. That makes me the majority shareholder."

"Technically, yes," Harold Pemberton Jr. said, his voice smooth as silk and twice as slippery. "But ownership and leadership are two different things. The board has a fiduciary responsibility to ensure that the company is managed by someone with the appropriate experience and qualifications."

"And you don't think I'm qualified?"

"With all due respect," Margaret Chen interjected, "your background is in journalism, not business. Running a company like Edgecombe Holdings requires a very specific skill set."

Lillian leaned back in her chair, letting the silence stretch until it became uncomfortable. She'd learned this technique from years of interviewing reluctant sources—sometimes the most revealing information came not from what people said, but from what they said when they thought they had to fill the silence.

"You're right," she said finally. "I don't know much about running a business. But I know a lot about asking questions. And I have some questions about the Cascade Project."

The reaction was immediate and electric. Margaret Chen's face went pale. David Kowalski suddenly found his coffee cup fascinating. Harold Pemberton Jr.'s smooth smile faltered for just a moment before snapping back into place.

But it was Victoria's reaction that was most telling. For just an instant, her mask slipped, and Lillian saw something that might have been fear or might have been rage or might have been both.

"I'm not familiar with any Cascade Project," Victoria said, her voice carefully controlled.

"Really? Because according to the shipping manifests I've been reviewing, we've been moving a lot of cargo under that designation. Cargo that doesn't appear in any of our public filings."

"Perhaps you're misunderstanding the documentation," Harold said. "Corporate accounting can be quite complex for someone without the proper background."

"Perhaps," Lillian agreed. "Or perhaps someone's been cooking the books."

The silence that followed was so complete she could hear the rain against the windows forty-two floors up. Around the table, the board members exchanged glances that spoke volumes about guilt and complicity and the kind of secrets that could destroy careers and lives.

"I think," Victoria said finally, "that we should table this discussion until you've had more time to familiarize yourself with the company's operations."

"I think," Lillian replied, "that we should have a full audit of all company operations, starting with the Cascade Project."

"That's not necessary—"

"I'm calling for a vote," Lillian said, her voice cutting through Victoria's objection like a blade. "All in favor of a comprehensive audit of Edgecombe Holdings' operations?"

She raised her hand. Around the table, the other board members sat frozen, caught between their loyalty to Victoria and their legal obligations to the majority shareholder.

"The motion fails," Victoria said quickly. "We need a quorum to—"

"Actually," Harold Pemberton Jr. said, his lawyer's instincts overriding his personal loyalties, "Ms. Ferguson's controlling interest gives her the authority to order an audit without board approval."

Victoria's face went white with fury. "Harold—"

"I'm sorry, Victoria, but the law is quite clear on this matter."

Lillian stood up, smoothing down her skirt with hands that were steadier than she felt. "Then it's settled. I'll be hiring an independent auditing firm to review all company operations, with particular attention to any projects or activities that aren't reflected in our public filings."

She walked toward the door, then paused and looked back at the table full of shocked faces.

"Oh, and one more thing. I'll be taking over Marcus's office. I assume someone can have his things cleared out by Monday?"

She didn't wait for an answer. She walked out of the boardroom with her head high and her heart pounding, aware that she'd just declared war on her own family and won the first battle.

But as the elevator descended toward the lobby, she couldn't shake the feeling that the real war was just beginning.

## Chapter 5: The Current Beneath

Miles was waiting in the lobby when she emerged from the elevator, his German Shepherd features alert and concerned. He took one look at her face and stood up from the leather chair where he'd been pretending to read a magazine.

"How bad?" he asked.

"Bad enough that I think Marcus was right to be scared."

They walked out of the building together, past the security guards and the marble columns and the corporate art that probably cost more than most people made in a year. The rain had stopped, but the sky remained gray and threatening, like the city itself was holding its breath.

"Tell me," Miles said as they got into the car.

She told him about the board meeting, about the way everyone had reacted when she mentioned the Cascade Project, about the fear she'd seen in Victoria's eyes. As she talked, she felt the pieces of the puzzle beginning to shift and rearrange themselves in her mind, forming patterns that were still incomplete but increasingly ominous.

"So what's the next step?" Miles asked.

"I need to get into Marcus's office. And I need to find someone who can tell me what the Cascade Project really is."

"Someone inside the company?"

"Someone outside it. Someone who might have been watching from the sidelines, taking notes."

She pulled out her phone and scrolled through her contacts until she found the name she was looking for: Danny Reeves, investigative reporter for the Seattle Times. They'd worked together years ago, before her breakdown, and he'd always been the kind of journalist who specialized in following money trails and corporate corruption.

"Danny? It's Lillian Ferguson... Yes, I know it's been a while... Listen, I need a favor."

Twenty minutes later, they were sitting in a coffee shop in Pioneer Square, the kind of place where the coffee was strong and the conversations were quiet and nobody paid attention to anyone else's business. Danny looked older than she remembered, his hair grayer and his face more lined, but his eyes still held the sharp intelligence that had made him one of the best investigative reporters in the city.

"So," he said, stirring sugar into his coffee, "you've inherited the family business."

"Something like that."

"And you think there's a story there."

"I know there's a story there. The question is whether you're interested in helping me find it."

Danny leaned back in his chair, studying her with the same methodical attention she'd once brought to her own sources. "What makes you think I know anything about Edgecombe Holdings?"

"Because you've been watching them for years. I can see it in your eyes."

He laughed, a sound without much humor. "You always were good at reading people."

"So? What do you know?"

Danny was quiet for a long moment, weighing his words. When he finally spoke, his voice was low and careful.

"Your family's company has been on my radar for about five years now. Nothing I could ever prove, just... patterns. Shipping routes that don't make sense. Financial transactions that seem designed to obscure rather than illuminate. Connections to people and organizations that most legitimate businesses wouldn't touch with a ten-foot pole."

"What kind of people?"

"The kind who launder money for drug cartels. The kind who traffic in human beings. The kind who make problems disappear for the right price."

Lillian felt something cold settle in her stomach. "You're talking about organized crime."

"I'm talking about the gray area where organized crime and legitimate business intersect. Where the line between legal and illegal gets so blurred that nobody can tell the difference anymore."

"And you think Edgecombe Holdings is operating in that gray area?"

"I think your brother was trying to get out of it when he died."

The words hung in the air between them like smoke from a gun. Lillian thought about Marcus's letter, about his fear that they were going to kill him for trying to make things right.

"What do you know about the Cascade Project?" she asked.

Danny's expression grew even more serious. "Where did you hear that name?"

"Marcus mentioned it in a letter. Said I should look into it if something happened to him."

"Jesus." Danny ran a hand through his graying hair. "Lillian, if you're asking about Cascade, then you're in deeper than I thought."

"What is it?"

"I don't know. Not exactly. But I've been tracking financial flows for years, and every time I get close to something big, something that might blow the lid off the whole operation, the trail leads back to a shell company called Cascade Development. And every time I try to dig deeper, I hit a wall."

"What kind of wall?"

"The kind that's built by people with enough money and power to make journalists disappear."

Lillian felt a chill that had nothing to do with the Seattle weather. "Are you saying—"

"I'm saying that three reporters have tried to investigate Cascade over the past five years. One of them lost his job. One of them had his house burned down. And one of them..." Danny paused, his face grim. "One of them drove his car off a bridge on a clear, dry night."

The coffee shop suddenly felt too small, too exposed. Lillian looked around at the other customers—a businessman reading the Wall Street Journal, a college student typing on a laptop, an elderly woman knitting—and wondered if any of them were watching, listening, reporting back to someone who might not want this conversation to continue.

"Danny," she said quietly, "I need to know what my family's been involved in."

"Even if it destroys them?"

"Especially if it destroys them."

He studied her face for a long moment, then nodded slowly. "All right. But we do this my way. Carefully. Quietly. And if things get too dangerous, we walk away."

"Agreed."

"There's someone you need to meet. Someone who used to work for your company, until he found out too much and had to disappear."

"Who?"

"A guy named Tommy Chen. Margaret Chen's nephew. He was an accountant for Edgecombe Holdings until about two years ago. Then he quit suddenly, moved to Portland, changed his name. But he's been keeping records. Insurance, he calls it."

"Will he talk to me?"

"He might. If I vouch for you. If you can convince him that you're not part of whatever your family's been doing."

Lillian thought about the board meeting, about the fear in Victoria's eyes, about Marcus's letter and his warning not to trust anyone.

"When can we meet him?"

"Tomorrow. But Lillian..." Danny reached across the table and touched her hand. "Once we do this, there's no going back. Whatever we find, whatever we uncover, it's going to change everything."

She thought about Miles, waiting for her at home. She thought about their quiet life in Wallingford, their small house with its garden and its mortgage and its comfortable routines. She thought about the choice between safety and truth, between peace and justice.

"I know," she said. "But I can't live with not knowing anymore."

Danny nodded and stood up, leaving money on the table for the coffee. "I'll call you tonight with the details. And Lillian? Watch your back. If your brother was right, if they really killed him for trying to expose this... then you're next on their list."

As he walked away, Lillian sat alone in the coffee shop, staring out at the gray Seattle sky and wondering what she'd gotten herself into. But even as fear crept up her spine like cold fingers, she felt something else: the electric thrill of a story that was bigger than anything she'd ever investigated, with stakes higher than she'd ever imagined.

The past and present collapsed into each other, family secrets shaping everything that came after, truth and lies becoming so intertwined that separating them might destroy everything.

But she was going to try anyway.

## Chapter 6: The Accountant's Tale

Portland's industrial district looked like something out of a cyberpunk fever dream—abandoned warehouses and rusted shipping containers, neon signs reflecting in puddles of oil and rainwater, the kind of urban decay that spoke of dreams deferred and promises broken. Lillian followed Danny's directions through a maze of narrow streets, past chain-link fences topped with razor wire and buildings that looked like they'd been designed by architects who'd given up on beauty and settled for functionality.

Tommy Chen lived in a converted warehouse that had been divided into artist lofts, though from the outside it looked more like a fortress than a home. Danny had called ahead, but even so, it took ten minutes of knocking before the door opened to reveal a thin Asian man in his thirties, his eyes darting nervously between Lillian and the street behind her.

"You're alone?" he asked.

"Just me."

"Danny said you were Marcus's sister."

"I am."

He studied her face for a long moment, then stepped aside to let her in. The loft was sparse but clean, with exposed brick walls and industrial lighting that cast harsh shadows across the concrete floor. A bank of computer monitors dominated one corner, their screens filled with spreadsheets and financial documents that looked like hieroglyphics to Lillian's untrained eye.

"Coffee?" Tommy asked, though his hands were shaking too much to pour it steadily.

"Please."

He busied himself with the coffee maker, using the routine to calm his nerves. Lillian waited, letting him set the pace, using the time to study the apartment. The windows were covered with blackout curtains. The door had at least three different locks. This was a man who lived in fear.

"Danny says you want to know about Cascade," Tommy said finally, handing her a mug.

"I want to know what my family's been doing."

"Your family's been laundering money for some very bad people."

The words hit her like a physical blow, even though she'd been expecting something like this. Hearing it confirmed was different from suspecting it.

"How long?" she asked.

"At least ten years. Maybe longer. It started small—a few questionable transactions, some creative accounting to minimize tax liability. But it grew. And grew. Until it became the primary business model."

Tommy walked over to his computer setup and pulled up a series of spreadsheets. The numbers meant nothing to Lillian, but the patterns were clear enough—money flowing in from sources that weren't identified, flowing out to destinations that were equally mysterious.

"The legitimate businesses—the fisheries, the shipping, the media properties—they're all fronts now," Tommy continued. "They generate just enough legitimate revenue to provide cover for the real operation."

"Which is?"

"Moving money for drug cartels, human traffickers, arms dealers. Anyone who needs to clean dirty money and doesn't mind paying a premium for the service."

Lillian felt sick. "And everyone knew? Victoria, Charlotte, Reginald?"

"Victoria runs the operation. Charlotte provides political cover—she's got connections in state government, federal agencies, law enforcement. Reginald... Reginald just spends the money and tries not to think about where it comes from."

"What about Marcus?"

Tommy's expression softened slightly. "Marcus tried to stop it. About two years ago, he started asking questions, demanding to see the real books. He threatened to go to the FBI if Victoria didn't shut down the laundering operation."

"What happened?"

"Victoria convinced him to give her time to wind things down gradually. Said it would be too dangerous to stop all at once, that their clients weren't the kind of people who accepted sudden changes in business relationships."

"But she didn't wind it down."

"She expanded it. And when Marcus found out..." Tommy shrugged. "Well, you know what happened to Marcus."

Lillian set down her coffee mug with hands that weren't quite steady. "You're saying they killed him."

"I'm saying he had a heart attack at age forty-five, with no history of heart disease, three days after he told Victoria he was going to the FBI with or without her cooperation."

The silence that followed was heavy with implication. Lillian thought about Marcus's letter, about his fear that they were going to kill him. She thought about the peaceful expression on his face in the casket, and wondered if it was really peace or just the absence of struggle.

"Why didn't you go to the police?" she asked.

Tommy laughed bitterly. "With what? Suspicions? Theories? The kind of people your family works with, they don't leave evidence lying around. And they have friends in law enforcement, friends who make sure that inconvenient investigations get buried."

"But you kept records."

"Insurance. In case they decided I knew too much."

He pulled up another set of files on his computer—bank records, shipping manifests, correspondence between Edgecombe Holdings and a network of shell companies that stretched across three continents.

"This is everything I could gather before I had to run," he said. "Names, dates, amounts, routing numbers. Enough to bring down the whole operation, if it ever gets into the right hands."

"The FBI?"

"Maybe. If you can find someone who isn't already bought and paid for."

Lillian studied the documents, her journalist's mind automatically cataloging the information, looking for patterns and connections. It was overwhelming—a web of corruption that stretched far beyond her family's company, involving politicians and law enforcement officials and business leaders whose names she recognized from the society pages.

"Tommy," she said quietly, "I need copies of all this."

"Why? What are you planning to do?"

"I'm planning to finish what Marcus started."

Tommy stared at her for a long moment, then shook his head. "You don't understand what you're up against. These people don't just destroy careers or reputations. They destroy lives. They make people disappear."

"Then why are you still alive?"

"Because I ran. Because I disappeared. Because I've been very, very careful not to attract their attention."

"But you're willing to help me."

"I'm willing to give you the information. What you do with it is your choice. But Lillian..." He leaned forward, his eyes intense. "If you do this, if you really try to expose them, you need to understand that there's no going back. They'll come after you with everything they have. And they have a lot."

Lillian thought about Miles, about their quiet life in Seattle, about the choice between safety and justice. She thought about Marcus, lying in his grave with secrets that might never be told if she walked away now.

"I understand," she said.

Tommy nodded slowly, then began copying files to a series of encrypted flash drives. As he worked, Lillian found herself thinking about the nature of family, about the bonds that tied people together and the secrets that tore them apart. Family relationships were like rivers, flowing beneath the surface of daily life, shaping everything even when you couldn't see them.

But some rivers carried poison.

"There's something else," Tommy said as he handed her the flash drives. "Something I haven't told Danny or anyone else."

"What?"

"Your brother wasn't the only one who tried to stop this. There was someone else, someone on the outside who was getting close to the truth."

"Who?"

"A federal prosecutor named Sarah Martinez. She was building a case against several money laundering operations in the Pacific Northwest. Edgecombe Holdings was on her list."

"Was?"

"She died in a car accident six months ago. Single vehicle, late at night, no witnesses. Just like the journalist Danny told you about."

Lillian felt the walls of the loft closing in around her. "How many people have they killed?"

"I don't know. But I know they won't hesitate to kill more if they think it's necessary."

She stood up, tucking the flash drives into her purse. "Thank you, Tommy. For the information, for the risk you're taking."

"Don't thank me yet. Thank me if you're still alive in six months."

As she walked back to her car through the maze of industrial streets, Lillian felt the weight of the flash drives in her purse like stones in her pocket. She had the evidence now, the proof of what her family had been doing. But she also had a target on her back, and the knowledge that the people she was up against had killed before and wouldn't hesitate to kill again.

This was the point in every investigation where you realize that the case is bigger and more dangerous than you thought, where you have to decide whether to push forward or walk away.

But she'd already made her decision. She was going to push forward, no matter what the cost.

The question was whether she'd live long enough to see justice done.

## Chapter 7: The Glass House

The Edgecombe Holdings building looked different at night—less like a monument to capitalism and more like a predator crouched on the waterfront, its windows glowing with the cold light of fluorescent bulbs and computer screens. Lillian sat in her car across the street, watching the late-shift security guards make their rounds, timing their patterns with the methodical precision of someone planning a break-in.

Which, she supposed, was exactly what she was doing.

She'd spent the past week studying the building's security systems, using her new position as majority shareholder to request access codes and floor plans under the guise of familiarizing herself with company operations. The security chief, a former police detective named Rodriguez, had been helpful and professional, never suspecting that she was planning to use the information against the company she now controlled.

Corporate power structures created their own vulnerabilities—the very systems designed to protect the powerful could be turned against them.

Her phone buzzed with a text from Miles: *Are you sure about this?*

She typed back: *No. But I'm doing it anyway.*

*Be careful. I love you.*

*Love you too.*

She put the phone away and checked her watch. 11:47 PM. The night security shift would be making their rounds on the upper floors, leaving the lobby and lower levels relatively unguarded for the next twenty minutes. It was now or never.

She crossed the street with the confident stride of someone who belonged there, her heels clicking against the wet pavement in a rhythm that spoke of purpose rather than stealth. The security guard at the front desk looked up as she approached, his expression shifting from boredom to attention.

"Ms. Ferguson? Working late tonight?"

"Couldn't sleep," she said, flashing him a smile. "Thought I'd come in and get familiar with my brother's office."

"Of course. You'll need to sign in."

She signed the visitor log with a flourish, aware that she was creating a paper trail but knowing that it couldn't be helped. Sometimes the best way to hide was in plain sight.

The elevator ride to the forty-second floor felt like ascending through layers of guilt and complicity. Each floor represented another level of the conspiracy, another group of people who'd chosen profit over principle, money over morality. By the time the doors opened, she felt like she was entering the heart of darkness itself.

Marcus's office was at the end of a long hallway lined with portraits of previous company executives—stern-faced men in expensive suits who stared down at her with expressions of disapproval and judgment. The office door was locked, but her new access codes worked perfectly, the electronic lock disengaging with a soft click that sounded unnaturally loud in the empty hallway.

The office was exactly what she'd expected—mahogany desk, leather chairs, floor-to-ceiling windows offering a panoramic view of the city below. But it was also strangely impersonal, as if Marcus had been careful not to leave too much of himself in a place where he couldn't trust the walls.

She went straight to the desk, pulling out the flash drives Tommy had given her and plugging one into Marcus's computer. The files opened like a roadmap to hell—bank records, shipping manifests, correspondence that detailed a decade of criminal activity with the casual efficiency of a grocery list.

But she wasn't here just to review what she already knew. She was here to find the missing pieces, the connections that would tie everything together in a way that even the most corrupt prosecutor couldn't ignore.

She started with the desk drawers, searching for anything that might have been overlooked by whoever had cleaned out Marcus's personal effects. Most of the drawers were empty, but in the bottom drawer of the filing cabinet, hidden behind a stack of old annual reports, she found what she was looking for: a manila envelope marked "Insurance" in Marcus's handwriting.

Inside were photocopies of documents that made Tommy's files look like a children's book. Bank records showing transfers of hundreds of millions of dollars. Shipping manifests for cargo that officially didn't exist. And most damning of all, a series of emails between Victoria and someone identified only as "The Client," discussing the elimination of "problems" and the need for "permanent solutions."

One email in particular made her blood run cold:

*V—The journalist is getting too close. Recommend immediate action. Make it look accidental. —TC*

*TC—Agreed. Will handle personally. What about the prosecutor? —V*

*V—Already taken care of. Car accident, very tragic. —TC*

*TC—Good. What about your brother? He's becoming a liability. —V*

*V—I'm working on it. Family is always more complicated. —TC*

*TC—Not that complicated. Heart attacks happen all the time. —V*

Lillian felt sick as she read the exchange, dated just two weeks before Marcus's death. Here was proof—not just of the money laundering, but of murder. Multiple murders, carried out with the casual efficiency of a business transaction.

She photographed each document with her phone, then copied the files to another flash drive. As she worked, she became aware of a sound in the hallway—footsteps, moving with the measured pace of someone trying not to be heard.

She froze, listening. The footsteps stopped outside Marcus's office.

*Shit.*

She quickly closed the files and shut down the computer, then moved to the window, looking for another way out. But forty-two floors up, there was nowhere to go except down, and that wasn't an option unless she wanted to end up like the journalist and the prosecutor.

The door handle turned slowly, and she realized with a sinking heart that whoever was out there had an access code too.

The door opened to reveal Victoria, her blonde hair perfect despite the late hour, her expression cold as winter steel. Behind her stood a man Lillian didn't recognize—tall, broad-shouldered, with the kind of face that suggested violence was just another tool in his professional toolkit.

"Hello, sister," Victoria said, stepping into the office. "Working late?"

"I could ask you the same thing."

"I could say I was worried about you. Coming to the office at midnight, all alone. It's not safe, you know. Terrible things can happen to people who wander around empty buildings at night."

The man behind Victoria closed the door and positioned himself in front of it, making it clear that leaving wasn't an option. Lillian felt her heart rate spike, but she forced herself to remain calm, to think like the investigative journalist she'd once been.

"What do you want, Victoria?"

"I want you to stop playing detective. I want you to sell your shares and disappear back to your quiet little life with your husband. I want you to forget about Marcus and his paranoid fantasies."

"And if I don't?"

Victoria's smile was sharp enough to cut glass. "Then you'll join him."

The threat hung in the air between them like smoke from a gun. Lillian thought about the emails she'd just read, about the casual way Victoria had discussed murdering their own brother.

"You killed him," she said. "You killed Marcus."

"Marcus killed himself. He just didn't realize it at the time."

"And the journalist? The prosecutor?"

"Occupational hazards. They chose to stick their noses where they didn't belong."

Victoria moved closer, her heels clicking against the hardwood floor with the same military precision Lillian remembered from childhood. But there was something different about her now, something that spoke of lines crossed and boundaries erased.

"You see, Lillian, you never understood how the world really works. You always thought there were rules, that good and evil were clearly defined, that justice would prevail in the end. But the world isn't a newspaper story with a neat beginning, middle, and end. It's a business, and business is about profit."

"Even if it means murder?"

"Especially if it means murder. Do you have any idea how much money flows through our operation? How many people depend on us for their livelihoods? How many politicians and law enforcement officials we keep on our payroll?"

Victoria was close enough now that Lillian could smell her perfume—something expensive and cloying that reminded her of funeral flowers.

"We're not criminals, Lillian. We're entrepreneurs. We provide a service that people need, and we do it more efficiently than anyone else. The fact that some people get hurt along the way... well, that's just the cost of doing business."

"And what about family? What about loyalty?"

"Family is a luxury we can't afford. Loyalty is a weakness that gets people killed."

Victoria nodded to the man by the door, who began moving toward Lillian with the purposeful stride of someone who'd done this before. But as he reached for her, Lillian did something that surprised everyone in the room, including herself.

She laughed.

"What's so funny?" Victoria demanded.

"You are. All of you. You think you're so smart, so powerful, so untouchable. But you made one crucial mistake."

"Which is?"

"You assumed I came here alone."

As if on cue, the office door burst open to reveal Danny Reeves and two FBI agents, their weapons drawn and their faces grim. The man who'd been advancing on Lillian froze, his hand halfway to his jacket.

"FBI!" one of the agents shouted. "Nobody move!"

Victoria's face went white with shock and rage. "How—"

"Insurance," Lillian said, pulling the wire from under her shirt. "I've been recording everything for the past ten minutes. Your confession, your threats, your casual discussion of multiple murders. It's all on tape."

The next few minutes were a blur of handcuffs and Miranda rights and the kind of controlled chaos that comes when a carefully constructed criminal enterprise suddenly collapses. Victoria was led away in handcuffs, her perfect composure finally cracking as she realized that her world was ending.

As the FBI agents processed the scene, Danny appeared at Lillian's elbow.

"You okay?" he asked.

"I will be."

"That was incredibly dangerous, what you did."

"I know. But it was the only way to get them to confess. They never would have admitted to murder if they thought anyone else was listening."

Danny nodded, then looked around the office with its mahogany furniture and panoramic views. "So what happens now?"

Lillian thought about the question, about the company she now controlled and the employees who depended on it for their livelihoods. She thought about the legitimate businesses that had been corrupted by greed and the communities that had been harmed by her family's crimes.

"Now," she said, "we clean house. We shut down the criminal operations, cooperate fully with the investigation, and try to salvage what's left of the legitimate businesses."

"And your family?"

"My family made their choices. Now they'll live with the consequences."

As she walked out of the building for what she knew would be the last time, Lillian felt something she hadn't experienced in years: the satisfaction of a job well done, a story told, a truth revealed. It wasn't the ending she'd hoped for when this all began, but it was the ending that justice demanded.

Family secrets shaped everything that came after, the past and present collapsing into each other, truth and lies becoming so intertwined that you couldn't separate them without destroying everything.

But sometimes destruction was necessary. Sometimes you had to burn down the house to save the foundation.

## Chapter 8: The Reckoning

Six months later, Lillian stood on the same pier where she'd played as a child, watching the ferries cross Elliott Bay in the gray morning light. The water was choppy, stirred by a wind that carried the scent of rain and salt and the promise of storms to come. Behind her, the city stretched toward the mountains, its glass towers reflecting the clouded sky like mirrors in a funhouse.

The trials were over. Victoria had been sentenced to twenty-five years for conspiracy, money laundering, and three counts of murder. Charlotte had received fifteen years for her role in providing political cover for the operation. Reginald, who'd cooperated with prosecutors in exchange for a reduced sentence, would serve five years in minimum security—a slap on the wrist that still felt like justice of a sort.

The company was gone, dissolved and its assets distributed to victims of the crimes it had facilitated. The legitimate businesses had been sold to competitors who promised to maintain the jobs and honor the contracts. It wasn't perfect, but it was better than letting the corruption continue.

"Penny for your thoughts," Miles said, appearing beside her with two cups of coffee from the vendor at the end of the pier.

"Just thinking about endings," she said, accepting the coffee gratefully. "How they're never as clean as you want them to be."

"Any regrets?"

She considered the question, watching a seagull dive for fish in the choppy water. "About exposing them? No. About the way it had to happen? Maybe."

"They made their choices, Lil. You just made sure they faced the consequences."

"I know. But they were still family."

Miles was quiet for a moment, understanding that some wounds took time to heal, that some betrayals cut deeper than others.

"What's next?" he asked.

"I've been thinking about that. Danny offered me a job at the Times. Investigative reporting, following the money, exposing corruption. It's what I used to do, what I was good at."

"And?"

"And I think I might take it. The world needs people who are willing to ask hard questions, to follow the evidence wherever it leads, to speak truth to power even when it's dangerous."

"Even when it means making enemies?"

"Especially then."

They stood in comfortable silence, watching the city wake up around them. Somewhere in those glass towers, other conspiracies were being hatched, other crimes were being planned, other families were being torn apart by greed and ambition. But there were also people like Danny, like the FBI agents who'd risked their careers to pursue justice, like Tommy Chen who'd kept records even when it put his life in danger.

Justice was never perfect, never complete, but still worth pursuing. You had to keep fighting even when you knew you couldn't win every battle.

"There's something else," she said. "I've been thinking about writing a book. About the investigation, about what we uncovered, about the way corporate crime hides in plain sight."

"A memoir?"

"More than that. A guide, maybe. A roadmap for other people who find themselves in similar situations. How to follow the money, how to build a case, how to stay alive when powerful people want you dead."

Miles smiled. "Psychological depth meets procedural investigation?"

"Something like that."

Her phone buzzed with a text message. She glanced at it and felt her pulse quicken.

"What is it?" Miles asked, seeing her expression.

"Danny. He says there's something I need to see. Something about a shipping company in Tacoma that might be connected to human trafficking."

"Another story?"

"Another story."

She looked out at the water one more time, thinking about the currents that ran beneath the surface, the forces that shaped the world in ways most people never saw or understood. Then she turned and walked back toward the city, toward the next investigation, toward the next truth that needed to be told.

The story never really ended, just transformed into something new. The city kept growing and changing, creating new forms of corruption and new opportunities for justice.

Behind her, the pier stretched out into the gray water like a bridge between past and future, between the family she'd lost and the work she'd found. The wind picked up, stirring the waves and carrying the scent of rain.

It was going to be a long day. But she was ready for it.

## Epilogue: The View from Here

*One year later*

The book launch was held at Elliott Bay Book Company, in the same neighborhood where Lillian had grown up, where she'd first learned to love stories and the power they held to change the world. The reading room was packed with journalists, activists, law enforcement officials, and ordinary citizens who'd been following the Edgecombe Holdings case in the news.

"*Thicker Than Water: A Family's Fall from Grace*" had been on the bestseller list for six weeks, praised by critics as both a gripping true crime narrative and a practical guide to investigating corporate corruption. More importantly, it had inspired a new generation of investigative journalists and citizen activists to start asking hard questions about the companies and institutions that shaped their lives.

Lillian stood at the podium, looking out at the crowd of faces—some familiar, some strange, all united by a shared belief that truth mattered more than comfort, that justice was worth pursuing even when it came at a personal cost.

"When I first inherited my family's company," she began, "I thought I was getting a gift. What I actually got was a responsibility—the responsibility to confront the truth about who we were and what we'd done, no matter how painful that truth might be."

She talked about the investigation, about the people who'd helped her and the people who'd tried to stop her. She talked about the importance of following the evidence, of asking uncomfortable questions, of refusing to accept easy answers when the stakes were high.

"Corporate crime thrives in darkness," she said. "It depends on complexity and confusion, on the assumption that ordinary people can't understand financial documents or shipping manifests or the intricate web of shell companies that hide criminal activity. But that assumption is wrong. Anyone can learn to follow the money. Anyone can learn to ask the right questions. Anyone can learn to shine a light into the darkness."

During the Q&A that followed, a young woman in the back row raised her hand.

"Ms. Ferguson, do you ever regret exposing your family? Do you ever wish you'd just taken the money and walked away?"

Lillian thought about the question, about the nights she'd lain awake wondering if she'd made the right choice, about the family gatherings that would never happen again, about the relationships that had been severed forever.

"I regret that it was necessary," she said finally. "I regret that my family chose profit over principle, that they chose to hurt people rather than help them. But I don't regret exposing the truth. The truth is never the enemy, even when it's painful."

After the reading, she signed books and talked with readers, each conversation a reminder of why the work mattered. A prosecutor from Portland who was using her techniques to investigate a suspected money laundering operation. A journalist from Phoenix who was following a similar trail of corporate corruption. A college student who was writing her thesis on the intersection of family dynamics and white-collar crime.

As the crowd began to thin, Danny appeared at her elbow.

"Good turnout," he said.

"Better than I expected."

"Ready for the next one?"

She looked at him curiously. "The next what?"

"Investigation. I've been tracking a pattern of suspicious real estate transactions in Vancouver. Shell companies, offshore accounts, the whole nine yards. Looks like it might be connected to Chinese organized crime."

Lillian felt the familiar electric thrill of a new story, a new mystery to unravel. "When do we start?"

"Tomorrow, if you're interested."

She thought about Miles, waiting for her at home with dinner and a bottle of wine and the quiet comfort of their life together. She thought about the risks and the dangers and the possibility that this next investigation might be even more dangerous than the last one.

But she also thought about the victims—the people whose lives had been destroyed by the crimes she'd helped expose, the families who'd been torn apart by violence and greed, the communities that had been poisoned by corruption.

"I'm interested," she said.

As she walked home through the Seattle streets, past the coffee shops and bookstores and the small businesses that made up the fabric of the city, Lillian reflected on the strange turns her life had taken. A year ago, she'd been a quiet housewife living in obscurity. Now she was a bestselling author and investigative journalist, with enemies in high places and a target on her back that would probably never disappear completely.

Identity was fluid—people became different versions of themselves in response to circumstances, the past and present and future all existing simultaneously in human consciousness.

But she was also thinking about the principle that everybody counts or nobody counts—that justice wasn't just for the wealthy and powerful, but for everyone who'd been hurt by those who thought they were above the law.

And she was thinking about how technology and corporate power created new forms of oppression but also new opportunities for resistance, where ordinary people could fight back against systems that seemed too big and too powerful to defeat.

The rain had started again, the kind of gentle Seattle drizzle that made everything look soft and dreamlike. She pulled her coat tighter and quickened her pace, eager to get home to Miles and the warmth of their small house.

But even as she walked toward the comfort of home and family, part of her mind was already working on the next story, the next investigation, the next truth that needed to be told. Because that's what journalists did—they asked questions, they followed the evidence, they shined lights into dark places.

And sometimes, if they were very lucky and very careful and very persistent, they changed the world.

*The End*

**Author's Note:**

*Thicker Than Water* explores the intersection of family loyalty and moral responsibility, examining how corporate power structures can corrupt even the most intimate relationships. The novella combines methodical investigative procedures with psychological depth and urban dystopian elements, asking difficult questions about complicity, justice, and the price of truth.

The story suggests that corruption thrives not just in the shadows, but in the gray areas where legitimate business and criminal enterprise intersect, where family bonds become tools of manipulation, and where the pursuit of profit overrides basic human decency. But it also suggests that ordinary people have the power to fight back—through careful investigation, moral courage, and the willingness to sacrifice comfort for justice.

In the end, *Thicker Than Water* is a story about choosing truth over family, justice over safety, and the difficult but necessary work of holding the powerful accountable for their crimes.
