# Songs We Used to Sing

*A Lillianverse Story*

---

## Chapter One: The Weight of Keys

The rain hadn't stopped since morning, and Lillian Edgecombe stood outside the old terraced house in Leith, Edinburgh, watching water cascade from gutters that had seen better decades. Her badger paws, thick and capable, turned the key over and over in her palm—her mother's key, the one that had arrived by post with a note so spare it could have been a telegram: "In case you ever come home."

That was nearly a year ago, before the accident that wasn't really an accident but a slow-motion catastrophe of neglect and stubbornness. Before the funeral where she'd stood at the back, her black coat too tight across her shoulders, watching strangers mourn a woman they'd known better than her own daughter had. Before Lillian's own world had cracked open like an egg, spilling out all the unresolved words that now had nowhere to go.

The door was the same chipped blue she remembered, though the paint clung with less conviction now. She'd sneaked out of this door at seventeen, clutching her guitar and a crumpled paper bag of leftover scones, thinking she was escaping toward something magnificent. Now, at forty-two, she understood that most escapes were really just elaborate forms of circling back.

She rang the bell twice before remembering she had the key. Old habits, her mother would have said, die harder than old mothers.

Inside, the house exhaled lavender and black tea, the scent so familiar it made her chest tight. The wallpaper—roses on cream, faded to the color of old photographs—clung to the walls with what Nora Ephron might have called the desperation of memory, though Lillian thought it looked more like stubbornness. Her mother had been stubborn about everything: the wallpaper, the tea service that came out only for company that never came, the way she'd refused to acknowledge that her daughter had become someone she didn't recognize.

In the sitting room, dust motes danced in the afternoon light, settling on photo frames that hadn't been touched in months. Her parents' wedding—her father, a gentle badger with kind eyes, her mother, a sleek otter with ambitions that had curdled into resentment. A blurry Polaroid of Lillian at a school recital, age twelve, her mouth open in song, her eyes already looking somewhere else. A newer photo she didn't recognize—a cousin's child, maybe, or a neighbor's grandchild. Life had gone on here, even if she hadn't been part of it.

She sank onto the old couch, and the springs groaned like old bones. The silence wasn't empty—it was expectant, waiting for her to fill it with something other than regret. Outside, the rain kept falling, and inside, for the first time in months, Lillian finally let herself breathe.

But breathing, she discovered, was only the first step. The hard part was figuring out what came next.

## Chapter Two: The Archaeology of Morning

Lillian hadn't been to a local shop in years, not since she'd moved to London and discovered that you could have anything delivered, that human contact could be reduced to a polite nod to the delivery driver. But Edinburgh mornings demanded a different kind of participation, and she found herself walking the streets of her childhood like an archaeologist, cataloging what had survived and what had been buried under progress.

The bakery two blocks down—gone. The marmot couple who'd run it, who'd made bannocks that could cure homesickness and heartbreak in equal measure, had been replaced by a sleek café where the coffee came in mason jars and the baristas looked like they'd been cast for their cheekbones. She stood outside for a moment, watching young professionals tap their phones while waiting for drinks that cost more than her mother used to spend on groceries for a week.

Instead, she found herself in a tiny grocer's tucked between a laundrette and a betting shop, the kind of place that existed in the margins of gentrification. Hand-written price tags, jars of rhubarb jam with curling labels, a proprietor who looked up from his newspaper and nodded as if her presence was neither surprising nor unwelcome.

She picked up a basket—wicker, worn smooth by countless hands. Apples, their skin still bearing the honest imperfections of actual orchards. A head of cabbage, solid and unpretentious. Guinness, out of habit more than desire. A loaf of bread that felt substantial in her paws, the kind that required actual chewing.

It wasn't much, but it felt like something. She needed something.

Back at the house, she set everything on the kitchen counter and surveyed her domain. The kettle was rusted at the spout, the fridge hummed with the labored breathing of old age, and the whole house leaned into its oldness like a comfortable chair. But when she opened the window, the damp Edinburgh air swept through, pushing out the stillness and bringing with it the sounds of a city that had learned to accommodate both its history and its ambitions.

That night, she sat at the kitchen table with a plate of bread and cheese and a half-empty glass of stout. She'd found a small notebook in one of the drawers—her mother's handwriting on the first few pages, shopping lists and phone numbers for people who were probably dead now. Lillian turned to a clean page and wrote three words: "What comes next?"

Then she crossed them out and wrote: "What came before?"

Then she crossed that out too and just sat there, listening to the house settle around her like an old song finding its rhythm.

But it was the first time in months she felt like she might make something again, even if she didn't know what that something was.

## Chapter Three: The Archaeology of Almost

She almost didn't recognize him, which was ridiculous because Kasper Ferguson had the kind of face that stayed with you—sharp angles and intelligent eyes, the sort of Doberman handsomeness that had made her stupid at twenty-five and suspicious at thirty. Now, at forty-two, she just felt tired.

He stood in the doorway of the same grocer's she'd visited the week before, older but not diminished, his coat too expensive for the drizzle and his posture still carrying that particular Doberman confidence of someone who'd never doubted his place in the world. When their eyes met, she watched him blink twice, as if she might be a mirage brought on by too much coffee and not enough sleep.

"Lillian," he said, and her name in his mouth sounded like a question he wasn't sure he wanted answered.

She raised an eyebrow—a gesture that had once driven him to distraction and now felt like putting on an old coat. "Didn't think I'd see you back in Morningside."

"I could say the same." His smile was polite, careful, the kind of expression you practiced in mirrors before difficult conversations. "I was visiting my sister. Thought I'd swing by the old place. Didn't expect to see you."

She looked at his coffee cup, noting the way his fingers wrapped around it like he was trying to warm something that had gone cold long ago. "You still take it black."

"Some things don't change."

"Plenty do," she replied, not cold but firm, the way you might correct a child who'd gotten their facts wrong.

They stepped outside, and the sky threatened rain with the casual menace of Scottish weather. They stood awkwardly near the bus stop, the wind rustling Kasper's coat and brushing past her as if it were trying to usher them along toward some predetermined conclusion.

"I heard about the academy," he said finally, and she felt her shoulders tense the way they always did when people mentioned her former life.

"I'm fine," she said, which was what she always said, even when it wasn't true.

He hesitated, and she could see him weighing his words like a man trying to calculate the exact amount of pressure needed to open a door without breaking it. "I didn't come to dig anything up. Just... glad you're still around. You were always the most stubborn creature I knew."

"Still am," she said. "Even if the world got louder."

Kasper nodded, and for a moment they stood in the kind of silence that held the weight of everything they'd never said to each other. Then he reached into his pocket and pulled out a folded receipt, writing something on the back with a pen that looked like it cost more than her weekly groceries.

"Just in case," he said, handing it to her. "You always had things to say. Might be time to let people hear them again."

She watched him walk away, his stride still confident but somehow gentler, as if the years had taught him that not every destination required conquest. The wind followed him down the street, and she stood there holding a piece of paper that felt heavier than it should have.

That night, she didn't open the window. She lit a candle instead—one of her mother's, lavender-scented and half-melted from years of occasional use. And in the flickering light, she wrote three lines that didn't make her want to tear up the page and start over.

It wasn't much, but it was something. And something, she was beginning to remember, was how everything started.

## Chapter Four: The Democracy of Imperfection

The studio looked like it had been converted from something else—maybe a pet supply shop, maybe a small café that had given up on its dreams. Cheap insulation foam clung to the walls with the determination of barnacles, and the air carried the faint scent of sawdust and old vinyl, as if the building itself was trying to remember what it used to be.

Lillian ran a paw along the keyboard's edge, the plastic cool and unfamiliar beneath her pads. She'd forgotten how foreign instruments could feel when you'd been away from them too long, like trying to speak a language you'd once been fluent in but had let rust from disuse.

The rat behind the soundboard couldn't have been older than twenty-five, all thin-framed glasses and the kind of casual confidence that came from never having failed at anything important yet. He had earbuds in even while talking, as if he needed a soundtrack for every conversation.

"Lillian Edgecombe, right? Kasper said you might come."

She nodded, taking in the equipment that looked both more sophisticated and more jury-rigged than anything she'd worked with before. "Didn't say you were this young."

He grinned, the kind of smile that suggested he'd heard this before and had learned to take it as a compliment. "Didn't say you were this famous, either."

She barked a single dry laugh and shook her head. "I'm not."

But even as she said it, she could feel the weight of her former reputation settling around her shoulders like a coat she'd outgrown. There had been a time when her name meant something in certain circles, when music journalists had used words like "promising" and "distinctive" and "worth watching." That was before the academy, before the slow erosion of confidence that came from teaching other people to do what you'd stopped believing you could do yourself.

The session was meant to be casual—a test run, no pressure, no expectations. But the moment she sat down in the booth and the microphone blinked to life, her chest tightened with the familiar panic of performance, the fear that she'd open her mouth and nothing would come out, or worse, that something would come out and it would be terrible.

"Just hum if you want," the rat said through the monitor, his voice tinny and encouraging. "Start wherever feels right."

Lillian closed her eyes and tried to remember what "right" felt like. The hum came low and cautious, like a question she wasn't sure she wanted answered. Then it shifted, found a scale, leaned into it with the tentative confidence of someone testing the strength of ice before stepping onto it.

Then a word. Then a verse. A scratch of a lyric she didn't even know she'd remembered—half a song she'd written at the academy but never performed, something about the space between what you wanted and what you were willing to settle for.

It came out rough, unpolished, the kind of imperfect that would have made her cringe five years ago. But now it just felt real, like the difference between a photograph and a mirror.

The rat didn't say much when she stopped. He just pushed a button, leaned forward, and said, "That's something."

She left the studio without a copy, without a commitment, without any of the usual negotiations that had once defined her relationship with her own voice. But her phone buzzed later that night: "Want to come again Thursday? You've got something weird and raw. I like weird and raw."

She stared at the message for a long time, then looked around her mother's kitchen—at the stack of unopened bills, the chipped mug on the counter, the note from her landlord she'd tucked behind the radio like a secret she wasn't ready to face.

Then she typed back: "Thursday. Same time."

Because weird and raw, she was beginning to understand, might be exactly what she had to offer.

## Chapter Five: The Cartography of Almost

The second studio session had barely begun when Kasper appeared in the doorway like a memory that had decided to take physical form. No text, no call, no warning—just a tall, broad-shouldered Doberman silhouette framed in glass, holding a paper bag from the café across the street with the careful precision of someone bearing gifts to a shrine.

Lillian froze at the sight of him, her paws still positioned over the keyboard as if she'd been caught in the middle of something private. He looked older than he had the week before, or maybe she was just seeing him more clearly now—the way time had softened the sharp edges of his face, the way his eyes carried everything they'd never said to each other.

"Didn't mean to interrupt," he said, stepping in slowly, the way you might approach a wild animal you weren't sure would bolt or bite. "I was just passing by."

She narrowed her eyes, not unkindly but with the skepticism of someone who'd learned not to trust coincidences. "You live in Manchester now."

He held up the bag, and she could smell the familiar sweetness of fresh baking. "They still sell your favorite scones. Fig and walnut."

The rat behind the soundboard took one look at the tension crackling between them and wisely vanished into the editing room, leaving them alone with the weight of their shared history.

They stood in silence for a moment, and Lillian could feel the years stretching between them like a bridge she wasn't sure she wanted to cross. Then she gestured to a bench along the back wall, worn leather that had probably seen a thousand awkward conversations.

"Sit, if you're staying. Just don't ask to duet."

Kasper chuckled, the sound rusty from disuse. "No danger of that."

He watched as she recorded a scratch vocal over a new loop, this one more vulnerable than the last—less about perfection, more about breath and the spaces between words. He didn't interrupt, didn't offer suggestions, just sat there like a witness to something he wasn't sure he had the right to see.

When she stepped out of the booth, wiping her brow with the back of her paw, he handed her the scone wrapped in tissue paper that smelled like home.

"I heard about your mum," he said quietly, and the words hung in the air like smoke.

Lillian froze for a moment, her teeth already sunk into the pastry. "She went quickly."

"She always liked me," he said, a half-smile ghosting over his muzzle like a memory of better times.

"She said you were a terrible flirt and probably too handsome to be trusted."

He laughed, but there was no triumph in it, just the rueful acknowledgment of someone who'd learned that being handsome and charming wasn't the same as being good.

They ate quietly, the past humming between them like low feedback from an amplifier. Old gigs where they'd shared the stage and the spotlight. Long nights in cramped vans, arguing about setlists and sleeping arrangements. The show they'd almost landed together, the future they'd nearly built before it all fell apart in the way that futures sometimes do—not with a bang but with a series of small disappointments that accumulated like dust.

"I've got a baby now," Kasper said suddenly, his eyes not meeting hers. "Her name's Eloise."

Lillian blinked, surprised at how much that stung, like pressing on a bruise she'd forgotten she had. "Congratulations."

"She has your laugh," he added, just above a whisper. "Don't know how that's possible."

The silence that followed was different from the ones before—heavier, more complicated, full of all the things they'd never figured out how to say to each other.

"I'm glad you're happy," Lillian said finally, and meant it, mostly.

He smiled, and it was honest in a way that made her chest tight. Tired, maybe, but honest. "So are you going to make something real out of this music thing again?"

"I don't know," she said, and the admission felt like stepping off a cliff. "Maybe for me, this time."

As he reached the door, he looked back once, and she could see in his face the ghost of the young dog who'd once believed they could conquer the world together.

"That's the best reason," he said.

And then he left, no lingering, no promises, no attempts to rewrite the past. Just a quiet memory and the lingering scent of fig and walnut, sweet and complicated and gone too soon.

## Chapter Six: The Architecture of Midnight

The rain had returned by midnight—not the aggressive downpour of afternoon storms, but the soft, persistent hiss of Scottish weather settling in for the long haul. Lillian stood barefoot in her small kitchen, bathed in the amber glow of the under-cabinet light, a cup of lukewarm chamomile tea cooling in her paws.

She hadn't meant to stay up so late, but silence had a way of stretching time, making minutes feel like hours and hours feel like the space between heartbeats. The flat was quiet, but not empty—from the other room, a cello case leaned against the couch like a patient friend, and a stack of lyric sheets sat half-sorted on the table, evidence of a life slowly reassembling itself.

She heard a rustle behind her, then the creak of floorboards that had been complaining about footsteps for decades. Kasper appeared in the doorway wearing an oversized hoodie and thick socks, rubbing at one eye like a child woken from a dream he wasn't ready to leave.

"You're up," he said, his voice low and careful in the way of people who understood that midnight conversations required different rules.

She looked over her shoulder, taking in the way he filled the doorway without quite entering, as if he wasn't sure of his welcome. "So are you."

He shrugged and stepped into the kitchen, his presence immediately making the small space feel smaller. "Your kettle whistles like a train crash. Hard to ignore."

Lillian smirked, remembering how he'd always complained about her appliances, the way they seemed designed to announce their every function to the neighborhood. "It's got personality."

"You always did say that about your terrible appliances," he said, leaning against the counter with his arms folded, and she could hear in his voice the echo of a hundred similar conversations from their shared past.

A beat passed. Lillian sipped her tea, still staring ahead at the window where rain traced silver paths down the glass. Then, without thinking, she said, "I'm scared, Kasper."

The words hung in the air between them, more honest than anything she'd said in months.

He looked at her but didn't interrupt, didn't rush to fill the silence with reassurances or solutions. He'd learned, somewhere in the years since they'd last done this dance, that some confessions required witness more than response.

"I feel like I'm trying to knit myself back together with threads that aren't mine anymore," she continued, her voice barely above a whisper. "And everyone keeps telling me how strong I am, but what if I'm just good at pretending?"

He was quiet for a long time, and she could feel him weighing his words the way you might test the strength of a rope before trusting it with your full weight.

"I don't know how to be what I used to be either," he said finally.

That struck something deep in her chest, a chord she'd forgotten she had. She looked at him fully now, saw how time and regret had softened the sharp lines of his Doberman face. He was still beautiful in that jagged way that had once made her stupid with wanting, but gentler now, worn smooth by whatever storms he'd weathered in the years since they'd last stood in a kitchen together at midnight.

"I miss some things," she admitted. "Not everything. But some."

He nodded, understanding passing between them like a shared language they'd never quite forgotten how to speak. "Me too."

They stood there for a while, not lovers, not even quite friends, just two people in a kitchen at midnight, trying to believe they could still find versions of themselves worth waking up as.

"Want some tea?" she offered finally, already reaching for a second mug.

He hesitated, then smiled faintly. "Only if the kettle doesn't try to kill me."

She smirked and filled the kettle, and as it began its familiar, terrible whistle, she thought about the strange democracy of shared spaces, the way two people could occupy the same small kitchen and somehow make it feel like home.

## Chapter Seven: The Cartography of Morning Light

The rain had passed by morning, leaving behind a world rinsed clean and gleaming. Dew clung to the grass outside Lillian's modest garden window, and light poured into the kitchen in soft, golden beams that made everything look like it had been painted by someone who believed in second chances.

The scent of toast and marmalade hung in the air, earthy and simple and grounding in the way that only breakfast could be. Lillian sat at the kitchen table, her chin resting on her paw, eyes half-lidded in the kind of contentment that came not from grand gestures but from small, undisturbed moments that felt like gifts you hadn't known you were waiting for.

Across from her, Kasper was buttering a slice of toast with the surgical precision of someone who'd learned to find meditation in mundane tasks. His fur was still slightly tousled from sleep, and he wore one of her old jumpers—too small for him, stretched at the shoulders—but he didn't seem to mind. He hadn't asked to stay the night, and she hadn't offered. It had just happened, like a book sliding closed after a long, unfinished chapter.

"You don't snore," she remarked, teasing him with the casual intimacy of someone who'd shared enough mornings to know the difference between observation and accusation.

"You do," he replied without missing a beat, and she could hear the smile in his voice even though he wasn't looking at her.

She laughed—an easy, unguarded laugh that felt like it had been buried under months of dust and effort. It surprised her, the way joy could sneak up on you when you weren't paying attention, like finding money in an old coat pocket.

"Didn't think you'd still be here," she added, more softly, testing the weight of the admission.

He looked up then, his eyes meeting hers across the small table. "I wasn't sure either. But this felt good. Normal."

She didn't reply right away. Instead, she sipped her coffee and looked out the window at the street just beginning to wake up. Somewhere, a jogger's footsteps echoed down the sidewalk with the steady rhythm of someone who'd made peace with routine. A bus hummed in the distance, carrying people toward whatever small dramas and quiet victories awaited them. Her little flat, for once, didn't feel like an exile. It felt like home.

"I don't want anything complicated, Kasper," she said finally, the words coming out more honest than she'd intended.

"I know," he replied, and there was something in his voice that suggested he'd been thinking the same thing. "I don't either. Just breakfast. For now."

They ate in silence for a few minutes, the kind of comfortable quiet that came from not needing to fill every moment with words. He finished his toast, and she poured him another cup of coffee, the domestic ritual feeling both foreign and familiar, like speaking a language you'd once been fluent in but had let rust from disuse.

At some point, she reached across the table and let her paw brush his, a gesture so small it might have been accidental if not for the way neither of them pulled away.

And in that moment, sitting in her mother's kitchen with morning light streaming through windows that had seen decades of similar scenes, Lillian thought that maybe this was what healing looked like—not the dramatic transformation she'd been expecting, but the quiet accumulation of moments that felt like home.

## Chapter Eight: The Democracy of Applause

The theatre wasn't grand, and that was exactly what made it perfect. In fact, it was the same small venue where Lillian had once taught voice lessons part-time in her twenties, back when she'd still believed that teaching was just a temporary detour on her way to something bigger. Wooden seats worn smooth by decades of audiences, a modest velvet curtain that had seen better years, a slightly uneven stage floor that required performers to know where they were stepping.

But it was full—brimming with warmth and chatter and the particular kind of anticipation that came from people who'd chosen to spend their evening in a small room listening to music instead of staying home with their televisions and their comfortable disappointments.

Backstage, Lillian adjusted the collar of her long, midnight-blue coat, embroidered subtly with silver stitching that caught the light when she moved. She wore no heavy makeup, just a soft shimmer at the eyes that made her look like herself, only more so. She looked into the mirror—not to check her appearance, but to steady herself, to find in her own reflection the person she was trying to become.

Behind her, the rest of the ensemble was tuning instruments and whispering jokes, the nervous energy of performers who'd learned to trust each other enough to be vulnerable in public. The soft-eyed squirrel with her cello, the shy rat hidden behind his bass, the wild dog vocalist who'd become her unlikely harmony partner—they'd all found their way to this moment through their own complicated paths.

She caught her own gaze in the mirror and for a moment saw all the versions of herself layered like transparencies: the young badger who'd once believed that talent was enough, the teacher who'd learned that it wasn't, the woman who'd stood in her mother's kitchen just months ago wondering if she had anything left to offer the world.

She took a deep breath, tasting the familiar cocktail of terror and excitement that came before every performance, and walked out into the light.

The opening notes were slow, deliberate—a gentle swell of cello, the whisper of brushes on drums, the first tentative touch of piano keys like thoughts forming in real time. Lillian stood center stage, her paws wrapped around the microphone stand like it might disappear if she let go, and felt the weight of every eye in the room.

And then she sang.

Her voice was not perfect. It cracked in places—emotion, not error—and carried the particular roughness that came from years of living rather than years of practice. It was the kind of voice that told stories instead of just carrying melodies, that made you lean forward in your seat not because it was beautiful but because it was true.

In the third row, Kasper sat with his arms crossed over his chest, watching her with an expression she couldn't quite read but didn't need to. His presence was both comfort and complication, a reminder of all the songs they'd never finished together and all the ones she was learning to sing alone.

The song built slowly, each instrument finding its place in the architecture of sound they were constructing together. And as it did, something built inside her too—not confidence, exactly, but something deeper and more sustainable. The recognition that this was what she was meant to do, not because she was the best at it, but because it was the truest thing she knew how to offer.

When the last note faded, the applause wasn't thunderous—it was something better. It was real, honest, the kind of recognition that came not from spectacle but from connection. A standing ovation not of obligation but of gratitude, from people who understood they'd witnessed something genuine in a world that often felt performed.

As she stepped back from the microphone, a single tear slipped down her cheek. Not from sadness, not from joy, but from the particular wholeness that came from finally being exactly where you belonged.

She didn't need to say a word. The music had said everything that mattered.

And for the first time in longer than she could remember, Lillian Edgecombe was home.

## Epilogue: The Songs We Keep

Six months later, Lillian stood in the same kitchen where she'd learned to make tea for two again, watching Kasper attempt to fix her terrible kettle with the determined optimism of someone who'd never met a problem he couldn't engineer his way out of.

"It's not broken," she said, leaning against the counter with her arms folded. "It's got character."

"It's got a death wish," he replied, not looking up from his work. "And possibly a vendetta against anyone who wants hot water."

She laughed, the sound easy and unguarded in the way that had become natural again. Outside, Edinburgh was settling into autumn, the light growing shorter and more precious, the air carrying the promise of winter and the memory of all the seasons that had come before.

The album—their album, though they'd never quite figured out how to talk about the pronouns involved—had been released quietly, without fanfare or grand expectations. It wasn't a comeback because she'd never really left, just learned to sing more quietly for a while. The reviews had been kind, using words like "mature" and "authentic" and "worth the wait," though she'd stopped reading them after the first few.

What mattered was the music itself, the way it had found its audience one listener at a time, the way people wrote to tell her that her songs had helped them through their own small apocalypses and quiet resurrections.

"There," Kasper said, stepping back from the kettle with the satisfaction of someone who'd just negotiated a successful peace treaty. "Try it now."

She filled it with water and set it on the stove, and when it began to heat, it made a sound that was almost musical—still distinctive, still unmistakably itself, but no longer quite so determined to wake the dead.

"Better?" he asked.

"Different," she said, which was not the same thing but was perhaps more honest.

They stood there in the kitchen that had become theirs without either of them quite deciding it should be, listening to the kettle's new song and the comfortable silence that had learned to exist between them. Not the silence of things unsaid, but the silence of things that didn't need saying, of two people who'd found their way back to each other not by rewriting their history but by learning to live with it.

Outside, the first leaves were beginning to fall, and somewhere in the distance, someone was practicing piano—scales at first, then fragments of melody, then something that might have been the beginning of a song.

Lillian smiled and reached for two mugs, because some things, she'd learned, were worth keeping. The terrible kettle, the small kitchen, the man who'd learned to fix things without trying to fix her, the music that had taught her the difference between being heard and being understood.

And in the space between the kettle's whistle and the first sip of tea, she heard it—the faint echo of all the songs they'd sung and all the ones they had yet to discover, waiting patiently for their turn to be born.
