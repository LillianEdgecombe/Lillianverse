# MANIAC
*A Lillianverse Novella*

## Chapter 1: Secret Rhythms

The clay was still warm between her fingers when the music began to move through her. Lillian Edgecombe had been working late in her pottery studio again, the familiar ritual of centering clay on the wheel becoming a meditation that quieted the restless energy that had been building in her chest for weeks. But tonight, as the retro funk track pulsed from her old radio—something with a bass line that seemed to vibrate through the floorboards and into her bones—she found herself standing, her hands still clay-slicked, her body beginning to sway.

At forty-two, Lillian had learned to move through the world with a particular kind of careful grace. Her badger body was solid, curvy in ways that made her students at the music academy look at her with a mixture of admiration and bewilderment. She was the professor who could make a room full of twenty-year-olds fall silent with a single note, whose voice could wrap around jazz standards like silk around skin. But movement—real movement, the kind that came from somewhere deeper than technique—that was something she kept locked away, something she did only when she was certain she was alone.

Tonight, though, the music was insistent. It pulled at her hips, made her shoulders roll, and before she could think to stop herself, she was dancing. Not the careful, measured movements she'd learned in those long-ago classes, but something raw and immediate. Her feet found the rhythm, her arms carved shapes in the air, and for the first time in months—maybe years—she felt completely, utterly alive.

Her hips swayed with a weight and momentum that was entirely her own—not the sharp, angular movements of the dancers she'd seen on television, but something rounder, more grounded. Her belly, soft from years of good meals and contentment, moved with its own rhythm beneath her clay-stained shirt, rolling gently as she turned. Her arms, fuller than they'd been in her twenties, caught the light as they swept through the air, the flesh of her upper arms moving with a kind of liquid grace that spoke of strength rather than weakness.

She moved like water, like fire, like something that had been caged and was finally, finally free. Her thighs, solid and powerful from years of standing at the pottery wheel, carried her across the studio floor with surprising lightness. When she spun, her whole body participated—her breasts shifting beneath the fabric of her shirt, her hips leading the turn, her arms extended for balance. The studio around her—with its shelves of glazed bowls and half-finished sculptures—became a stage, and she was both performer and audience, lost in the pure joy of her own body in motion. Her badger features, usually so composed and maternal, were transformed by the music. Her dark eyes gleamed, her mouth was slightly open as she breathed hard, and there was something almost feral in the way she claimed the space around her.

The song built to a crescendo, and she spun, her arms extended, her head thrown back, and in that moment she was not a music professor or a potter or even a wife and mother. She was simply herself, unfiltered and unashamed.

When the music ended, she stood in the sudden silence, her chest heaving, sweat beading on her forehead. The clay on her hands had dried, cracking slightly, and she stared at her reflection in the dark window, hardly recognizing the woman looking back at her. This woman looked dangerous. This woman looked like she had secrets.

"Jesus Christ, Lil."

She spun around, her heart hammering, to find Miles standing in the doorway. Her husband's German Shepherd features were slack with something that might have been awe, his usually practical expression replaced by something softer, more wondering. He was still in his work clothes—the sensible button-down and khakis that marked him as the competent, reliable man he'd always been—but his eyes were wide, almost stunned.

"How long have you been standing there?" she asked, and her voice came out rougher than she'd intended, still breathless from the dancing.

"Long enough," he said quietly. He stepped into the studio, moving carefully, as if he were approaching something wild that might bolt. "Lil, I had no idea you could—" He stopped, shook his head. "That was incredible."

She felt heat rise in her cheeks, a mixture of embarrassment and something else, something that felt dangerously like pride. "It's nothing. I was just—the music was on, and I—"

"Don't," he said, and there was something almost fierce in his voice. "Don't diminish it. What I just saw..." He ran a hand through his hair, a gesture she recognized as his way of processing something that didn't fit into his orderly worldview. "You were beautiful. You were—Christ, Lil, you were magnificent."

They stood looking at each other across the clay-dusted floor, and Lillian felt something shift between them, some long-dormant current suddenly alive again. Miles had always loved her voice, had always been proud of her musical accomplishments, but this was different. This was him seeing her in a way he never had before, seeing some part of her she'd kept hidden even from herself.

The moment stretched between them, fragile and electric, until the sound of the mail slot clattering broke the spell. Miles glanced toward the front of the house, then back at her.

"I should—" he started.

"Yes," she said quickly. "Yes, you should check that."

He nodded, but he didn't move immediately. Instead, he looked at her one more time, his expression soft and wondering, and said, "We should talk about this. About what I just saw."

After he left, Lillian stood alone in her studio, her body still humming with the memory of movement. She looked down at her hands, at the dried clay cracking on her fingers, and felt something she hadn't felt in years: possibility.

In the morning, over coffee and the comfortable routine of breakfast, Miles handed her a flyer that had come in the mail. The paper was glossy, professional, with bold letters that seemed to leap off the page: "London All-Stars Dance Tournament – Open Auditions."

"Probably just junk mail," he said, but his voice was carefully neutral, and when she looked up at him, she saw something in his eyes that looked like hope.

Lillian stared at the flyer, at the images of young, lithe bodies frozen in perfect poses, and felt her stomach clench with something that was equal parts terror and longing. The woman who had danced in her studio last night seemed like a dream now, something too wild and impossible to have been real.

But the flyer was real. The auditions were real. And somewhere deep in her chest, that restless energy was stirring again, whispering that maybe, just maybe, it was time to stop hiding.

## Chapter 2: The Big "No"

The Royal Academy of Dance sat like a monument to aspiration in the heart of Covent Garden, its Georgian facade promising elegance and tradition to anyone brave enough to walk through its doors. Lillian had been here before, years ago, when she was still young enough to believe that wanting something badly enough might be sufficient to make it happen. Now, at forty-two, she stood on the pavement outside, clutching the audition flyer in one hand and her dance bag in the other, wondering if she had lost her mind entirely.

The morning was crisp, with that particular London quality of light that made everything look both beautiful and slightly unreal. Around her, the city moved with its usual purposeful energy—tourists consulting maps, office workers hurrying toward the tube, street performers setting up for the day. She felt separate from all of it, suspended in a bubble of her own making, where the only sound was the rapid beating of her heart.

"Lillian? Lillian Edgecombe?"

She turned to find a woman approaching her, someone vaguely familiar with the kind of carefully maintained appearance that spoke of years in the dance world. It took her a moment to place the face—Caroline Winters, a classmate from her brief stint at dance college twenty years ago. Caroline looked exactly as Lillian remembered her: tall, angular, with the kind of posture that suggested she'd never forgotten she was a dancer, even if she was now clearly a teacher.

"Caroline," Lillian said, forcing a smile. "How lovely to see you."

"What are you doing here?" Caroline's eyes flicked to the flyer in Lillian's hand, and her expression shifted from polite surprise to something that looked uncomfortably like pity. "Don't tell me you're thinking of auditioning for this."

"I was considering it," Lillian said, and hated how defensive she sounded.

Caroline laughed, a sound that was meant to be kind but landed like a slap. "Oh, darling, no. This is for the young ones, the serious dancers. It's not—" She paused, clearly searching for a diplomatic way to phrase what she was thinking. "It's not really for cabaret types, if you know what I mean."

Cabaret types. The words hit Lillian like a physical blow, carrying with them all the assumptions and dismissals she'd been trying to outrun her entire adult life. She was the music professor, the one with the sultry voice and the maternal presence. She was safe, contained, categorizable. She was not the kind of woman who danced.

"I see," Lillian said quietly.

"I'm sure you understand," Caroline continued, her voice taking on the patronizing tone that Lillian remembered from their college days. "These competitions are incredibly demanding. The choreography is complex, the physical requirements are... well, they're quite specific. And the other dancers—they've been training for this their entire lives. They're twenty-two, twenty-five at the oldest. They have the bodies for it, the stamina. It's just not realistic for someone our age to think—"

"Our age?" Lillian interrupted, and there was something sharp in her voice that made Caroline take a step back.

"Well, yes. I mean, we're not young anymore, are we? We have to be practical about these things. There are other ways to stay involved in dance—teaching, perhaps, or—"

"Thank you for the advice," Lillian said, cutting her off. "I'll certainly take it under consideration."

She turned and walked toward the academy's entrance before Caroline could say anything else, her heart pounding with a mixture of fury and determination. Behind her, she could hear Caroline calling her name, but she didn't turn around. She couldn't. If she stopped now, if she let Caroline's words sink in the way they wanted to, she would never make it through those doors.

The lobby of the academy was exactly as she remembered it—all polished wood and mirrors, with photographs of famous dancers lining the walls like saints in a cathedral. But now it was filled with bodies, dozens of them, all young and lean and moving with the kind of unconscious grace that spoke of years of training. They stretched against the walls, chatted in small groups, ran through combinations with the casual perfection of people for whom movement was as natural as breathing.

Lillian felt ancient among them, her forty-two-year-old body suddenly heavy and awkward in her dance clothes. She had chosen her outfit carefully—black leggings, a fitted top that flattered her curves without being too revealing—but now she felt overdressed, too formal, like someone playing at being a dancer rather than actually being one.

The audition itself was a blur of movement and music, of trying to keep up with combinations that seemed designed to showcase everything she wasn't. The choreographer—a sharp-featured woman in her thirties who moved like a blade—called out instructions with the kind of casual authority that assumed everyone in the room spoke the same physical language. And they did, all of them except Lillian.

She stumbled through the warm-up, her body remembering some things and forgetting others. Her thighs, thick and powerful from years of standing at the pottery wheel, trembled slightly as she attempted the leg extensions that came so easily to the younger dancers. When she bent forward in the stretches, her soft belly pressed against her thighs, creating a fullness that the fitted top couldn't quite contain. Her breasts, substantial and natural, moved with each breath as she tried to keep up, the fabric of her sports bra working overtime to provide support during the more vigorous movements.

The jazz combination was better—closer to the kind of movement that had felt so natural in her studio—but even then, she was always a beat behind, always struggling to catch up. Her hips, wide and generous, found the rhythm more easily here, swaying with a weight and presence that was entirely her own. When she turned, her whole body participated—her arms, fuller than they'd been in her twenties, creating graceful arcs through the air, her torso twisting with surprising fluidity despite the soft layer of flesh that cushioned her ribs. Around her, the other dancers moved like water, like they were born knowing these steps, but when she caught glimpses of herself in the mirror, she saw something different—not the sharp angles and hollow spaces of youth, but curves and substance, a body that took up space unapologetically.

The contemporary piece was the worst. It required a kind of emotional vulnerability that she wasn't prepared for, a willingness to be seen that went against every instinct she'd developed over the years. As she moved through the floor work, her body pressed against the studio floor with a weight that spoke of years of good meals and contentment. Her arms, soft and strong, supported her as she rolled and reached, the flesh of her upper arms moving with each gesture. When the choreography called for her to arch her back, her belly curved outward naturally, and she could feel the fabric of her top riding up slightly, exposing a sliver of pale skin. She tried to lose herself in the music, to find that feeling she'd had in her studio, but the mirrors were everywhere, reflecting back not just her struggles and limitations and age, but also the undeniable reality of her body—substantial, present, refusing to disappear.

When it was over, she stood in the center of the room, her chest heaving, sweat stinging her eyes. The other dancers were barely breathing hard. They chatted among themselves, already moving on to whatever came next, while she felt like she'd just run a marathon.

The choreographer looked at her clipboard, then at Lillian, and there was something in her expression that might have been sympathy. "Thank you," she said, and the dismissal was clear.

Lillian nodded, gathered her things, and walked out of the studio on legs that felt like they might give out at any moment. In the lobby, she caught sight of herself in one of the mirrors—red-faced, disheveled, looking every one of her forty-two years—and felt something inside her crumble.

Outside, the London afternoon felt harsh and unforgiving. The tourists and office workers and street performers were still there, still moving through their lives with purpose and direction, but now she felt like she was watching them from behind glass. She had tried to step into their world, to claim a space that wasn't meant for her, and she had been found wanting.

Caroline was waiting for her on the pavement, her expression carefully composed. "How did it go?" she asked, though they both knew the answer.

"About as well as you'd expect," Lillian said.

"I'm sorry," Caroline said, and she did sound genuinely sorry. "I know it's disappointing, but really, it's for the best. You have so much to offer in other areas. Your voice, your teaching—"

"Yes," Lillian said. "I know."

She walked away before Caroline could say anything else, her dance bag heavy on her shoulder, the audition flyer crumpled in her fist. Behind her, the academy stood like a fortress, its doors closed to people like her—people who had forgotten their place, who had dared to dream beyond their designated roles.

But as she walked through the London streets, something stubborn and defiant was stirring in her chest. Caroline's words echoed in her mind—cabaret types, our age, not realistic—and with each repetition, they lost a little of their power. She had danced in that audition. She had stumbled, yes, and struggled, but she had danced. And for a few moments, in between the mistakes and the self-doubt, she had felt that same wild joy she'd discovered in her studio.

Maybe that was enough. Maybe that was everything.

## Chapter 3: Miles' Support

The house felt different when she returned, as if the very air had shifted in her absence. Lillian dropped her dance bag by the front door and stood in the hallway, listening to the familiar sounds of home—the hum of the refrigerator, the tick of the grandfather clock, the distant murmur of the television from the living room. Everything was exactly as she'd left it, but she felt like a stranger in her own life.

She climbed the stairs slowly, her legs still aching from the audition, and went straight to the bedroom. The curtains were drawn against the afternoon light, creating a cocoon of shadows that felt like exactly what she needed. She crawled into bed fully clothed, pulled the duvet over her head, and let herself sink into the kind of darkness that felt like giving up.

She must have dozed, because the next thing she knew, there was a gentle knock on the bedroom door. "Lil?" Miles' voice was soft, careful. "Can I come in?"

She didn't answer, but she heard the door open anyway, heard his footsteps on the carpet. The bed dipped as he sat down beside her, and she felt his hand rest lightly on what he probably thought was her shoulder but was actually her hip under the duvet.

"How did it go?" he asked.

"How do you think it went?" Her voice was muffled by the covers, thick with something that might have been tears if she'd allowed herself to cry.

"That bad?"

She pulled the duvet down just enough to look at him. He was still in his work clothes, but he'd loosened his tie and rolled up his sleeves, and there was something in his expression that made her chest tighten. Not pity—she couldn't have borne pity—but something gentler, more complex.

"I was terrible," she said. "Completely, utterly terrible. I couldn't keep up with the combinations, I looked ridiculous next to all those young dancers, and the choreographer looked at me like I was someone's embarrassing mother who'd wandered into the wrong room."

Miles was quiet for a moment, his hand still resting on her hip. "You're allowed to be upset," he said finally. "But don't believe for a second that they know what they're seeing."

"Miles—"

"No, listen to me." He shifted on the bed, turning to face her more fully. "What I saw in your studio last night—that wasn't someone who couldn't dance. That was someone who dances like breathing, like it's the most natural thing in the world. If they couldn't see that, if they were too busy looking at ages and body types and whatever other bullshit criteria they use, then they're the ones who are missing something, not you."

She stared at him, this practical, sensible man who fixed problems for a living and never said anything he didn't mean. "You have to say that. You're my husband."

"I don't have to say anything," he said, and there was something almost fierce in his voice. "I could have said you tried your best, or that it was brave of you to audition, or any of the other things people say when they're trying to make someone feel better about failing. But that's not what I'm saying. I'm saying they were wrong."

He paused, ran a hand through his hair in that gesture she knew so well. "And I'm saying I'm sorry I didn't tell you sooner. What you did last night—Christ, Lil, you were breathtaking. You were the most beautiful thing I've ever seen, and I've been married to you for fifteen years. I should have said that then, instead of just standing there like an idiot."

Something inside her chest loosened, just a little. "You weren't an idiot."

"I was. I was so stunned I couldn't even speak. You moved like—" He shook his head, searching for words. "Like you were made of music. Like the music was moving through you and you were just letting it happen. It was the most honest thing I've ever seen."

She pulled the duvet down further, studying his face. "Honest?"

"You weren't performing. You weren't trying to be anything other than exactly what you were in that moment. And what you were was magnificent."

They looked at each other in the dim light of the bedroom, and Lillian felt something shifting between them, some long-dormant current coming back to life. This was the man she'd married, the one who saw her clearly even when she couldn't see herself. But it was also something new, something that had been born in her studio last night when he'd watched her dance.

"I don't know if I can do it again," she said quietly. "The audition—it felt like being torn apart. Like every insecurity I've ever had was on display for everyone to see."

"Then don't do it for them," Miles said. "Do it for you. Do it because when you dance, you become someone I've never seen before, someone who's been hiding inside you all this time. Do it because you deserve to feel that alive."

He stood up, held out his hand to her. "Come on."

"Where?"

"Kitchen. I want to see her again—the woman from last night."

"Miles, I can't. I'm exhausted, and I probably look terrible, and—"

"You look like someone who's been through a battle," he said. "Which is exactly what you are. Now come on. Dance with me."

She let him pull her out of bed, let him lead her downstairs to the kitchen where they'd shared so many ordinary moments over the years. He found her phone, scrolled through her music until he found something slow and jazzy, something that wouldn't demand too much of her battered confidence.

"I don't know how to dance with a partner," she said as the music began.

"Neither do I," he admitted. "We'll figure it out."

And they did, slowly, clumsily, with the kind of gentle experimentation that felt like coming home. He held her carefully, as if she were something precious that might break, and she let herself lean into his strength, let herself trust that he would catch her if she fell.

They moved together in the familiar space of their kitchen, past the breakfast table where they'd shared coffee that morning, around the island where she'd prepared thousands of meals. But now it felt transformed, made sacred by their movement, by the way they were learning each other's bodies in a completely new way.

"This is nice," she murmured against his shoulder.

"This is just the beginning," he said, and spun her gently, carefully, until she was laughing despite herself.

When the song ended, they stood holding each other in the middle of their kitchen, and Lillian felt something she hadn't felt since before the audition: hope. Not the wild, desperate hope that had driven her to try out in the first place, but something quieter, more sustainable. The hope that came from being seen, from being loved exactly as she was.

"Thank you," she said.

"For what?"

"For reminding me who I am."

He kissed her then, soft and sure, and she tasted possibility on his lips. Tomorrow, she would have to decide what came next, whether to give up on dancing or find another way forward. But tonight, in the kitchen where they'd built their life together, she was exactly where she needed to be.

## Chapter 4: The Callback

The phone call came three days later, while Lillian was grading papers in her office at the academy. She'd been trying to lose herself in the familiar routine of work, marking rhythm exercises and sight-reading assignments with the kind of methodical attention that usually helped quiet her mind. But every few minutes, she found herself staring out the window at the London street below, watching people move with the unconscious grace she'd been so aware of since the audition.

When her phone rang, she almost didn't answer it. The number was unfamiliar, and she'd been avoiding calls from anyone who might want to discuss her humiliating performance at the dance audition. But something made her pick up on the fourth ring.

"Lillian Edgecombe?"

"Yes?"

"This is Jessica Chen from the London All-Stars Dance Tournament. I'm calling about your audition on Monday."

Lillian's stomach dropped. She'd been expecting this call, the polite rejection that would officially close the door on her brief, disastrous foray into the dance world. "I see."

"We'd like to invite you back for callbacks."

The words didn't make sense at first. Lillian stared at her computer screen, at the half-graded assignment that suddenly seemed to belong to someone else's life. "I'm sorry, what?"

"Callbacks. This Saturday at ten AM, same location. You'll be working with potential partners and learning some of the competition choreography." Jessica's voice was brisk, professional. "I have to say, you were quite the surprise. The judges are calling you our unexpected wildcard."

Wildcard. The word echoed in Lillian's mind long after she'd hung up the phone, after she'd somehow managed to finish grading papers and teach her afternoon classes and drive home through the London traffic that seemed to move in slow motion around her. She sat in her car in the driveway for ten minutes, staring at her house, trying to process what had just happened.

Miles found her there when he came home from work, still sitting behind the wheel with the engine off.

"Lil? Everything all right?"

She turned to look at him, this practical man who'd married a music teacher and somehow ended up with a wife who was apparently a wildcard. "They called me back."

"Who called you back?"

"The dance competition. They want me to come to callbacks on Saturday."

His face went through a series of expressions—surprise, confusion, and then something that looked like fierce pride. "Of course they did."

"Miles, I was terrible. You didn't see me—"

"I saw you in your studio," he said firmly. "I saw you in our kitchen. And apparently, they saw something too, even if you can't see it yourself."

Saturday morning arrived gray and drizzling, the kind of London weather that seemed designed to dampen enthusiasm. But as Lillian walked into the academy for the second time, she felt different. Not confident, exactly, but more solid somehow, more present in her own skin. She'd spent the week practicing in her studio, not trying to recreate the combinations from the audition but simply moving, letting her body remember what it felt like to be alive.

The callback was smaller, maybe twenty dancers instead of the fifty who'd auditioned. She recognized some faces from Monday, the young ones who'd moved with such casual perfection, but there were others too—dancers who looked like they might actually be closer to her age, though still younger, still more traditionally trained.

"All right, everyone," Jessica Chen called out, her voice cutting through the chatter. "We're going to start by pairing you up and teaching you some partner work. This is about chemistry as much as technique, so don't overthink it."

Lillian found herself paired with a red panda who introduced himself as Kael. He was probably in his late twenties, with the kind of lean, muscled build that spoke of years of serious training. His handshake was firm, professional, but she caught the way his eyes flicked over her body, the quick assessment that clearly found her wanting.

"So," he said as they found a space on the floor, "what's your background?"

"Music, mostly. Some dance training, but it's been a while."

"Right." His smile was polite, distant. "Well, let's see what we're working with."

The choreography was complex, demanding, the kind of partner work that required absolute trust and perfect timing. Kael moved through it with the fluid precision of someone who'd been dancing since childhood, but he held back when he danced with her, his movements careful and controlled, as if he were dancing with someone fragile.

When he placed his hands on her waist for the first lift, she could feel his hesitation through his fingertips. Her body, solid and substantial in his grip, seemed to surprise him. Her hips, wide and curved, provided a different kind of anchor than he was used to—not the sharp hipbones of the younger dancers, but something rounder, more grounded. As he lifted her, her thighs, thick and powerful, pressed against his sides, and she could sense his adjustment to her weight, the way he had to engage different muscles to support her fuller frame.

"You can lift me," she said after the third time he'd failed to complete a sequence that clearly required her to leave the ground.

"Are you sure? I don't want to—"

"I'm sure."

He lifted her then, but tentatively, and she could feel his surprise when she proved to be lighter than he'd expected, more responsive to his lead. Her arms, soft but strong from years of pottery work, wrapped around his shoulders with surprising grace. When she extended her legs in the aerial position, her thighs created different lines than the stick-thin dancers he was accustomed to—curves instead of angles, substance instead of negative space. Her belly, soft beneath her fitted top, pressed briefly against his chest during one of the closer holds, and she felt him tense slightly before adjusting to accommodate her body's natural shape. They ran through the combination again, and this time it was better, though she could still feel him holding back, still sense his assumption that she was the weak link in their partnership.

During the break, she overheard him talking to another dancer, a sleek young woman with the kind of perfect turnout that spoke of years at the barre.

"How's your partner?" the woman asked.

Kael glanced over at Lillian, who was stretching by the window. "She's... enthusiastic. But I mean, look at her. She's got to be forty, and she moves like someone who learned to dance from YouTube videos. I don't know what the judges were thinking."

The words stung, but they also sparked something in Lillian's chest, something that felt like the beginning of anger. She'd spent her entire adult life being underestimated, being dismissed as the safe choice, the maternal figure, the one who could be counted on to stay in her lane. But she was tired of lanes. She was tired of being careful.

When they resumed rehearsal, she danced differently. Not recklessly, but with a kind of focused intensity that seemed to surprise everyone in the room, including herself. She attacked the choreography instead of tiptoeing around it, threw herself into the lifts and turns with the kind of commitment that left no room for doubt.

Kael noticed the change immediately. His movements became sharper, more responsive, as if her intensity was contagious. They moved through the combination with a kind of electric precision, and when they finished, there was a moment of silence in the studio before the other dancers began to applaud.

"Better," Jessica Chen said, making notes on her clipboard. "Much better."

After the callback, Lillian sat in her car in the academy parking lot, her body aching in the best possible way. She felt wrung out, exhausted, but also more alive than she'd felt in years. Her phone buzzed with a text from Miles: "How did it go?"

She stared at the message for a long moment, trying to find words for what had happened in that studio. She'd been dismissed, underestimated, written off—and then she'd proven them wrong. Not completely, not perfectly, but enough to matter.

"I think I remembered something," she texted back.

"What?"

"How to fight."

That evening, as she soaked in a hot bath, she thought about Kael's words, about the assumptions he'd made about her age, her body, her abilities. She thought about Caroline's dismissive comments, about the choreographer's polite but clear dismissal at the first audition. And she thought about the moment in the studio when she'd stopped trying to be what they expected and started being exactly who she was.

Tomorrow, she would find out if she'd made it into the competition. But tonight, she already felt like she'd won something more important: the right to take up space, to be seen, to refuse to disappear just because the world had decided she was past her expiration date.

Miles knocked on the bathroom door. "Lil? Dinner's ready."

"Coming," she called, and pulled herself out of the bath with muscles that protested but felt strong, felt capable of carrying her wherever she decided to go next.

## Chapter 5: The Body Talks

The rehearsal studio in Bermondsey was nothing like the elegant academy in Covent Garden. It was a converted warehouse with concrete floors, fluorescent lighting, and mirrors that had seen better days. But it was theirs now, hers and Kael's, for three hours every Tuesday and Thursday evening, and Lillian had come to love its honest shabbiness, the way it stripped away pretense and demanded only truth.

They'd been working together for two weeks since the callbacks, preparing for the competition that was now just a month away. The partnership was still uneasy, built on mutual necessity rather than genuine connection, but they were learning each other's bodies, finding a rhythm that worked even if it didn't always feel natural.

Tonight, though, something was different. Kael had been pushing harder than usual, running through combinations with a kind of relentless perfectionism that left no room for mistakes. Lillian could feel the sweat stinging her eyes, could feel her muscles beginning to shake with fatigue, but she pushed through it, determined not to be the one who called for a break.

"Again," Kael said as they finished a particularly demanding sequence. "You're still behind on the turn sequence."

"I'm not behind," Lillian said, breathing hard. "I'm interpreting it differently."

"You're behind," he repeated, his voice flat. "This isn't jazz class, Lillian. This is competition choreography. There's no room for interpretation."

She wanted to argue, but she was too tired, too focused on just keeping up. They ran through the sequence again, and again, and each time she felt herself getting further from the music, further from the joy that had brought her here in the first place.

It was during the lift sequence that it happened. She was tired, her concentration fractured, and when Kael lifted her for the overhead press, she shifted her weight wrong, came down at an angle that sent a sharp spike of pain through her left knee.

She hit the ground hard, her knee buckling under her, and for a moment the pain was so intense that she couldn't breathe. She sat on the studio floor, clutching her leg, trying not to cry out.

"Shit," Kael said, but his voice carried more annoyance than concern. "Are you all right?"

"I think so," she managed, though the pain was radiating up her thigh and down her shin in waves that made her feel sick.

## Epilogue: The City Spins

The results were announced an hour later, in the theater's elegant lobby where the competitors and their families gathered around tables laden with champagne and canapés. Lillian stood with Miles, her hand in his, watching as the judges prepared to announce the winners.

She didn't win. The first-place trophy went to a pair of twenty-something dancers whose technical perfection was undeniable, whose youth and training showed in every line of their bodies. Second place went to another young couple, and third to dancers who had been competing professionally for years.

But as the applause died down and people began to disperse, something unexpected happened. A woman approached her—someone Lillian didn't recognize, with the kind of sharp, intelligent face that suggested she was someone important in the dance world.

"Lillian Edgecombe?" the woman said, extending her hand. "I'm Sarah Chen, artistic director of the London Contemporary Dance Company. I was wondering if we could talk."

"About what?"

"About choreography. About what you did out there tonight." Sarah's eyes were bright with interest. "You choreographed that routine yourself, didn't you? I could tell—it had your voice in it, your perspective. We should talk about what that might look like in a professional context."

Before Lillian could respond, another person appeared at her elbow—a young dancer, maybe twenty-two, with the kind of earnest face that reminded Lillian of her students.

"Excuse me," the young woman said, "I just wanted to say—what you did tonight, it reminded me why I started dancing. I've been so focused on being perfect, on looking like everyone else, that I forgot dance was supposed to be about truth. Thank you for showing me that."

And then there were others—dancers, teachers, choreographers, people who had seen something in her performance that went beyond winning or losing. They wanted to talk about her approach to movement, about what it meant to bring life experience to dance, about the possibility that the art form might be bigger and more inclusive than they'd previously imagined.

Miles squeezed her hand, and when she looked at him, his eyes were bright with tears he wasn't bothering to hide.

"Proud of you," he murmured.

"Even though I didn't win?"

"Especially because you didn't win. You did something more important—you changed how people think about what's possible."

Later, as they walked through the London streets toward their car, the city seemed different around them. The same rain-slicked pavements, the same mix of tourists and locals, the same energy that had always defined this place they called home. But now Lillian moved through it differently, with a kind of confidence that came from having claimed her space in the world.

"What are you thinking about?" Miles asked as they reached their car.

"About that little girl in the dance studio. About Mrs. Pemberton and what she said." Lillian looked up at the London sky, at the lights reflecting off the low clouds. "I keep thinking about how different my life might have been if she'd said something else that day. If she'd told me I was beautiful when I danced, that I should keep going, that the world needed to see what I could do."

"And?"

"And then I think about how maybe it happened exactly the way it was supposed to. Maybe I needed to live all those years, to become the woman I am now, before I was ready to dance like that. Maybe the girl who danced tonight couldn't have existed without all the years of standing still."

Miles stopped walking and turned to face her, his hands framing her face in the gentle way that had become as familiar as breathing over their fifteen years together.

"Dance with me," he said.

"Here? In the street?"

"Here. Now. Because you can, because you want to, because the world is wide enough for both of us to move through it however we choose."

So they danced, there on the rain-slicked pavement outside the theater where she'd just performed, with the London traffic moving around them and the city lights reflecting in puddles at their feet. It wasn't choreographed, wasn't perfect, wasn't anything more than two people who loved each other moving together through space. But it was theirs, and it was real, and it was exactly what Lillian had been searching for all along.

As they swayed together in the gentle rain, she thought about the conversation with Sarah Chen, about the possibilities that were opening up before her like doors she hadn't even known existed. She thought about the young dancer who had thanked her, about all the people who had approached her after the performance with stories of their own dreams deferred, their own moments of being told they didn't belong.

"I think I want to teach," she said against Miles' shoulder.

"You already teach."

"No, I mean really teach. Movement, dance, but not just technique. I want to teach people how to find their own voice in their bodies, how to move like themselves instead of like someone else's idea of what a dancer should be."

Miles pulled back to look at her, rain droplets caught in his hair, his eyes bright with understanding. "A revolution."

"A quiet one. One student at a time, one class at a time. One person learning that they don't have to disappear just because the world thinks they should."

They drove home through the London night, the city spinning around them in its eternal dance of lights and movement and possibility. In the morning, there would be reviews to read, phone calls to return, decisions to make about what came next. But tonight, there was just this—the feeling of having claimed something that had always belonged to her, the knowledge that she had finally, finally learned to take up space.

At home, they made tea and sat in their kitchen, the same space where Miles had first asked her to dance, where she had begun to remember who she was beneath all the careful layers she'd built up over the years. The competition trophy sat on the counter—not first place, but the special recognition award for "Most Inspiring Performance," which somehow felt more meaningful than any traditional prize.

"No regrets?" Miles asked, watching her over the rim of his mug.

"None," she said, and meant it. "Well, maybe one."

"What's that?"

"I wish I could go back and tell that nine-year-old girl that Mrs. Pemberton was wrong. That badgers do dance, and beautifully, and that she should never, ever stop moving."

Miles reached across the table and took her hand, his thumb tracing over her wedding ring, over the calluses from pottery work, over the small scars that told the story of a life lived fully.

"Maybe you can't tell her," he said. "But you can show her. Every time you dance, every time you teach someone else to find their voice in movement, every time you refuse to be smaller than you are—you're showing her that it was all worth it. The waiting, the hiding, the years of standing still. It was all leading to this."

Outside, London continued its ancient dance, the city that had witnessed countless transformations, countless moments of people becoming who they were always meant to be. And inside their kitchen, Lillian Edgecombe sat with her husband and felt the future spreading out before her like a stage waiting to be claimed, like music waiting to be danced to, like a story that was just beginning to be told.

She had learned to move through the world differently now, with the confidence of someone who knew her own worth, who had claimed her space and refused to give it back. She was forty-two years old, a badger in a world that preferred swans, a woman who had discovered that it was never too late to become who you were always meant to be.

And tomorrow, she would dance again.

*End of Maniac*

**Author's Note:** This novella explores themes of artistic awakening, age discrimination, and the courage required to pursue dreams that society deems inappropriate for one's station in life. Drawing inspiration from the psychological depth of Elena Ferrante, the social observation of Zadie Smith, and the sensual introspection of Anaïs Nin, "Maniac" tells the story of a woman who refuses to accept that her story is over, who discovers that revolution can be as simple as refusing to stand still.

The character of Lillian Edgecombe represents all those who have been told they are too old, too different, too much or not enough to pursue their passions. Her journey from the pottery studio to the competition stage is not just about dance—it is about reclaiming agency, about the radical act of taking up space, about the transformative power of being seen for who you truly are.

In the tradition of the great female writers who have explored the interior lives of women, this story suggests that our most profound revolutions often happen not in the public sphere, but in the quiet moments when we choose to honor our authentic selves, when we decide that our dreams matter regardless of what the world tells us about our limitations.

Lillian's story continues in the broader Lillianverse, where characters navigate the complex terrain between who they are expected to be and who they choose to become. Her dance is both literal and metaphorical—a movement toward freedom, toward self-acceptance, toward the radical possibility that it is never too late to become magnificent.
