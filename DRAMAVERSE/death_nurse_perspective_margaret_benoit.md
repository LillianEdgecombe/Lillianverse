# Till Death Do Us Apart — The Nurse’s Account

I used to think there was a right way to walk into a room where someone was about to be told their child had died. After twenty years in uniforms of various shades of blue and green, after waiting in more corridors than I care to count, I imagined I had learned a choreography of sorts: the quiet knock, the forward lean, the softening of the mouth that signalled both authority and gentleness.

It still felt wrong, that morning in the lounge with the penguins.

The TV in the corner had frozen, as it often did, on a nature documentary. A line of emperor penguins shuffled across a blown-out white horizon, their movements looped by whatever glitch the remote refused to solve. They had been crossing that endless ice field for three days, as far as I was concerned. Perhaps that is why, when the phone rang at the nurses’ station and the receptionist mouthed “Hospital,” I had the sense of someone finally reaching the edge of a world they had believed was circular.

“Camden General,” the voice said. There was that professional sympathy I recognise in my own tone sometimes, when I am not careful. “We have next of kin listed as Mr and Mrs Ferguson at the Harrow Lodge Care Home. Are you able to put us through?”

Of course I was able. The cordless phone sat warm in my hand. I could have pressed the button, transferred the call, let the hospital doctor — registrar? consultant? junior in an apologetic tie? — deliver the news personally. That would have been, strictly speaking, the correct route. No one could have faulted me.

Instead I heard myself say, “You can tell me first,” and wrote down the words that would sit between them and the rest of their lives.

Scaffolding. Installation. Fall. Instant.

I have repeated those words to myself often enough that they have lost, if not their meaning, then their certainty. At the time, though, they felt horribly precise. A scaffold is supposed to hold. An installation is supposed to be tested. A fall, in a hospital’s mouth, is always a euphemism for the real violence of impact. “Instant” is the mercy clause they offer the living, not the dead.

I stubbed out my cigarette behind the staff entrance before going back in. Not because of the no-smoking signs. I simply could not bear to carry that particular smell into the room with me.

They were where they always were at that hour. She — Lillian — at the low table under the window, glasses halfway down her muzzle, working on a Monet jigsaw as if art could be reassembled one pastel fragment at a time. He — Miles — crossing the room with painful care, tray in one hand, cane in the other, bringing her tea. They moved around one another the way long-married people do when the choreography has outlived the music: no flourishes, no surprises, just a series of small, practiced avoidances of collision.

“Mr and Mrs Ferguson?”

They both looked up. The penguins marched in silence behind them.

I have seen bodies stiffen before the news itself arrives. There is a way shoulders square, a way fingers curl around upholstery, as if some deep animal part senses the blow in the air pressure. Lillian’s hand paused above a puzzle piece. Miles’ cane tapped once against the linoleum and then was very still.

“There’s a call,” I said, and heard how inadequate the sentence was even as I spoke it. “From Camden. About your daughter.”

There are worse jobs than this. I remind myself of that often. But I know — and here I borrow the language of the reflective essays I used to read before my shifts bled into all my other reading — that to be the person who says “your daughter” in that tone is to step briefly into a role with more power over a life than any registrar or consultant will ever have. We do not like to admit that, as nurses. We prefer to talk about care.

“Put it through,” Lillian said. Her voice was very even. It was the only thing in the room that was.

I handed her the phone and stepped back, as if distance could make the next thirty seconds less intimate. I watched her listen. I watched his ears tilt towards her, his whole body leaning in, the way dogs — and he is still, under the cardigan and the cane and the pension, a German Shepherd — orient to the sound of their own names. I did not hear the doctor’s words. I did not need to. I watched the information land.

There were no cries. No cinematic wails. If anything, the silence deepened. Lillian’s free hand went to her glasses, straightened them with unnecessary precision. Miles’ cane slipped, rolled a few inches, stopped at the skirting board. Those were the only movements.

“Thank you,” she said into the phone, and I had to look away then. Gratitude is a reflex so deeply trained into some women that they apologise to the person closing the coffin.

It is unfashionable, I know, to admit that we watch our residents. We are supposed to say we “observe,” as if the clinical term excused the voyeurism. I had been watching the Fergusons for months. They were the kind who made the job bearable: they asked our names, remembered them, said please and thank you even when confused, even when in pain. They did not speak to us as if we were furniture.

Perhaps that is why I did not leave them alone after the call, as protocol would have allowed. I made my hourly checks into something closer to a vigil.

When I looked in the next time, she had moved her chair to the window, though the curtains were still half-drawn. Her white braid fell like a rope down the back of her black cardigan. She sat upright, hands resting on her knees, in the posture of someone waiting to be addressed by a higher authority. He sat on the bed with a thick art catalogue resting on his knees, still in its plastic wrap. He turned it over and over, as if the barcode might yield a different outcome if examined from enough angles.

On the floor beside them sat a cardboard box I did not recognise. Later I learned it had arrived the week before, forwarded from the gallery. At the time, it was simply another container in a room full of containers: medicine lockers, laundry bags, pedal bins. This one, however, was full of their daughter.

Childishly proportioned drawings — a fox on an escalator, bodies that looked half-machine, half-flower — lay spread across the table the following hour, the box itself empty. There was a letter on top of them, an acceptance email printed out and carefully folded, with a line in Lillian’s handwriting at the bottom: We are so proud of you, starling. I retreated before she reached that part. There are limits even to my curiosity.

If Margaret Benoit were writing this — and it is a conceit of mine that she sometimes is, through me — she would pause here to examine what it means to stand in a doorway and watch other people’s grief while pretending to check their charts. She would ask whether my attention, so meticulously trained on the micro-movements of their hands and shoulders, constituted care or appropriation. I am not sure I can answer her. I know only that I could not stop looking.

The funeral was, in architectural terms, tasteful. I have come to loathe that word. The chapel had the smooth, expensive emptiness of spaces designed to offend nobody in particular. Metal chairs with padded seats, white walls, a carefully chosen plant in each corner, its leaves so uniformly green that I had to resist the urge to touch them and confirm they were real. The air smelled faintly of coffee from the attached café and something floral from the sprays on the coffin.

We had argued, mildly, about who would accompany them. Officially, it was the driver’s job to take residents to outside appointments. Unofficially, I took the keys. “It will be easier if it’s someone they know,” I told my manager, which was true and not the whole truth. I wanted to see how the art world mourned one of its own. I wanted to see how they mourned her.

Sarah was twenty-eight. I had not met her. I knew her as a name on a next-of-kin form and as a golden retriever in the framed photograph by their bed: wiry, bright-eyed, standing in front of some complicated sculpture with her arms flung wide. The daughter of a badger and a German Shepherd, as if the universe had taken their solidity and distilled it into something quicker, sharper.

Her work hung above us, or rather, rotated above us. The gallery had installed a selection of pieces in the chapel: chrome limbs suspended from the ceiling, joints bent at angles that suggested both protection and threat; a glass heart split open along a clean seam; something that looked like a spine made of streetlights. A recorded voice murmured intermittently through hidden speakers, distorted but recognisably young, female, amused.

I stood at the back. Nurses are good at standing at the back. We are furniture with opinions.

The art people filled the front rows. I recognised them from the catalogue covers I flick through in the staff room: sharp silhouettes in black, haircuts that cost more than my uniform, shoes that had never met a hospital corridor. They had, many of them, brought their grief outfits — dark, architectural, flattering. A few were crying in ways that seemed almost polite, eyes damp but mascara intact. Some checked their phones with the furtive skill of people whose work is never entirely off.

Lillian looked wrong in that room, which is to say she looked real. The severity of her coat, the tired dignity of her posture, the way her white braid cut a line through all the tasteful black. Miles looked as if he had stepped into the wrong play. His cane seemed out of place against the polished floor, the rubber tip squeaking softly when he shifted his weight.

When she stood up to speak, the murmuring stopped. Not because of any obvious authority in her voice — she does not project, not in the theatrical sense — but because there is something in the way certain people stand beside a coffin that makes others listen. Those of us who deal in bodies learn to read that posture: it says mine.

“I wrote this last night,” she began. “Because I needed to talk to her one more time.”

If this were a medical case note, I would write: affect remains controlled, speech coherent, occasional pauses suggest significant internal effort. As a human being, I can tell you that each sentence landed like a hand laid carefully on a wound.

She spoke of Sarah’s mind — “impossible” was the word she chose — and of the way she had taken up space without apology. She spoke, quite calmly, of fury: at the absence of a helmet, at the decision to climb the scaffold alone, at the confidence that she could bend metal, and perhaps gravity, to her will.

It is not fashionable, at funerals of the young, to be angry at the dead. Grief is supposed to be clean, especially when sponsored by institutions. Lillian contaminated the occasion with something closer to truth. The art people shifted in their seats. One or two looked down at their programmes, as if a different script might be hiding in the fine print.

Afterwards, outside in the narrow garden with its ornamental grasses and tasteful bench, the rain finally committed to being rain. Miles sat heavily, hands over his face, shoulders shaking. Lillian kept one paw — hand; she is more woman than species to me now, whatever her markings — on his back. She did not pat or shush or tell him to be strong. She simply stayed beside him, the way she had been beside him in the lounge when the penguins were still marching.

The sculpture arrived at the home two weeks later, on my shift. This is the kind of coincidence my colleagues chalk up to fate and I ascribe to the rota. Either way, I watched the delivery men wrestle the crate through the lobby with the practised boredom of people who move expensive, fragile objects for a living.

The label on the side read FOR THEM in thick, blocky handwriting. The same handwriting, I realised, as the notes in the margins of the catalogue I had seen on Miles’ knees. All caps, as if shouting through time.

We do not have a policy for art installations in residents’ rooms. We have policies for oxygen cylinders and sharps disposal, for falls risk and pressure sores. No one has yet written a guideline for how much bronze and glass a person is allowed to keep by their bed when they are dying.

“We’ll put it by the window,” Lillian said. There was no question mark.

When they peeled off the bubble wrap and lifted away the final sheet of foam, I understood the risk Sarah had taken. The piece was small enough to sit on the low chest of drawers, no larger than a bedside lamp, but it contained more weight than some of our residents’ entire lives. Two curved bronze forms leaned towards each other, not quite touching, their inner surfaces lined with glass that caught the light in shards. There was an opening between them, a hollow that read, to my unschooled eye, as both shelter and demand.

“It’s her,” Lillian said. Not “it reminds me of her” or “she would have liked this.” Simply: it is her.

I have been taught to be sceptical of that kind of identification. In nursing school they call it projection. In the quieter conversations we have with each other in the staff room, after too much coffee and not enough sleep, we call it survival. People pour themselves into objects because the alternative — acknowledging that flesh does not keep — is intolerable.

Still, even I could see her there, in the angle of the curves, in the refusal of the surfaces to give you a single clean reflection. You saw yourself, looking at it, but distorted, doubled, fragmented. It was, I suppose, fitting that the last work she made for them was a device that forced them to see their own faces when they tried to see hers.

Nights in care homes are longer than days. The institutional lighting flattens everything. Corridors extend beyond what seems spatially possible. I have grown used to moving through that stretched-out time with a trolley, a drug chart, an endless supply of small talk.

So when I heard their voices raised after midnight, I stopped.

I am not proud of this. I should have knocked, asked if they needed anything, given them the dignity of choosing whether to let me intrude. Instead I stood in the sliver of darkness outside their door and listened.

“I don’t want to call you. I don’t want help.”

Lillian, breathless, furious. The anger was not directed at him, not exactly; it was the kind of anger people reserve for their own failure to live up to an image. She had been, for weeks, the composed one. The one who made lists, who dealt with solicitors, who read letters aloud. Here, at last, she admitted the cost.

“Lillian, love—”

“Don’t love me right now. Don’t be kind. I can’t take it.”

There it was, the sentence I have heard in different languages, different wards, from different mouths. A request to postpone gentleness until it no longer feels like an indictment. We assume people want comfort. Often they want permission to fall apart.

“We trained her,” Lillian said, and here I stopped breathing. “We trained her to run headlong into everything. We told her she could bend the world. That she didn’t have to wait for permission. We built her that way.”

It is an unfashionable thought, in a culture that tells daughters to lean in. To wonder whether the confidence we spend years cultivating in them might be, in certain circumstances, a risk factor. I would not dare write that in a care plan. Lillian said it in the dark, to the one person who could not contradict her without contradicting his own grief.

“We raised her to live,” Miles said. “Not to survive the world by shrinking in it.”

The argument — if it was an argument — dwindled into sounds rather than words. The thump of a body collapsing into a chair. The soft clatter of a cane against wood. The rustle of fabric as two people shifted their weight until it fit together again.

I moved on then, guiltily, taking my blood pressure cuff and my notes. But their conversation followed me down the corridor, threaded itself through the routine of obs and night checks. It lodged somewhere between my professional and personal selves, next to all the other questions we are not paid to answer: what does it mean to encourage someone to be fully themselves in a world that is structurally hostile to that self? What, precisely, were they apologising for?

The letter arrived on an afternoon that might as well have been any other. The solicitor was younger than some of our residents, in a suit that did not quite fit across the shoulders. He held the envelope with the wary respect of someone handling live ammunition.

“Dated three years ago,” he told them. “She asked me to deliver it… in the event.”

The event. As if death were a scheduling issue.

I did not stay. I have, despite everything I have just confessed, some sense of propriety. But our walls are thin. Voices travel. At the drug trolley I heard Lillian begin to read aloud, because of course she did. There are some texts you cannot absorb alone.

I caught fragments as I passed their door on my way to the next room. Sarah’s voice, wry even on paper: if you’re hearing this, I did something stupid; do not let them name a bench after me; for God’s sake don’t give it all to a cat charity. Jokes about rehab and the family’s unfortunate habit of forgiving her. An insistence that she had never, not once, doubted that she was loved. Instructions, half-serious, about how long they were allowed to wallow before they had to go back to arguing about biscuits and politics.

It was, in its way, the most ethical thing I have ever seen an artist do. She anticipated the audience that mattered most — two elderly anthros in a care home lounge — and gave them a script that did not erase her responsibility for her own death, but did not let them take all of it either.

Spring came the way it always does in London: abruptly, with blossom on trees that had looked dead all winter and a sudden, disorienting warmth on the skin. Our garden, which had been a patch of mud and cigarette ends for months, transformed almost overnight into something photographic. The cherry tree fluffed itself into improbable pink. The residents started asking to be wheeled out again.

I found the Fergusons under that tree one late afternoon, on a break between medications and a care review. They had not asked for help. They had simply walked, slowly, down the ramp and along the path, that old choreography still functioning despite everything. They sat close together on the bench, hands intertwined, watching the petals fall.

If this were a hospital note, I would write: mood brighter, affect congruent, engaging in meaningful activity. It would be a lie of omission. What I saw was not brightness, exactly. It was something quieter, more interesting: the way people look when they have accepted, if not forgiven, the fact that the world can end on an ordinary Tuesday and then continue without their consent.

They were discussing, I learned later, where to send Sarah’s final collection. Which gallery would handle it with the right combination of reverence and competence. They talked about contracts and curators, about archives and access. They did not, as far as I could tell, talk about scaffolding.

I watched them through the conservatory glass until I felt I was trespassing again. Then I went back to my charts, to my obs, to the repetition that structures my days. I gave Mrs Patel her evening insulin. I checked Mr Levy’s dressings. I signed my name in the boxes that prove I have done what I am paid to do.

But the image stays with me when the shifts blur and the penguins on the television march in their endless loop. Two old bodies under a tree, a badger and a German Shepherd who have spent a lifetime making space for each other, now learning how to carry a third presence between them, invisible and insistent. Petals falling around them like a secret celebration.

We like to tell ourselves, in this line of work, that love does not end when life does. It is the kind of sentiment that fits well on leaflets in waiting rooms. What I have seen is less tidy. Love changes register. It moves from being something you perform with another person to something you rehearse with yourself. It becomes, to borrow the critical vocabulary my younger colleagues use about art, a question of how to curate absence.

The Fergusons have, in their way, become experts in that curation. They live now with a sculpture that reflects their own faces back at them, with a letter that refuses them certain kinds of self-hatred, with a tree that blooms on schedule no matter who is there to sit under it. They live, too, with the knowledge that they raised a daughter so alive that the world, ultimately, could not contain her safely.

I am only a nurse. My job is to measure pulses, manage medications, negotiate with families about visiting times. Yet I think I will remember them longer than many of the more dramatic cases from my hospital days. Not because their grief was singular — it was not; scaffoldings fail, daughters die at work, parents outlive children more often than we admit — but because they allowed that grief to alter them without calcifying into performance.

If there is a right way to walk into a room like that, I have not yet found it. I suspect, after all these years, that the rightness lies not in the choreography but in the willingness to stay and watch, to let what you see revise what you thought you knew about how people break and how, slowly, they continue.
