# The Last Five Minutes

The alert had come too late for anyone to do anything with it.
No sirens, no coordinated broadcast. Just a sudden, choking silence across the networks, followed by a single line of text on every device in the house:

**IMPACT IN FIVE MINUTES. SEEK SHELTER.**

Shelter. As if walls could matter. As if there were any safe place in the world.
Lillian had stood in the doorway between the kitchen and the living room, staring at the message on her phone as if it were a prank. The house was too peaceful for the end of the world: afternoon light spilling across the tiles, the faint smell of tea still drifting from the pot she’d set down minutes earlier.
Miles found her like that. He didn’t ask questions. He just touched her arm, gentle but firm, and guided her toward the bedroom.
Their only shelter.
They lay down on top of the blankets, still dressed, still wearing their shoes. No time to undress, no ritual to follow. The bed dipped under their combined weight, familiar and mundane.
Miles curled behind her, one arm draped across her middle. Lillian held onto his wrist with both hands, gripping tight not to keep him from leaving, but because holding him made the fear bearable.
The house breathed with them. A low, distant vibration trembled through the floorboards.
“Are you scared?” she whispered through her own shivers.
Miles’ voice was raw, low. “Yeah.”
She swallowed. “Me too.”
He pressed his forehead between her shoulder blades. They stayed like that for a long moment, listening to the distant rumble outside—the sound of something vast and ancient coming undone.
Another tremor rolled through the house. The bedside lamp toppled with a soft thud. A hairline fracture zigzagged down the wall above the wardrobe.
Miles tightened his hold around her as if the Earth’s shaking were trying to pull them apart.
Lillian turned toward him, shifting so they faced each other. Their snouts nearly brushed. Her eyes were wide, shimmering with the kind of fear that strips away adulthood and leaves only something young and breakable.
Miles cupped her cheek. His thumb trembled where it rested under her eye.
The floor lurched again.
She didn’t look away.
“I don’t want to die,” she breathed.
Miles’ throat worked, like he had to swallow around something sharp. “I know. Me neither.”
No promises. No comfort. Just truth.
The world outside groaned, deep and seismic, like mountains tearing open.
Lillian’s fingers found Miles’. She wove their paws together, squeezing hard. “I’m glad it’s you,” she said. Quiet. Unsteady. Utterly sincere.
Miles’ breath broke on the way out. “I’m right here.”
They pressed their foreheads together. Their breaths synced without thinking—shaky, uneven, terrified.
The clock on the wall kept ticking, absurdly normal. Each click a countdown.
The final tremor wasn’t a tremor at all. It was everything: the ground heaving, the window frame rattling, the bed jolting under them.
They clung to each other, bodies pressed impossibly close, as if proximity could stop the universe from splitting.
A white glow flared at the edges of the curtained window, swallowing the colors in the room.
Miles whispered her name. It barely carried over the roar.
Lillian shut her eyes and held his paw with all the strength she had left.
