# Silverburn

## 1 · The Wrong Step

The path shouldn’t have been difficult.

It was the same track Lillian had walked as a teenager when she needed to escape the house for an hour, the same ribbon of dirt and stones that had once felt almost too tame after the wilder bits of Pentlands and Borders. A sensible hill, close enough to the city that you could see the Forth bridges if the air was clear, far enough that the hum of traffic thinned to something more like weather.

Today it was simply there, waiting, which ought to have made it harmless.

The light was the cool, forgiving kind she still thought of as Scottish—even after twenty years of London’s yellowed glare, her body recognised this pearly overcast as home. The grass was wet but not treacherous, the mud on the lower slopes tacky underfoot, the rocks dry and freckled with lichen higher up. She had done everything right: good boots, proper socks, a stick she'd borrowed from the stand by her parents’ back door rather than attempting the climb without a third point of contact.

And yet.

“You sure this is the route?” Miles asked, not because he doubted her, she knew that, but because engineers were constitutionally incapable of leaving a load-bearing decision unqueried.

He stood a step below her on the narrow track, shoulders filling the space between the gorse on one side and open drop on the other, his ears pricked forward under his beanie. His breath came in small white bursts. He’d unzipped his jacket despite the cold.

“Positive,” she said. “I used to come up here with Milla when we were meant to be revising. I know every bush on this hill.”

“You know them as a seventeen-year-old,” he said mildly. “The hill hasn’t changed. You have.”

She glanced back at him, one eyebrow lifting over the rim of her glasses. “Rude,” she said.

“Accurate,” he replied, and gave her that apologetic half-smile which always, irritatingly, softened the sting of truth.

Her body had changed. There was no point pretending otherwise. At forty-two her weight sat deeper in her joints, her badger solidity more pronounced. The same broad shoulders, the same wide hips, but more of her—more softness at her middle, more curve at the back of her arms, more belly resting against the waistband of her walking trousers. She was strong, but the strength had a different texture now. Less spring, more momentum.

Still, this was her hill. The familiarity of it had tugged at her from the moment they’d driven past the Silverburn turn-off the day before, the sign a brief flash of white on green between roundabouts. They were here to check on the house, ostensibly—to air it out, to make lists of things her parents should get fixed, to reassure themselves that the alarm still worked. But she had wanted this bit from the moment they booked the train: one afternoon, just the two of them, walking until the city fell away.

“If you’re worried,” she said, “you can go behind me and catch me if I roll downhill. Use that shepherd core.”

“That’s not how gravity works,” he said, but he did step closer until she could feel the heat of him at her back.

They climbed. The path steepened, then levelled. Her breath settled into a manageable pattern. The old ache in her left knee—souvenir of a badly timed leap off a stage monitor in 2011—murmured a protest, then quietened as the joint warmed. Her ankle, the right one, felt solid. Her tail swayed in a slow counter-balance behind her.

Silverburn spread out below them in strips of field and housing estate, the little shopping centre like a child’s toy set dropped between the two. Beyond that, the grey spine of the bypass; beyond that, Edinburgh, its sandstone softened by mist.

“It’s smaller,” Miles said after a while. “Your hill.”

“You’re taller,” she said. “And we used to start from further down. I’m cheating; Dad drove us halfway.”

He snorted. “Of course he did.”

They stopped at a rock outcrop where the wind hit harder. Lillian turned to face him, leaning on her stick. He reached automatically for the strap of her rucksack, easing it off one shoulder to check the weight.

“You didn’t need to bring the thermos as well,” he said. “We’re only out for a couple of hours.”

“We’re in Scotland,” she said. “There is no such thing as ‘only’ when it comes to being prepared.”

He rolled his eyes and kissed the tip of her muzzle. The gesture was brief, domestic, tucked into the shelter of the rocks where the wind couldn’t quite reach—a small proof that their bodies still fitted together without thought.

It should have been the peak moment. If he told the story later, this was where it would crescendo: a middle-aged couple on a hill, joking about thermoses, the city below them, everything more or less intact.

The wrong step came three minutes later, on the way down.

It wasn’t dramatic. There was no cinematic slip on scree, no flailing arms or slow-motion plummet. Her boot met a patch of flattened grass, damp and slick, just as the path tilted. The angle was minor, the sort of adjustment her body had made without comment thousands of times. This time, something miscalculated. Her heel skidded. Her weight tipped. She felt the jolt ripple up from ankle to knee to hip, a misfired chord through familiar strings.

There was a small, efficient noise from inside her leg—deep, not loud; like a knuckle cracking in a joint she hadn’t known she had.

“Oh,” she said.

Her stick bit into the mud. Her other foot planted hard. For a second her whole body hung in a narrow balance between sitting down on purpose and going down by default.

Miles’ hand was on her elbow almost before she’d finished the vowel. The cane in his other hand jabbed into the ground, anchoring them both.

“Lill.”

“I’m fine,” she said automatically. It was a reflex as ingrained as any of his.

She stood still, breathing, taking inventory. The world did its usual things around her: wind in the grass, distant dog bark, a crow’s rough call somewhere overhead. Her ankle throbbed in a way that was, at first, indistinguishable from the regular complaints of a limb asked to work. Then the pain sharpened, brightening like a light being turned up.

She shifted her weight experimentally onto the injured leg. The joint answered with a precise, furious bolt that made her vision stutter.

“All right,” she said. “Not fine.”

Miles’ grip on her elbow tightened. He didn’t say “hospital”. Not yet. He knew her too well.

“Let’s sit,” he said instead. “Just for a minute. We’re not in a rush.”

They eased themselves onto a low rock, an awkward choreography with his knee and her ankle negotiating the angles. The stone was cold through her trousers; she could feel moisture seeping in. The air smelled of wet earth and last year’s heather.

“Scale of one to ten?” he asked. “Ten being ‘I want morphine, please’.”

She flexed her ankle a fraction and had to clamp her jaw shut on a noise. “Four,” she said, when she could speak. “Climbing towards five in a show-off way.”

He watched her face rather than her leg; it was one of the things she loved about him, even when it irritated her. He took people’s reports of their own pain seriously, but he also understood that her numbers were calibrated differently. This was the man who had once finished a gig with a cracked rib because the kit hadn’t been packed in a way that made leaving sound plausible.

“We should head back,” he said. “Slowly.”

“I can go on,” she said, before she had time to weigh the sentence. The old stubbornness spoke first, the part of her that had powered through flu to teach, through cramps to perform, through exhaustion to sit in planning meetings she could have sent an email for.

He tilted his head. “Why?”

The question disarmed her more than any argument. Why. Not can you, or should you, but what would this prove, and to whom.

She looked down at her ankle. Already, under the fur and fabric, she could feel something swelling, the tightness of an overinflated drumhead. The stick lay at her feet, mud on its ferrule. Beyond it, the path dropped away towards the town, a line of small decisions she would have to make with every step.

“Because we’re here,” she said softly. “Because I wanted this.”

He didn’t tell her that wanting and being able were two different things. He had spent enough years listening to other people make that distinction for her body. He simply waited, his hand still on her elbow, his presence as steady as the rock under them.

The wind gusted once, bringing with it the smell of the river. Below, a car door slammed. Someone laughed. Life went on, unaware of the small recalibration happening halfway up the hill.

“All right,” she said. “We go back. Slowly.”

She lifted the stick, tested the angle. When she stood, the joint screamed, then grudgingly settled into a negotiated compromise. She could bear weight, if she thought about each placement. The pain was there, insistent but containable, a new thread woven through the familiar pattern of her stride.

They descended like that: Lillian setting the pace, Miles half a step behind and to her bad side, his hand hovering at her back without quite touching, ready to arrest any new slide. The hill did what hills always do—it ignored them utterly. The path twisted. The grass brushed her calves. Her ankle complained. With each careful step she felt the edges of herself more sharply: where her body ended, where the ground began, where the past slid into the present.

By the time they reached the car, her four had become a six.


## 2 · Refusal

The house smelled wrong before they opened the door.

It wasn’t a bad smell, exactly. No damp, no mould, no telltale sweetness of something gone off. Just the absence of the usual layers. No recent cooking, no fresh tea, no floral blast from her mother’s questionable plug-in diffusers. The air that greeted them when Miles turned the key was stale in a way that made the house feel like a paused video—an image waiting for someone to press play.

“Windows first,” he said, and held the door wide to let her limp in ahead of him.

She stepped over the threshold more cautiously than pride wanted. The hallway was exactly as she remembered: the same narrow runner on the floor, the same framed photograph of Edinburgh Castle at dusk, the same dent in the skirting where she’d once swung a schoolbag harder than intended. Her ankle throbbed in time with her pulse, a deep, nagging beat under the lighter aches of knees and hips.

“Sit,” Miles said. “I’ll do the rounds.”

“I can—”

“Lill.”

The way he said her name carried a whole paragraph: You limped from the car to the door. You nearly swore in front of Mrs McTavish next door when you slipped on that leaf. This is not the hill to die on. Sit down.

She lowered herself onto the bench by the radiator, the one her father had built from reclaimed church pews. Her tail flicked against the wood. The house ticked around her as Miles moved from room to room, opening windows, muttering about airing. Cold March air crept in under the door and up her trouser leg. She could hear the faint rattle of the kitchen sash, the clank of the living-room casements, each sound reconnecting the house to the outside world.

She pressed her thumb into the tendon above her ankle, testing the pain. The joint protested. A slow heat was building there now, the kind that suggested swelling, inflammation, an internal argument between bone and soft tissue.

It would pass. Sprains did. She had had enough rehearsal injuries over the years to know the cycle: pain, ice, rest, resentment, slow return, too early push, fresh complaint, repeat. What she didn’t have, at the moment, was patience for hospitals. Waiting rooms, fluorescent lights, triage nurses who would glance at her chart and tell her, politely, to lose weight as if it were a sticking plaster she’d forgotten to remove.

Miles came back to the hall, cheeks pink from the cold air, ears flicking. “You’re clammy,” he said, setting his hand lightly on her forehead.

“I’m fine,” she said reflexively, and then, to head off the look, “It’s just adrenaline. And the hill. And your terrible jokes.”

He huffed. “You should let me take you to A&E.”

“Absolutely not,” she said. “We’ve just got here. We’re staying two nights. I am not spending my one day in Edinburgh in a waiting room smelling like Dettol and despair.”

“They have wheelchairs,” he said. “You like wheels.”

She gave him a flat stare. “I have spent twenty years avoiding being asked if I need a little cart for the big badger body,” she said. “I’ll manage.”

He sat beside her on the bench, the wood creaking under their combined weight in a familiar, comforting protest. His knee brushed hers. “Managing,” he said quietly, “is not the same as not making it worse.”

She looked down at her ankle again. The swelling was visible now, distorting the line of the boot. The leather strained a little at the laces. When she flexed her toes, the movement was possible but limited, as if someone had poured wet plaster around the joint and it was setting in place.

“We’ll ice it,” she said. “Elevation, anti-inflammatory, all that good BBC health page advice. If it’s worse in the morning, we’ll reconsider.”

He watched her, weighing the words. It was one of the fundamental negotiations of their marriage: her right to judge what she could and couldn’t do with her own body, his responsibility as the person who would have to pick up the pieces if she misjudged. They had had versions of this conversation about tours, about promotions, about protest marches, about kinks and calories and every other decision that carried weight.

“All right,” he said finally. “One night. But you let me fuss.”

“You always fuss,” she said. “It’s your defining characteristic.”

“Rude,” he said mildly, and squeezed her hand.

He carried her bag upstairs despite her protests, then came back for her with a chair from the dining room. They did not bother with the stairs lift her parents had had installed; she refused to think of that as an option for herself yet. Instead, she took the steps slowly, one at a time, both paws on the banister, his presence behind her a solid reassurance. By the time they reached the landing, sweat prickled under her fur despite the chill.

The bedroom that had once been hers was now “the spare room”: neutral walls, neutral bedding, her parents’ careful erasure of the more obvious traces of her teenage years. Still, the bones of it were the same. The window looked out over the back garden, the wardrobe door stuck slightly at the bottom, the ceiling had the faint water stain from the leak in ’98.

Miles set the chair at the foot of the bed, fussed with pillows, then went downstairs for ice. She unlaced her boot with fingers that felt clumsier than they should. When she peeled sock from fur, the ankle underneath was already puffy, the markings there blurred.

She exhaled slowly through her nose. Not catastrophic. Unpleasant. A problem for later, when the adrenaline wore off and the house settled.

“You’re thinking terrible thoughts,” Miles said when he came back up, tray balanced on one hand, tea towel-wrapped ice pack on the other.

“You don’t know that,” she said.

“You have the ‘if I ignore it, it might disappear or kill me, either way it’s sorted’ face,” he said. “I married that face. I know it.”

She laughed despite herself. The sound loosened something in her chest. “Shut up and give me the ice,” she said.

He did, gently, adjusting the angle of the pillow under her calf until the joint lay higher than her heart. Practical magic. Her body eased into the arrangement with the reluctant gratitude of a diva accepting a seat.

“We’ll see how it is in the morning,” he said again, more to reassure himself than her.

Tomorrow, he would go back to London for two days of meetings he couldn’t move. She would stay here, in Silverburn, in the childhood house that had always made her feel both snug and watched. That had been the plan from the beginning: she would have time with the house, with her own past, without him hovering.

She hadn’t planned on the hill inserting itself into that solitude in the form of pain.

“It’ll be fine,” she said, softer now. “Go and check the boiler, Mr Systems.”

He kissed her forehead, ears brushing her hair. “Yes, Professor,” he said.

When he left the room, the quiet that followed had a different weight. It carried the scent of the carpet cleaner her mother favoured and the faint metallic tang of old radiators, layered over with the sharper, fresher smell of melted ice against her fur.

Her ankle pulsed in the silence, marking time for the house.


## 3 · The House Without Witnesses

Miles left before eight, because that was what trains did: they left too early from Waverley, they arrived too late at King’s Cross, they sliced days into unhelpful segments. He had offered, of course, to rebook, to stay another day, to cancel the meetings. She had refused with the same stubbornness that had sent her onto the hill in the first place.

“You go,” she’d said, sitting up in bed with her ankle propped on a second pillow. “I’ll be fine. It’s not the first time I’ve been alone in this house.”

He had looked like he wanted to argue. Then he had looked at her ankle—less swollen this morning, but stiff—and at her face, and decided to trust the version of her that believed herself capable.

“Call me,” he’d said. “For anything.”

“Go,” she’d replied, and meant it.

Now the house settled around her in his absence.

Silverburn, in late morning, sounded different than it had when she was a teenager. The distant rush of the bypass was louder, there were more car doors, more delivery vans. Somewhere a dog barked in a half-hearted way. The occasional rumble of a tram reached even this far out when the wind turned. Inside, the house itself returned to its baseline noises: the fridge’s low hum, the boiler’s intermittent cough, the creak of old floorboards as they shifted fractionally in the temperature change.

She sat at the kitchen table, mug of tea between her paws, ankle resting on a third chair. The ice pack sat in a bowl nearby, pooled water faintly clouded. Her phone lay face down, its black back a small, stubborn square.

The kitchen was almost exactly as it had always been. The pine cupboards her father had built himself, the countertop her mother had insisted on replacing when Lillian was at university because she wanted something that “didn’t look like student living”. The same drawer sticks; the same one closes with a click; the same clock ticks slightly fast.

Being here without her parents felt like sneaking into a museum before opening hours. Every object awaited its usual handler. The kettle expected her mother’s hand. The coat rack by the door waited for her father’s flat cap, currently hanging instead in his new flat in the retirement complex across town.

She had grown used to visiting them elsewhere. To being the daughter who arrived with cake in a cardboard box, who admired the new stairlift, who plugged in extra lamps to combat institutional gloom. The house, though, had remained an idea, a postcard on the mental mantelpiece. Being in it again like this, with time and pain and nobody to mediate her relationship with it, felt like stepping into an old song at a different tempo.

She finished her tea, braced her paws on the arms of the chair, and stood. The ankle held. The pain was a dull, insistent ache now, like a bruise being pressed. She could walk, if she respected its sulk.

Fine. She would meet the house on those terms.

She moved slowly through the downstairs rooms, one hand always within reach of a wall or piece of furniture. The living room held its familiar shapes: the two-seater sofa, sagging a little, the armchair by the window, the low coffee table with its faint ring from the time she’d put down a mug of hot chocolate without a coaster and her mother had tutted, then never sanded it out. The shelves still held the same mixture of books and ornaments: her father’s history tomes, her mother’s gardening magazines, the ceramic badger she herself had made as a school project, its glaze slightly bubbled where she’d misjudged the firing.

“You’ve aged well,” she murmured, touching its ear.

Upstairs was more of a challenge. She took the steps sideways, one at a time, her injured foot planted, her good one doing most of the lifting. The banister was solid under her hand, the varnish worn smooth in the places where fingers had slid over it for thirty years.

The spare room—her old room—waited at the end of the landing. The poster of Kate Bush that had once dominated one wall was gone, as were the fairy lights and the string of postcards. In their place: a framed watercolour of a harbour, anonymous and inoffensive. The bed was new, or newish: a sensible double with a plain headboard. The wardrobe, however, was the same, its door sticking at the bottom.

She opened it, tugging past the resistance, and inhaled the smell of old wood and older fabric. Her parents had cleared most of her past out from here, relocating the clothes she’d left behind to charity shops long ago. A few things remained: a black dress that might once have been glamorous and now simply looked small; a suit with padded shoulders; a battered duffel coat.

She reached for the coat, fingers closing on the sleeve. The weight of it in her hand pulled her back briefly to a winter morning, leaving for Trinity with a scarf wound high and her head full of arpeggios. The house had smelled of toast and her father’s aftershave. The coat had smelled of city; it always did, by then.

Now it smelled of dust and disuse. She let the sleeve fall.

The bathroom mirror was less forgiving than the wardrobe. The glass had warped slightly over the years, humidity bowing it minutely at the centre. She stood in front of it, one hand on the sink, her ankle throbbing, and watched herself fragment at the edges.

She had seen her body in a thousand reflective surfaces: on stage, in dressing-room mirrors, in the black gloss of grand pianos, in the uncertain shimmer of puddles outside venues. She had trained herself not to flinch. This, though—the combination of angle, light, memory—caught her.

The badger in the mirror was solid in a way teenage Lillian had feared and secretly desired. Rounded cheeks, the lines at the corners of her eyes deeper than last time she’d really looked, the double chin softened but definite. Her shoulders still broad, powerful. Her chest generous under her T-shirt. Her belly, under the waistband of her leggings, not quite contained. Her tail, visible in the lower edge of the reflection, swayed slowly, a metronome.

Behind her, the bathroom showed itself in smaller details: the chipped tile near the loo, the towel rail slightly crooked, the toothbrush mug with the faded logo from some long-vanished festival. A body in a space that had once been too small for her teenage rage and now felt, if anything, too big for her careful movements.

She lifted her T-shirt, just enough to expose the bruise already blooming around her ankle. Purple and yellow, swelling against the pale fur. Her body’s own abstract painting, created without her consent.

“You daft cow,” she told herself in the mirror, without heat. The badger there looked back, unimpressed but not unkind.

The house said nothing. It offered its floors and walls and doorframes, its familiar door catches and light switches, as if to say: you know how to do this. You learned in me.

She lowered the T-shirt, turned away from the mirror, and began the small work of the day: negotiating the geography of a house that remembered her before she was this version of herself, with a body that remembered everything that had happened since.


## 4 · Regression

By mid-afternoon, the ankle throbbed in a steady, resentful rhythm. Not sharp enough to be alarming; just insistent enough that she could not forget it. She had tried ignoring it in favour of tasks—running the washing machine once, just to be sure it still worked; checking the spare set of keys; assuring herself that the back door bolt still slid—but pain has its own kind of patience. It waited until she sat down and then made its presence known again.

She encamped in the living room, on the sofa that had seen the better part of her adolescence. The cushions sagged in familiar places. The upholstery smelled faintly of old fabric and the lavender spray her mother favoured. She built herself a small nest: pillows under her calf, ice within reach, a folded towel under her heel so the rough weave of the cushion didn’t catch her fur.

The coffee table in front of her held the remnants of her parents’ recent lives here: her father’s crossword book, a gardening magazine with a circle around an article about slug deterrents, a mug with a ring of tannin at the bottom. The TV remote lay on the arm of the sofa, exactly where her mother always left it, as if she might walk back in any minute and resume battle with it.

She had brought work, of course. A folder from the Academy with term planning notes, a slim volume of scores she meant to annotate, a book one of her students had insisted she read. They sat neatly stacked on the side table, untouched.

Instead, she found herself watching the pattern of light on the ceiling as the clouds shifted. The house creaked in the small, satisfying ways of a structure adjusting to heat, cold, damp, movement. Each sound tugged a memory.

Here, on this sofa, she had once curled herself around a school report, the words “could apply herself more consistently” underlined in red. In that chair, her mother had sat shelling peas, the rhythm of her hands soothing and maddening. On that patch of worn carpet, she and Miles had danced badly to some scratchy vinyl at seventeen, arms full of each other and the future.

Her ankle pulsed. Her thoughts slid sideways.

Teenage Lillian had hated this house, briefly, as only a teenager could. Hated its neatness, its smallness, the way it seemed to contain expectations about what a good girl should be. She had stomped up those stairs with righteous fury after arguments about curfews, about boys, about music. She had slammed that bedroom door so often that the paint around the frame still carried the faint spider-web cracks.

She tried to summon specific scenes. The time she’d told her parents she wanted to apply to Trinity instead of a “proper” university. The night they’d driven her to her first gig and sat in the back, trying not to embarrass her. The afternoon her mother had found the first flyer for a protest and asked, not unkindly, whether she was trying to get arrested already.

The memories arrived, but without the emotional charge they’d once carried. They felt like scenes from a play she had been in long ago, the lines still in her mouth but no longer attached to actual stakes.

The body, however, remembered differently. Lying there, ankle aching, the house’s air in her lungs, she felt a familiar adolescent restlessness rising in her chest. The sense of being too big for the space—not physically; the sofa held her weight fine—but conceptually. Of wanting to push against walls that had nothing to do with plasterboard.

Back then, the solution had been to leave: to get on a bus, to go into town, to stand in record shops and pretend that being in proximity to the music industry meant she already belonged. Later, it had been to go to London, to share flats with too many people and not enough hot water, to stake a claim on stages that were, technically, not designed for bodies like hers.

Now, with her ankle telling her exactly how far she wasn’t going, the impulse had nowhere obvious to go.

She reached for the Academy folder, flipped it open, then closed it again. It belonged to the life back down south, the one where she was Head of Department, where her days were measured out in lesson plans and meetings and emails about funding. That version of her was capable, authoritative, recognisable to herself.

Here, on this sofa, in socks and an old hoodie, with an ice pack slowly sweating into a tea towel, she felt more like the Lillian who had once hidden behind this piece of furniture to avoid an unexpected visitor. Not in competence—she had more of that now; it ran under her skin like a second bloodstream—but in vulnerability. In the sense that, if she slipped, there might not be a net.

She closed her eyes. The house breathed around her. The radiator clicked. Somewhere, a pipe sighed.

Was this regression, she wondered, or simply another layer in the ongoing, messy business of being the same person over time. The ankle certainly thought it was a step backwards. Her mind, with its habits of analysis and music, wanted to make a pattern of it: injury pulling her back into an old space, forcing a confrontation with the self that had first dreamed of leaving.

She opened her eyes again. The ceiling was still there. The bruise at her ankle throbbed in time with her heart.

“All right,” she said quietly, to the empty room. “What do you want from me?”

The house did not answer. It simply continued to be itself: solid, slightly too warm, full of objects that had outlived their original purpose. A stage set waiting for someone to walk on and choose which role to play.


## 5 · The Mirror Scene

The bathroom mirror, later, did not care about any of that. Its job was simple: reflect what stood before it, distorted only by its own age.

She went in after a slow, careful trek up the stairs, her ankle complaining at each step. The grab rail her parents had had installed by the bath was a small insult and a quiet relief under her paw. She used it without comment.

In the mirror, the badger looking back at her braced both paws on the sink. Steam from the hot tap fogged the lower corners of the glass, softening the line of her tail where it curved behind her. Above, her face remained clear.

Forty-two, the mirror said. Not unkindly. Just factual.

Her fur around the eyes was a little thinner than she remembered. The masked markings had softened at the edges, the white more prominent against the dark. The bags under her eyes spoke of late-night marking and early-morning trains as much as age. Her mouth had the beginnings of lines carved by equal parts laughter and annoyance.

She lifted her T-shirt again, this time deliberately. The bathroom’s harsh overhead light picked out every curve, every fold. Breasts, heavy and familiar in their sports bra. Belly, rounded, resting over the waistband of her leggings in a way teenage Lillian would have sworn she would never allow. Hips, solid. Thighs, pressed together. The ankle, swollen and discoloured, anchored it all in current time.

Her body had carried her through gigs and protests, through teaching days that ran into evenings, through nights of love and grief and all the messy bits in between. It had not done so unscathed. The scar on her knee from that monitor incident. The faint white lines on her belly from late growth spurts, from weight gained during tour years and never entirely lost. The old, deep ache in her lower back when she stood too long at the piano.

“You did this,” she told her reflection quietly. “You brought us up the hill. You brought us down again. You’ve been asking for gentler surfaces for a while, and I pretended not to hear.”

The badger in the mirror did not argue. She tilted her head slightly, as if considering the point.

She thought of her students’ bodies, all ages and sizes, slumping in practice rooms, straightening on stage. She had spent years telling them that their worth did not hinge on the narrowest version of themselves, that they deserved stages and seats and clothes that accepted their actual shape. She had fought venue managers about seating, airline staff about belt extenders, festival organisers about accessible backstage routes.

She had, in short, made a career of telling other people that they were allowed to take up space.

And here she was, in her parents’ bathroom, looking at her own body as if it were an enemy for the crime of reminding her that gravity existed.

“Hypocrite,” she said mildly, and turned the tap off.

The bruise around her ankle had deepened, the colours richer now, the skin hot to the touch. She laid her fingers gently against it. Pain flared bright, then settled. Her body’s way of saying: acknowledged.

She wrapped the joint in a compression bandage she’d found in the old first-aid tin, the packet yellowed but the elastic still sound. The act felt practical, almost tender. She smoothed the fabric over fur, making sure it sat snug but not cutting into anything. Her hands knew this kind of work; they had done it for Miles when he’d strained a wrist lugging amps, for Sarah when she’d fallen off a bike, for Asim when he’d insisted on attempting stage dives in venues designed for folk evenings.

Back in the mirror, the bandage looked neat, the bruise hidden. The rest of her remained as she was. No sudden transformation. No cinematic swelling music. Just a badger in a bathroom, getting older.

The regression, she realised, was not in becoming this version of herself in this house. It was in the temptation to read her body the way she had at sixteen: as something to fight, to discipline, to mould into worthiness. That script was still there, apparently. Muscle memory wasn’t just in the legs.

“We’re not doing that,” she told her reflection. “We’ve done that. It was boring.”

The badger in the mirror almost smiled.


## 6 · The Attempted Task

She decided, later, to cook something simple. Partly because she was hungry and the biscuits in the cupboard did not count as a meal, and partly because she wanted a task with steps and an outcome—something the house and her body could collaborate on rather than simply coexist in.

Soup, she decided. Lentil, the way her mother made it. The ingredients were basic enough that the risk of failure was low, and the process was familiar enough to be comforting without requiring full cognitive capacity.

Standing at the counter, though, she felt the first wobble in that plan.

The kitchen units had been installed when she was a teenager, at a height that suited her mother. They had never felt particularly low then; she’d been shorter, her perspective different. Now, with the extra curve of her belly and the ache in her ankle, she realised how much of her comfortable London kitchen depended on the fact that they had designed it around her and Miles’ actual bodies.

Here, she had to lean forward more than usual to chop an onion. The weight shift sent a series of complaints up her leg. She adjusted: moved the chopping board closer to the edge, widened her stance, braced her good foot further back. Small system recalibrations. Miles would have been proud.

The knife moved through the onion with satisfying precision. The sizzle as it hit the pan with oil was a familiar chorus. Carrot, celery, garlic followed. The smell filled the kitchen quickly: savoury, sweet, grounding. She measured out red lentils from the jar her mother kept in the larder, their small orange bodies ticking against the metal scoop.

By the time she reached for the stock pot on the top shelf, though, her ankle had moved from acceptable complaint to sharp protest. She stretched up anyway, fingers just brushing the handle. Pain flared as she rose onto the toes of her bad foot.

“Don’t be an idiot,” she told herself, out loud this time.

She dropped back onto her heels, breathing through her teeth. The stock pot remained stubbornly out of reach.

In her own kitchen, the pots she used regularly lived on a lower shelf. She and Miles had organised it that way after one too many near misses with wobbling stools. Here, her mother’s logic applied: the big pots up high, the things you only needed occasionally tucked out of everyday path.

It would be a simple thing to drag a chair across, to climb, to ignore the way her ankle already throbbed. She had done far worse with far fewer complaints from her joints in the past. That was, she realised, the problem. Entire decades of ignoring minor aches in favour of getting the job done had trained her into these exact moments.

She stared at the pot. Then she stared at the pan where the onions were beginning to catch. The smell edged towards burnt.

“Right,” she said. “Fine.”

She turned off the heat, limped to the fridge, and took out the Tupperware her mother had labelled LENTIL SOUP, 12/02 in a familiar, looping hand. The freezer held three more identical boxes. Her mother had always cooked as if there were more mouths in the house than there actually were.

Lillian set the box on the counter, left the onions to cool in the pan, and put the Tupperware in the microwave. The whirr of it filled the kitchen. Her ankle pulsed in time with its rotation.

Failure, she thought, watching the plastic container fog at the edges, came in funny sizes. This wasn’t a catastrophe. No-one would go hungry. The soup would still be eaten. The task was technically done. But the little, sharp humiliation of not being able to reach a pot in her parents’ kitchen lodged somewhere under her sternum.

She tried, briefly, to reroute it. To tell herself that using the resources available—her mother’s prepared food, her own common sense—was a form of competence. It worked in theory. Her body, still wired for old scripts, filed the moment under “things you ought to have been able to do”.

The microwave beeped. Steam burst out when she peeled back the lid. The smell was almost identical to her own soup, which wasn’t surprising; she’d learned the recipe here. Lentils, carrot, a bay leaf, a pinch of smoked paprika she’d introduced to her mother years ago as a treat.

She poured the defrosted soup into a clean pot sitting at a reachable height and warmed it on the hob. The onions she’d half-cooked went into a bowl for later, a small, stubborn refusal to waste effort. The soup tasted fine when she sat at the table, ankle up, spoon moving from bowl to mouth in slow, deliberate motions.

It was a small failure. It would not make a story. When she spoke to Miles later, she would probably skip that part entirely. Yet it sat there, between her ribs, a reminder that her sense of herself as infinitely competent in domestic spaces conditional on her body doing what she expected without question.

The body, apparently, had entered its questioning phase.


## 7 · Memory Without Comfort

The box lived under the bed, where boxes always did in this house. She found it more by inevitability than intent. There are only so many tasks you can do in a childhood bedroom before you start looking for the physical archive of yourself.

The cardboard was soft at the corners, the lid sagging a little in the middle. Her mother’s handwriting ran across one side in marker: LILLIAN’S SCHOOL STUFF. She sat on the edge of the bed, ankle extended, and pulled the box towards her with the heel of her good foot.

Inside, the past lay in layers. Exercise books with her name in loopy adolescent script. Programmes from school concerts. A handful of badges. A photograph of her at thirteen, holding a clarinet and scowling at the camera in a way that suggested the scowl was more about the hair clip than the instrument.

She lifted things out carefully, the way you handle paper that has lived too long in one place. A music theory workbook with her teacher’s red ticks marching down the margins. A copy of a school report: “Lillian continues to show strong aptitude in music and English. She can be forthright in her opinions.” Her mother had underlined that last sentence with a biro and written “yes” in the margin.

At the bottom of the box she found a notebook she didn’t immediately recognise. The cover was cheap cardboard, the spiral metal slightly bent. Inside, on the first page, her sixteen-year-old self had written, in capitals: PLANS.

She smiled despite herself. Of course. She had always been a planner—set lists, lesson plans, protest routes. The arrogance of naming a notebook PLANS felt like a younger version of the same habit, less tempered by reality.

She read.

Trinity by 18.  
Royal Academy by 21.  
International touring by 25.  
Teaching later if necessary.  
Have house with garden (not too big).  
Never get stuck.

The handwriting was firm, decisive. This Lillian had not yet met a mortgage advisor. She had not yet sat in an audition waiting room and watched a panel look at her and then look at the floor. She had not yet gone on her first march. She had not yet met Sarah, or held Miles’ hand in a registrar’s office, or written emails about risk assessments for outdoor performances.

“Never get stuck,” she repeated under her breath.

Her ankle pulsed in time with the words.

It would have been easy, perhaps, to feel tenderness for this younger self. To see the certainty on the page and smile at its naïvety, to think, You had no idea, poor love. The emotion that rose in her chest was not tenderness, exactly. It was something closer to irritation, edged with envy.

This girl—this version of her—had assumed movement as a given. The idea of being physically unable to leave a house, even temporarily, would have struck her as melodramatic. Freedom, for her, had been a conceptual thing: the ability to choose where to live, who to love, what to sing about. The body had been the vehicle, not the question.

Lillian flipped further into the notebook. Song lyrics, half-finished. Lists of people she admired. Notes from a lesson where she had clearly been bored: tiny sketches of drums, pianos, stage lights. A paragraph about wanting to feel “like Kate Bush looks in that video, like I’m bigger than the room but in a good way”.

She did not recognise the certainty of this voice. She recognised the impulses—the desire for scale, the refusal to apologise—but the lack of doubt felt alien. Even now, with a successful band, a job she loved, a marriage that had survived both poverty and success, she did not operate without at least three simultaneous versions of every decision in her head.

Memory, she thought, had never offered her the simple comfort people in novels seemed to get. She could not look back and think, That was easier. It hadn’t been. It had been smaller, perhaps, but not simpler. Different constraints. Different scripts.

She closed the notebook. The box sat open on the bed, papers spilling around her. The room smelled faintly of dust and the perfume her mother used to wear to church.

She thought of Sarah, of the crates of sketchbooks and maquettes in her Manchester studio. What would her daughter make of her own teenage notebooks in twenty years? Would she see the same distance, the same fracture between the self that planned and the self that lived those plans in altered form?

The thought was both comforting and lonely. Comforting, because fracture seemed to be the rule, not the exception. Lonely, because there is a particular isolation in realising that no version of you has ever felt uncomplicatedly at home in your own narrative.

She put the notebook back in the box, closed the lid, and slid it under the bed again. The ankle protested the movement. The bruise throbbed in time with the house’s quiet.


## 8 · Night

Night deepened the house’s noises.

In London, their bedroom soundscape was layered: neighbours’ televisions, distant sirens, the low hum of city infrastructure. In Loughton, more recent years had given them owls, the odd fox scream, the rustle of the garden in wind. Here, in Silverburn, the house resettled into its old orchestra: the central heating’s occasional shudder, the creak of the landing, the faint rattle of a loose windowpane when the wind breathed just so.

She lay on her back in the spare-room bed, ankle elevated on two pillows, duvet tucked around her. The imprint of previous nights spent here—after Christmas dinners, between terms, during that brief stint when she’d moved back in before London called again—sat in the mattress. Her body recognised the give underneath her, the way the springs surrendered in specific spots.

Without Miles’ weight beside her, the bed felt larger, less anchored. Her paw, reaching out in the dark, met only cool sheet.

Pain at night behaved differently. During the day, there were distractions: tasks, lists, images. At two in the morning, the ankle’s ache filled more of the available space. It wasn’t unbearable. It was simply insistent. Each throb arrived like a reminder: you are here; you are fragile; you have limits.

Her mind, taking its cue from her body, began its own rehearsals.

She thought of the hill again, of the moment her boot had skidded. How quickly it had gone from nothing to too late. How little warning she’d had. How entirely unremarkable the ground had looked until it wasn’t.

Then, as minds do at that hour, hers looped to other moments of sudden tilt. The phone call about the Royal Academy rejection. The day she’d seen Sarah for the first time on that cold street, thin as anything and defiant. The doctor’s appointment where she’d been told, gently, that carrying a pregnancy to term was unlikely and that her womb was, in effect, a place dreams went to sulk. The night Asim had called from a protest kettle, voice steady but words edged with metal.

None of those had involved a misjudged patch of grass. All of them, though, had contained their own small, efficient noises—cracks in the expected that reverberated through joints, through choices, through time.

The house held those memories uneasily. It had not been there for all of them; some belonged to London, to tours, to hotel rooms, to academy offices. Yet this was where she had first learned that the ground could shift: exam results arriving, friendships fracturing, her parents’ marriage showing its hairline splits under the veneer of good manners.

She listened to the wind worrying at the window. The branch of the back-garden tree scratched intermittently at the glass. Pipes somewhere in the walls gurgled.

She considered calling Miles. The phone lay on the bedside table, screen dark, its small weight both comfort and accusation. He had told her to call. For anything. That was the deal.

She imagined the conversation. His sleepy voice, the rustle of hotel sheets, the click of a bedside lamp. His questions: pain level, mobility, whether she’d taken more ibuprofen. His concern, which always contained more system-mapping than panic. He would want data. She would want… what? Reassurance that she hadn’t made a stupid decision? Permission to feel sorry for herself?

She didn’t want to see his face in her mind’s eye when she described herself here, in this bed, in this house, pinned by her own ankle and her own history. She didn’t want to hand him that image to carry back to London with him, to see it reflected in the way he moved their next rehearsal chair, their next kitchen stool.

“Later,” she whispered into the dark. “When this is past tense.”

The ankle throbbed in gentle disagreement.

Outside, a fox barked once, sharp and startled, then fell silent. A car went past on the road, tyres hissing on damp tarmac. Somewhere, a neighbour’s toilet flushed. The small systems of the street continued their work.

Sleep, when it came, came in patches. Dream fragments: a stage on a slope, her piano rolling away; a house with no doors; her younger self running up a hill and looking back with impatience. Between those, stretches of wakefulness where she watched the shadows shift on the ceiling and felt the outline of her body more acutely than she usually allowed herself.

The house did not offer wisdom. It offered walls, pipes, floorboards, a bed. Her body offered the reminder that she did not live outside physics. Her mind offered questions. None of them resolved before morning.


## 9 · The Phone Call That Doesn’t Happen

In the morning, the pain had changed, not improved. It had settled, like silt in a glass of water. Less sharp at the surface, heavier at the bottom.

She tested the ankle before getting out of bed, flexing gently. The movement was possible, limited. Weight-bearing produced a deep, complaining ache, but no knife-stab. Acceptable, she decided, within the parameters of “we’re not going to hospital unless something is pointing in the wrong direction”.

The kettle downstairs took longer than usual to boil. Or perhaps she was simply more aware of the seconds passing. She made tea, leaning on the counter, one paw on the worktop, ankle braced. The mug was the one with the cartoon badger on it, a Christmas gift from Sarah years back, the glaze worn at the handle where fingers had gripped it thousands of times.

She sat at the kitchen table, phone beside her, and watched the screen remain blank.

Miles would be in a meeting by now, probably already rearranging chairs so that everyone could see the projector. He’d have checked his phone in the taxi, seen no red exclamation marks, and filed that under “no news is good news”. She could text him. She could send a photograph of the ankle, if she were feeling dramatic. She could call and say, It’s worse; come back; I’ve made a mistake.

She drank her tea instead.

Avoidance, she told herself, was not particularly noble. It was just avoidance. Yet there was a sliver of something else in the decision not to call. A desire to keep this experience contained, not because she didn’t trust him with it, but because she wasn’t ready for it to become part of their joint narrative. Not yet.

So much of their life together was shared: the mortgage, the band, Sarah, the Academy gossip, the small domestic victories and petty irritations. There were very few things that belonged solely to her. Some of that was by choice; she had invited him into almost every corner. This, she realised, she wanted to examine alone for a while: her body in this house, her pain on these stairs, her thoughts in these rooms.

She turned the phone over, thumb running along the edge. The battery percentage stared up at her. No missed calls. No messages. The small, rectangular silence of it felt both generous and accusing.

She imagined, briefly, how this would look from another angle. A nurse—Anaya, perhaps; she thought of the woman from the care home years in some parallel universe—would tell her firmly that refusing help was a form of stubbornness masquerading as strength. That asking was not a failure. She knew this. She had said versions of that speech to her students, to Sarah, to friends. She had held in her paws the hands of people who thought they were being noble by not wanting to be a burden.

“Hypocrite,” she told herself again, wryly.

The thing about being good at care, she reflected, is that you become very skilled at seeing what other people should do. Applying that lens inward was always harder.

She put the phone in her pocket and, slowly, carefully, began another circuit of the house. It was smaller, today. Not physically; the walls were where they had always been. But the space within them felt narrowed by her awareness of every threshold, every change in floor level, every stretch of hallway that would need negotiating.

She made a list in her head as she moved: tasks she could realistically do (strip the spare bed, open the loft hatch and prop it, check the fuse box), tasks she could not (clear the gutters, shift the heavier boxes in the garage). The latter felt like admissions of defeat. The former felt like small, manageable acts of maintenance—of the house, of herself.

At lunchtime, she sat in the living room again, ankle throbbing, and wrote the list down on actual paper. The act of writing gave the day shape. Proof, later, that she had not simply lain here and floated.

She did not call Miles.


## 10 · Morning After

Time, even under the dictatorship of pain, moved.

By the next morning the ankle had settled into a new equilibrium. Still swollen, still tender, but more predictable. She had learned its range. The walk from bed to bathroom: manageable with the grab rail. The stairs: slow, sideways, two paws on the banister. The kitchen: tolerable if she sat whenever she could. Each movement had become more deliberate. No unnecessary trips.

The house, in response, seemed to ease off its own commentary. The noises faded back into background. The novelty of her presence here wore off, on both sides.

She stripped the spare bed, folding sheets with care and stacking them in the linen cupboard. She opened the loft hatch and squinted up into the dark, decided that whatever was up there could remain unexamined for another month. She tested the smoke alarms. She wrote down meter readings.

The domestic tasks grounded her in a way the hill had not. Each had a beginning and an end. Each carried with it the small satisfaction of completion. None required more from her ankle than it had to give.

Her thoughts, in this calmer physical state, felt less jagged. The dramatic questions of the first night—what if this is the start of the end, what if my body is betraying me, what if I can’t do the things I love—receded to a quieter murmur. New, more measured questions took their place. How do I adjust the rehearsal schedule when I get home so I’m not on my feet for three hours straight? Can we move the piano stool up one notch and see if that helps my back? Should I persuade the Academy to buy more chairs with arms?

She sat at the kitchen table again, ankle on a chair, notebook open this time, and turned some of those questions into notes. This was how she coped with most things that frightened her: by turning them into plans. The teenager’s PLANS notebook, updated with better data.

The house, watching her—as much as a structure can watch—seemed indifferent to these plans. It had seen her draft many lives at this table: practice schedules, packing lists, setlists, lesson plans. It had seen some come to pass, some be abandoned, some be forgotten.

She realised, as she wrote, that she did not feel angry at the hill anymore. Or at her ankle. Or even at herself. Irritated, yes. Impatient, always. But the sharp blame of the first day had dissolved into a more familiar texture: the low-level annoyance of someone whose instrument needed retuning more often than she would like.

She could live with that. She had, in fact, been living with versions of that for years; she had simply been calling it other things.

Outside, a delivery van reversed noisily. Children’s voices floated in from the street. Someone’s washing machine thumped. The world did what it always did when individuals had minor crises: it continued.

She closed the notebook, moved her ankle gently, and felt the edges of discomfort without flinching.


## 11 · The Parents’ Return

Her parents did not burst into the house with the drama of sitcom mothers and fathers. They arrived exactly as she might have predicted: at the time they’d said they would, with bags of food, with coats zipped, with the keys already in her father’s hand.

“We didn’t have to come,” her mother said, kissing her on both cheeks anyway, her perfume enveloping Lillian in a fug of floral familiarity. “You told us you were fine.”

“I am fine,” Lillian said, from her position at the kitchen table with her ankle inconspicuously elevated. “Mostly. The house is still standing.”

Her father clucked at her, the way he always had when facts did not align with his wishes. “You should have phoned sooner if you’d hurt yourself,” he said, putting the bags down with more force than necessary. “We’d have come straight away.”

This was true, and not useful. She smiled, acknowledging both. “You’re here now,” she said. “That’s what matters.”

They moved through the rooms in a kind of parental audit: checking the windows, tutting at a loose skirting board, opening the fridge and making small noises about how little fresh food there was. Her mother rearranged the spices. Her father tested the smoke alarm again. Their presence altered the house’s soundscape instantly. Doors opened and closed. Cupboard doors clacked. The kettle went on.

Lillian watched them, affection and mild exasperation braided together. Her ankle ached in a way that was easier to ignore with their commentary filling the air.

“You’ve done a good job,” her mother said eventually, sitting down opposite her with a cup of tea. “Keeping an eye on things. We worry, you know, when it sits empty.”

“It’s wood and bricks, Mum,” Lillian said. “It doesn’t get lonely.”

“You say that,” her mother replied, looking around. “But houses… they like to be used.”

She thought of the previous night, of the way the house had seemed to breathe louder in the dark. She did not contradict her.

“And you?” her father asked, nodding at her bandaged ankle. “Does that like to be used at the moment?”

“It likes to be elevated,” she said. “And occasionally swore at.”

He made a face somewhere between sympathy and amusement. “You always did overdo it,” he said. “Even as a bairn. If there was a wall to climb, you’d climb it. If there was a note to hit, you’d hit it twice.”

Her mother reached out and patted her paw. “You’re too hard on yourself,” she said, which was her way of saying the opposite.

They did not stay long. Their own new flat awaited, with its own routines, its own kettle, its own carefully arranged furniture. They had come to check, to reassure themselves that both house and daughter remained intact. Having done so, they left her again in the space that had once been theirs.

The masks, she realised when the door clicked shut, had indeed reappeared. Hers, certainly—polite, competent, uncomplaining. Theirs too: concerned, practical, a little controlling. Beneath, everyone had flinched at her injury in their own way. None of them had named it aloud.

She looked down at her ankle. The bandage peeked white from under her trouser leg. The bruise underneath, she knew, was changing colours again, moving through its own calendar of healing.

The house exhaled, a minute shift in air pressure as the door fully closed.


## 12 · Leaving

Miles arrived back in late afternoon, bringing with him London air and the faint smell of train coffee.

He let himself in without knocking, because it was his house too now, in the ways that mattered. His footsteps in the hall were as familiar as the creak of the floorboards.

“How’s the hill’s revenge?” he called, toeing off his shoes.

She was on the sofa again, ankle up, notebook on her lap. She turned her head, smiled. “Mellowed into a grudge,” she said. “Manageable.”

He came in, crossed the room in three strides, and crouched beside the sofa, his hand going automatically to her bandaged ankle. He didn’t squeeze, just laid his palm there, warm through the fabric, feeling for heat.

“Colour’s better,” he said. “You should still get it looked at properly when we’re back.”

“Yes, dear,” she said, and this time there was no resistance in it.

They left Silverburn the next day. There was no ceremonial farewell, no standing in the doorway for a last lingering look. She had changed the beds, emptied the fridge of anything perishable, ensured the windows were locked. Her father had pressed the keys into her hand and then taken them back again, the ownership dance ongoing.

At the front door, she paused, hand on the frame. The wood felt the same as it always had: solid, slightly rough, the paint chipped in the same place as when she’d jammed her violin case into it at twelve.

She did not feel the swell of emotion she’d expected—no cinematic montage of childhood scenes, no lump in her throat. What she felt was something closer to accuracy. This house had been the stage for the first act of her life. It had contained joy and frustration, safety and constraint. It had not been all one thing. Neither had she.

“You all right?” Miles asked, standing a step below her on the path, bags in hand.

“Aye,” she said. “Ready.”

He offered her his arm. She took it, partly for balance, partly because this was one of the small rituals that still pleased her: walking together, weight shared, their bodies adjusting to each other automatically.

The car door made its familiar thunk. Her ankle protested as she swung her legs in, then settled once she had them propped comfortably. The seatbelt lay across her chest and belly. Miles adjusted the angle so it didn’t cut in, a small, precise act of care.

As they drove away, Silverburn receded in the rear-view mirror: the house, the hill beyond it, the line of trees. She watched it until the bend of the road took it out of sight.

She did not think, I am leaving my childhood behind. That would have been too neat, too dishonest. She thought instead about physio appointments, about adjusting rehearsal schedules, about buying a new pair of boots with better ankle support. Practical footnotes to an incident that had revealed, more than it had created, a set of truths about her body and her relationship with it.

“We’ll get you looked at,” Miles said, focusing on the road. “MRI, if needed. We’ll bully the GP into taking it seriously.”

“You’ll charm them,” she said. “I’ll bully.”

He smiled. “Division of labour,” he said.

The bypass slid under them. The city gathered itself around them again, then loosened as they hit the motorway. Her ankle ached. Her body relaxed incrementally as distance from the house increased, then re-tensed as she thought of stairs at home, of London pavements, of academy corridors.

Continuity, she realised, was less about sameness and more about carrying the knowledge of change without pretending it hadn’t happened. She was leaving Silverburn with more information about herself than she’d had going in. Not all of it comfortable. None of it, really, optional.


## 13 · Aftermath

The ankle healed.

Not in a miraculous, one-morning-she-woke-and-it-was-fine way. In the slow, boring way of bodies: swelling down, bruises fading, ligaments knitting themselves back together while physios in soft trainers explained exercises and warned her about rushing.

She did the exercises, mostly. Calf raises at the kitchen counter, balancing on one leg while the kettle boiled. Resistance bands looped around her foot while she sat on the sofa watching crime dramas. She attended her appointments. She nodded when they talked about strengthening, about long-term support, about footwear.

Months later, she could walk up the academy’s stairs without thinking of Silverburn. She could stand through a whole rehearsal without needing to sit down halfway. She could step onto small stages without her brain immediately presenting a slideshow of possible angles of fall.

The knowledge, however, remained. It lived somewhere behind her ribs, alongside other, older knowings: that institutions will always look for reasons not to admit you; that loving someone means accepting their risk appetite; that you can do everything “right” and still misjudge a patch of grass.

Every so often, in the quiet between classes, she would feel a phantom ache in the ankle and look up, half-expecting to see the hill through her office window. Instead she’d see Loughton’s trees, the academy car park, a slice of sky the wrong colour for Scotland. She would breathe, move her foot, feel the joint respond, and let the feeling pass through.

She did not dramatise the story when she told it. “I overdid it on a hill,” she would say if someone asked about the bandage, about the slight limp. “Middle-aged badger hubris. I’m fine.” She did not tell them about the notebook under the bed, the “Never get stuck” line. That part was hers.

In small, practical ways, she adjusted. She and Miles moved the piano stool up a notch. They bought a bath mat with better grip. She insisted, at the next academy facilities meeting, on a proper audit of stair rails and chair heights. The students rolled their eyes at the emails about safe lifting of amps, but they listened.

She did not go back to Silverburn that year. Her parents came to London instead, stayed in the guest room, commented on the garden. The house on the outskirts of Edinburgh remained where it had always been, its windows facing the same way, its hill rising behind it. It existed in her mind now less as the container of her childhood and more as the place where she had been forced, briefly, to see her body and her history in the same unforgiving mirror.

Sometimes, when she was warming up before a gig, hands on the piano keys, ankle flexing under the pedal, she would think of the phrase her mother had used: houses like to be used. Bodies, too. Not used up, not punished, but inhabited. Honoured.

She pressed the sustain pedal, felt the mechanism respond, heard the chord bloom. Her ankle held. The bruises were gone. The memory of them sat quietly in the background, a footnote she could choose to read or not.

The song began. The hill, the house, the injury—none of them were the point, and all of them, in their way, had altered the way she stood to sing.
