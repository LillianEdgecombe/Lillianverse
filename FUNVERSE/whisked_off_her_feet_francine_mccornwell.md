## Whisked Off Her Feet

### Chapter 1 — The Smell of Trouble (and Cardamom)

On the morning everything changed, Lillian Edgecombe’s bakery smelled exactly the way she liked her life to feel: warm, busy, and a little bit sticky.

Steam fogged the front windows of Badger’s Crust, blurring the view of the leafy London side street into patches of green and grey. Inside, the air was thick with sugar, butter, and cardamom; cinnamon rolled in soft ribbons from the ovens at the back. The big industrial mixer hummed like a contented animal. Trays clinked. The clock over the door ticked toward eight.

Lillian stood at the centre of it all—a broad-shouldered badger in a flour-dusted apron, paws moving with the unhurried precision of someone who could fold dough in her sleep. Her hair was tied back in a loose braid that brushed the middle of her back; her glasses slid down her nose every so often, and she pushed them back up with the back of her wrist without breaking rhythm.

The tray of almond danishes in her paws was heavy. It pulled pleasantly at her shoulders, a reminder that her body, like the bakery, had weight and purpose. She liked that. She liked the way her belly rested against the edge of the stainless-steel counter when she leaned in to check the browning on a batch of scones. She liked the way the chairs in the tiny front room sighed and accepted her when she finally sat down at the end of a day.

“Doors open in ten!” she called, voice carrying over the whirr of the mixer and the hiss of the espresso machine warming up.

“Yes, chef,” came a muffled reply from the back, where Tamsin, her twenty-something fox barista, was coaxing life out of the temperamental grinder.

Lillian smiled. Chef. That still felt faintly ridiculous. She had never trained in a Michelin kitchen. Her degrees were in music. Once upon a time she had imagined her forties spent in rehearsal rooms, not kneading dough at five in the morning—but then life had folded, like laminated pastry, and here she was, living proof that you could build a second act out of sugar and stubbornness.

She slid the tray of danishes into the front display, adjusting them until the spirals caught the light. Outside, Mrs Patel walked past with her schnauzer and waved through the window. Lillian wiggled floury fingers back.

It was, in other words, a normal morning.

Right up until the bell above the door jingled early.

It wasn’t the usual early-bird regular—the nurse coming off nights, the dog walker, the university student with a permanent essay crisis. The figure that stepped in was taller. Broader. Sharper around the edges.

He filled the doorway in a way that made the room momentarily feel smaller, like the walls had leaned in for a better look. A German Shepherd, his fur a sleek dark sable that softened into tan along his muzzle and chest; ears pricked, eyes cool and assessing. His shirt was crisp white cotton, sleeves rolled to show forearms dusted with just enough flour to suggest he actually worked in a kitchen, not just posed in one. His scent—soap, steel, the faint ghost of coffee—cut through caramel like a new note in a chord.

Lillian’s brow twitched.

“Morning,” he said, gazing around with the polite interest of an engineer inspecting a system. “Charming. Rustic. Like a dollhouse made of carbs.”

Her fur bristled.

“Excuse me?” she said.

He gave a small bow, half-mocking, half-automatic courtesy.

“Miles,” he said. “I’ll be opening across the street tomorrow. Croque et Crème.”

Her stomach dropped, not theatrically but with the quiet, practical thud of a shelf overloading. So this was him. The chef everyone had been gossiping about. The one with the neon signage going up in the sleek black unit opposite, stainless-steel counters gleaming through papered windows, Instagram account already boasting soft-focus shots of pastry under spotlights.

Lillian wiped her paws on her apron, leaving pale handprints like battle paint.

“I see,” she said, keeping her voice even. “Well. Welcome to Fernwell Lane. I hope your grand opening is a smashing success. Just don’t be surprised if your customers wander over here for the real thing.”

His mouth curved. It wasn’t quite a smile. It was the expression of a man who had never met a challenge he didn’t want to index and optimise.

“Oh, I don’t worry about that,” he said. “Once they taste my raspberry–lavender mille-feuille, they’ll forget this place even exists.”

“Oh, honey,” she said, the word sweet and edged. “Your mille-feuille can’t hold a candle to a warm cinnamon roll at six in the morning. Not everything needs a mousse dome and edible gold.”

They stood there for a heartbeat, heat rising like dough under a tea towel. Lillian was aware, in irritating detail, of how her apron pulled across her belly, of how his shirt sat just so across his shoulders. He smelled annoyingly good. It was offensive.

Then he winked—actually winked—and turned on his heel.

“See you around, neighbour,” he said over his shoulder.

The door chimed shut behind him.

Lillian stared at the space he’d vacated, claws twitching against the counter.

“Oh, he’s going to be a problem,” she muttered. “A very tall, smug, probably-fairly-talented problem.”

Behind her, the oven timer pinged. Real life demanded attention. She exhaled, shook flour from her paws, and went to rescue the scones.

Later, when the rush hit and the regulars came in with their familiar orders and gossip, she caught herself glancing at the papered-up shopfront across the street more than once. In the reflection on the glass, she saw her own round face, her large glasses, the set of her jaw.

Fine, she thought. Let him have his neon. She had cardamom and community and chairs that knew the weight of her customers. Let’s see whose held longer.

The day rolled on. The dough rose. The tills rang. Fernwell Lane hummed its ordinary tune.

Underneath it, a new rhythm had started, soft and insistent, like a second kettle beginning to boil.

### Chapter 2 — New Kid on the Block

Croque et Crème opened on a drizzly Thursday with the kind of launch that made Lillian’s fur want to crawl off her body and start a union.

There were balloons—matte black and gold, because apparently whimsy was out. There were fairy lights. There were influencers. Lillian could tell they were influencers because they arrived in small, curated clusters, each carrying a camera bigger than their bag and speaking in sentences that began with “My followers are going to die when they see—”

She watched from behind her counter, arms folded over her apron, as a queue formed across the street. It was an aesthetically pleasing queue. People in good coats. People who pronounced “croissant” properly. People who had, until last week, been her cinnamon roll crowd.

“Traitors,” she said under her breath, then winced at herself. “No. Curious. They’re allowed to be curious.”

“We could put up a sign,” Tamsin suggested, steaming milk. “‘Free emotional support with every scone.’”

“We already do that,” Lillian said. “We just don’t monetise it.”

The bell rang. Mrs Patel walked in, shaking rain from her umbrella.

“Have you seen the commotion?” she asked, peering past Lillian’s shoulder. “You’d think the Queen was opening a doughnut shop.”

“Apparently he’s Michelin,” Tamsin said. “Used to work in Paris. Or Shoreditch. Or somewhere with tiles that aren’t chipped.”

Mrs Patel sniffed. “If his cakes are half as good as your naan bread experiment was bad, he’ll do fine.”

“It was fusion,” Lillian said weakly.

“It was a crime,” Mrs Patel said fondly. “Now, two cheese scones, please. And I’m not going over there. I don’t trust anywhere that doesn’t smell like burnt edges.”

Lillian smiled, warmth uncurling under her sternum. Her bakery smelled like burnt edges because things lived here. Loaves caught; sugar dripped; not everything was perfect. She liked that. The world had tried to teach her, for most of her life, that she should be smaller, neater, less. The bakery was her daily refusal.

Mid-morning, between the commuter rush and the lunch crowd, the door across the road opened, and Miles stepped out.

He wore a different shirt—dark this time, sleeves rolled in the same precise way. The apron around his waist was black, tied neat. The signage behind him glowed: Croque et Crème, in slender white letters.

He scanned the street, eyes landing briefly on Badger’s Crust. On her. He lifted a paw in a half-salute.

Lillian lifted the tongs she was holding in response, like a peace offering that could double as a weapon.

He grinned. Then a photographer called his name, and he turned, body aligning toward the lens with the ease of someone used to being looked at.

Lillian turned back to her trays.

“You’re glaring,” Tamsin said mildly.

“I’m observing,” Lillian said. “Through narrowed eyes.”

She tried to be gracious. Really, she did. She even went so far as to send Tamsin over on her break with a box of cinnamon rolls and a neutral note: *Welcome to the lane — L.*

Tamsin returned ten minutes later, cheeks pink from the rain and something else.

“Well?” Lillian asked, trying to sound like she didn’t care.

“He was…” Tamsin searched for a word. “Polite. Very focused. Kitchen’s beautiful. All stainless steel and light. Smells like vanilla and fear.”

“Fear?” Lillian asked.

“Yeah,” Tamsin said. “Everyone’s moving like a TV crew might appear at any moment.”

That lodged somewhere in Lillian’s mind and stayed there.

That night, when she finally trudged home to her little flat above the bakery, feet aching, she put her swollen ankles up on the battered coffee table and did what she tried not to do: she looked him up.

The internet obliged.

Chef Miles Ferguson, formerly of several restaurants with single-word names, all vowels and exposed brick. Photos of him plating impossibly delicate desserts, head bent, paws steady. Articles about his “clean lines and bold flavour profiles.” One piece that described him as “the engineer of patisserie” and Lillian’s jaw clenched, because of course that was the phrase.

She read an interview where he talked about systems and precision and how “there’s no room for improvisation in a good kitchen,” and wanted, irrationally, to throw a rolling pin at her laptop.

“There’s always room,” she muttered, snapping it shut.

Her phone buzzed. A message from Sarah, her golden retriever daughter up in Manchester: a photo of a sculpture-in-progress—twisted metal and clay—and a caption: *My tutor says it’s “structurally improbable.” I said so am I.*

Lillian’s chest loosened.

*Structurally improbable is our family brand,* she typed back. *How’s your week? Also, the enemy has opened across the street.*

Three dots appeared. Then:

*Send photos of pastries. I need to know whether to disown him on principle.*

She laughed, out loud, in the empty room.

“It’ll be fine,” she told herself, aloud. “There’s room on this street for more than one bakery.”

The words sounded reasonable. Her shoulders were less convinced.

Still, she slept. The bakery would be there in the morning, and so would she. Flour, fat, and sheer, stubborn presence.

Across the street, light from Croque et Crème glowed late into the night.

### Chapter 3 — Sweet Streets

The Sweet Streets Bake-Off Festival arrived in Loughton the way big things always arrived in small pockets of the city: with flyers, noise, and the faint whiff of corporate sponsorship.

Lillian found out about it when a laminated poster appeared on her door one Tuesday, stuck under the bell with more enthusiasm than adhesive skill.

SWEET STREETS BAKE-OFF! it proclaimed, in pink and teal letters. LOCAL TALENT. BIG FLAVOURS. LIVE TV.

She peeled it off the glass, reading the smaller print as she carried a tray of cooling traybakes to the front.

“Oh no,” she said.

“Oh yes,” said Tamsin, appearing at her elbow with the uncanny timing of a barista who knew when drama was brewing. “They’ve been putting those up all over. There’s a hashtag already.”

Lillian peered at the details.

Open-call for local bakers. Two-day public festival in the town square. Final rounds filmed for a ten-part streaming series. Prize money large enough to make her palms sweat: enough to fix the temperamental oven she pretended was charmingly vintage, maybe even redo the cramped seating area.

“We should sign up,” Tamsin said. “Imagine the exposure.”

“I have plenty of exposure,” Lillian said. “Some of these aprons are practically transparent at this point.”

“The good kind,” Tamsin said. “People will see you. The whole country could see you.”

That, Lillian thought, was not the straightforward benefit Tamsin seemed to think it was. She had spent forty-two years learning to be visible on her own terms—on stage, in classrooms, now behind this counter. The idea of cameras slicing her into edit-friendly pieces made her fur itch.

“We’re busy,” she said. “The festival weekend will be mad enough without me vanishing into some marquee to juggle cake tins for an audience that leaves rude comments about my knife skills.”

She propped the poster on the counter anyway. Throughout the morning, customers asked about it.

“You have to do it,” insisted Mrs Patel. “I want to be able to point at the telly and say, ‘That’s my baker. She taught me that naan bread is not always a good idea.’”

“You’d win,” said the nurse from the early shift, blowing on her coffee. “Your cinnamon rolls cured my ex-boyfriend. Of being my boyfriend.”

Lillian smiled, demurred, deflected. The idea lodged itself in her mind like a seed in a crack in the pavement.

At lunchtime, Miles came in.

It was not the first time he’d crossed her threshold. Since opening, he’d been in a handful of times—once to borrow parchment paper, once to ask if she’d had any trouble with late deliveries (she had), once to buy a loaf of her sourdough with the sheepishness of a man temporarily out of bread.

Each time, the bakery had felt marginally smaller.

Today, he walked in with his coat unbuttoned, black apron still on, expression guardedly friendly. His eyes landed on the poster before they landed on her.

“Of course they’re doing a festival,” he said. “Nothing attracts cameras like sugar and small business owners.”

“And inflated ideas about drama,” Lillian said. “They must be desperate. Real bakers have better things to do.”

His mouth twitched. “So you’re not entering.”

She folded her arms.

“Absolutely not,” she said. “I have a bakery to run.”

“Shame,” he said. “You’d be a favourite. Judges love ‘authentic’.”

“Is that what I am now?” she asked. “Authentic?”

“Among other things,” he said, a little too easily.

Something in her bristled. She reminded herself that he was not under obligation to phrase his compliments in ways that didn’t press on old bruises. He knew her only as a rival, not as the woman who had once been told by a conservatory panel that her body was “unsuited to certain roles.”

“You’re entering, then,” she said.

He shrugged. “Probably. The restaurant group likes it when the chefs look… engaged with community. Makes the spreadsheets feel warm.”

She snorted.

“Well, good luck,” she said. “May all your sugar work set at the right temperature.”

“Why, Edgecombe,” he said, “if I didn’t know better, I’d think you cared.”

Before she could reply, the bell jingled again as a gust of air brought in three students arguing about croissants. The moment scattered.

It wasn’t until closing time, when she was scraping dried batter from the mixing bowl, that she noticed the second sheet of paper tucked under the original poster. An extra page, smaller, from the festival organisers.

SPECIAL FEATURE, it read. RIVALS ROUND — TEAM UP WITH YOUR NEAREST COMPETITOR FOR A CHANCE AT DOUBLE PRIZE MONEY.

Her stomach sank.

She imagined a camera cutting from her sign—Badger’s Crust—to his—Croque et Crème. The little arrow graphic between them. The producers leaning back in their swivel chairs, grinning.

“No,” she told the empty bakery. “Absolutely not.”

The next morning, she was at her laptop at six, filling in the online registration form.

Exposure, she told herself. Prize money. A new oven. It would be irresponsible not to.

She hesitated at the line that asked: *Do you consent to being paired with another local business?*

She checked yes. Then, in the box marked *If yes, please state any preferences*, she wrote, against her better judgment: *Badger’s Crust is directly opposite Croque et Crème. There is… existing tension.*

She hit submit. The confirmation email pinged back immediately.

“You’re mad,” she told herself, and went downstairs to turn on the oven.

### Chapter 4 — Whisk Wars

The festival took over the town square in a blur of bunting, temporary stalls, and an alarming number of clipboards.

By the time Lillian arrived on the first morning, Fernwell Lane felt like an arm of a much louder body. The square was full of people; the air smelled of sugar and smoke and wet grass. A sound system crackled to life, announcing the host’s name with more gusto than the host himself seemed to feel.

Badger’s Crust had been assigned a stall along one side of the square, a modest tent with a banner that looked handmade because it was. Lillian stacked trays of pastries on the front table, conscious of the cameras drifting past, catching establishing shots.

“You look great,” Tamsin said, fussing with the display. “Very… primary protagonist.”

“I feel like a trifle somebody forgot to assemble,” Lillian said. “Do I have sugar on my face?”

“Yes,” Tamsin said. “It’s on-brand.”

The crowd buzzed. Children darted between stalls. A drone hummed overhead, adding a faint, insectile note to the soundscape.

“Contestants, to the marquee, please!” called a runner with a headset.

Lillian wiped her paws, squared her shoulders, and walked towards the big white tent at the centre of the square.

Inside, long stainless-steel benches stretched in two rows. Cameras hung from rigs on the ceiling; lights cast everything in a soft, unforgiving glow. Stations were labelled with laminated cards. She found her name—LILLIAN EDGEOMBE, BADGER’S CRUST—printed in cheerful font.

Beside it, another card: MILES FERGUSON, CROQUE ET CRÈME.

Of course.

He was already there, weighing flour with the stern concentration of a man calibrating machinery. His fur looked almost silver under the studio lights. He glanced up as she approached, and something that might have been relief flickered across his face.

“Edgecombe,” he said. “Ready for your close-up?”

“I’m here to bake, not act,” she said. “The cameras are incidental.”

He smiled. “Tell that to the producers.”

As if summoned, a woman with a headset and a clipboard materialised.

“Lillian, Miles,” she said, beaming. “I’m Dani, your segment producer. We are so excited about you two. The rivals! The neighbours! The tension! It’s perfect.”

“We’re not tense,” Lillian said.

Miles raised an eyebrow.

“We’re healthily competitive,” she amended.

“Even better,” Dani said. “So, first round is individual bakes. Then we’ll do a little piece to camera about your ‘journey,’ and tomorrow—”

“The team round,” Miles said. “We read the brief.”

“Great,” Dani said. “Just remember: be yourselves. But slightly louder.”

Lillian thought of her students at the Academy, the way she had told them that you didn’t need to shout to be heard. She swallowed a retort. Now was not the time to deliver a lecture on authenticity to a woman with glitter eyeliner and a schedule.

The host—a cheerful otter in a pastel blazer—bounded to the front, mic in paw.

“Bakers,” he said, voice booming through the speakers, “welcome to Sweet Streets!”

Applause swelled. Cameras panned. Lillian inhaled, tasting sugar and nerves on the air.

“Your first challenge,” the host continued, “is to create a tray of twelve signature pastries that represent your business. You have three hours. Your time starts… now!”

The tent erupted into motion.

Lillian moved on instinct. Her paws found the mixing bowls, the scale, the flour. Her body remembered the steps like choreography: measure, sift, stir, knead. The dough beneath her claws felt familiar, grounding.

She decided on cardamom cinnamon rolls—a cliché, maybe, but they were what her customers thought of when they thought of her. She added orange zest for brightness, thought of Sarah’s comment about “layers of comfort,” and smiled despite herself.

Beside her, Miles worked with smaller movements, everything precise. He weighed ingredients to the gram, whisked with controlled, economical strokes. The smell of butter and raspberry drifted from his station.

“Of course you’re making mille-feuille,” she muttered.

“It’s what people expect,” he said.

“You could surprise them,” she said.

He glanced at her.

“I am,” he said. “I’m talking while I work.”

“Careful,” she said. “You’ll ruin your brand.”

The banter came easier than she wanted to admit. It slid along the groove of something that felt suspiciously like chemistry, if chemistry had claws and a tendency to overprove.

Dani swooped in periodically with questions.

“Lillian, talk to me about your flavours,” she said, camera hovering.

“They’re nostalgic,” Lillian said, rolling dough. “Comforting. Loud in a different way.”

“And how do you feel being across from Miles?” Dani asked.

Lillian didn’t look up.

“Distracted,” she said. “He keeps using words like ‘lamination schedule’.”

Miles snorted.

The three hours blurred. Flour dusted their fur; sugar stuck to their paws; their bodies settled into the familiar ache of long work, layered with the unfamiliar prickle of being watched.

When time was called, Lillian stepped back, breathing hard, and looked at her tray.

Twelve rolls, glazed and golden, spirals glossy with sugar. Not perfect—one slightly larger than the others, one a bit wonky—but undeniably hers.

Miles’ mille-feuille sat on the next bench, layers razor-thin and deadly neat, raspberry filling peeking in precise bands.

They were both, inconveniently, beautiful.

The judges—three of them, all variously stern and twinkly—moved down the line. They tasted. They commented. Lillian heard “generous” and “warm” and “surprisingly sophisticated”; she heard “a little rustic” and decided to take it as a compliment.

At the end of the round, as the contestants milled and the crew reset, she and Miles found themselves side by side, leaning against the back wall.

“Not bad,” he said.

“You too,” she said. “For a scented candle.”

He huffed.

“Tomorrow’s going to be… interesting,” he said.

“Tomorrow,” she said, “is where they make us share a whisk. May God have mercy on their souls.”

For a moment, they stood there, shoulder to shoulder, watching the tent empty. Lillian was very aware of the heat of him, of how his breathing synced unconsciously to the rhythm of the room.

Then Dani called them for interviews, and the moment was lost to questions about “flavour journeys” and “on-screen chemistry.”

Lillian looked at the lens, at the red recording light, and thought, Fine. Watch. See if I care.

Her heart, traitor that it was, cared a great deal.

### Chapter 5 — The Slow-Burn Challenge

Day two brought sunlight streaming through the marquee’s high windows and a new challenge on the board:

CREATE A UNIFIED DESSERT MENU — THREE SMALL BITES THAT REFLECT THE HARMONY BETWEEN YOUR TWO PALATES.

Lillian read it twice and then looked at Miles.

“Harmony,” she said. “Right.”

He was already scribbling notes on the pad at their shared station, ears twitching in small, irritated movements that had nothing to do with sound.

“We can do a progression,” he said. “Something clean to start, something richer, then something nostalgic.”

“We could also consider joy,” she said. “Texture. Memories. The fact that not everyone wants to eat dessert that looks like a geometry lesson.”

He glanced up, eyes bright.

“You think my food is joyless,” he said.

“I think your food is… precise,” she said diplomatically. “Like you’d file a complaint if a crumb landed in the wrong place.”

“And yours?” he asked.

“Mine believes in crumbs,” she said. “And hands. And licking spoons.”

Dani hovered, practically vibrating.

“This is perfect,” she whispered to the camera. “Keep going. But like, to each other.”

They did, because they had three hours and ingredients and mild panic.

They compromised, eventually, because there was no other way forward. Pistachio–cardamom shortbread from her side, lemon–lavender ganache from his. Honey-glazed choux bites that somehow married his love of structure with her fondness for messy joy.

Lillian moved through the kitchen with her usual purposeful heft: paws sure on the floor, hips bumping drawers closed, tail flicking when timers went off. She wiped sweat from her brow with the back of her wrist and left a streak of flour on her cheek.

Miles noticed. He said nothing. He wiped his own face with a tea towel and pretended the heat under his fur was just the ovens.

At one point, reaching for the same bowl of sugar, their paws bumped. It was nothing. It was everything.

“Watch it, pooch,” she said, voice catching.

“Maybe don’t hover,” he said, ears a fraction too erect.

They didn’t look at each other for a full minute after that.

When the judges tasted their trio later—shortbread, choux, a tiny layered verrine—they murmured and scribbled and exchanged glances.

“This,” said the oldest judge, “tastes like two people trying very hard not to admit they work well together.”

Lillian stared at her paws.

Miles stared at the table.

They were both smiling when they walked out of the tent.

### Chapter 6 — The Boiling Point

The first proper row was about apples.

The challenge was deceptively simple: reimagine a traditional family dessert from your partner’s culture. The producers loved it. They said things like “heritage” and “stories on plates.” Lillian heard “free therapy with knives.”

In the prep kitchen, away from the cameras, tension simmered.

“I’m not doing another bloody tarte tatin,” she said, pacing. “Your family’s version sounds like a dental lawsuit waiting to happen.”

Miles crossed his arms. His shoulders were tight; his tail had gone very still.

“It’s called caramelisation,” he said. “Maybe if you didn’t drown everything in cardamom and nostalgia—”

“Oh, I’m the problem now?” she said, voice rising.

“You’re always the problem when we’re asked to collaborate,” he snapped. “You’re so busy making everything… meaningful that you forget there are techniques.”

“And you’re so busy making everything perfect you forget people have to eat it with their actual mouths,” she shot back. “Not everyone wants dessert that looks like a modern art installation about austerity.”

Other teams slowed their chopping. Someone turned down a radio. The air thickened.

“You think I don’t care if people enjoy my food?” he demanded.

“I think you care more about what it looks like on a plate than how it feels going down,” she said. “Sometimes I feel like I’m baking with a scented candle.”

The words landed. His ears twitched back, as if physically struck.

Silence fell, heavy and immediate.

Lillian heard her own breathing, rough in the tiled room. She saw the hurt flicker behind his eyes, quickly hidden.

“You know what,” she said, suddenly very tired, “maybe you’d be better off baking with a mirror. At least that way, someone might actually agree with you.”

He blinked.

Then he turned and walked out, apron strings swinging.

The door snapped shut behind him.

Lillian stood there, hands shaking, dough dried on her claws. Anger sat hot in her chest, layered with something that felt dangerously like regret.

In the contestant lounge later, she sat on a windowsill with a burnt pastry in her paw, staring out at the festival lights. The greenhouse beyond the square glowed softly, its glass panes veined with light.

Miles was nowhere in sight.

It should have felt like victory. It didn’t.

Back at the B&B that night, in a bed that smelled like detergent and not her life, she lay awake, replaying the argument. His expression when she’d called his work joyless. The way he’d stepped in front of her when Chef Genevieve had shouted about the flour incident earlier that day.

She turned onto her side, the mattress dipping under her weight, and thought, You’re forty-two, Lillian. You know better than to confuse proximity with intimacy. He’s a rival, not a… thing.

Her chest disagreed.

Outside, in the quiet of the courtyard, Miles sat on a bench, phone dark in his paw, head tipped back against the wall. He thought of his grandmother’s pain au chocolat, of his father’s pride, of the years he’d spent turning feelings into clean lines on plates.

He thought of Lillian’s remark, and of the way her eyes had looked right before she’d said it. He thought of the way she moved in the kitchen—sure, grounded, no apology in the way she took up space.

“You’re an idiot,” he told himself.

He resolved, somewhere between midnight and the first birdsong, to apologise.

Sleep, when it came, was thin and full of sugar.

### Chapter 7 — Flour Storm

The kitchen was quiet at dawn.

The crew hadn’t arrived yet; the judges were presumably still asleep in their hotels, dreaming of ganache. The marquee smelled of metal and yesterday’s work. Light leaked in around the edges of the tent.

Lillian was there anyway, elbow-deep in flour.

She’d come early to test doughs, to see whether she could coax a version of Miles’ family dessert into something that felt like hers. Apples, caramel, pastry. Simple on paper, complicated in execution, and apparently an emotional minefield.

She had just reached for the rolling pin when the flap of the tent rustled.

Miles stepped in from the side entrance, carrying a crate of fresh herbs from the greenhouse. His fur was slightly ruffled, as if sleep had argued with him. There were faint shadows under his eyes. He paused when he saw her; she froze when she saw him.

For a moment, they stood like two spatulas wedged in the same drawer.

“Couldn’t sleep?” she said.

“Too many ideas,” he said. “Not enough apologies.”

She blinked.

He set the crate down, wiped his paws on his apron.

“About yesterday,” he said. “I shouldn’t have… dismissed your way of doing things. Or made you feel like… sentiment is a flaw. It isn’t. It’s your strength.”

She stared at him. The boiler of her temper, which had been simmering all night, hissed quietly and settled.

“I shouldn’t have called you a scented candle,” she said. “Or implied you only care about looks. You clearly care. Too much, maybe. Which is hypocritical of me to criticise, because… same.”

A corner of his mouth twitched.

“So we’re both wrong,” he said. “Excellent. Symmetry.”

“You and your symmetry,” she said, but there was no heat in it.

A bag of flour, left too close to the edge of her station, chose that moment to slide.

Time slowed.

She lunged for it. He lunged too.

They collided. The bag split.

Flour exploded in a soft, omnidirectional cloud.

Lillian sputtered, eyes squeezed shut. Her fur was instantly dusted white; it clung to her whiskers, her eyelashes. She wiped at her face, which only made it worse.

Miles, equally coated, coughed. A little puff of flour shot from his muzzle, like an offended dragon.

They stared at each other.

“You look like a haunted truffle,” he said.

She let out a noise somewhere between a scoff and a laugh.

“And you,” she said, “look like someone just exorcised your dignity.”

For a moment, they both held it together.

Then they cracked.

Laughter burst out, sudden and unstoppable. It shook her shoulders, made her belly bounce under the apron. It bent him double, his ears flattening with the force. It echoed off the empty metal surfaces, filled the tent with something that felt cleaner than any apology.

The flap banged open.

Chef Genevieve, their sternest judge, stood there with a clipboard and the expression of someone about to discover a health code violation.

“What in sugar’s name happened here?” she demanded.

Miles stepped instinctively in front of Lillian, shoulders broad, shielding her from the worst of the view.

“Just a test run,” he said. “The flour didn’t approve.”

Genevieve eyed the white-dusted floor, the bags, their faces.

“You have five minutes to clean this up and start again,” she said. “If you waste ingredients, you waste my time. And my time is expensive.”

She turned on her heel and left.

Silence returned, carrying a faint static hum.

Lillian exhaled, long and slow.

“You didn’t have to do that,” she said. “Jump in front of me.”

“I know,” he said. “But I didn’t want her to see your haunted truffle impression. It was… too powerful. Might have unbalanced the judging.”

She shoved his shoulder. Lightly.

“Idiot,” she said.

“Sure,” he said. “But a charming one.”

They looked at each other again, flour softening every edge.

This time, the moment lingered.

“So,” he said. “Apples?”

“Apples,” she agreed.

They swept the floor together, white dust rising and settling. They weighed sugar and sliced fruit. They argued, but softer now, about pastry thickness and spice blends.

By the time the crew arrived, the kitchen looked almost respectable again. If anyone noticed the faint ghosts of their footprints in flour under the benches, they didn’t comment.

The cameras rolled. The challenge began. And from somewhere under the layer of performance, Lillian felt something like trust take root.

### Chapter 8 — Showdown and Softness

The final challenge was the kind of instruction that made producers clap their hands and contestants quietly panic:

CREATE A FUSION DESSERT THAT TELLS YOUR PERSONAL STORY AS A DUO.

“No pressure,” Lillian muttered, reading the card. “Just our entire emotional arc in sugar.”

Miles stood beside her at their station, jaw set.

“We could do something structural,” he said. “Layers. Two distinct elements that hold together.”

“We could,” she said. “Or we could do something messy and honest that collapses if we don’t hold it from both sides.”

He looked at her then, properly. The tent around them buzzed with last-day energy: cameras swooping, host making puns, other teams whispering over bowls. In the middle of it, their station felt like an island.

“What do you want it to say?” he asked.

Lillian considered.

She thought of her first morning in the bakery, hands shaking as she’d unlocked the door. Of the customers who had become friends; of late-night deliveries and early-morning dough; of the way her body had slowly learned to trust chairs and counters again after years of being told it took up too much space.

She thought of him walking into her shop like a storm wrapped in linen. Of the way he’d weighed flour and apologies with equal precision. Of his laugh, cracked open by flour.

“I want it to say that we’re better together,” she said. “But only because we both keep our edges.”

He nodded slowly.

“Okay,” he said. “Fruit for you. Chocolate for me. Layers like your cakes. Structure like my tarts. Nothing perfect. Everything intentional.”

They moved.

He handled the chocolate: dark, glossy, tempered to a shine. She handled the fruit: berries and plums cooked down with spices that smelled like her childhood kitchen and the first winter in Brixton when she and Miles had made jam out of desperation and boredom.

They built the cake together—sponge, soaked with fruit syrup; layers of chocolate cream; a top scattered with sugared petals and shards of caramel that caught the light like stained glass.

The cameras loved it. The host loved it. Tamsin, watching from the wings, cried quietly into a tea towel.

Lillian felt strangely calm.

Her body knew this posture: slightly bent over the bench, paws working, weight balanced. Her heart knew this tension: a performance that mattered, not because of scores or edits, but because of what it would mean in the small, real world they’d return to.

“Ganache?” she asked, without looking up.

“Chilled and ready,” he said, not looking up either.

The silence between them was no longer spiky. It hummed like a mixer on a low, steady speed.

As they plated the final slices, he spoke.

“You know I didn’t mean it,” he said quietly. “What I said about your baking being all nostalgia. You put more intention into a single scone than most places put into their entire menu.”

Her paws stilled, briefly.

“I know,” she said. “Do you know I didn’t mean it when I said your food was joyless?”

He huffed.

“I suspected,” he said. “Eventually.”

“You just… hide it under all that shine,” she went on. “The joy. But it’s there. In the way you fuss over layers. In how you stand between me and Chef Genevieve like she’s a poorly stacked shelf.”

He glanced at her then, eyes warm.

“You drive me mad,” he said. “With your chaos. And your cardamom. And the way you walk into a room like you’ve already made space for everyone else.”

Her chest tightened.

“You drive me mad,” she said. “With your symmetry. And your spreadsheets. And the way you pretend you don’t care what anyone thinks when you obviously do.”

The timer buzzed.

Time.

They stepped back together.

The judges approached.

“Tell us about your bake,” Chef Genevieve said.

Lillian inhaled, tasting chocolate and fear and something sweeter.

“It’s… us,” she said. “Two different ways of surviving kitchens and criticism. Old recipes and new techniques. Structure and spillage. It only works if both parts hold.”

Miles added, “And if someone keeps an eye on the boiler.”

The judges smiled, tasted, murmured. Words like daring and balanced and heartfelt floated back to them.

When the winners were finally announced—names elongated for drama, music swelling—Lillian felt Miles’ paw brush hers at their sides.

“The winners of Sweet Streets,” the host proclaimed, “are… Lillian and Miles!”

Noise crashed over them. Applause. Cheering. A confetti cannon, which Lillian felt was unnecessary but visually effective.

A bouquet was shoved into her arms; a golden whisk trophy into his. Cameras zoomed in on their faces.

She smiled for the lenses, for the crowd, for her students who would undoubtedly watch. Underneath it, something steadier glowed.

Outside, behind the tent, away from the noise, they stood by the orchard fence.

The afternoon sun filtered through the branches, dappling his fur and her apron. The air smelled of grass and sugar and distant frying.

Lillian set the bouquet down on the low wall. Her arms ached pleasantly; her feet throbbed; there was flour at the edge of her vision, dusting his whiskers.

“About earlier,” he began.

“We don’t have to talk right now,” she said quickly, suddenly shy in a way that felt ridiculous for a woman her age.

He took a breath.

“I want to,” he said. “Lillian, I thought I had everything figured out. My flavours. My career. My place in the kitchen. And then you appeared—stubborn and brilliant and messy—and turned my mise en place upside down.”

She snorted, even as her eyes stung.

“You always did over-season your metaphors,” she said.

He smiled, small and real.

“I think I’m in love with you,” he said.

The world narrowed: tent noise gone, cameras forgotten. Just him, shoulders slightly hunched like he expected a blow; just her, breathing in, out, steady as a metronome.

“I think,” she said slowly, “I’ve been in love with you since you called my bakery a dollhouse made of carbs.”

His ears flicked.

“I—what?” he said.

“You walked in,” she said. “Assessed everything in thirty seconds. I hated you on sight. It was very suspicious.”

He laughed, the sound cracking on the edges.

“So this is… enemies to… bakery co-owners,” he said.

“Something like that,” she said.

He stepped closer. She met him halfway.

Their kiss tasted of chocolate and wild berries and icing sugar. His paws were careful on her shoulders, as if he understood that she was both soft and unbreakable. Her paws curled in his shirt, feeling the steady beat of his heart under the fabric.

Somewhere in the distance, the host made another pun. Somewhere, Tamsin probably squealed into a microphone. Here, pressed against the low fence, Lillian let herself lean, just a little.

For once, this was not being watched. It was being seen.

## Whisked Off Her Feet — New Chapters

### Chapter 9 — The First Date

Two days after the confetti and the cameras, Fernwell Lane went back to pretending it was normal.

The festival banners came down. The marquee in the square deflated into a tired heap of canvas. The golden whisk trophy sat on a shelf in Lillian’s tiny flat above the bakery, already gathering a respectable film of dust and stray glitter, because she kept forgetting to wipe it when she did the rest of the cleaning.

Badger’s Crust smelled as it always did in the early evening: sugar and coffee and the ghost of everything that had baked that day. The last customers had drifted out an hour ago. Tamsin had gone home, promising to send Lillian the link to an online clip of “their bit,” which Lillian had absolutely not agreed to watch.

She stood at the front window instead, wiping down the glass with slow, circular motions. Outside, streetlights glowed a soft amber. Rain had come and gone, leaving the pavement damp and reflective, like someone had lacquered Fernwell Lane.

Across the street, Croque et Crème’s neon sign glowed steadily. The door was locked; chairs already up on tables inside. She could see the edge of the counter, the faint shine of stainless steel.

She could also see him.

Miles stood just inside his own window, head bent as he wiped down a surface with the same unhurried concentration she recognised from the tent. His sleeves were rolled, fur at his forearms damp and a little ruffled. His ears flicked as he worked. In the reflection, their movements echoed: wipe, pause, stare at nothing in particular.

The distance between the two shopfronts had never looked so wide.

Her phone buzzed on the counter behind her.

She ignored it. Wiped another slow circle. Told herself that the heavy hum under her ribs was just post-festival adrenaline and not anything ridiculous like hope.

The bell over her door jingled.

Lillian turned, cloth in paw.

Miles stood in the doorway of Badger’s Crust, framed by the warm light and the faint curl of steam from the kitchen. He wore a dark shirt and no apron, a concession to the fact that it was technically after hours. His fur was brushed but not perfect; there was a streak of something pale on his jaw, as if he’d missed a spot when he’d washed up.

He held himself like a man who had rehearsed this moment in his head and not enjoyed any of the drafts.

“We’re closed,” Lillian said automatically, then winced. “I mean, we’re… I was just finishing up.”

“I know,” he said. His voice sounded different without the tent’s microphones or the background roar of a festival. Softer. “I watched you turn the sign around. Twice.”

She glanced at the door, where the chalkboard still read CLOSED in her looping hand. It was, in fact, very firmly closed.

“Did you want something?” she asked, then mentally kicked herself, because of course he did, nobody crossed a street in this kind of awkward slow-motion for no reason.

He swallowed. His ears angled, betraying nerves his face tried to smooth over.

“Lillian,” he said. “I was wondering if you would be available to… debrief the festival outcome over a meal. At a neutral location.”

She stared at him.

“Miles,” she said, wiping her paws on her apron to give herself something to do. “Are you asking me on a date or a post-mortem?”

He held her gaze for a long heartbeat.

“Yes,” he said.

She felt the laugh start low in her chest and work its way up, loosening something as it went. It came out softer than she expected.

“All right,” she said. “Let me get my coat before you change your mind and decide an Excel spreadsheet would be more efficient.”

“I already have one,” he said gravely. “It’s in three colours. But I promise not to show it to you until dessert.”

She rolled her eyes. Her hands shook only a little as she untied her apron and hung it on its hook, feeling the familiar give of the fabric, the way it still held the shape of her body. She grabbed her coat from the peg by the stairs—navy, a bit worn at the cuffs—and shrugged into it, shoulders settling against the weight.

Miles stepped aside to let her pass. The gesture was automatic and careful, his broad body angling so she had space to move comfortably through the doorway. He caught the door before it could swing back into her hip.

Outside, the air was cool and damp, carrying the smell of rain on stone and distant takeaway from the high street. The glow from their two shop windows spilled onto the pavement, two rectangles of light facing each other like a pair of eyes.

“Neutral location,” she said. “So not here and not your temple of stainless steel?”

“Tempting as it is to feed you in my natural habitat,” he said, “I thought it might be… less fraught if we went somewhere neither of us has to think about the margins.”

“Look at you,” she said. “Already considering my stress levels. Careful, someone might mistake you for a decent boyfriend.”

He froze for a fraction of a second at the word. Boyfriend. Then his shoulders relaxed.

“Let’s walk,” he said.

They did.

They took the long way, down Fernwell Lane and around the small park, past the bus stop where teenage saxophonists liked to practice in summer. Lillian felt the pavement under her paws, the way it shifted slightly where roots had pushed up the slabs. She was aware of her body in the way she always was in public—of the way her coat sat across her back, of the place where the belt cut across her middle—but she was also aware of the warmth at her side.

Miles matched his pace to hers without comment. When they reached a narrow bit of pavement between a lamppost and a bin, he moved ahead to take the tighter space, leaving her the wider arc.

He didn’t say anything about it. Neither did she.

The restaurant he’d chosen was tucked down a side street, one of those places with soft lighting and mismatched chairs that had clearly been vetted for sturdiness. The host, a raccoon in a crisp shirt, greeted Miles by name and led them to a corner table with a bench on one side and a generous chair on the other.

“Bench,” Miles said quietly as they approached. “If you prefer.”

Lillian eyed the chair, giving it the same quick assessment she gave every seat in her life. Solid legs. No spindly arms. Plenty of room.

“Chair,” she said, and sat.

The chair received her without complaint. Wood pressed warmly against her thighs. She let her weight settle, feeling the small flood of relief that always came when furniture did what it promised.

Miles took the bench, back to the wall, eyes scanning the room the way he did a kitchen—checking exits, routes, how people moved. The dining room smelled of roasted garlic and tomatoes and a faint undernote of citrus from a dish someone else had ordered.

They opened their menus.

For the first twenty minutes, they talked about work.

It was inevitable. The festival sat between them like a third plate. They dissected challenges, judges’ comments, production quirks. Miles ranted quietly about the way the editors had cut a whole sequence of tempering because “apparently nobody wants to see actual technique on television”; Lillian complained about how many times they’d used the word rustic.

“They could at least have alternated with ‘generous’,” she said, spearing a roasted pepper from her starter. “Or ‘unapologetic.’”

“They used that in my profile,” he said. “About my plating.”

“There you go,” she said. “You’re unapologetic, I’m rustic. Between us we’re almost one respectable adjective.”

He smiled, small and genuine.

Their mains arrived. Her plate was a rich, slow-cooked stew with thick slices of bread; his was a precise stack of grilled fish and vegetables, each element placed with care. The food was good, comforting without trying too hard.

They ate. They talked about suppliers, about the sudden uptick in customers who started sentences with “I saw you on—”

“I got three emails today,” Lillian said, “from people I haven’t seen since university. Apparently I have ‘inspired’ them to start baking again.”

“That sounds… nice?” he said.

“One of them once told me I’d never get cast as anything but an aunt,” she said. “Because of my ‘stage presence.’”

His jaw tightened.

“Name?” he asked.

“No murder on the first date,” she said. “Maybe on the third.”

He huffed a laugh.

The conversation looped, safe and familiar. It was only when the plates were cleared and the dessert menus placed between them that something shifted.

Lillian picked up the card. The options were laid out in tiny, elegant font: dark chocolate tart with hazelnut praline, deconstructed cheesecake with seasonal berries, lemon posset with shortbread.

“Of course,” she said under her breath. “Of course it’s deconstructed.”

Miles leaned in to read, brow furrowing.

“Look at this,” he said, tapping a line. “They’re trying to get a set on that tart with this base? It’ll collapse if you breathe on it.”

“Oh, I see,” she said. “They’re balancing the crumble in a little tower. Why do restaurants hate plates that stay flat?”

He made a small, affronted noise.

“There’s nothing wrong with verticality,” he said. “If you secure your layers.”

“Miles,” she said, staring at him over the menu. “You are in a restaurant as a civilian. You’re allowed to just… eat.”

“You’re the one critiquing their soul,” he said. “I’m merely concerned about structural integrity.”

“Their soul is suffering from a mousse overdose,” she said. “Look—this cheesecake used to be a perfectly nice thing until someone decided to reinterpret it as a pile of rubble.”

They went back and forth, almost without noticing. Dessert became a proxy war: he defended technique; she defended joy. They debated sponge-to-cream ratios, the ethics of shards, the tyranny of slate.

Halfway through a sentence about the tyranny of slate, Lillian stopped.

Miles stopped too, head turning towards her in the same moment.

They stared at each other across the small table, menus halfway between them.

“We’re doing it again,” she said.

“Arguing over dessert in front of an audience,” he said.

They both glanced around. The couple at the next table were pointedly not listening with the intensity of people who had absolutely been listening.

Lillian felt heat rise under her fur. Then, unexpectedly, she laughed.

It came out startled and a little wild. Miles’ mouth twitched, then curved.

The laughter spread, unspooling the tension that had tightened between her shoulders since he’d walked in. She laughed at the absurdity of it—how the universe had taken her quiet little bakery life and folded in this tall, earnest, infuriating man until they were laminated together in front of half the country.

He laughed too, quietly at first, then with his whole body, shoulders shaking. His ears flattened slightly with the force of it.

The couple at the next table smiled into their wine.

“We’re impossible,” Lillian said, wiping a tear from the corner of her eye.

“We are,” he agreed. “And yet.”

He set the dessert menu down. His paw brushed hers as he did. It was a light touch, fingers just grazing fur, but it jolted through her with the clarity of a bell.

She didn’t pull away.

“Lillian,” he said, voice softer. “May I ask you a question?”

“That depends,” she said. “Is it about lamination schedules?”

“No,” he said. “It’s about me.”

She tilted her head.

“Proceed,” she said.

He hesitated, eyes dropping to the tablecloth and then back up to her face.

“Why did you ask me, during the last challenge,” he said slowly, “why I needed everything to be perfect?”

She blinked.

Of all the things she’d expected, that wasn’t it. That question had been thrown at him in the heat of ganache and cameras, half-exasperation, half-genuine curiosity. She’d forgotten the exact words; he clearly hadn’t.

“Because you do,” she said. “Need it, I mean. You tighten like a badly tuned string if something’s a millimetre off. And because I… wanted to know what would happen if something wasn’t. For you.”

He sat back slightly, as if making room inside his chest for the answer.

“When I was a sous-chef at my first serious job,” he said, “I was responsible for plating desserts during a critic’s visit. The whole kitchen was on edge. The head chef—he was old-school. Believed in fear as a motivational tool.”

Lillian listened, paws resting loosely on the table, dessert menu forgotten.

“I sent out a plate with a smear two centimetres too wide,” Miles went on. “Nobody at the table noticed. The critic wrote a very kind review. But in the kitchen…”

He trailed off. His paw curled slightly against the tablecloth.

“He took the plate,” Miles said. “And he smashed it on the floor. In front of everyone. Shouted about precision. About standards. About how if I couldn’t control a smear, I had no business calling myself a chef.”

Lillian’s stomach tightened.

She had watched him, in the tent, set plates with the care of someone placing something fragile between teeth of gears. She had thought, more than once, that his neatness was arrogance. Hearing this, she could see the outline of something else: a young man in a hot kitchen, someone else’s temper shattering ceramic and confidence in the same motion.

“I cleaned up the shards,” he said. “And I promised myself I would never give anyone that opening again. That my plates would be so… exact that nobody could find the crack to wedge their anger into.”

He looked up at her then, eyes very clear.

“That’s the short version,” he added. “There are longer ones. But the gist is: imperfect work feels dangerous. Not because I don’t know people are allowed to be human. Because it’s the only way I learned to feel safe.”

Silence settled between them, warm and dense.

Lillian swallowed around the knot in her throat.

“I’m sorry,” she said. “He was wrong.”

“He was effective,” Miles said. “Which is not the same thing.”

She thought of the conservatory panel who had told her she was “unsuited” to certain roles. Of the students who had apologised for their bodies taking up space on stage. Of every chair she’d eyed with suspicion.

“Do you know what’s funny?” she said. “In a bleak, sitcom-from-hell way?”

“You tell me,” he said.

“I learned the opposite lesson,” she said. “That no matter how perfect I was—how in tune, how technically precise—some people would only see my body. My size. My fur. So I stopped trying to disappear into perfection. I went the other way. More colour. More noise. More crumbs.”

He blinked.

“Now I own a bakery where the house speciality is sugar and stubbornness,” she went on. “And I’ve built a life where I only have to be precise about the things that actually matter. Like oven temperatures. And people.”

His paw moved again, deliberate this time, covering hers.

His pads were warm and slightly rough. The contact was steady, not tentative.

“You’re very good at people,” he said quietly.

“I’m learning,” she said. “Starting with one extremely high-maintenance German Shepherd.”

He huffed a laugh. It brushed against the edge of something like a sob.

“I can… try,” he said. “To be less… rigid. With you. If you can tolerate the odd symmetrical garnish.”

“Deal,” she said. “On the condition that you accept crumbs on the table and feelings in the room.”

“I’m getting used to both,” he said.

The waiter appeared like a conjured spell, asking about dessert. They ordered two things they hadn’t argued about: lemon posset and the chocolate tart. They shared both, spoons clinking, conversation turns lighter again, but threaded now with something deeper.

By the time they stepped back out into the street, the rain had washed the air clean. The pavements shone under streetlights. The town had quieted to the soft background noise of buses and distant traffic.

“I’ll walk you home,” Miles said.

“It’s three streets,” she said.

“Humour me,” he said.

She did.

They walked side by side, their shadows overlapping on the wet pavement. As they approached Fernwell Lane, the familiar smell of her bakery reached her before the sign came into view: flour, sugar, the deep, yeasty comfort that had become her life.

The windows of Badger’s Crust glowed against the dark. The sign over the door looked suddenly small; she saw it through the eyes of the country, of people who had watched the show and might one day look up from a bus stop and recognise it.

They stopped in front of the door.

“Well,” she said. “Thank you for the… debrief.”

“Thank you for reminding me that desserts are allowed to have souls,” he said.

They stood there, bodies angled towards each other, the gap between them small and full of possibility.

Lillian felt her heart knock once, hard, against her ribs.

“Miles,” she said, because if she didn’t say something she might combust. “If this is going to be a thing—”

“It is,” he said, a little too fast.

Her mouth quirked.

“Then I should warn you,” she went on, “I come with a bakery, a band, a daughter, and a worrying number of ceramic mugs.”

“I come with spreadsheets, a complicated relationship with symmetry, and a tendency to overthink everything,” he said. “Fair trade?”

“We’ll see,” she said.

He took a breath.

“May I kiss you,” he asked, “now that there isn’t a confetti cannon involved?”

The question settled over her like a warm blanket. Asked, not assumed; offered, not demanded.

She stepped closer.

“Yes,” she said.

He leaned in.

The kiss was not the wild, adrenaline-laced crash behind the tent. It was slower, grounded. His mouth was warm and careful, his paws resting on her shoulders as if he understood, intimately, that she was both soft and solid. She curled her paws into his shirt, the fabric smooth under her claws, feeling the steady thump of his heart.

The world around them narrowed to the circle of light spilling from the bakery window, the cool air on her cheeks, the faint taste of lemon and chocolate on his tongue.

When they parted, their foreheads rested against each other for a moment.

“Okay,” she said, breathless but steady. “Let’s begin.”

He smiled, wide and unguarded, and for the first time since he’d walked into her life and called her bakery a dollhouse made of carbs, she let herself believe that this might be something that lasted longer than a season of television.

Above them, the sign creaked slightly in the night breeze and held.

### Chapter 10 — Meeting Sarah

The following Saturday, Lillian cleaned her flat as if the cameras were coming back.

They weren’t, of course. The production crew had long since moved on to their next project. There were no clipboards in sight, no drones buzzing overhead. Just her tiny living room with its sagging sofa, the shelves full of mismatched mugs and scores, the sturdy coffee table bearing the scars of years of poorly-coastered mugs.

Still, she vacuumed as if the floor would be inspected. She wiped down surfaces that had not seen a cloth in months. She rearranged cushions, then rearranged them again. She checked the framed photos on the wall—her and Sarah at sixteen, flour on their noses; her band on stage; an old, slightly grainy shot of herself in a concert hall dress that no longer fit her body but still fit her memory.

On the low bookcase by the window, the golden whisk trophy watched her with patient, metallic amusement.

“Don’t start,” she told it, straightening a stack of music journals that did not need straightening.

Downstairs, the bakery hummed with Saturday business. Tamsin was more than capable of handling the rush; Lillian had told herself that a hundred times as she climbed the stairs. Today, the work she needed to do was here.

Today, Miles was going to meet Sarah properly.

He’d seen her in glimpses, on video calls when he happened to be in the background, in the photos on Lillian’s phone, in the half-finished sculptures that appeared in the flat when Sarah came home from university. He knew her in the way you know someone who is always just offstage.

Lillian knew her differently.

She knew the weight of Sarah’s head on her lap when she’d first fallen asleep in Lillian’s living room at sixteen, exhausted from pretending she was fine. She knew the way Sarah’s laughter started high and spiralled down into a snort she tried to hide. She knew the exact look Sarah got when she didn’t trust someone with Lillian’s heart.

“It’ll be fine,” she muttered, fluffing a cushion for the third time. “You are a grown badger. You have survived conservatoire juries and reality television and the invention of cronuts. You can introduce your girlfriend to your daughter without combusting.”

She paused.

“Boyfriend,” she corrected under her breath, and felt her stomach flip.

She was still standing in the middle of the room, holding a cushion like a shield, when the door downstairs opened and closed.

Footsteps on the stairs: a familiar weight distribution, the creak of the third step.

Miles appeared in the doorway, a Tupperware box in one paw and a bottle of wine in the other, because of course he’d brought offerings.

He wore a soft grey jumper instead of a chef’s jacket. It made him look more like the man who had laughed with her in the flour cloud than the one on glossy magazine pages. His ears were tipped slightly back, betraying nerves his posture tried to disguise.

“Hello,” he said. “I come bearing sugar and alcohol.”

“Good,” she said. “We’ll need both.”

He stepped into the room, eyes flicking around, taking in the space that was hers alone: the comfortable clutter, the stacks of books, the plant that refused to die in the corner.

“It smells like you up here,” he said, then flushed. “I mean, like the bakery, but also… you. Flour and tea and… something citrus.”

“Lemon cleaner,” she said. “Don’t get poetic, you’ll scare the furniture.”

He smiled, tension easing a fraction.

“How can I help?” he asked.

“You can sit,” she said. “And not look like you’re about to perform heart surgery on my family.”

He hesitated, then obeyed, choosing the corner of the sofa that had the best support. The sofa received him with only a minor protest. He placed the Tupperware and the bottle carefully on the table, as if setting mise en place.

Lillian sat opposite him in her favourite armchair, the one that had a dip perfectly tailored to her body. The fabric hugged her hips and thighs; the armrests were wide enough that she didn’t have to scrunch herself in.

They sat in companionable silence for a moment, listening to the muffled clatter from the bakery below.

“I’m more nervous than I was before the final,” he admitted.

“So am I,” she said. “And I love that girl more than any panel of judges.”

He looked at her, expression softening.

“She adores you,” he said. “That much is obvious from the way she talks about you.”

“She talks about me?” Lillian asked.

“She sends me photos sometimes,” he said. “Of your early cellos. Of the first time you let her frost a cake. Of that sculpture she made of you and the bakery, all welded spoons and clay.”

Lillian blinked.

“I didn’t know that,” she said.

He shrugged, suddenly shy.

“I think she was… checking my responses,” he said. “Like sending me a syllabus for a course she expected me to pass before I was allowed to call myself your partner.”

Lillian’s heart rose to her throat and sat there.

“How did you do?” she asked.

He gave a small, crooked smile.

“I’m here, aren’t I?” he said.

As if on cue, the buzzer by the door sounded—a quick, impatient press.

Lillian stood, thighs pressing against the edge of the armchair, heart thudding.

“That’ll be her,” she said.

“I guessed,” he said.

She shot him a look that was half affection, half plea for luck, then went downstairs to let her daughter in.

Sarah hit the hallway like weather.

She was a golden retriever in her late twenties, hair pulled up in a messy knot that looked accidental and was probably curated in a mirror. She wore paint-splattered jeans, an oversized jumper, and a backpack that looked like it contained either art supplies or a small badger.

“Mum,” she said, wrapping Lillian in a hug before the door had fully closed. “You smell like cinnamon and terror.”

“I cleaned,” Lillian said into her shoulder. “Don’t make fun of me.”

Sarah pulled back, eyes bright, tail wagging in a way that would have knocked over a lesser coat rack.

“I would never,” she said. “Where is he?”

Lillian swallowed.

“Upstairs,” she said. “Trying not to hyperventilate into my cushions.”

“Good,” Sarah said. “Can’t have a man in my mother’s life who doesn’t respect soft furnishings.”

She bounded up the stairs two at a time. Lillian followed at a more measured pace, feeling the familiar ache in her knees and the unfamiliar flutter of this particular fear.

In the doorway of the living room, Sarah stopped.

Miles had stood when he heard them, because of course he had. He faced the door with his shoulders back, paws relaxed at his sides, expression politely neutral—the same look he’d worn for judges, now repurposed for her daughter.

Sarah took him in: tall, broad, ears a little too upright. Tupperware on the table, wine bottle glinting.

“So,” she said, folding her arms. “You’re the spreadsheet guy.”

Miles blinked.

“And you’re the one who thinks ‘structurally improbable’ is a compliment,” he said.

A beat. Then Sarah’s mouth curved into a slow smile.

“It is,” she said.

Something in the room loosened.

Lillian exhaled a breath she hadn’t realised she’d been holding. She moved in, closing the door behind them.

“Sarah, this is Miles,” she said, the words feeling simultaneously enormous and perfectly ordinary. “Miles, this is Sarah. My favourite human.”

“You say that to everyone,” Sarah said, nudging her with an elbow.

“I really don’t,” Lillian said.

Miles stepped forward, offering a paw.

“It’s good to meet you properly,” he said. “Without a camera in your mum’s face.”

Sarah shook his paw, grip firm.

“I’ve seen plenty of your face on my phone,” she said. “Tamsin sends me memes.”

“Of course she does,” Lillian muttered.

They moved into the room. Lillian gestured Sarah to the armchair; she flopped into it, limbs everywhere, as if she hadn’t once been the teenager who perched politely on edges of seats. Miles resumed his place on the sofa, leaving a generous gap between himself and the cushion beside him, a visible invitation and boundary in one.

Lillian took the opposite end, grounding herself in the familiar dip of the cushion.

“I brought tarts,” Miles said, lifting the Tupperware. “Raspberry and pistachio. And… a white wine that allegedly pairs well with awkward introductions.”

“If it pairs with pastry, I’m in,” Sarah said. “Introduce your desserts to my face.”

They ate. The tarts were very Miles: precise layers, clean flavours, a presentation that somehow survived the journey in plastic. Sarah made appreciative noises, then pretended not to when Lillian raised an eyebrow.

The conversation started where most conversations in their family did: work.

Sarah talked about her courses in Manchester, about a tutor who loved her structural experiments and another who thought her work was “too literal” for high art, whatever that meant. She described a piece she was working on—twisted scrap metal and clay that suggested a city collapsing into a kitchen.

Miles listened with genuine, focused interest. He asked questions, not just the polite ones about exhibits and marks, but practical ones about materials and balance.

“How are you stabilising the central column?” he asked. “If you’re hanging that much weight off one side, you’ll need reinforcement here.”

He gestured, air-sketching an invisible armature.

Sarah sat up a little straighter.

“Rebar,” she said. “Hidden in the core. Welded cross-brace at the base. I’m using clay to disguise it so it looks like it shouldn’t stand up.”

“So the illusion is of fragility,” he said, “but the reality is engineered strength.”

Her eyes lit.

“Exactly,” she said. “It’s about how people think certain bodies or structures shouldn’t be possible and yet—”

She stopped, glancing at Lillian, then back at Miles.

“Anyway,” she finished. “It’s due in three weeks.”

“I’d love to see pictures,” he said. “If you’re comfortable sharing.”

She searched his face for a beat, as if checking for patronising glint. Finding none, she nodded.

“I’ll send them,” she said. “As long as you promise not to send back a spreadsheet.”

“No spreadsheets,” he said solemnly. “At most, a strongly-worded admiration email.”

Lillian watched them from her corner of the sofa, heart doing an odd, double-time rhythm. The exchange felt… right. Not forced. Not staged. Just two people who saw the world in terms of load-bearing lines and the stories bodies told in space.

She slipped into the kitchen to put the kettle on, partly because it was the done thing and partly because she needed the small cover of rattling mugs to collect herself.

From the doorway, she watched them as the kettle hummed.

Sarah had pulled a sketchbook from her bag. She flipped to a page and angled it towards Miles. The drawing was of Lillian in the bakery, seen from behind the counter: broad shoulders, hair in a braid, apron tied tight. The linework caught the way she leaned into the work, weight forward, unselfconscious.

“This is incredible,” Miles said quietly. “You’ve got the posture exactly. The way she stands when she’s thinking about four things at once.”

Lillian swallowed.

“You can tell that from a sketch?” Sarah asked.

“I can tell that because I’ve been staring at that posture across a tent for weeks,” he said. “And across the street for longer.”

Sarah’s mouth twitched.

“You’re very earnest,” she said.

“Occupational hazard,” he replied.

“Mum needs earnest,” Sarah said. “She’s got sarcasm covered.”

Lillian busied herself with mugs and teabags, cheeks warm.

When she carried the tray back in, balancing it against her belly, Sarah jumped up to help without being asked, taking two cups from the tray. Miles moved to take the heavier teapot.

They navigated the small space together without collision, as if they’d been sharing cramped kitchens all their lives.

Later, when plates were empty and the tea had cooled, Sarah sat cross-legged on the floor, back against the sofa, sketching idly. Miles perched on the edge of the coffee table, sorting through the postcards and flyers she’d scattered there from recent exhibitions.

“So,” Sarah said, pencil moving. “What’s your damage?”

Miles blinked.

“I’m sorry?” he asked.

“Artists’ shorthand,” she said. “What made you like this? The precision. The spreadsheets. The… intense concern for the thickness of sauces.”

Lillian opened her mouth to intervene, then closed it. She had told Sarah, early on, that if she wanted honesty from people she needed to ask the questions that mattered. She couldn’t begrudge her for doing exactly that now.

Miles took a breath.

“Short version?” he said. “I grew up in a family where being useful was love. I discovered that I could make things—machines, recipes, schedules—behave if I just learned enough about them. People were harder. Kitchens were somewhere in the middle.”

Sarah’s pencil paused.

“And you decided to major in kitchens,” she said.

“More or less,” he said. “There was an incident with a tarte tatin and a plate. It’s a long story. Your mother has heard the director’s cut.”

Lillian’s chest pulled tight at the memory of the smashed plate, the way his voice had changed when he told her.

Sarah watched him for a moment, then resumed sketching.

“You’re very controlled,” she said. “But you’re here in my mum’s living room admitting vulnerability. That’s… promising.”

He huffed a laugh.

“Is this an interview?” he asked.

“This is the preliminary round,” she said. “There will be follow-up questions.”

“I’ll prepare a slideshow,” he said.

“If you do, I’m out,” she said.

They grinned at each other.

Lillian sat back, letting the scene soak into her. The light from the window caught the edges of their fur, the curve of Sarah’s jaw so familiar and yet wholly her own. The room smelled of tea and pastry and the faint metallic tang of the golden whisk.

“What do you think,” Sarah asked suddenly, looking up at Lillian, “about all of this?”

She gestured—at the room, at Miles, at herself.

Lillian considered.

“I think,” she said slowly, “that six months ago I was mostly worried about oven temperatures and paying the electricity bill. And now I’m worried about all of that plus whether my daughter and my boyfriend like each other.”

Miles’ ears flicked at the word again. Boyfriend. He didn’t correct it.

“I think,” she went on, “that I am… happy. Which is frankly terrifying. But also…”

She trailed off, searching for the right phrase. Sarah watched her, pencil hovering over paper. Miles’ gaze was steady and gentle.

“…right,” she finished. “It feels right.”

Sarah’s face softened.

“Okay,” she said. “Then I’m in.”

“Just like that?” Miles asked.

“Just like that,” she said. “For now. Subject to review if you ever make her cry on purpose.”

“Fair,” he said quietly.

He didn’t promise he never would; he’d been alive long enough to know better. But he looked at Lillian with a kind of awe that made her stomach flutter, and she believed he’d do his best.

When Sarah left later, bag heavy on her back, she hugged them both at the door. She squeezed Miles with more strength than he’d anticipated; he made a small, undignified oof that she pretended not to hear.

“Take care of each other,” she said, stepping back onto the stair.

“We will,” Lillian said.

“I intend to,” Miles said.

She gave them one last assessing look, then grinned.

“You’re disgusting,” she said fondly. “I love it.”

The door closed behind her, leaving the flat humming with her absence.

Lillian leaned back against it, feeling the solid wood against her spine. Her body ached from a day of cleaning and a morning in the bakery. Her heart felt oddly light.

Miles came to stand beside her, close enough that their shoulders touched.

“You all right?” he asked.

She nodded.

“She likes you,” she said.

“She tolerated me,” he said. “There’s a difference.”

“For Sarah?” Lillian said. “Not much.”

He exhaled, tension leaving his shoulders in a visible wave.

“Good,” he said softly.

She turned her head, resting her cheek briefly against his upper arm, letting herself lean.

“This,” she thought, not for the first time, “is what it’s supposed to feel like.”

### Chapter 11 — The Badger & The Hound

The first time Lillian and Miles stood in what would become The Badger & The Hound, it looked nothing like a bakery.

It looked like a mistake.

The space was a long, narrow former hardware shop halfway down Fernwell Lane, technically between Badger’s Crust and Croque et Crème if you followed the line of the street. The windows were dusty; the floor was bare concrete; the walls had the defeated beige of paint that had given up.

Dust motes floated in the strips of light from the high front windows. Somewhere, a pipe gurgled in a way that suggested opinions about the future.

Lillian stood in the middle of the room, paws on her hips, breathing in the smell of plaster and old nails. It did not smell like sugar. Yet.

Miles stood beside her, a rolled-up blueprint tube under his arm. He was already frowning at the ceiling.

“The load-bearing beams are in decent shape,” he said. “We’ll need to reinforce where the ovens go, but the structure will hold.”

“Good,” she said. “I was worried the building might have the same stamina as I do after a double shift.”

He glanced at her, the corner of his mouth curving.

“You’ve carried more weight than this place ever has,” he said.

From the back of the room, Sarah’s voice floated up.

“Less flirting, more measuring,” she said. “Some of us are here on a volunteer basis.”

She sat cross-legged on an overturned crate, sketchbook open in her lap, pencil moving as she tried to capture the bones of the space. A tape measure lay beside her, already unfurled.

“You volunteered aggressively,” Lillian called.

“I have a vested interest in the narrative coherence of this street,” Sarah said.

Her pencil scratched. She caught the line of Lillian’s shoulders, the way Miles tilted his head when he thought, the intrusive confidence of the old radiators.

Miles popped the cap off the blueprint tube and shook out a roll of paper. It fell open with a satisfying snap, lines and measurements already neatly inked.

“Here,” he said, kneeling to spread it on a cleanish patch of floor. “Counter here, ovens along this wall. Seating in the front half. We can put in a small stage in the corner for your band.”

Lillian knelt opposite him, joints protesting in chorus with the floor. She leaned over the paper, reading the lines as if they were music.

“Where do people queue?” she asked. “I don’t want them bottlenecking at the door.”

“Here,” he said, tracing a path with his paw. “We can do a gentle curve past the display. It keeps the flow moving, reduces clumping.”

“You and your clumping,” she said. “People aren’t batter.”

“People are absolutely batter,” he said. “Unpredictable, prone to overmixing, and likely to spill if left unattended.”

Sarah snorted.

They went back and forth, adjusting imaginary counters and sinks until the space on the paper felt like something she could picture with her eyes open.

The arguments started when they reached the part of the blueprint labelled SIGNAGE & BRANDING.

“It makes sense to keep Badger’s Crust,” Lillian said. “It has history. People know it. It smells like… us.”

“It smells like you,” Miles said. “Which is fantastic. But this is new. It’s both of us. It could be… Ferguson & Edgecombe Patisserie. Clean. Professional. It would look good on invoices.”

“I’m not putting my name second,” she said, bristling. “I’ve been on this street longer. And it sounds like a law firm.”

“Edgecombe & Ferguson, then,” he said. “It scans better in English.”

“You literally just said—”

“I’m talking about rhythm, not hierarchy,” he said. “And anyway, names like that signal a partnership. Two equals.”

“Names like that signal that everyone inside wears the same colour apron and nobody is allowed to lick the spoon,” she said. “This isn’t a consulting firm, Miles. It’s a place where Mrs Patel can come in with her dog and complain about her son-in-law.”

He scrubbed a paw over his face, leaving a faint streak of dust on his fur.

“Badger’s Crust was your second act,” he said. “This—” he gestured around them “—this is mine. The first thing I’ve done entirely on purpose, not because a restaurant group thought it was a good idea. I want… to see my name on it. On something that isn’t going to close when a spreadsheet says it should.”

The words hung in the air between them, raw and earnest.

Lillian’s anger deflated, leaving something more complicated behind.

“I know,” she said quietly. “I do. I just…”

She looked towards the imaginary front of the shop, seeing overlapping ghosts of signs: her handwritten chalkboard, his sleek neon. The thought of erasing Badger’s Crust from the street made her stomach twist.

“I don’t want to vanish,” she said. “Again. I spent too many years being the person in the background. The accompanist, the friend, the ‘plus size’ in the corner. The bakery is the first time my name has been the one on the door.”

Miles’ expression softened.

“I would never ask you to vanish,” he said.

“You’re literally asking me to take my name off my shop,” she said.

“I’m asking you to share it,” he said. “With me. With us. With whatever this is.”

“This,” she said, “is me trying not to throw a tape measure at you.”

Sarah sighed theatrically from her crate.

“You’re both being idiots,” she announced.

They looked at her, mid-glare.

“Excuse me?” Lillian said.

“You heard me,” Sarah said. “You’re framing it like it’s one or the other. Old versus new. Hers versus his. When you could, wild idea, have both.”

Miles frowned.

“Both,” he repeated.

Sarah stood, stretching. She padded over, sketchbook in paw, and dropped it on top of the blueprint.

On the page, in bold pencil lines, a badger and a German Shepherd stood back to back, each holding a whisk. Above them, in hand-lettered script, were three words.

THE BADGER & THE HOUND.

“You’re the stubborn, brilliant badger,” she said, tapping Lillian’s drawn self. “You’re the loyal, methodical hound.” She tapped Miles. “It’s not just a name, it’s a story. Everyone who walks in will know immediately who’s holding this place up.”

Lillian stared at the drawing.

The badger was broad and grounded, weight settled in her hips, eyes bright behind her glasses. The hound was tall and slightly angular, ears pricked, stance alert but not stiff. They fitted together the way their movements in the tent had, the way they did in her kitchen.

Miles looked from the sketch to the room, eyes flicking as if overlaying the logo onto the blank wall above the imaginary counter.

“The Badger & The Hound,” he said slowly. “It sounds like somewhere you could bring a family. Or a date. Or a laptop. It sounds… warm.”

“It sounds like a pub,” Lillian said.

“Exactly,” Sarah said. “People love pubs. They trust a pub. You’re not a law firm; you’re a place people come when their day has been terrible and they need cake and to see someone who knows their usual order.”

Lillian’s chest did that annoying flutter again.

“Artisan Bakes with Bite,” Sarah added, scribbling the tagline under the sketch.

“It’s ridiculous,” Lillian said.

“It’s perfect,” Miles said at the same time.

They looked at each other.

“Fine,” Lillian said. “I like it. A little.”

“I love it,” Miles said. “Unreservedly.”

Sarah grinned, all teeth.

“Good,” she said. “Because I already mocked up an Instagram logo.”

The arguments shifted after that.

They still bickered—over paint colours (she wanted deep teal, he wanted light grey; they compromised on a soft blue that made the room feel bigger), over tile patterns (hexagons versus rectangles, a saga in three acts), over whether to have one big communal table or several smaller ones.

But underneath the bickering sat the certainty of the name, a shared story they could both live inside.

They painted together, rollers squeaking against the walls. Lillian’s strokes were broad and enthusiastic; Miles’ were precise, corners sharp. They ended up speckled with paint, flecks drying on fur. Sarah documented the process with her phone and her sketchbook, catching the moment when Lillian tried to reach a high spot, overbalanced, and Miles steadied her with a paw at her back.

They assembled counters, cursing flat-pack instructions in three languages. They argued about where to put the mixer so that Lillian had enough room to move and still see the door. They measured tables, tested chairs, sat in each one to make sure it received Lillian’s weight with respect and comfort.

“If a chair can’t hold me,” she said, lowering herself into one with experimental caution, “it doesn’t get to live here.”

“Same goes for people,” Miles muttered, adjusting the screw on a wobbly leg.

They hung shelves. They argued about mug capacity. They wired in softly glowing pendant lights that turned the bare walls into something closer to home.

On a late evening near the end of the renovation, Lillian stood alone in the middle of the half-finished bakery. The floor was still bare concrete, but the walls were painted. The counter was in. The ovens, her ovens and his ovens, sat side by side like two solid hearts.

She inhaled.

It still smelled faintly of plaster and paint, but underneath she caught a hint of something else: flour from the test batch of bread she’d baked that afternoon, sugar from the caramel Miles had trialled, the ghost of cardamom she had brought with her like a signature.

Her body was tired in the pleasant, deep way that meant she’d used it fully. Her shoulders ached; her knees reminded her that she was not twenty; her hands bore paint and tiny nicks from wood.

Miles came in from the back, carrying a wrapped package.

“Come outside,” he said.

“If this is another surprise delivery of tiles, I’m going to stage a coup,” she said, but she followed him to the door.

The street was quiet. The sky above Fernwell Lane had settled into that deep city indigo, not entirely dark, lit by streetlamps and the glow from flats above shops. The old Badger’s Crust sign across the way was dark now; the space would become something else soon—a rehearsal room, she told herself. A studio. The memory of it would live here.

Miles set the package down and unwrapped it.

The sign gleamed in the lamplight: THE BADGER & THE HOUND in cream letters on a deep blue background, the ampersand curling like a tail. In one corner, a tiny illustration of a badger and a hound sharing a whisk.

Lillian’s throat closed.

“We can still change it,” he said quickly. “If you hate it. If it feels wrong.”

She couldn’t speak for a moment. She reached out instead, fingertips brushing the painted surface. The wood was smooth, solid under her paw.

“It feels… like us,” she said finally. “Like all the bits. The stubbornness, the spreadsheets, the cardamom, the… whatever it is you do with blowtorches.”

“Sugar work,” he said automatically.

“That,” she said.

Sarah appeared beside them, having materialised from somewhere with the uncanny stealth of adult children. She carried a small step ladder under one arm.

“Are we doing this?” she asked.

Miles nodded.

They worked together to hang the sign.

Sarah held the ladder steady, feet planted wide. Miles climbed, careful with his weight, and bolted the brackets into the brick with the ease of someone who had spent his youth helping his father with DIY projects that were never as simple as they looked. Lillian handed up screws, tools, a damp cloth to wipe away dust.

When he climbed down, the three of them stepped back.

The sign sat above the door, solid and sure.

THE BADGER & THE HOUND.

Lillian felt something in her chest settle into place, like dough reaching the right proof.

“It looks like it’s always been there,” Sarah said quietly.

“It hasn’t,” Lillian said. “We did this. We made this.”

Miles’ paw brushed hers. She curled her fingers around his, the contact easy now, familiar.

“It’s not just a business,” he said.

“It’s a home,” she finished.

They stood there for a long moment, three silhouettes on a quiet street, looking up at the name they’d chosen. Cars passed at the end of the lane, headlights briefly lighting the sign. Somewhere, a fox laughed in a garden.

Down the road, someone would one day point to that sign and say, That’s where the badger and the hound from the telly ended up. That’s where the cinnamon rolls taste like forgiveness and the tarts taste like precision and if you sit there long enough, you start to believe there might be space for you, too.

For now, it was just theirs.

“We should go to bed,” Lillian said eventually. “We have a bakery to open in the morning.”

“We have our bakery to open,” Miles corrected.

She let herself smile, wide and unguarded, letting the word our settle over her like flour.

Together, they turned off the interior lights. The sign glowed softly in the streetlamps, holding its place against the night.

### Chapter 12 — Flour, Fire, and Forever

Six months later, Fernwell Lane smelled different.

Richer, somehow. More layered.

The chalkboard sign outside the corner shop had been replaced. Where it once read BADGER’S CRUST in slightly wobbly hand-lettering, it now announced:

THE BADGER & THE HOUND  
Artisan Bakes with Bite

Underneath, in smaller letters, someone—probably Sarah on a visit—had added: *No scented candles were harmed in the making of these pastries.*

Inside, the space had expanded.

Literally: the wall between Badger’s Crust and what had once been a storage unit had been knocked through, giving them more room for tables. The paint was fresh, a soft cream that made the room feel brighter. The chairs were sturdier, their legs reinforced under Miles’ supervision. The counters had been rearranged so that there was space for both Lillian’s big mixer and the kind of delicate equipment Miles insisted on.

Metaphorically: the air held more stories now.

Jazz played quietly from a speaker in the corner, trumpet and piano dancing under the clink of cups. People sat at tables, talking, reading, working. The bakery had become, even more than before, a third space between home and work where people came to exist in their bodies without apology.

Behind the counter, Lillian piped rosettes onto a lemon–lavender cake, the bag steady in her paw. Her apron was a new one—navy, with a small embroidered logo of a badger and a shepherd sharing a whisk. Her hair was tied up, tendrils escaping around her face; there was flour on her cheek, as there always was by mid-morning.

Miles pulled a tray of caramelised tarts from the oven with the practiced ease of a man who had repeated a motion until it became meditation. He set them on the cooling rack, the caramel still bubbling faintly, and checked the oven temperature out of habit.

“Stop fussing,” she said, without looking up. “They’re fine.”

“I fuss because I care,” he said. “About structural integrity.”

“Of tarts or of your ego?” she asked.

“Both,” he said. “But in different ways.”

He moved past her to refill the display, bodies brushing. They fit now, in the literal sense—they had rearranged the workspace so they could pass each other without muttered apologies—but also in the way their movements anticipated each other. If she reached for the sugar, he slid the jar within reach. If he needed space to temper chocolate, she shifted her trays without thinking.

“Order up,” called Tamsin, sliding a cappuccino and a slice of cake onto the pass. “For table three—the influencer couple who keep taking photos of the floor.”

“We did choose very photogenic tiles,” Miles said.

“We chose tiles that would hide crumbs,” Lillian said. “Their aesthetic value is a bonus.”

Sarah had been down twice since the relaunch, each time with a new sketchbook. The last time, she’d drawn them in the kitchen: Lillian bent over a mixing bowl, Miles at the oven, their bodies angled towards each other without realising. She’d titled it *Load-Bearing (Bakery Edition)* and hung a print near the door.

Locals loved it. Tourists took photos. Someone online had called them “aspirational anti-diet culture baking parents,” which made Lillian choke on her tea for a full thirty seconds.

In the afternoons, when the rush dipped, they sometimes had a moment to breathe.

Today, around three, the crowd thinned briefly. Mrs Patel left with her usual bag of cheese scones; a group of teenagers shuffled out, licking icing from their paws. The hum quieted to a gentle murmur.

Miles leaned his elbows on the counter, stretching his back. His spine clicked, soft and satisfying.

“You all right?” Lillian asked, finishing a swirl of frosting.

“Fine,” he said. “Just… adjusting to successful small business ownership.”

She smiled.

“We’ve always owned a small business,” she said.

“Yes,” he said. “But now we own it together. And people come in because they saw us bicker on television.”

“Authenticity,” she said dryly. “Our brand.”

He glanced at her, warmth in his eyes.

“Our brand is you glaring at me over a bag of flour,” he said. “Everything else is garnish.”

The bell chimed. A little girl darted in, dragging her harried-looking father.

“Mum says we can get the cake from the telly!” she announced.

Lillian laughed.

“We don’t have the exact one,” she said. “But we have its cousin.”

They boxed up slices; the father paid; the girl waved as they left.

“Does it ever feel… strange?” Miles asked, watching them go. “That people know us from that?”

“Less strange than it did at first,” she said. “At least here they see the whole of us. Not just the edit.”

He nodded.

They had both watched the series when it aired. Alone, at first. Then together, pausing every few minutes to comment on angles, decisions, how his voice sounded pitched in post-production. They’d cringed at some of their own words; they’d texted in real time with Sarah, who’d sent emojis and unsolicited critiques of the colour grading.

In the end, it had been… not terrible. They were recognisable to themselves. That counted for a lot.

On the wall behind the counter, Lillian had pinned a photo: the two of them holding the golden whisk trophy, both grinning like idiots, confetti in their fur. It sat among other pictures: her and Sarah in the old bakery; Miles in his first chef’s whites; the band on stage in some long-ago bar.

“We did all right,” she said.

“We did,” he said.

Tamsin cleared her throat.

“Hate to interrupt the domestic montage,” she said, “but there’s a food blogger at table two asking if you’d like to do a podcast about ‘fat joy and pastry as resistance.’”

Lillian blinked.

“That’s… specific,” she said.

“On brand,” said Miles.

Lillian thought of the little girl, of the comments online, of her younger self, hunched on a bus seat with a paper bag of biscuits, wishing the world had more women who looked like her and ate without apology.

“Tell them,” she said, “that we’ll think about it. And that if they spell my name wrong, they get decaf.”

Tamsin saluted and went back to the espresso machine.

“You know you’re going to say yes,” Miles said.

“Probably,” she agreed. “Someone has to say out loud that this”—she gestured to her body, the bakery, him—“is not a cautionary tale.”

He reached across the counter, resting his paw over hers. His palm was warm, calloused from years of kitchen work and, before that, other tools.

“It’s a happy ending,” he said.

“Careful,” she said. “We’re not at the credits yet.”

“We can have more than one,” he said. “Epilogues. Sequels.”

She squeezed his paw.

“Greedy,” she said.

“Always,” he said.

The bell chimed again as new customers came in—a teacher marking papers, a courier on a break, a couple sharing headphones. The bakery filled up; the jazz picked up; the ovens beeped.

Lillian let his paw go and reached for a tray. He moved to the oven. They stepped around each other in the small space they had made big enough, together.

Outside, a light rain began, dots of water beading on the chalkboard sign. Inside, the smell of sugar and cardamom and caramel wrapped around everyone who walked in, warm and unapologetic.

Somewhere in a living room, a rerun of Sweet Streets played, the episode where flour exploded and two bakers laughed until they forgot the cameras. Somewhere in a student flat in Manchester, Sarah sketched yet another version of them, lines capturing weight and movement and something like joy.

Here, in the bakery on the corner of Fernwell Lane, a badger and a shepherd baked and bickered and kissed between orders when they thought no one was watching.

Sometimes people were. Sometimes they weren’t.

Either way, the dough rose.
