## Chapter 7 – The Cosplay Disaster (Past)

"That I love you, and that you love me, and that's worth more than all the historically accurate gowns in the world."

## Chapter 8: Weight & Change (Present)

*Seven years later*

The thing about time is that it has a way of smoothing out the sharp edges of memory, turning dramatic moments into gentle anecdotes and transforming the people we were into the people we become. "I think I was writing the story I hoped might be true. And then, somehow, it became true."

Miles kissed the top of my head, his arms tightening around me. "I love that story. Both versions—the one you wrote, and the one we're living."

As I drifted off to sleep, I realized that this—this ability to grow and change while remaining fundamentally ourselves, this capacity to love someone through their transformations—this was what real partnership looked like.

Not the fairy tale version where two people meet and live happily ever after without ever changing, but the real version where two people choose each other again and again, through all the versions of themselves they become.

## Chapter 9: Dancing with Hobbits (Present Day)

*Present day*

"Are you ready?" Miles asked, adjusting his cloak one final time and checking that his sword was properly secured to his belt. His German Shepherd ears were alert, picking up the subtle sounds of convention activity beginning in the hallways around us.

I looked at myself in the hotel mirror, taking in the sight of the sorceress who looked back at me. The costume fit properly now—we'd had it altered to accommodate my changed body—but it was still recognizably the same character I'd been playing for eight years. The deep purple velvet, the silver embroidery, the dramatic sleeves that made me feel like I could cast actual spells.

My reflection showed a European Badger who was both familiar and strange. At two hundred pounds, I was still substantial, still unmistakably myself, but the changes were evident in every line. My face had a different quality now—the roundness that had once defined it softened but still present, with gentler curves to my cheeks and a less pronounced double chin. My distinctive black and white facial markings seemed more defined now, no longer softened by the fuller cheeks of my heavier days. My neck was more visible than it had been at my heaviest, though it remained soft and full.

The costume itself told the story of my transformation. Where once the bodice had strained against my substantial breasts, now it fit with room to breathe, though I remained full-figured and curved. My arms, while still substantial and soft, no longer strained the elaborate sleeves as they once had. My belly, which had once pressed against the fabric with unapologetic roundness, now rested gently beneath the flowing skirt, soft and curved but no longer dominating my silhouette.

But it was more than just the physical changes. The way I held myself was different—more confident, less apologetic for the space I occupied. My posture spoke of someone who had learned to inhabit her body with comfort rather than resignation.

"I think so," I said, then paused. "Actually, no. I'm terrified."

"Of what?"

It was a fair question. What was I afraid of? The convention itself? The people who might recognize me? The possibility that I'd changed too much, or not enough?

"I'm afraid," I said slowly, working it out as I spoke, "that I won't know how to be myself anymore. The person I was eight years ago, the person I was last year, the person I am now—they all feel like different people sometimes. What if I get down there and realize I don't belong anywhere?"

Miles was quiet for a moment, considering this with the seriousness it deserved.

"Do you remember," he said finally, "what you told me about your novel? About how the best characters aren't the ones who stay the same, but the ones who keep growing?"

"Vaguely. I say a lot of things about writing."

"You said that static characters are boring because they don't reflect how real people actually live. You said that the most interesting stories are about people who become more themselves through change, not less."

I looked at him in the mirror, this man who remembered my throwaway comments about craft and turned them into wisdom when I needed it most.

"You think I've become more myself?"

"I think you've become the person you always were underneath all the doubt and fear. The person who was brave enough to approach a stranger at a convention because she was curious about his costume. The person who was willing to share her stories even when she wasn't sure they were good enough. The person who could laugh at her own disasters and find beauty in imperfection."

"That person was also four hundred pounds."

"That person was also brilliant and kind and funny. The weight was just... packaging. Important packaging, part of your story, but not the whole story."

I turned away from the mirror to face him directly. "Do you really believe that?"

"I believe that you're the same person who made me laugh until I cried during that first gaming session. I believe you're the same person who spent three days researching the historical accuracy of lute strings for a throwaway line in your novel. I believe you're the same person who tried to make us medieval costumes and nearly burned down your flat in the process."

"And you love all those versions of me?"

"I love the continuity between them. I love that you're still you, just... more so. More confident, more comfortable in your own skin, more willing to take up space in the world—not physical space, but emotional space, creative space, the space that matters."

I felt something settle in my chest, a kind of peace I hadn't realized I'd been missing.

"Okay," I said. "Let's go be ourselves at a convention."

The elevator ride down to the main floor was filled with the familiar pre-convention energy—people in various states of costume checking their phones, adjusting accessories, and radiating the particular excitement that came from being surrounded by fellow enthusiasts.

A group of hobbits got on at the third floor, their prosthetic feet making soft shuffling sounds against the elevator carpet. They were clearly friends, chattering about their plans for the day, and their easy camaraderie reminded me of why I'd fallen in love with convention culture in the first place.

"Excuse me," one of them said, turning to look at Miles and me. "Are you guys doing the Arthurian photoshoot this afternoon?"

"We hadn't planned on it," I said. "Is there one?"

"There's always one," another hobbit chimed in. "Usually around three o'clock in the main ballroom. You two would be perfect—you've got that authentic medieval look going on."

"Thank you," Miles said. "We might check it out."

As the elevator doors opened onto the main floor, the hobbits shuffled out ahead of us, their conversation already moving on to other topics. But their casual inclusion of us in their plans, their assumption that we belonged in the photoshoot, felt like a small gift.

The convention floor was exactly as I remembered it—a controlled chaos of costumes and enthusiasm, vendor booths and impromptu gatherings. The familiar sounds washed over me: the rustle of fabric, the click of cameras, the excited chatter of people discovering new things and reconnecting with old friends.

"It's exactly the same," I murmured to Miles as we made our way through the crowd.

"And completely different," he replied, understanding what I meant.

He was right. The convention itself was unchanged—the same mix of elaborate costumes and casual clothes, the same energy, the same sense of community. But I was seeing it through different eyes, experiencing it in a different body, approaching it with a different understanding of who I was and what I had to offer.

We spent the morning wandering through the vendor hall, admiring craftsmanship and chatting with artists. I bought a new set of dice from a craftsperson who carved them from exotic woods, and Miles found a reproduction medieval songbook that made his eyes light up with the kind of joy that reminded me why I'd fallen in love with him in the first place.

"Lillian? Lillian Hartwell?"

I turned to find a woman approaching us, her face familiar but not immediately placeable. She was dressed as a warrior princess, complete with armor that looked like it had been crafted by someone who actually knew what they were doing.

"Yes?" I said, still trying to place her.

"It's Sarah! Sarah Chen. We met at WorldCon three years ago. You did that amazing panel on historical accuracy in fantasy literature."

The memory clicked into place. Sarah had been in the audience, asking thoughtful questions about the balance between authenticity and accessibility in worldbuilding.

"Sarah! Of course. How are you?"

"I'm great! And you look..." she paused, clearly taking in my changed appearance. "You look wonderful. Different, but wonderful."

"Thank you. This is Miles, my partner."

"The musician! I remember you mentioning him during the panel. It's lovely to meet you."

We chatted for a few minutes about her current projects—she was working on a fantasy novel set in a world inspired by medieval China—and I found myself relaxing into the familiar rhythm of convention conversation. The easy exchange of ideas, the shared enthusiasm for craft and creativity, the sense of being among people who understood the particular joy of building imaginary worlds.

"You should definitely submit a panel proposal for next year's WorldCon," Sarah said as we prepared to part ways. "Your perspective on historical research is so valuable, and you have such a gift for making complex topics accessible."

"I'll think about it," I said, and realized I meant it.

As Sarah disappeared back into the crowd, Miles squeezed my hand.

"See?" he said. "Still you."

"Still me," I agreed.

The afternoon photoshoot turned out to be everything the hobbits had promised and more. The main ballroom had been transformed into a medieval court, complete with banners and props that made everyone's costumes look even more impressive. There were knights and ladies, wizards and warriors, dragons and unicorns—a fantastic assemblage of fantasy brought to life.

Miles and I found ourselves naturally gravitating toward the Arthurian section, where a photographer was organizing group shots of various characters from the legends. I was positioned as Morgana le Fay, with Miles as Sir Gareth beside me, surrounded by other enthusiasts who had chosen to embody different aspects of the stories we all loved.

"This is perfect," the photographer said, adjusting his camera settings. "Everyone looks absolutely fantastic. Can we get Morgana to look a bit more mysterious? Maybe a slight smile, like you know something the rest of us don't?"

I found the expression easily, drawing on years of inhabiting this character, and felt the familiar thrill of stepping into a role that was both performance and truth. I was Morgana le Fay, powerful and complex, beautiful and dangerous. I was also Lillian Hartwell, writer and gamer, partner and friend. The two identities didn't conflict—they enhanced each other.

The camera clicked, capturing the moment, and I realized that this was what I'd been afraid of losing—not my size or my old identity, but this sense of belonging, this feeling of being part of something larger than myself.

But I hadn't lost it. It had evolved, just as I had.

After the photoshoot, we found ourselves in the hotel bar with a group of other convention-goers, sharing stories and laughing over drinks. The conversation ranged from serious discussions about representation in fantasy media to ridiculous debates about the practical implications of various magical systems.

"You know what I love about conventions?" said a woman dressed as a steampunk inventor, gesturing with her cocktail. "It's the only place where you can have a completely serious argument about dragon economics and nobody thinks you're weird."

"Dragon economics are important!" protested a man in elaborate wizard robes. "If dragons hoard gold but never spend it, what does that do to the local currency?"

"Inflation," I said without thinking. "Massive inflation, followed by economic collapse when the dragon dies and all that gold floods back into the market."

The group erupted in laughter and enthusiastic agreement, launching into an even more detailed discussion of fantasy economics that would have sounded completely insane to anyone outside our particular community.

As the evening wound down, Miles and I made our way back to our room, both of us tired but happy. In the elevator, I caught sight of our reflection in the mirrored walls—two people in elaborate costumes, looking like they belonged exactly where they were.

"Wait," I said suddenly, stopping Miles before he could unlock our door. "I want to do something."

"What?"

I pulled out my phone and opened the camera app. "I want to take a picture. Of us, together, in costume."

Miles raised an eyebrow. "You want to take a selfie? You, who has spent eight years avoiding convention photos?"

"I want to document this moment. This version of us, this feeling of belonging, this..." I gestured at our costumes, at the convention badges hanging around our necks, at the general atmosphere of joy and acceptance that surrounded us. "All of this."

Miles smiled, understanding immediately. He positioned himself beside me, and I held up the phone, angling it to capture both of us in the frame. In the screen, I could see us as we were—a European Badger and a German Shepherd, dressed as fantasy characters, surrounded by the controlled chaos of a convention hotel.

"Say 'dragon economics,'" I said.

"Dragon economics," we said together, and I pressed the button.

The photo captured us perfectly—Miles grinning beneath his knight's helm, me with my sorceress's mysterious smile, both of us looking genuinely happy and completely ourselves. Our convention badges were visible in the shot, small plastic rectangles that marked us as part of this community, this gathering of people who understood that imagination was not childish but essential, that stories mattered, that it was possible to be both serious adults and enthusiastic fans.

"Perfect," I said, looking at the image on my phone's screen.

"Are you going to post it?" Miles asked.

I considered this. For years, I'd avoided social media photos, uncomfortable with how I looked, worried about judgment from people who didn't understand my interests or my lifestyle. But looking at this picture, I saw two people who belonged exactly where they were, who had found their community and their joy.

"Yes," I said. "I think I am."

I opened my social media app and uploaded the photo, adding a simple caption: "Eight years of conventions, countless adventures, and still discovering new things about ourselves and each other. #DragonCon #CouplesCosplay #StillLearning"

Within minutes, the likes and comments started rolling in—friends from various parts of our lives, fellow convention-goers, people from our gaming groups and writing communities. The responses were uniformly positive, full of heart emojis and enthusiastic comments about our costumes and our obvious happiness.

"Look at this," I said, showing Miles the screen. "People are actually happy for us. They're not judging or criticizing—they're celebrating with us."

"Of course they are. Why wouldn't they be?"

"I don't know. I guess I spent so long expecting judgment that I forgot most people are actually kind."

Miles pulled me close, careful not to damage our costumes. "That's what I love about you—you're always learning, always growing, always becoming more yourself."

"Even when 'more myself' looks completely different from who I used to be?"

"Especially then."

As we finally made it into our room and began the careful process of removing our elaborate costumes, I thought about the day we'd had. The easy acceptance from the hobbits in the elevator, the successful photoshoot, the engaging conversations with fellow fans, the moment of courage that led to posting our photo online.

None of it had been about my size, old or new. It had been about connection, creativity, shared interests, and the simple joy of being part of a community that valued imagination and authenticity.

"You know what?" I said as I carefully hung up my sorceress robes. "I think I'm ready to submit that panel proposal Sarah mentioned."

"Really? What would you want to talk about?"

"Transformation in fantasy literature. How characters grow and change while remaining fundamentally themselves. How the best stories aren't about becoming someone different, but about becoming more fully who you were meant to be."

Miles smiled, settling onto the hotel bed in his pajamas. "That sounds like a story worth telling."

"I think so too."

As I got ready for bed, I caught sight of myself in the bathroom mirror—no longer in costume, just me in my pajamas, my face clean of makeup, my hair mussed from removing the elaborate headpiece. I looked tired but content, different from the woman who had attended her first convention eight years ago, but recognizably the same person.

The badges from the day's events sat on the nightstand—small plastic proof that I had been here, that I had participated, that I belonged. Tomorrow we would go home, back to our regular lives and ordinary clothes, but we would carry this with us: the memory of a day when we had been exactly who we were meant to be, surrounded by people who understood and celebrated that authenticity.

"Good night, Morgana," Miles said sleepily from the bed.

"Good night, Sir Gareth," I replied, turning off the light.

In the darkness of the hotel room, I could hear the distant sounds of the convention continuing around us—people in the hallways, music from the parties, the general hum of a community celebrating itself. It was the sound of belonging, of finding your tribe, of discovering that there was room in the world for all the different ways of being human.

Or, in our case, all the different ways of being anthropomorphic animals who happened to love fantasy literature and elaborate costumes.

I fell asleep smiling, my convention badge still within reach, a small plastic reminder that some transformations aren't about becoming someone else—they're about becoming more fully yourself.
