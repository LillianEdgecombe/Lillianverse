# Lillianverse — Working Title TBD

## Episode 1 — The Invitation

The doors were too tall.

They rose out of the stone like a dare: oak polished to a mirror sheen, brass handles big enough for a stage production of *Macbeth*, a plaque between them that read, with tasteful understatement: **Guests Only Beyond This Point**.

Miles stood on the gravel and squinted up at the facade. The building smelled of money and disinfectant; the sea behind it smelled of salt and old storms. Between the two, the Estate sat like a compromise that thought it was a solution.

“That’s not a door,” he said. “That’s a test.”

From the passenger seat, Lillian smiled without looking at him. She adjusted her glasses with one paw, watching the procession of sleek cars slide into place. Their own hatchback, dust on the wheel arches and a stubborn rattle in second gear, looked like a delivery van that had taken a wrong turn.

“Every test has an answer, love,” she said. “We just don’t know if this one is multiple choice or essay yet.”

In the back seat, Sarah snorted. “Definitely an essay,” she said. “Probably with footnotes.”

She had her sketchbook already open on her lap, one paw hooked over the headrest. Golden retriever, short copper hair, rings on every finger soot-stained from clay. Her gaze flicked between the entrance, the valets, the guests. The pencil in her claws began to move.

The Estate rose above them: glass, stone, and the kind of architectural confidence that came from never having had a planning permission denied. Palm trees lined the drive, imported and pampered, their trunks wrapped in fairy lights that would make the whole place glow like a set after dark.

“We go in,” Miles said. “We smile. We endure. We’re back in Loughton by Sunday.”

“We’re not at the dentist,” Lillian said mildly. “We’re at a strategic retreat.”

She put invisible quotation marks around the phrase with one hand. Rings glinted: wedding band, Trinity ring, thick silver band Sarah had cast for her. Her long black hair was pinned up, already catching the sea breeze. The dress she wore—dark, with threads of gold—belonged on a stage. That was deliberate.

“We are here,” she said, “because one of the academy’s largest patrons wanted to ‘foster cross-sector dialogue about the future of anthro arts and leadership.’” More quotation marks. “He invited the Head of the Academy. That’s me. I refused to come unless I could bring my two favourite structural engineers.”

“I thought I was here as your husband,” Miles said.

“You are,” she said. “And as my drummer, and my structural engineer. And Sarah is our sculptor, our moral compass, and our emergency exit strategy.”

“So if this turns into a cult,” Sarah said, climbing out, “we leave early.”

“If this turns into a cult,” Lillian said, shutting the car door, “we leave with the silverware.”

Inside, the lobby was a theatre: marble floors, chandeliers, an installation of hanging glass that looked like frozen rain. Anthros and humans mingled in clothes that managed to be both casual and bank-balance loud. A banner announced:

**THE FUTURE OF CULTURAL LEADERSHIP — A PRIVATE CONVERSATION**

Lillian’s posture shifted by degrees as they crossed the floor. The same badger who padded around their kitchen in a dressing gown became the Head of the Academy: shoulders back, chin lifted, an easy, unsmiling warmth that told the room she knew exactly where she belonged.

At the reception desk, identical rabbits in matching suits smiled with weaponised blandness.

“Good afternoon,” Lillian said. “Lillian Edgecombe. This is my husband, Miles Ferguson, and our daughter, Sarah Ferguson.”

Keycards, glossy folders, maps of the Estate—pools, ‘conversation lounges,’ a ‘reflection grove’ of curated woodland—changed hands. The word *networking* appeared too often.

In their suite, the view of the cove had been framed the way a stage manager framed an entrance. The sea slotted neatly between two headlands, as if it existed purely for this window.

“It’s like someone built a resort on the idea of aspiration,” Sarah said, dropping her bag. “Everything’s just slightly too shiny.”

Miles checked the bathroom taps by habit. Everything worked with effortless precision. That meant someone else’s effort had been erased.

“He’s here,” Lillian said softly from the window.

On the terrace below, a lion held court: mane touched with silver, suit tailored past fashion into comfort, laugh big enough to carry. Harald St. Croix, patron of the arts, philanthropist, kingmaker. In Lillian’s world, he was oxygen and weather.

Miles watched the way people orbited him. Some leaned in too far; others pretended not to care. He recognised the choreography. He’d seen the same patterns in boardrooms and stockrooms, in smaller, uglier buildings.

“You don’t have to impress anyone,” Lillian said, reading him in the glass.

“You brought me to a place where the taps probably have job titles,” he said. “I reserve the right to be nervous.”

“You’re here to see how the machine works,” she said. “And to tell me when it’s about to throw a cog.”

“And I’m here to draw it,” Sarah said, already sketching the lion’s silhouette.

They went down for the welcome gala at seven, the lobby transformed into a soft-lit aquarium of glass and fabric. A jazz quartet fiddled discreetly in the corner. Waiters moved like choreography.

Harald spotted Lillian and came striding over, arms already widening.

“Ms Edgecombe!” he boomed. “The pride of the academy.”

“Harald,” she said, submitting to the double paw clasp. “Thank you for hosting us.”

“My pleasure,” he said, eyes already moving. “And this must be the famous Miles. The engineer. The drummer. The man who keeps your world from collapsing.”

Miles shook his hand, feeling the calculated warmth in the grip. “Good to meet you,” he said. “Thank you for inviting us.”

“‘Inviting,’” Harald repeated, amused. “My dear fellow, I begged. Lillian makes everything look legitimate.”

He turned to Sarah, library of names flicking behind his eyes. “And Sarah. The sculptor. The broken armature piece was extraordinary.”

“Thank you,” she said. “The world keeps breaking things. I just follow.”

Harald laughed, pleased with himself for presumably discovering Marxism in a gallery. He moved on, the conversation dissolving behind him like foam.

Miles exhaled. “Essay question, that one,” he muttered.

“We’ll give him short answers,” Lillian said.

Sarah stopped, her paw tightening on her glass. “Okay,” she said. “That’s weird.”

“What is?” Miles asked.

She tilted her head towards the bar.

A German shepherd stood there, laughing at something Harald had said. Same height. Same build. Same ears. Same sable and tan fur. The suit fit better. The haircut was sharper. The posture was edited.

He turned, profile catching the light, and Miles felt the floor shift under his feet.

The man at the bar had his face.

Not a resemblance. Not *you remind me of someone.* It was like looking into a mirror that had spent ten years being paid more.

The twin’s gaze swept the room, caught on them, and stopped.

Recognition hit both ways, not as surprise but as a correction, as if the universe had been holding its breath and now, finally, exhaled.

The glass in Miles’ paw felt heavy. His heart stuttered, then resumed at a tempo he used for drum fills when he wanted to rattle the room.

“Oh,” Lillian said quietly. “Well. That’s interesting.”

The twin smiled, all charm and curiosity, and started walking towards them.

## Episode 2 — The Better Brother

By breakfast, the Estate had already begun to edit the story.

Word spread fast: separated at birth, adoption agencies, long-lost twins reunited at a billionaire’s retreat. It was too good a narrative to resist. The details could be filled in later.

Dan—as he insisted, with a practised laugh—did most of the filling.

“Daniel Ferguson,” he’d said last night, pulling Miles into a hug that trespassed on familiarity. “Call me Dan. And you must be the version of me who made sensible life choices.”

They’d compared basics in the soft glow of chandeliers: same birth date, same hospital in Manchester, different agencies. Parallel lives misfiled.

Now, in daylight, Sarah and Miles watched Dan work the terraces.

“He’s good,” Sarah said, pencil flicking over the page as she sketched him from the balcony of the coffee bar. “Annoyingly good.”

Dan moved through the crowd like he’d been born in it. He remembered names, laughed at the right volume, touched arms just enough. He told stories that sounded like Miles’ but louder, streamlined. The Trinity years became a charming anecdote about young talent and institutional blindness. The Royal Academy rejection was a funny story with a triumphant moral: *so I built my own path*.

“He’s you,” Sarah said. “If you believed any of this.”

“He’s me if I specialised in making rich people feel brave,” Miles said.

“He’s you if you never learned to shut up,” Sarah added.

Dan had a job title that meant nothing and everything: “cultural consultant.” He “helped philanthropists think strategically about impact.” He “translated between sectors.” In plain terms, he told people like Harald where to place their money and how to feel heroic while doing it.

“You should talk to him properly,” Sarah said. “Not on their stage.”

“Later,” Miles said. “When he’s not performing.”

“He’s always performing,” she said.

She was right. When Miles found Dan alone in one of the smaller lounges, he was on the phone, voice lowered but still carrying.

“No, listen,” Dan said. “This place is a convergence. St. Croix, the arts council, three tech billionaires, and half of London’s cultural gatekeepers. If we play this right, we can build a fund that actually does something beyond keeping the same five theatres open. Yes. I know. I’ll send you the deck.”

He hung up, saw Miles, and brightened.

“Brother,” he said. “Come in. Sit. Or do we prefer ‘control sample’ and ‘experimental condition’?”

“Miles is fine,” Miles said, taking a seat opposite him. Up close, the resemblance was worse. Same bones, different polish.

“Sorry for being a bit… on last night,” Dan said. “It’s not every day you meet your own alternate timeline.”

“Likewise,” Miles said. “I’d have worn a better shirt.”

Dan laughed. “Don’t. If you dress like me, they’ll short-circuit. They’re already struggling.”

He poured coffee from a pot on the low table, handed Miles a cup without asking how he took it. Of course he knew. He’d read the brief.

“You knew you were adopted?” Miles asked.

“Of course,” Dan said. “It was practically a personality trait in my twenties. You?”

“Yes,” Miles said. “I just didn’t spend much time imagining there was another one of me out there selling narrative to lions.”

Dan grinned. “It’s a living,” he said. “I started out doing communications for NGOs, then politics. Turns out it’s easier to move billionaires than backbenchers. Fewer scruples.”

“That’s not reassuring,” Miles said.

“You’d rather they stayed unbothered?” Dan asked. “I get them into rooms where they have to listen to people like you. Like Lillian.”

“Do you?” Miles asked. “Or do you get people like us into rooms where we have to perform gratitude for them?”

Dan tipped his head, conceding the point. “Sometimes it’s both,” he said. “You want my honest pitch? I live in the gap between what’s possible and what they’re willing to risk. I widen it if I can. I limit the damage when I can’t.”

He sipped his coffee, watching Miles over the rim. “You don’t like this place,” he said.

“I don’t like systems that confuse generosity with ownership,” Miles said.

“Ah,” Dan said. “You’re going to be fun at dinner.”

He set his cup down, leaning forward.

“Look,” he said. “This weekend is going to turn into a story whether we like it or not. Two brothers—that’s what they’re calling us already—reunited at the court of a benevolent lion. If we’re smart, we can shape that story so it does something useful.”

“Useful for who?” Miles asked.

“For us,” Dan said. “For the people we care about. For your students. For artists who don’t have the luxury of sneering at glassware.”

“I’m not sneering,” Miles said. “I’m auditing.”

Dan laughed. “Same skill, different output,” he said. “Anyway. Small request.”

Miles braced. “Go on.”

“You tell good stories,” Dan said. “The kettle exploding in the Brixton flat. The blackout gig. The wedding in the council office. They’re good material. When I tell them, it sounds like I’m borrowing someone else’s life. If we treat them as shared—our story, not yours—it plays better. People love mythology.”

Miles stared at him. “You want to merge biographies,” he said.

“Just in here,” Dan said, gesturing at the room, the weekend. “It helps us both. You get some of my shine; I get some of your grit. They stop seeing you as the awkward plus-one and start seeing you as part of a narrative arc.”

“I’m not a subplot in your pitch,” Miles said.

Dan held up his paws. “No offence. Look, I’m not going to steal your childhood. I’m suggesting a temporary joint venture.”

“In which my life becomes one of your decks,” Miles said.

Dan’s smile flattened. “Perception is currency,” he said. “You can either pretend that’s beneath you, or you can use it.”

“You do realise,” Miles said, “that while you’re workshopping our origin story, half this building is already deciding which one of us is the ‘real’ one? Which one’s the upgrade?”

“And you care,” Dan said.

“I care that they’re replacing me in real time,” Miles said. “I told someone yesterday about fixing the lights in our first flat so Lillian could practise after midnight. Tonight she congratulated you on the same story and you took the bow.”

“I improvised,” Dan said. “It flowed.”

“So does sewage,” Miles said.

Dan flinched, laughed to cover it, and poured more coffee.

“You’re fun when you’re angry,” he said. “We’ll get along.”

They didn’t, particularly. But the room liked the idea that they did. It was a better story: two halves of a whole, one practical, one visionary. The narrative began to calcify around that shape.

By the end of the day, Miles had been complimented three times on things Dan had said. Dan had been congratulated on Lillian’s initiatives. The room was rewriting them with every conversation.

In bed that night, with the sea murmuring under the window and Lillian’s breathing slow beside him, Miles stared at the ceiling.

“You’re not interchangeable,” she said in the dark.

“I thought you were asleep,” he said.

“I was listening,” she said. “You two sound nothing alike once the novelty wears off.”

“They don’t hear it,” he said.

“They’re listening for a lullaby,” she said. “You’re giving them a drum solo.”

He laughed, then didn’t.

“Tomorrow,” she said, “they’ll start deciding who you are. Don’t help them.”

He wasn’t sure he had a choice.

## Episode 3 — The Patron

Harald St. Croix understood that power was theatre and ledger.

The “fireside conversation” the next morning was proof. There was no fire, just cleverly lit fake logs and a halo of candles. Harald sat in an armchair designed to make him look relaxed and central. Opposite him, a fox journalist in a sharp suit asked questions that had been agreed in advance.

“You’ve been called a kingmaker,” the fox said. “How do you respond to that?”

Harald laughed, head thrown back, mane catching the light. The sound had been calibrated for rooms like this: big enough to be infectious, not so big as to seem vulgar.

“I’m just a man who loves the arts,” he said. “I made some money in boring things. This is how I stay interested in my own life.”

The audience chuckled. Lillian, sitting near the back with Miles and Sarah, underlined *boring things* in her notebook. She’d heard the origin story before: logistics, property, something with warehouses. Miles had his own quiet opinions on how boring it really was to control the literal supply of food.

“Do you feel the burden of that power?” the fox asked. “When you decide which projects live or die?”

Harald spread his paws. “I don’t do it alone,” he said. “I rely on brilliant people—curators, directors, advisors. I listen. That’s why we’re here. To listen, to learn, to make sure that when we place our bets, we’re backing the right people.”

*Right*, Lillian underlined. Whose definition.

The Q&A that followed was carefully chaotic. Questions about scholarships, about regional funding, about representation. Harald parried, conceded, leaned forward at the right moments. He gave just enough criticism of government policy to sound independent, just enough humility to sound safe.

When Dan stood to ask a question, the room leaned in.

“Harald,” he said, not using the microphone, trusting his voice to carry. “We’ve talked a lot about the good you’ve done. The institutions you’ve kept alive. But there’s a structural question we’re skirting.”

Harald smiled, wary. “There usually is, with you.”

“How do you make sure your own power is accountable?” Dan asked. “You convene us. You anoint. You exclude. Who tells you when you’ve got it wrong? Who can remove you, if necessary?”

He’d chosen the words carefully. The room felt the edge under the politeness.

Harald’s smile didn’t falter. “By inviting people like you to poke holes in my ego,” he said. “By making sure rooms like this are full of voices that challenge me. Daniel here is one of my fiercest critics, and I treasure that.”

Laughter. Applause. The challenge was reframed as proof of Harald’s courage. Dan sat down, and three people told him afterwards how brave he’d been for “speaking truth to power.”

“He gave Harald a gift,” Sarah said quietly on the terrace later. “Now he gets to say he loves criticism.”

“He banked the question,” Miles said. “Now it’s a line in his obituary instead of an alarm.”

The rest of the day was panels and workshops, each one orbiting Harald’s tastes. Creative risk wrapped neatly around donor preference. Lillian did her part, arguing for bursaries and rehearsal time with her usual, lethal politeness.

That evening, at a smaller dinner, Harald held court at the head of a long table in a private dining room. Dan sat at his right hand. Miles, invited, claimed a rare victory and ignored the email.

“I’m not spending breakfast flattering a lion,” he told Lillian in bed, deleting the message. “He has enough admirers.”

“Sleep is radical,” she said, kissing his shoulder. “Get some.”

In the private dining room the next morning, the chairs at Harald’s right and left were occupied: Dan on one side, Eliza North—the programmes manager Miles had noticed juggling clipboards and crises—on the other. A few senior guests filled the remaining seats, chosen less for insight than for brand value.

The coffee was strong. The conversation stronger.

“We have to tighten,” Harald said, between bites of bacon. “Too many little projects nibbling at the fund. We need focus. Big plays.”

“Community centres aren’t ‘little,’” Eliza said, carefully. “They’re the only reason some kids ever touch an instrument.”

“Sentiment is expensive,” Harald said. “We’ll keep enough to look good. The rest—we reallocate.”

Dan watched Eliza’s jaw tighten, felt the temperature in the room drop by a degree.

“We can design a process,” he said smoothly. “Transparent criteria. Sunset clauses. No one gets surprised.”

“Excellent,” Harald said. “You see? This is why you’re here. You make the pill go down easy.”

He tapped his pill case—sleek leather, embossed initials—on the table for emphasis. His humour had a sharpness Eliza recognised. She’d spent years being the person who made his whims operational.

“Your cardiologist sent guidelines,” she said. “No caffeine. Controlled diet. You promised to follow them.”

“My cardiologist worries for a living,” Harald said, pouring more coffee. “I’m not dead yet.”

Dan laughed with him. Eliza didn’t.

Later, none of that would make it into the official statements. What would matter, to the narratives that formed, was simpler: who poured the coffee, who handled the pills, who was in the room, and who wasn’t.

## Episode 4 — The Death

The Estate woke to the sound of its own panic, smoothed and prerecorded.

An alarm tone rose and fell, tasteful and unhurried. A calm voice over the speakers asked guests to remain in their rooms “while we respond to an incident.” The phrase landed in Miles’ nervous system like a warehouse alert: leak, fire, collapse.

He was out of bed before the message finished, checking the corridor. The carpet swallowed footsteps; the air smelled of lemon and adrenaline.

“Stay here,” he told Lillian.

“Don’t be ridiculous,” she said, already reaching for her glasses. “If something’s happening, I’d rather see it than wait for the press release.”

Sarah appeared in the doorway of her room, hoodie thrown on over yesterday’s jeans. “Security’s on the lifts,” she said. “Polite brick walls. ‘Minor issue.’”

They met the brick wall: a stag in a dark suit, earpiece in one ear, palms raised.

“We have a situation,” he said. “Please remain on this floor.”

“Define ‘situation,’” Miles said.

“A guest has been taken ill,” the stag said. “Medical staff are attending.”

“Which guest?” Lillian asked.

“I’m not—”

From below, a shout. Not a scream—this was a place that didn’t allow screaming—but something sharp enough to cut through the scripted calm.

“You can escort us,” Lillian told the stag, “or you can explain to my students why their Head of Academy waited politely while a patron died downstairs.”

He hesitated for exactly one second, then stepped aside. “I’ll come with you,” he said.

In the main hall, the performance had broken. Staff moved in purposeful lines. A medical team crouched around a figure on the floor near the stage, partially screened by hastily wheeled planters. Guests clustered at a distance, murmuring, phones half raised then guiltily lowered.

Harald lay on the marble, shirt open, mane spread like a halo gone wrong. A medic pumped his chest rhythmically. Another prepared a syringe. Someone called out numbers in a calm voice that only made the scene worse.

Dan stood a few metres away, hands clenched, expression stripped of charm. There was coffee on his shirt.

“Don’t look,” Miles said automatically to Sarah.

She looked anyway. “I’m not a child,” she said, voice thin.

They watched as the compressions continued. Stopped. Continued. Stopped. The medic’s shoulders sagged. A sheet was drawn up.

The sound that went through the room wasn’t a single noise but a chord: gasps, sobs, whispered *oh God* and the quieter exhale of people adjusting to a new fact.

Harald St. Croix was dead.

The Estate’s machinery juddered, then shifted gear. Staff ushered guests back. Announcements were made: tragic loss, unforeseeable event, support services available. The word *police* appeared, smoothed by “as a matter of routine.”

In a smaller room, later, a senior manager in a grey suit briefed a handful of “key stakeholders.” Lillian had been summoned because of the academy’s dependence on Harald’s fund. Miles refused to be left out. Sarah came because no one tried hard enough to stop her.

“Mr St. Croix collapsed during a private breakfast,” the manager said. “He was attended immediately. Despite best efforts, he could not be revived. We ask that you refrain from speculation while the authorities—”

“Who was at breakfast?” Lillian asked.

The manager blinked. “I don’t think—”

“You know the list,” she said. “Say their names.”

“Mr Daniel Ferguson,” he said reluctantly. “Ms Eliza North. Two board members. A representative from the arts council. Mr… Miles Ferguson.”

Heads turned.

Miles sat forward slowly. “I was in bed,” he said. “I deleted the invite.”

“Our records—”

“Your records are wrong,” he said. “Or someone else answered to my name.”

“It’s easily confused,” someone said mildly. “You do look very similar.”

Sarah made a sound that wasn’t a laugh. “He wasn’t there,” she said. “You can check the cameras. Unless you’ve only installed them where you think the interesting people go.”

The manager flushed. “The police will review all relevant footage,” he said. “In the meantime, please. Discretion.”

Discretion lasted until the first WhatsApp message reached someone’s agent.

By afternoon, the building hummed with rumours.

Heart attack. Poison. Affair. Old enemies. New enemies. Staff plot. Political hit. Personal vendetta.

In the middle of it, reality narrowed to practicalities: the police would come. Statements would be taken. People would be quietly ranked, in the room’s collective imagination, by how likely they were to have nudged the lion towards the floor.

Miles could feel himself sliding down that list.

## Episode 5 — Consensus

The detectives arrived with the air of people who had no patience for the Estate’s choreography.

DS Asha Patel was a mongoose in a worn suit, notebook in one paw, pen in the other. DC Brendan Shaw was a mastiff whose jacket didn’t quite fit across his shoulders. They moved through the building without deference, and the building did not know what to do with them.

They interviewed staff first. Then board members. Then guests. They asked boring questions in infuriating detail.

“What time did breakfast start?”

“Who poured the coffee?”

“Who handled Mr St. Croix’s medication case?”

They took notes on who flushed, who deflected, who performed grief and who sank into it.

They spoke to Dan in a small lounge.

“He was fine,” Dan said. “We were talking about a new fund. He made a joke about his cardiologist. He took a sip of coffee. Then he clutched his chest and went down.”

“What was the joke?” Patel asked.

Dan rubbed his temples. “He said if his cardiologist could see him now, he’d double his fees. Then he laughed and said, ‘Can’t live forever.’”

“And the coffee?” Shaw asked. “Who poured it?”

“Staff,” Dan said. “Same pot for everyone. This isn’t one of those places where the patron gets special beans.”

“You’d be surprised,” Shaw said.

They spoke to Lillian in a side room that smelled of polish and exhaustion.

“How well did you know Mr St. Croix?” Patel asked.

“Professionally,” Lillian said. “He funds a third of my academy’s operations. I’ve seen him bully politicians and charm ushers. He liked to sit in the front row and pretend he wasn’t directing the play from there.”

“Did you have disagreements?” Patel asked.

“We had different understandings of the word ‘access,’” Lillian said. “He thought it meant scholarships for people he could put on brochures. I think it means salaries that let my staff pay rent.”

“Any reason to wish him harm?” Patel asked.

“I wished his influence less absolute,” Lillian said. “I didn’t wish him dead. He was more useful alive, where I could argue with him.”

They spoke to Miles in a smaller, blander room.

“You weren’t at breakfast,” Patel said.

“No,” Miles said. “I chose sleep over small talk.”

“Several witnesses insist they saw you in the room,” Shaw said.

“They saw a German shepherd at Harald’s right hand,” Miles said. “They assumed it was the quieter one.”

“You don’t like this world,” Patel said.

“I don’t like systems that make people’s livelihoods depend on being in the right room at the right time,” he said. “I married one of its most skilled operators. I respect the work. I distrust the architecture.”

“Do you resent Mr St. Croix?” she asked.

“I resent that my wife has to flatter men like him to get rehearsal space for kids who can’t afford to fail,” he said. “I resent that he can shrug and call it philanthropy.”

“That sounds like motive,” Shaw said mildly.

“That sounds like every person in this building,” Miles said. “No one here is neutral.”

He left the room feeling as if he’d walked through mud. Outside, conversations had shifted.

“They’re looking hard at Miles,” someone told someone else within Sarah’s earshot. “He’s the one with the chip on his shoulder.”

“Or the staff,” another said. “You know how resentful they can get.”

“Daniel was there,” a third added. “But he’s… I don’t know. He’s one of us.”

Consensus, in rooms like this, wasn’t a poll. It was a slow coagulation of prejudice, convenience, and fear.

Miles Ferguson was trouble. Daniel Ferguson was a bit sharp, but brilliant. Staff were a risk class. Harald had been both beloved and tolerated. Eliza was invisible.

In their suite, Lillian leaned against the window and watched people cross the terraces in clusters.

“They want a culprit,” she said. “Not a cause.”

“They want to go back to their donor pitches with a clean conscience,” Sarah said. “A neat story about a tragic aberration.”

“And a villain they don’t have to look in the mirror to see,” Miles said.

The room, as a whole, started to settle on names. Not because of evidence, but because of narrative need.

## Episode 6 — The Investigator

Lillian Edgecombe had spent her life drawing stories out of people. She knew how to listen in ways that made them forget they were talking.

She turned that skill outward.

The Estate had provided “reflection lounges”: soft sofas, tea, bowls of almonds. She colonised one on the second floor, arranged a small ceremony of cups and biscuits, and sent a few messages.

Artists found her first. They always did.

“He believed in me when no one else did,” a dancer said, hands wrapped around a mug. “Who’s going to replace that?”

“He also told you to lose weight for the poster,” Lillian reminded her gently. “He liked his protégés decorative.”

The dancer flinched, then nodded.

“He told me my work was ‘regional’,” a playwright said bitterly. “As if that was an insult.”

“And then funded your London transfer,” Lillian said. “So you’d owe him for your legitimacy.”

They talked. She prodded. She mapped.

With technologists, the vocabulary shifted.

“This is a reputational risk,” a startup founder said. “We only partnered with the foundation because Harald’s name carried weight.”

“You built products on the assumption that one lion would always be in the room,” Lillian said. “That’s not a business model. That’s superstition.”

Staff were harder. They had more to lose. She caught them in corridors, on smoke breaks, in the staff canteen between shifts.

“You were on duty that morning,” she said to a fox server.

“We’re not supposed to talk about it,” the fox said.

“If you don’t,” Lillian said, “they’ll write the script without you. And they’ll cast you as scenery, or as the villain.”

The fox looked at the floor. “He fought with Toby,” she said finally. “About the menu. About his heart.”

Toby, the kitchen supervisor, had been in the background all weekend, apron always slightly stained, eyes taking everything in. Lillian found him staring at a cup of tea that had gone cold.

“You tried to stop him,” she said, sitting down opposite.

He snorted. “You can’t stop a man like that,” he said. “You can only warn. The doctor sent strict orders. No caffeine. Low salt. He laughed. Said he’d lived this long on instinct.”

“You served him what he asked for,” she said.

“He signed my paycheque,” Toby said. “He made it very clear that if I didn’t, he’d sign my redundancy letter instead.”

Guilt sat on his shoulders like a physical weight. It wasn’t deserved. Not alone.

“You told the police?” she asked.

“They asked about poison,” he said. “About tampering. I told them the ingredients, the suppliers, the procedures. I didn’t tell them he’d decided his ego outranked his cardiologist.”

She filed it away and talked to Patel that evening over institutional tea in paper cups.

“You’re doing my job,” Patel said.

“No,” Lillian said. “You’re doing your job. I’m doing mine. I collect stories. I’m just refusing to let them be cut down to a headline.”

She told Patel about the fight over breakfast. About the guidelines. About the way everyone’s sense of safety depended on one man choosing not to care if he lived.

“Our toxicology report shows a stimulant in his system,” Patel said. “Not caffeine. Something stronger. That complicates the ‘he did this to himself’ explanation.”

“It doesn’t undo it,” Lillian said. “It just adds another layer of stupid, desperate human choice on top.”

“You’re very good at making my life harder,” Patel said.

“I’ll buy you a drink when my academy still exists,” Lillian said.

They both knew that was the real investigation Lillian was running: not just who had killed Harald, but whether his death was going to be used to tighten the screws or loosen them.

## Episode 7 — The Ledger

Sarah hated spreadsheets. They made her eyes glaze and her hand itch for clay.

But she loved patterns. And Dan’s laptop was full of them.

He had given her access almost grandly, as if gifting her a tool. “Transparency,” he’d said. “No secrets between brothers’ families.”

She suspected he was mostly proud of the system he’d built.

On the screen, in a file blandly named *tracking.xlsx*, rows of names marched down the page. Columns labelled **Grant**, **Favour**, **Introduction**, **Intervention**, **Warning** stretched to the right. Each cell contained dates, shorthand, and codes.

“This is his ledger,” she said, sitting cross-legged on the floor of their suite, laptop open. “Harald’s social accounts. Who owed him. Who he owed. Who he’d bailed out. Who he’d quietly blacklisted.”

Miles leaned over the arm of the sofa. “He wrote it down,” he said. “Amateur mistake.”

“Dan wrote it down,” Sarah said. “Harald supplied the decisions; Dan supplied the architecture.”

Patterns popped. Names that appeared again and again with plus signs: safe favourites. Names with minus signs and notes like *too political* or *unreliable.* A column marked **Risk** scored from 1 to 5.

Another tab was simply labelled **DF**.

“That’s Dan’s own sheet,” Sarah said. “His personal ledger.”

Here, the names were different: donors, board members, journalists, civil servants. Columns: **Influence**, **Affinity**, **Leverage**.

“He’s gamified them,” she said. “People are points. Conversations are moves.”

She felt something harden in her chest.

“This isn’t just about art,” she said. “This is about control. If Harald was the lion in the front row, Dan built the cage.”

She exported a version of the ledger with names stripped, leaving only roles and scores. It still looked damning.

> *He kept score,* she wrote in an email to Lillian and Patel. *So did Dan. This is a system, not an accident.*

Then, because her brain needed something that wasn’t cells and numbers, she picked up her sketchbook and drew the ledger as a web: Harald in the centre, lines radiating to dozens of figures. Some lines thick with favours; others frayed with warnings.

On the edges, two German shepherds: one leaning in, one holding back.

Underneath, she wrote: **Who profits from the cut?**

## Episode 8 — The Fall Guy

On the third day, the tone of the interviews changed.

Until then, Patel and Shaw had been gathering. Now they began to press.

Miles was summoned again. This time the room was windowless, the table heavier. Shaw closed the door with a click that sounded like punctuation.

“We’ve had preliminary toxicology,” Patel said. “There was a stimulant in Mr St. Croix’s system. Not prescribed. Not caffeine. We need to understand how it got there.”

“You think I put it there,” he said.

“We think you had opportunity,” she said. “Your name is on the breakfast list. Witnesses saw someone they believe to be you in the room. You have expressed resentment towards the victim.”

“I expressed resentment towards the architecture,” he said. “If I wanted to bring this place down, I wouldn’t start with its most photogenic pillar.”

“Humour me,” she said. “Where were you between seven and nine that morning?”

He told her: asleep; shower; coffee with Lillian and Sarah; watching the sea. He described the deleted email. The fight over attending. The choice to stay in bed.

“Can anyone corroborate?” Shaw asked.

“My wife,” he said. “My daughter. The taps.”

“We’ll check the cameras,” Patel said. “That will help.”

“Will it?” he asked. “You’ve seen how people here look at me and Dan. They see whatever makes the story interesting.”

“We don’t rely on story,” she said. “We rely on footage.”

“Footage gets interpreted too,” he said.

She watched him for a long moment. “Do you think Daniel could have done it?” she asked.

He thought of Dan’s spreadsheet, of his belief in outcomes over process, of his ease with moral algebra.

“I think Daniel believes that if a system is producing the wrong results, you don’t wring your hands, you introduce a shock,” he said. “I think he believes in history books more than court records.”

“That’s not an answer,” she said.

“It’s the only one I have,” he said.

When he left the room, his shoulders felt heavier. Not because he thought they’d charge him—Patel wasn’t incompetent—but because he could feel the social verdict forming independently of legal ones.

In corridor conversations, small phrases accumulated:

“You know how quiet types get.”

“He hated this stuff.”

“It’s always the ones who don’t play the game.”

Lillian listened, logged, recalibrated.

“They’re rehearsing the scapegoat,” she told Sarah. “If the police take too long, the room will improvise justice.”

“Then we need a different story,” Sarah said. “Fast.”

## Episode 9 — The Wrong Questions

The official questions were narrow.

“Where were you.”

“What did you touch.”

“Who poured what.”

The real question Lillian heard in every conversation was broader and uglier:

“How do we get out of this without losing anything important.”

Important, in this context, meant money, status, access. Not truth.

At lunch with a mixed table—NGO director, tech philanthropist, regional theatre manager, journalist—she watched them circle around the word.

“This could derail so much good work,” the NGO director said. “We had three community projects lined up.”

“We have to think about confidence,” the philanthropist said. “If donors lose faith in the foundation, everyone suffers.”

“Confidence in what?” Lillian asked. “In one man’s ability to sign cheques?”

The journalist’s pen twitched. “Off the record?” he asked.

“There’s no off the record in this building,” she said. “Only delayed quotes.”

“Do you think it was murder?” he asked.

She considered. “I think a man with a heart condition, who ignored medical advice, died while sitting at the centre of a web of obligations, grudges, and dependencies,” she said. “If you define ‘murder’ as one clean act by a villain in a locked room, then no. If you define it as death accelerated by the choices of a lot of people who benefitted from his risk-taking and refused to stop him, then yes.”

“That won’t fit in a headline,” he said.

“That’s your problem,” she said. “Not mine.”

Later, she intercepted Patel on the terrace, the sea hitting the rocks below like punctuation.

“You’re asking the wrong main question,” Lillian said.

“We’re asking how a non-prescribed stimulant ended up in his system,” Patel said. “And who put it there.”

“You’re asking ‘who killed Harald,’” Lillian said. “You should be asking ‘who benefits from Harald being dead.’”

“We can’t charge someone with benefiting,” Patel said. “We need acts.”

“Acts happen inside structures,” Lillian said. “You know this. You choose where to look. I’m asking you not to stop at the first neat answer.”

Patel’s phone buzzed. She glanced at it, frowned. “We’ve found something,” she said. “Pill case. Residue. Someone’s been clever and lazy at the same time.”

“That’s this place in a nutshell,” Lillian said.

As Patel walked away, Lillian looked back at the building.

The wrong questions were cheap. The right one—*who benefits*—was expensive. It demanded that people with power admit the system served them too well.

She doubted they’d pay willingly.

## Episode 10 — The Killer

Eliza North cracked quietly.

There was no dramatic confrontation in a crowded drawing room. No pointing finger. Just a corridor that smelled of cleaning fluid and fatigue, a humming strip light, and DS Patel saying, “We need to talk about the pill case.”

Eliza looked smaller without her headset. Human, late thirties, shoulders permanently tense from carrying everyone else’s emergencies.

“You handled it,” Patel said. “You refilled it. You tracked his dosages. That’s in the logs.”

“That was my job,” Eliza said. “He liked to pretend he didn’t need systems. Someone had to make sure he didn’t forget his own heart.”

“You argued with him about the guidelines,” Patel said. “Witnesses heard it.”

Eliza laughed, the sound edged with hysteria. “I argued with him about everything,” she said. “He liked that. It made him feel magnanimous when he overruled me.”

“We found traces of a stimulant in the case,” Patel said. “Not the one his doctor prescribed. How did it get there?”

Eliza’s hands shook. She shoved them into her pockets.

“I just wanted him to feel it,” she said. “For once.”

Patel waited.

“He sat across from me at lunch and told me, very kindly, that my position was ‘evolving,’” Eliza said. “He wanted ‘fresh energy.’ Someone better at optics. He’d ‘make sure I landed somewhere appropriate.’ I’ve given this place ten years. Weekends, nights, my spine. And I was… evolving.”

She swallowed, throat working.

“I was tired,” she said. “Tired of being responsible for his whims. Tired of watching him gamble with other people’s lives like they were chips. Tired of knowing that if I walked away, it would all get worse.”

“So you decided to intervene,” Patel said.

Eliza nodded. “I swapped two of his pills. Just two. I took out the beta-blockers and put in something that would make his heart race. Not lethal. Just… intense. I thought he’d feel a scare, go to his doctor, maybe finally listen.”

“Where did you get the stimulant?” Patel asked.

Eliza snorted. “This place runs on them,” she said. “Half the tech kids would crash without a pharmacy. It wasn’t hard.”

“You didn’t think that combining that with coffee, bacon, stress—”

“I thought,” Eliza said, voice rising, “that the cardiologist’s orders would be followed. That the kitchen would keep him on decaf. That he wouldn’t decide he knew better than everyone who’d studied his heart. I thought the worst that would happen was that he’d feel what it’s like to be at someone else’s mercy for once.”

She sagged against the wall.

“He died,” Patel said, not cruel, just factual.

Eliza nodded, tears finally spilling. “I know,” she whispered. “Every time someone says his name, I hear it.”

“Why didn’t you come forward sooner?” Patel asked.

“Because then it wouldn’t just be my guilt,” Eliza said. “It would be their story. They’d turn me into a monster and pretend the system was fine.”

“You altered his medication,” Patel said. “That’s a crime.”

“So is building a world where one man’s whims decide whether entire communities get music education,” Eliza said. “But no one’s going to court for that.”

Patel’s pen stopped moving. “You understand I have to arrest you,” she said.

Eliza laughed once, hollow. “Of course,” she said. “Someone has to go in the box.”

News spread fast, stripped of nuance.

“It was a staffer,” people said in the bar. “Swapped his pills. Jealous. Burnt out. Snapped.”

“I knew it,” someone else said. “You can’t give that much responsibility to people on that pay grade.”

“At least it wasn’t one of us,” a third concluded, and the relief in the phrase was worse than any accusation.

Miles sat with Sarah in a corner, listening.

“They’ve learned nothing,” Sarah said.

“They’ve learned that they can keep their own reflection clean,” Miles said. “That’s all they wanted.”

Lillian joined them, face pale but steady.

“They’ll charge her,” she said. “Manslaughter. Maybe more.”

“She killed him,” Miles said softly. “Even if she didn’t mean to.”

“She pushed a man whose life was already at the edge,” Lillian said. “The rest of us built the cliff.”

## Episode 11 — The Architect

Lillian asked for the microphone that night.

It wasn’t on the schedule. The Estate manager looked panicked. The board members conferred in a huddle. The PR consultant muttered about “controlling the narrative.”

“You’ve been trying to control the narrative since we arrived,” Lillian said. “How’s that going?”

She stood on the small stage in the main hall, the chandeliers dimmed. There were no banners now. The words *THE FUTURE OF CULTURAL LEADERSHIP* lay folded in a cupboard, waiting to be repurposed.

“We have our killer,” she said, voice carrying without strain. “Eliza North altered Harald St. Croix’s medication. He died. The law will do what it does with that.”

Murmurs. An uneasy rustle.

“If we leave it there,” she said, “we are cowards.”

Silence.

“Harald built a system,” she went on. “One where his favour could make or break careers, institutions, cities. He liked to see himself as a benevolent king. Sometimes he was. He funded scholarships, rehearsal rooms, touring productions. Hundreds of people are where they are because he wrote a cheque.”

She paused.

“But he also decided who was ‘promising’ enough for his brand,” she said. “Who was ‘too political,’ ‘too regional,’ ‘too much.’ He overruled doctors. He overruled boards. He told staff like Eliza that their job was to make his whims sustainable. He trained us all to believe that the only way to get anything done was through him.”

She saw Dan, standing at the side of the room, jaw clenched.

“We benefitted,” she said. “I benefitted. My academy survives because of his money. I don’t pretend otherwise. But if we pretend that Eliza’s desperation erupted in a vacuum, we’re lying.”

“This is hardly the time—” a board member began.

“This is exactly the time,” Lillian said. “Because right now, you are writing statements. You are crafting messaging. You are deciding how much truth you can afford. And I am asking you to consider that maybe, for once, you can’t afford the lie.”

She looked up at the chandelier. “There is a ledger,” she said. “You all know it, even if you haven’t seen the file. A record of who got what, when, and why. Of favours granted and withheld. Of quiet warnings. Of people labelled ‘volatile’ and ‘expendable.’”

Whispers. Someone looked at Dan.

“Publish it,” she said. “All of it. Names, scores, notes. Let people see how decisions were made. Then we can talk about accountability.”

“That would be reputational suicide,” a philanthropist hissed.

“For whom?” Lillian asked. “For Harald, who is past caring? For the board, who built careers on proximity to his aura? For the rest of us, who’ve been playing blindfolded?”

Dan stepped forward. “And what then?” he demanded. “You dump a decade of messy decisions into the public square and walk away? The programmes collapse. The kids lose their scholarships. The theatres close.”

“Or,” she said, “we admit that no one person should ever have had that much unexamined power. We build structures that spread responsibility. We stop pretending that a lion’s whim is a governance model.”

“You would burn it all down on principle,” he said.

“I would expose it to air,” she said. “Rot needs darkness.”

She looked at the staff clustered at the back: Toby, servers, cleaners in uniforms they weren’t supposed to be seen in. She looked at the young artists, the middle managers, the people whose names had never made the donor brochures.

“Harald is dead,” she said. “Eliza will go to prison. That will satisfy the headlines. It will not fix what killed him. Only you can decide whether you want to live in that system a little longer, pretending you’re powerless, or whether you’re willing to make it smaller, messier, and more honest.”

She put the microphone down. Her paws shook, slightly.

Clapping started in the back. Toby, eyes fierce. Then the dancer. Then the playwright. The sound grew, uneven but real. Some people folded their arms. Others walked out.

Dan watched it all as a man watching a house he had designed catch fire in slow motion.

## Episode 12 — The Twin’s Logic

That night, on a terrace that smelled of salt and expensive insect repellent, Dan cornered Lillian.

“You don’t know what you’ve done,” he said.

She leaned on the railing, looking out at the dark water. “You’ll have to be more specific.”

“You’ve jeopardised everything,” he said. “The fund. The academy. The scholarships. The little bits of good we manage to pry out of these people.”

“By telling the truth,” she said.

“By making them feel accused,” he said. “They don’t react well to that.”

“I’m not interested in their reactions,” she said. “I’m interested in reality.”

He laughed, sharp. “Reality is numbers,” he said. “Budgets. Commitments. The difference between a roof over your rehearsal studio and a hole in the ceiling. You can’t pay the electrician with moral superiority.”

“I pay him with what we have,” she said. “And I teach my students that the strings attached are real.”

“I’ve spent a decade getting into rooms where people like you never get invited,” he said. “I’ve smiled until my face hurt. I’ve laughed at jokes that made me want to throttle the teller. I’ve sold my accent, my background, my body as proof that they’re doing the right thing. Do you think I did that for pleasure?”

“I think you did it because it works,” she said. “And because you like being the one who makes it work.”

He flinched. “Someone has to translate,” he said. “Would you prefer it be some white Oxbridge boy who thinks ‘diversity’ means commissioning a play about slavery once a decade?”

“False choice,” she said. “There are more than two options.”

“Not in that room,” he said. “Not yet. I’m the only anthro they let handle the levers. You’ve just told them I can’t be trusted with them.”

“I’ve told them no one should be trusted with them in secret,” she said.

He stared at her. “You’re going to cost yourself millions,” he said. “Maybe your job. For what?”

“For the possibility that the next Eliza won’t feel like her only choices are obedience or sabotage,” she said.

He swallowed. “And if you’re wrong?” he asked softly. “If they just walk away? If the board dissolves the fund, the donors drift, the government cuts more, and your academy sinks? What then? You tell the next generation that at least you were honest while the lights went out?”

“I tell them we didn’t build our lives on pretending we didn’t know,” she said.

He shook his head, turned away.

“You and Miles,” he said. “You think you’re above it all.”

“We’re in it up to our necks,” she said. “We just refuse to call it a tide and let it drown us.”

He left without saying goodbye.

His logic was internally sound: keep the machine running, nudge when possible, avoid spectacular failures that would give the enemies of public funding ammunition. It was also a logic that had made Eliza’s choice feel like the only way to be heard.

## Episode 13 — The Unmasking

The unmasking arrived as a link.

The journalist Lillian had sparred with published his piece two days after Eliza’s arrest. The headline was less lurid than it could have been:

> **THE PRICE OF PATRONAGE: INSIDE A CULTURAL KINGMAKER’S LEDGER**

The article laid out the system without melodrama. It described the spreadsheet—names anonymised, patterns intact. It quoted anonymous staff about threats and rewards in the same breath. It quoted artists about being both saved and controlled. It quoted Lillian’s speech.

It did not call Eliza a monster. It did not canonise Harald. It showed the machine.

Phones buzzed. Board members cursed. Donors called their lawyers. PR teams drafted statements about “legacy” and “necessary reforms.”

For once, the story refused to centre a single villain.

In the staff canteen, Toby read the article aloud to anyone who would listen. The dishwasher laughed bitterly every time he heard the phrase “robust governance.”

“He kept score,” a cleaner said. “We kept toilets.”

“Maybe now they see the difference,” Toby said.

In a hastily convened meeting, the board announced an “independent review” of the foundation’s processes. They promised transparency. They promised to publish criteria. They did not promise to relinquish control.

Dan sat at the edge of the table, watching his influence evaporate into committee-speak.

“We’ll need someone to manage stakeholder communications,” a lawyer said. “Daniel, you’re well-placed. Familiar face. Trusted advisor.”

He heard the subtext: *You’re also expendable if this goes badly.*

He thought of Lillian’s words about air and rot. He thought of his own ledger.

Later, he found Miles by a window.

“Congratulations,” he said. “You’ve survived your brush with being the villain.”

Miles looked at him. “It was never about me,” he said. “You tried to make it about me. The room obliged. The facts didn’t.”

“Facts are malleable,” Dan said.

“Less so when my wife and daughter are feeding them to a journalist,” Miles said.

Dan laughed, briefly. “You lot are exhausting,” he said.

“We live with this every day,” Miles said. “You just have to visit on weekends.”

The twins looked at each other, mirror and distortion.

The mask that had slipped wasn’t on Eliza’s face. It had been on the entire edifice of benevolent patronage. The article hadn’t torn it off completely, but it had shown the straps.

## Episode 14 — The Reckoning

Reckoning, at this level, rarely involved handcuffs. It involved calendar invites disappearing.

Within a week, two donors quietly withdrew from the foundation’s board “to focus on other commitments.” A government advisor who had been seen too often at Harald’s side was reassigned. A planned naming ceremony for a new wing was postponed indefinitely.

Eliza was charged with manslaughter. The press ran her mugshot next to flattering old photos of Harald. Comment pieces argued about “burnout,” “power imbalance,” and “the dangers of resentment.” None of them asked what it did to a person to be responsible for making a tyrant look generous.

The foundation announced a “transition plan.” Interim leadership. Consultation processes. Town halls.

Lillian was invited to one of the town halls. She went, because she had students who needed her to.

“We’re committed to learning from this,” a board member said, voice tremulous with rehearsed sincerity. “To ensuring that no one person ever wields such unilateral influence again.”

“Does that include you?” Lillian asked.

There was a pause. Someone wrote her question down as if that were an answer.

Dan sat two rows back, listening. When the meeting broke, he found her in the corridor.

“They’re angry with you,” he said. “More than with me.”

“Good,” she said. “I still have a job to go home to. I can afford their anger more than Eliza can afford their indifference.”

“You might not have that job much longer,” he said.

“Then I’ll build something else,” she said. “It’s what we do.”

He watched her walk away, shoulders squared. He felt, unexpectedly, tired.

The Estate, for its part, scrubbed the weekend from its brochure. Future guests would stay in rooms where a lion had died and never know. The tall doors would still rise, brass shining, test unchanged.

## Episode 15 — After the Party

They left on a morning that smelled of rain.

The car seemed smaller as they loaded it, the boot swallowing suitcases and sketchbooks and the weight of too many conversations. Staff held the doors. No one took a photo. The Estate preferred not to remember departures.

In the rear-view mirror, the building shrank, then disappeared behind a curve in the road. The sea remained, grey and uninterested.

“You’re quiet,” Lillian said, watching Miles’ ears in profile.

“Just thinking,” he said.

“Dangerous habit,” Sarah said from the back seat. “What about?”

“Systems,” he said. “How they pretend to be inevitable.”

“And?” Lillian asked.

“And how they break,” he said. “Sometimes with a pill. Sometimes with a speech. Sometimes just with someone refusing to play along.”

They drove in silence for a while. The motorway signs began to look familiar. The Estate’s curated horizon gave way to billboards and tower blocks, then to the softer green of the road out towards Loughton.

“Do you regret going?” Sarah asked.

“No,” Lillian said. “I’d rather see the wiring than guess at it.”

“Ask me after the next funding meeting,” Miles said.

She reached across the gear stick and squeezed his paw. “If they cut us,” she said, “we’ll find another way. I’ve sung in worse rooms than a church hall.”

“I’ll build the chairs,” Sarah said. “And the stage. And the kiln out back.”

Miles smiled, a little. “And I’ll fix the lights,” he said.

The house in Loughton smelled of tea, wood polish, and clay dust. The garden shed, when they opened it, smelled of tools and sawdust and safety.

Later, when the car was unloaded and the kettle had boiled twice, Miles sat on the shed step with a mug in his paws and watched the light change over the garden.

“You could have been him,” Sarah said, sitting beside him, sketchbook balanced on her knees.

“Which him?” Miles asked. “Harald? Dan?”

“Dan,” she said. “If you’d taken a different job. If you’d liked the taste of those rooms more.”

He thought about it. The boy he’d been at Trinity, playing drums in a practice room, listening to Lillian talk about arias and revolution. The man he’d become, counting pallets and shift schedules, finding comfort in things that either worked or didn’t.

“Maybe,” he said. “I don’t think I’d have been very good at it.”

“You’d have been very good at it,” she said. “That’s what scares me.”

He sipped his tea. “I chose shelves over ledgers,” he said. “I like knowing exactly what I’m responsible for. If I drop a crate, I see what breaks.”

“At the Estate,” she said, “everyone was dropping things and pretending they weren’t.”

“That’s why they needed someone to blame,” he said.

They sat in silence for a while. Birds argued in the hedge. Somewhere in the house, Lillian hummed as she unpacked, her voice weaving in and out of the noises of home.

“Do you think anything will change?” Sarah asked.

“At the foundation?” he said. “Some job titles. Some processes. A few more boxes ticked. The ledger will go digital and password-protected.”

“At the academy?” she asked.

He smiled. “Your mum will make life very uncomfortable for anyone who thinks they can buy her students’ souls on discount,” he said. “She’ll find new donors. Different ones. Or she’ll build something else again, from scratch, like she always has.”

“And you?” she asked.

He looked at his paws, at the callouses blackened by oil and drumsticks. “I’ll keep doing what I do,” he said. “Fixing the things I can reach. Refusing to pretend I don’t see the rest.”

She drew him then, as she had drawn Harald and Dan and the ledger: a man sitting on a shed step, mug in hand, gaze somewhere between the ground and the horizon. No chandeliers. No banners. Just a small, solid body holding more weight than it showed.

In Manchester, a woman read about Eliza North in the paper and wondered whether to tell her kids more about why they were adopted. In London, board members argued over clauses. In a prison cell, Eliza sat on a narrow bed and tried to remember what her life had been before Harald’s calendar dictated its rhythm.

In Loughton, the Fergusons’ house exhaled around them. The drum kit in the corner of the living room waited. The kiln in the shed cooled. The table in the kitchen, scarred and solid, anticipated the next set of plans laid out on it.

Harald St. Croix was dead. His system, wounded, adjusted. New hands reached for the levers.

The party was over. The music, stubborn, went on.
