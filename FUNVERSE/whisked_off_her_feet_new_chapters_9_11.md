## Whisked Off Her Feet — New Chapters

### Chapter 9 — The First Date

Two days after the confetti and the cameras, Fernwell Lane went back to pretending it was normal.

The festival banners came down. The marquee in the square deflated into a tired heap of canvas. The golden whisk trophy sat on a shelf in Lillian’s tiny flat above the bakery, already gathering a respectable film of dust and stray glitter, because she kept forgetting to wipe it when she did the rest of the cleaning.

Badger’s Crust smelled as it always did in the early evening: sugar and coffee and the ghost of everything that had baked that day. The last customers had drifted out an hour ago. Tamsin had gone home, promising to send Lillian the link to an online clip of “their bit,” which Lillian had absolutely not agreed to watch.

She stood at the front window instead, wiping down the glass with slow, circular motions. Outside, streetlights glowed a soft amber. Rain had come and gone, leaving the pavement damp and reflective, like someone had lacquered Fernwell Lane.

Across the street, Croque et Crème’s neon sign glowed steadily. The door was locked; chairs already up on tables inside. She could see the edge of the counter, the faint shine of stainless steel.

She could also see him.

Miles stood just inside his own window, head bent as he wiped down a surface with the same unhurried concentration she recognised from the tent. His sleeves were rolled, fur at his forearms damp and a little ruffled. His ears flicked as he worked. In the reflection, their movements echoed: wipe, pause, stare at nothing in particular.

The distance between the two shopfronts had never looked so wide.

Her phone buzzed on the counter behind her.

She ignored it. Wiped another slow circle. Told herself that the heavy hum under her ribs was just post-festival adrenaline and not anything ridiculous like hope.

The bell over her door jingled.

Lillian turned, cloth in paw.

Miles stood in the doorway of Badger’s Crust, framed by the warm light and the faint curl of steam from the kitchen. He wore a dark shirt and no apron, a concession to the fact that it was technically after hours. His fur was brushed but not perfect; there was a streak of something pale on his jaw, as if he’d missed a spot when he’d washed up.

He held himself like a man who had rehearsed this moment in his head and not enjoyed any of the drafts.

“We’re closed,” Lillian said automatically, then winced. “I mean, we’re… I was just finishing up.”

“I know,” he said. His voice sounded different without the tent’s microphones or the background roar of a festival. Softer. “I watched you turn the sign around. Twice.”

She glanced at the door, where the chalkboard still read CLOSED in her looping hand. It was, in fact, very firmly closed.

“Did you want something?” she asked, then mentally kicked herself, because of course he did, nobody crossed a street in this kind of awkward slow-motion for no reason.

He swallowed. His ears angled, betraying nerves his face tried to smooth over.

“Lillian,” he said. “I was wondering if you would be available to… debrief the festival outcome over a meal. At a neutral location.”

She stared at him.

“Miles,” she said, wiping her paws on her apron to give herself something to do. “Are you asking me on a date or a post-mortem?”

He held her gaze for a long heartbeat.

“Yes,” he said.

She felt the laugh start low in her chest and work its way up, loosening something as it went. It came out softer than she expected.

“All right,” she said. “Let me get my coat before you change your mind and decide an Excel spreadsheet would be more efficient.”

“I already have one,” he said gravely. “It’s in three colours. But I promise not to show it to you until dessert.”

She rolled her eyes. Her hands shook only a little as she untied her apron and hung it on its hook, feeling the familiar give of the fabric, the way it still held the shape of her body. She grabbed her coat from the peg by the stairs—navy, a bit worn at the cuffs—and shrugged into it, shoulders settling against the weight.

Miles stepped aside to let her pass. The gesture was automatic and careful, his broad body angling so she had space to move comfortably through the doorway. He caught the door before it could swing back into her hip.

Outside, the air was cool and damp, carrying the smell of rain on stone and distant takeaway from the high street. The glow from their two shop windows spilled onto the pavement, two rectangles of light facing each other like a pair of eyes.

“Neutral location,” she said. “So not here and not your temple of stainless steel?”

“Tempting as it is to feed you in my natural habitat,” he said, “I thought it might be… less fraught if we went somewhere neither of us has to think about the margins.”

“Look at you,” she said. “Already considering my stress levels. Careful, someone might mistake you for a decent boyfriend.”

He froze for a fraction of a second at the word. Boyfriend. Then his shoulders relaxed.

“Let’s walk,” he said.

They did.

They took the long way, down Fernwell Lane and around the small park, past the bus stop where teenage saxophonists liked to practice in summer. Lillian felt the pavement under her paws, the way it shifted slightly where roots had pushed up the slabs. She was aware of her body in the way she always was in public—of the way her coat sat across her back, of the place where the belt cut across her middle—but she was also aware of the warmth at her side.

Miles matched his pace to hers without comment. When they reached a narrow bit of pavement between a lamppost and a bin, he moved ahead to take the tighter space, leaving her the wider arc.

He didn’t say anything about it. Neither did she.

The restaurant he’d chosen was tucked down a side street, one of those places with soft lighting and mismatched chairs that had clearly been vetted for sturdiness. The host, a raccoon in a crisp shirt, greeted Miles by name and led them to a corner table with a bench on one side and a generous chair on the other.

“Bench,” Miles said quietly as they approached. “If you prefer.”

Lillian eyed the chair, giving it the same quick assessment she gave every seat in her life. Solid legs. No spindly arms. Plenty of room.

“Chair,” she said, and sat.

The chair received her without complaint. Wood pressed warmly against her thighs. She let her weight settle, feeling the small flood of relief that always came when furniture did what it promised.

Miles took the bench, back to the wall, eyes scanning the room the way he did a kitchen—checking exits, routes, how people moved. The dining room smelled of roasted garlic and tomatoes and a faint undernote of citrus from a dish someone else had ordered.

They opened their menus.

For the first twenty minutes, they talked about work.

It was inevitable. The festival sat between them like a third plate. They dissected challenges, judges’ comments, production quirks. Miles ranted quietly about the way the editors had cut a whole sequence of tempering because “apparently nobody wants to see actual technique on television”; Lillian complained about how many times they’d used the word rustic.

“They could at least have alternated with ‘generous’,” she said, spearing a roasted pepper from her starter. “Or ‘unapologetic.’”

“They used that in my profile,” he said. “About my plating.”

“There you go,” she said. “You’re unapologetic, I’m rustic. Between us we’re almost one respectable adjective.”

He smiled, small and genuine.

Their mains arrived. Her plate was a rich, slow-cooked stew with thick slices of bread; his was a precise stack of grilled fish and vegetables, each element placed with care. The food was good, comforting without trying too hard.

They ate. They talked about suppliers, about the sudden uptick in customers who started sentences with “I saw you on—”

“I got three emails today,” Lillian said, “from people I haven’t seen since university. Apparently I have ‘inspired’ them to start baking again.”

“That sounds… nice?” he said.

“One of them once told me I’d never get cast as anything but an aunt,” she said. “Because of my ‘stage presence.’”

His jaw tightened.

“Name?” he asked.

“No murder on the first date,” she said. “Maybe on the third.”

He huffed a laugh.

The conversation looped, safe and familiar. It was only when the plates were cleared and the dessert menus placed between them that something shifted.

Lillian picked up the card. The options were laid out in tiny, elegant font: dark chocolate tart with hazelnut praline, deconstructed cheesecake with seasonal berries, lemon posset with shortbread.

“Of course,” she said under her breath. “Of course it’s deconstructed.”

Miles leaned in to read, brow furrowing.

“Look at this,” he said, tapping a line. “They’re trying to get a set on that tart with this base? It’ll collapse if you breathe on it.”

“Oh, I see,” she said. “They’re balancing the crumble in a little tower. Why do restaurants hate plates that stay flat?”

He made a small, affronted noise.

“There’s nothing wrong with verticality,” he said. “If you secure your layers.”

“Miles,” she said, staring at him over the menu. “You are in a restaurant as a civilian. You’re allowed to just… eat.”

“You’re the one critiquing their soul,” he said. “I’m merely concerned about structural integrity.”

“Their soul is suffering from a mousse overdose,” she said. “Look—this cheesecake used to be a perfectly nice thing until someone decided to reinterpret it as a pile of rubble.”

They went back and forth, almost without noticing. Dessert became a proxy war: he defended technique; she defended joy. They debated sponge-to-cream ratios, the ethics of shards, the tyranny of slate.

Halfway through a sentence about the tyranny of slate, Lillian stopped.

Miles stopped too, head turning towards her in the same moment.

They stared at each other across the small table, menus halfway between them.

“We’re doing it again,” she said.

“Arguing over dessert in front of an audience,” he said.

They both glanced around. The couple at the next table were pointedly not listening with the intensity of people who had absolutely been listening.

Lillian felt heat rise under her fur. Then, unexpectedly, she laughed.

It came out startled and a little wild. Miles’ mouth twitched, then curved.

The laughter spread, unspooling the tension that had tightened between her shoulders since he’d walked in. She laughed at the absurdity of it—how the universe had taken her quiet little bakery life and folded in this tall, earnest, infuriating man until they were laminated together in front of half the country.

He laughed too, quietly at first, then with his whole body, shoulders shaking. His ears flattened slightly with the force of it.

The couple at the next table smiled into their wine.

“We’re impossible,” Lillian said, wiping a tear from the corner of her eye.

“We are,” he agreed. “And yet.”

He set the dessert menu down. His paw brushed hers as he did. It was a light touch, fingers just grazing fur, but it jolted through her with the clarity of a bell.

She didn’t pull away.

“Lillian,” he said, voice softer. “May I ask you a question?”

“That depends,” she said. “Is it about lamination schedules?”

“No,” he said. “It’s about me.”

She tilted her head.

“Proceed,” she said.

He hesitated, eyes dropping to the tablecloth and then back up to her face.

“Why did you ask me, during the last challenge,” he said slowly, “why I needed everything to be perfect?”

She blinked.

Of all the things she’d expected, that wasn’t it. That question had been thrown at him in the heat of ganache and cameras, half-exasperation, half-genuine curiosity. She’d forgotten the exact words; he clearly hadn’t.

“Because you do,” she said. “Need it, I mean. You tighten like a badly tuned string if something’s a millimetre off. And because I… wanted to know what would happen if something wasn’t. For you.”

He sat back slightly, as if making room inside his chest for the answer.

“When I was a sous-chef at my first serious job,” he said, “I was responsible for plating desserts during a critic’s visit. The whole kitchen was on edge. The head chef—he was old-school. Believed in fear as a motivational tool.”

Lillian listened, paws resting loosely on the table, dessert menu forgotten.

“I sent out a plate with a smear two centimetres too wide,” Miles went on. “Nobody at the table noticed. The critic wrote a very kind review. But in the kitchen…”

He trailed off. His paw curled slightly against the tablecloth.

“He took the plate,” Miles said. “And he smashed it on the floor. In front of everyone. Shouted about precision. About standards. About how if I couldn’t control a smear, I had no business calling myself a chef.”

Lillian’s stomach tightened.

She had watched him, in the tent, set plates with the care of someone placing something fragile between teeth of gears. She had thought, more than once, that his neatness was arrogance. Hearing this, she could see the outline of something else: a young man in a hot kitchen, someone else’s temper shattering ceramic and confidence in the same motion.

“I cleaned up the shards,” he said. “And I promised myself I would never give anyone that opening again. That my plates would be so… exact that nobody could find the crack to wedge their anger into.”

He looked up at her then, eyes very clear.

“That’s the short version,” he added. “There are longer ones. But the gist is: imperfect work feels dangerous. Not because I don’t know people are allowed to be human. Because it’s the only way I learned to feel safe.”

Silence settled between them, warm and dense.

Lillian swallowed around the knot in her throat.

“I’m sorry,” she said. “He was wrong.”

“He was effective,” Miles said. “Which is not the same thing.”

She thought of the conservatory panel who had told her she was “unsuited” to certain roles. Of the students who had apologised for their bodies taking up space on stage. Of every chair she’d eyed with suspicion.

“Do you know what’s funny?” she said. “In a bleak, sitcom-from-hell way?”

“You tell me,” he said.

“I learned the opposite lesson,” she said. “That no matter how perfect I was—how in tune, how technically precise—some people would only see my body. My size. My fur. So I stopped trying to disappear into perfection. I went the other way. More colour. More noise. More crumbs.”

He blinked.

“Now I own a bakery where the house speciality is sugar and stubbornness,” she went on. “And I’ve built a life where I only have to be precise about the things that actually matter. Like oven temperatures. And people.”

His paw moved again, deliberate this time, covering hers.

His pads were warm and slightly rough. The contact was steady, not tentative.

“You’re very good at people,” he said quietly.

“I’m learning,” she said. “Starting with one extremely high-maintenance German Shepherd.”

He huffed a laugh. It brushed against the edge of something like a sob.

“I can… try,” he said. “To be less… rigid. With you. If you can tolerate the odd symmetrical garnish.”

“Deal,” she said. “On the condition that you accept crumbs on the table and feelings in the room.”

“I’m getting used to both,” he said.

The waiter appeared like a conjured spell, asking about dessert. They ordered two things they hadn’t argued about: lemon posset and the chocolate tart. They shared both, spoons clinking, conversation turns lighter again, but threaded now with something deeper.

By the time they stepped back out into the street, the rain had washed the air clean. The pavements shone under streetlights. The town had quieted to the soft background noise of buses and distant traffic.

“I’ll walk you home,” Miles said.

“It’s three streets,” she said.

“Humour me,” he said.

She did.

They walked side by side, their shadows overlapping on the wet pavement. As they approached Fernwell Lane, the familiar smell of her bakery reached her before the sign came into view: flour, sugar, the deep, yeasty comfort that had become her life.

The windows of Badger’s Crust glowed against the dark. The sign over the door looked suddenly small; she saw it through the eyes of the country, of people who had watched the show and might one day look up from a bus stop and recognise it.

They stopped in front of the door.

“Well,” she said. “Thank you for the… debrief.”

“Thank you for reminding me that desserts are allowed to have souls,” he said.

They stood there, bodies angled towards each other, the gap between them small and full of possibility.

Lillian felt her heart knock once, hard, against her ribs.

“Miles,” she said, because if she didn’t say something she might combust. “If this is going to be a thing—”

“It is,” he said, a little too fast.

Her mouth quirked.

“Then I should warn you,” she went on, “I come with a bakery, a band, a daughter, and a worrying number of ceramic mugs.”

“I come with spreadsheets, a complicated relationship with symmetry, and a tendency to overthink everything,” he said. “Fair trade?”

“We’ll see,” she said.

He took a breath.

“May I kiss you,” he asked, “now that there isn’t a confetti cannon involved?”

The question settled over her like a warm blanket. Asked, not assumed; offered, not demanded.

She stepped closer.

“Yes,” she said.

He leaned in.

The kiss was not the wild, adrenaline-laced crash behind the tent. It was slower, grounded. His mouth was warm and careful, his paws resting on her shoulders as if he understood, intimately, that she was both soft and solid. She curled her paws into his shirt, the fabric smooth under her claws, feeling the steady thump of his heart.

The world around them narrowed to the circle of light spilling from the bakery window, the cool air on her cheeks, the faint taste of lemon and chocolate on his tongue.

When they parted, their foreheads rested against each other for a moment.

“Okay,” she said, breathless but steady. “Let’s begin.”

He smiled, wide and unguarded, and for the first time since he’d walked into her life and called her bakery a dollhouse made of carbs, she let herself believe that this might be something that lasted longer than a season of television.

Above them, the sign creaked slightly in the night breeze and held.

### Chapter 10 — Meeting Sarah

The following Saturday, Lillian cleaned her flat as if the cameras were coming back.

They weren’t, of course. The production crew had long since moved on to their next project. There were no clipboards in sight, no drones buzzing overhead. Just her tiny living room with its sagging sofa, the shelves full of mismatched mugs and scores, the sturdy coffee table bearing the scars of years of poorly-coastered mugs.

Still, she vacuumed as if the floor would be inspected. She wiped down surfaces that had not seen a cloth in months. She rearranged cushions, then rearranged them again. She checked the framed photos on the wall—her and Sarah at sixteen, flour on their noses; her band on stage; an old, slightly grainy shot of herself in a concert hall dress that no longer fit her body but still fit her memory.

On the low bookcase by the window, the golden whisk trophy watched her with patient, metallic amusement.

“Don’t start,” she told it, straightening a stack of music journals that did not need straightening.

Downstairs, the bakery hummed with Saturday business. Tamsin was more than capable of handling the rush; Lillian had told herself that a hundred times as she climbed the stairs. Today, the work she needed to do was here.

Today, Miles was going to meet Sarah properly.

He’d seen her in glimpses, on video calls when he happened to be in the background, in the photos on Lillian’s phone, in the half-finished sculptures that appeared in the flat when Sarah came home from university. He knew her in the way you know someone who is always just offstage.

Lillian knew her differently.

She knew the weight of Sarah’s head on her lap when she’d first fallen asleep in Lillian’s living room at sixteen, exhausted from pretending she was fine. She knew the way Sarah’s laughter started high and spiralled down into a snort she tried to hide. She knew the exact look Sarah got when she didn’t trust someone with Lillian’s heart.

“It’ll be fine,” she muttered, fluffing a cushion for the third time. “You are a grown badger. You have survived conservatoire juries and reality television and the invention of cronuts. You can introduce your girlfriend to your daughter without combusting.”

She paused.

“Boyfriend,” she corrected under her breath, and felt her stomach flip.

She was still standing in the middle of the room, holding a cushion like a shield, when the door downstairs opened and closed.

Footsteps on the stairs: a familiar weight distribution, the creak of the third step.

Miles appeared in the doorway, a Tupperware box in one paw and a bottle of wine in the other, because of course he’d brought offerings.

He wore a soft grey jumper instead of a chef’s jacket. It made him look more like the man who had laughed with her in the flour cloud than the one on glossy magazine pages. His ears were tipped slightly back, betraying nerves his posture tried to disguise.

“Hello,” he said. “I come bearing sugar and alcohol.”

“Good,” she said. “We’ll need both.”

He stepped into the room, eyes flicking around, taking in the space that was hers alone: the comfortable clutter, the stacks of books, the plant that refused to die in the corner.

“It smells like you up here,” he said, then flushed. “I mean, like the bakery, but also… you. Flour and tea and… something citrus.”

“Lemon cleaner,” she said. “Don’t get poetic, you’ll scare the furniture.”

He smiled, tension easing a fraction.

“How can I help?” he asked.

“You can sit,” she said. “And not look like you’re about to perform heart surgery on my family.”

He hesitated, then obeyed, choosing the corner of the sofa that had the best support. The sofa received him with only a minor protest. He placed the Tupperware and the bottle carefully on the table, as if setting mise en place.

Lillian sat opposite him in her favourite armchair, the one that had a dip perfectly tailored to her body. The fabric hugged her hips and thighs; the armrests were wide enough that she didn’t have to scrunch herself in.

They sat in companionable silence for a moment, listening to the muffled clatter from the bakery below.

“I’m more nervous than I was before the final,” he admitted.

“So am I,” she said. “And I love that girl more than any panel of judges.”

He looked at her, expression softening.

“She adores you,” he said. “That much is obvious from the way she talks about you.”

“She talks about me?” Lillian asked.

“She sends me photos sometimes,” he said. “Of your early cellos. Of the first time you let her frost a cake. Of that sculpture she made of you and the bakery, all welded spoons and clay.”

Lillian blinked.

“I didn’t know that,” she said.

He shrugged, suddenly shy.

“I think she was… checking my responses,” he said. “Like sending me a syllabus for a course she expected me to pass before I was allowed to call myself your partner.”

Lillian’s heart rose to her throat and sat there.

“How did you do?” she asked.

He gave a small, crooked smile.

“I’m here, aren’t I?” he said.

As if on cue, the buzzer by the door sounded—a quick, impatient press.

Lillian stood, thighs pressing against the edge of the armchair, heart thudding.

“That’ll be her,” she said.

“I guessed,” he said.

She shot him a look that was half affection, half plea for luck, then went downstairs to let her daughter in.

Sarah hit the hallway like weather.

She was a golden retriever in her late twenties, hair pulled up in a messy knot that looked accidental and was probably curated in a mirror. She wore paint-splattered jeans, an oversized jumper, and a backpack that looked like it contained either art supplies or a small badger.

“Mum,” she said, wrapping Lillian in a hug before the door had fully closed. “You smell like cinnamon and terror.”

“I cleaned,” Lillian said into her shoulder. “Don’t make fun of me.”

Sarah pulled back, eyes bright, tail wagging in a way that would have knocked over a lesser coat rack.

“I would never,” she said. “Where is he?”

Lillian swallowed.

“Upstairs,” she said. “Trying not to hyperventilate into my cushions.”

“Good,” Sarah said. “Can’t have a man in my mother’s life who doesn’t respect soft furnishings.”

She bounded up the stairs two at a time. Lillian followed at a more measured pace, feeling the familiar ache in her knees and the unfamiliar flutter of this particular fear.

In the doorway of the living room, Sarah stopped.

Miles had stood when he heard them, because of course he had. He faced the door with his shoulders back, paws relaxed at his sides, expression politely neutral—the same look he’d worn for judges, now repurposed for her daughter.

Sarah took him in: tall, broad, ears a little too upright. Tupperware on the table, wine bottle glinting.

“So,” she said, folding her arms. “You’re the spreadsheet guy.”

Miles blinked.

“And you’re the one who thinks ‘structurally improbable’ is a compliment,” he said.

A beat. Then Sarah’s mouth curved into a slow smile.

“It is,” she said.

Something in the room loosened.

Lillian exhaled a breath she hadn’t realised she’d been holding. She moved in, closing the door behind them.

“Sarah, this is Miles,” she said, the words feeling simultaneously enormous and perfectly ordinary. “Miles, this is Sarah. My favourite human.”

“You say that to everyone,” Sarah said, nudging her with an elbow.

“I really don’t,” Lillian said.

Miles stepped forward, offering a paw.

“It’s good to meet you properly,” he said. “Without a camera in your mum’s face.”

Sarah shook his paw, grip firm.

“I’ve seen plenty of your face on my phone,” she said. “Tamsin sends me memes.”

“Of course she does,” Lillian muttered.

They moved into the room. Lillian gestured Sarah to the armchair; she flopped into it, limbs everywhere, as if she hadn’t once been the teenager who perched politely on edges of seats. Miles resumed his place on the sofa, leaving a generous gap between himself and the cushion beside him, a visible invitation and boundary in one.

Lillian took the opposite end, grounding herself in the familiar dip of the cushion.

“I brought tarts,” Miles said, lifting the Tupperware. “Raspberry and pistachio. And… a white wine that allegedly pairs well with awkward introductions.”

“If it pairs with pastry, I’m in,” Sarah said. “Introduce your desserts to my face.”

They ate. The tarts were very Miles: precise layers, clean flavours, a presentation that somehow survived the journey in plastic. Sarah made appreciative noises, then pretended not to when Lillian raised an eyebrow.

The conversation started where most conversations in their family did: work.

Sarah talked about her courses in Manchester, about a tutor who loved her structural experiments and another who thought her work was “too literal” for high art, whatever that meant. She described a piece she was working on—twisted scrap metal and clay that suggested a city collapsing into a kitchen.

Miles listened with genuine, focused interest. He asked questions, not just the polite ones about exhibits and marks, but practical ones about materials and balance.

“How are you stabilising the central column?” he asked. “If you’re hanging that much weight off one side, you’ll need reinforcement here.”

He gestured, air-sketching an invisible armature.

Sarah sat up a little straighter.

“Rebar,” she said. “Hidden in the core. Welded cross-brace at the base. I’m using clay to disguise it so it looks like it shouldn’t stand up.”

“So the illusion is of fragility,” he said, “but the reality is engineered strength.”

Her eyes lit.

“Exactly,” she said. “It’s about how people think certain bodies or structures shouldn’t be possible and yet—”

She stopped, glancing at Lillian, then back at Miles.

“Anyway,” she finished. “It’s due in three weeks.”

“I’d love to see pictures,” he said. “If you’re comfortable sharing.”

She searched his face for a beat, as if checking for patronising glint. Finding none, she nodded.

“I’ll send them,” she said. “As long as you promise not to send back a spreadsheet.”

“No spreadsheets,” he said solemnly. “At most, a strongly-worded admiration email.”

Lillian watched them from her corner of the sofa, heart doing an odd, double-time rhythm. The exchange felt… right. Not forced. Not staged. Just two people who saw the world in terms of load-bearing lines and the stories bodies told in space.

She slipped into the kitchen to put the kettle on, partly because it was the done thing and partly because she needed the small cover of rattling mugs to collect herself.

From the doorway, she watched them as the kettle hummed.

Sarah had pulled a sketchbook from her bag. She flipped to a page and angled it towards Miles. The drawing was of Lillian in the bakery, seen from behind the counter: broad shoulders, hair in a braid, apron tied tight. The linework caught the way she leaned into the work, weight forward, unselfconscious.

“This is incredible,” Miles said quietly. “You’ve got the posture exactly. The way she stands when she’s thinking about four things at once.”

Lillian swallowed.

“You can tell that from a sketch?” Sarah asked.

“I can tell that because I’ve been staring at that posture across a tent for weeks,” he said. “And across the street for longer.”

Sarah’s mouth twitched.

“You’re very earnest,” she said.

“Occupational hazard,” he replied.

“Mum needs earnest,” Sarah said. “She’s got sarcasm covered.”

Lillian busied herself with mugs and teabags, cheeks warm.

When she carried the tray back in, balancing it against her belly, Sarah jumped up to help without being asked, taking two cups from the tray. Miles moved to take the heavier teapot.

They navigated the small space together without collision, as if they’d been sharing cramped kitchens all their lives.

Later, when plates were empty and the tea had cooled, Sarah sat cross-legged on the floor, back against the sofa, sketching idly. Miles perched on the edge of the coffee table, sorting through the postcards and flyers she’d scattered there from recent exhibitions.

“So,” Sarah said, pencil moving. “What’s your damage?”

Miles blinked.

“I’m sorry?” he asked.

“Artists’ shorthand,” she said. “What made you like this? The precision. The spreadsheets. The… intense concern for the thickness of sauces.”

Lillian opened her mouth to intervene, then closed it. She had told Sarah, early on, that if she wanted honesty from people she needed to ask the questions that mattered. She couldn’t begrudge her for doing exactly that now.

Miles took a breath.

“Short version?” he said. “I grew up in a family where being useful was love. I discovered that I could make things—machines, recipes, schedules—behave if I just learned enough about them. People were harder. Kitchens were somewhere in the middle.”

Sarah’s pencil paused.

“And you decided to major in kitchens,” she said.

“More or less,” he said. “There was an incident with a tarte tatin and a plate. It’s a long story. Your mother has heard the director’s cut.”

Lillian’s chest pulled tight at the memory of the smashed plate, the way his voice had changed when he told her.

Sarah watched him for a moment, then resumed sketching.

“You’re very controlled,” she said. “But you’re here in my mum’s living room admitting vulnerability. That’s… promising.”

He huffed a laugh.

“Is this an interview?” he asked.

“This is the preliminary round,” she said. “There will be follow-up questions.”

“I’ll prepare a slideshow,” he said.

“If you do, I’m out,” she said.

They grinned at each other.

Lillian sat back, letting the scene soak into her. The light from the window caught the edges of their fur, the curve of Sarah’s jaw so familiar and yet wholly her own. The room smelled of tea and pastry and the faint metallic tang of the golden whisk.

“What do you think,” Sarah asked suddenly, looking up at Lillian, “about all of this?”

She gestured—at the room, at Miles, at herself.

Lillian considered.

“I think,” she said slowly, “that six months ago I was mostly worried about oven temperatures and paying the electricity bill. And now I’m worried about all of that plus whether my daughter and my boyfriend like each other.”

Miles’ ears flicked at the word again. Boyfriend. He didn’t correct it.

“I think,” she went on, “that I am… happy. Which is frankly terrifying. But also…”

She trailed off, searching for the right phrase. Sarah watched her, pencil hovering over paper. Miles’ gaze was steady and gentle.

“…right,” she finished. “It feels right.”

Sarah’s face softened.

“Okay,” she said. “Then I’m in.”

“Just like that?” Miles asked.

“Just like that,” she said. “For now. Subject to review if you ever make her cry on purpose.”

“Fair,” he said quietly.

He didn’t promise he never would; he’d been alive long enough to know better. But he looked at Lillian with a kind of awe that made her stomach flutter, and she believed he’d do his best.

When Sarah left later, bag heavy on her back, she hugged them both at the door. She squeezed Miles with more strength than he’d anticipated; he made a small, undignified oof that she pretended not to hear.

“Take care of each other,” she said, stepping back onto the stair.

“We will,” Lillian said.

“I intend to,” Miles said.

She gave them one last assessing look, then grinned.

“You’re disgusting,” she said fondly. “I love it.”

The door closed behind her, leaving the flat humming with her absence.

Lillian leaned back against it, feeling the solid wood against her spine. Her body ached from a day of cleaning and a morning in the bakery. Her heart felt oddly light.

Miles came to stand beside her, close enough that their shoulders touched.

“You all right?” he asked.

She nodded.

“She likes you,” she said.

“She tolerated me,” he said. “There’s a difference.”

“For Sarah?” Lillian said. “Not much.”

He exhaled, tension leaving his shoulders in a visible wave.

“Good,” he said softly.

She turned her head, resting her cheek briefly against his upper arm, letting herself lean.

“This,” she thought, not for the first time, “is what it’s supposed to feel like.”

### Chapter 11 — The Badger & The Hound

The first time Lillian and Miles stood in what would become The Badger & The Hound, it looked nothing like a bakery.

It looked like a mistake.

The space was a long, narrow former hardware shop halfway down Fernwell Lane, technically between Badger’s Crust and Croque et Crème if you followed the line of the street. The windows were dusty; the floor was bare concrete; the walls had the defeated beige of paint that had given up.

Dust motes floated in the strips of light from the high front windows. Somewhere, a pipe gurgled in a way that suggested opinions about the future.

Lillian stood in the middle of the room, paws on her hips, breathing in the smell of plaster and old nails. It did not smell like sugar. Yet.

Miles stood beside her, a rolled-up blueprint tube under his arm. He was already frowning at the ceiling.

“The load-bearing beams are in decent shape,” he said. “We’ll need to reinforce where the ovens go, but the structure will hold.”

“Good,” she said. “I was worried the building might have the same stamina as I do after a double shift.”

He glanced at her, the corner of his mouth curving.

“You’ve carried more weight than this place ever has,” he said.

From the back of the room, Sarah’s voice floated up.

“Less flirting, more measuring,” she said. “Some of us are here on a volunteer basis.”

She sat cross-legged on an overturned crate, sketchbook open in her lap, pencil moving as she tried to capture the bones of the space. A tape measure lay beside her, already unfurled.

“You volunteered aggressively,” Lillian called.

“I have a vested interest in the narrative coherence of this street,” Sarah said.

Her pencil scratched. She caught the line of Lillian’s shoulders, the way Miles tilted his head when he thought, the intrusive confidence of the old radiators.

Miles popped the cap off the blueprint tube and shook out a roll of paper. It fell open with a satisfying snap, lines and measurements already neatly inked.

“Here,” he said, kneeling to spread it on a cleanish patch of floor. “Counter here, ovens along this wall. Seating in the front half. We can put in a small stage in the corner for your band.”

Lillian knelt opposite him, joints protesting in chorus with the floor. She leaned over the paper, reading the lines as if they were music.

“Where do people queue?” she asked. “I don’t want them bottlenecking at the door.”

“Here,” he said, tracing a path with his paw. “We can do a gentle curve past the display. It keeps the flow moving, reduces clumping.”

“You and your clumping,” she said. “People aren’t batter.”

“People are absolutely batter,” he said. “Unpredictable, prone to overmixing, and likely to spill if left unattended.”

Sarah snorted.

They went back and forth, adjusting imaginary counters and sinks until the space on the paper felt like something she could picture with her eyes open.

The arguments started when they reached the part of the blueprint labelled SIGNAGE & BRANDING.

“It makes sense to keep Badger’s Crust,” Lillian said. “It has history. People know it. It smells like… us.”

“It smells like you,” Miles said. “Which is fantastic. But this is new. It’s both of us. It could be… Ferguson & Edgecombe Patisserie. Clean. Professional. It would look good on invoices.”

“I’m not putting my name second,” she said, bristling. “I’ve been on this street longer. And it sounds like a law firm.”

“Edgecombe & Ferguson, then,” he said. “It scans better in English.”

“You literally just said—”

“I’m talking about rhythm, not hierarchy,” he said. “And anyway, names like that signal a partnership. Two equals.”

“Names like that signal that everyone inside wears the same colour apron and nobody is allowed to lick the spoon,” she said. “This isn’t a consulting firm, Miles. It’s a place where Mrs Patel can come in with her dog and complain about her son-in-law.”

He scrubbed a paw over his face, leaving a faint streak of dust on his fur.

“Badger’s Crust was your second act,” he said. “This—” he gestured around them “—this is mine. The first thing I’ve done entirely on purpose, not because a restaurant group thought it was a good idea. I want… to see my name on it. On something that isn’t going to close when a spreadsheet says it should.”

The words hung in the air between them, raw and earnest.

Lillian’s anger deflated, leaving something more complicated behind.

“I know,” she said quietly. “I do. I just…”

She looked towards the imaginary front of the shop, seeing overlapping ghosts of signs: her handwritten chalkboard, his sleek neon. The thought of erasing Badger’s Crust from the street made her stomach twist.

“I don’t want to vanish,” she said. “Again. I spent too many years being the person in the background. The accompanist, the friend, the ‘plus size’ in the corner. The bakery is the first time my name has been the one on the door.”

Miles’ expression softened.

“I would never ask you to vanish,” he said.

“You’re literally asking me to take my name off my shop,” she said.

“I’m asking you to share it,” he said. “With me. With us. With whatever this is.”

“This,” she said, “is me trying not to throw a tape measure at you.”

Sarah sighed theatrically from her crate.

“You’re both being idiots,” she announced.

They looked at her, mid-glare.

“Excuse me?” Lillian said.

“You heard me,” Sarah said. “You’re framing it like it’s one or the other. Old versus new. Hers versus his. When you could, wild idea, have both.”

Miles frowned.

“Both,” he repeated.

Sarah stood, stretching. She padded over, sketchbook in paw, and dropped it on top of the blueprint.

On the page, in bold pencil lines, a badger and a German Shepherd stood back to back, each holding a whisk. Above them, in hand-lettered script, were three words.

THE BADGER & THE HOUND.

“You’re the stubborn, brilliant badger,” she said, tapping Lillian’s drawn self. “You’re the loyal, methodical hound.” She tapped Miles. “It’s not just a name, it’s a story. Everyone who walks in will know immediately who’s holding this place up.”

Lillian stared at the drawing.

The badger was broad and grounded, weight settled in her hips, eyes bright behind her glasses. The hound was tall and slightly angular, ears pricked, stance alert but not stiff. They fitted together the way their movements in the tent had, the way they did in her kitchen.

Miles looked from the sketch to the room, eyes flicking as if overlaying the logo onto the blank wall above the imaginary counter.

“The Badger & The Hound,” he said slowly. “It sounds like somewhere you could bring a family. Or a date. Or a laptop. It sounds… warm.”

“It sounds like a pub,” Lillian said.

“Exactly,” Sarah said. “People love pubs. They trust a pub. You’re not a law firm; you’re a place people come when their day has been terrible and they need cake and to see someone who knows their usual order.”

Lillian’s chest did that annoying flutter again.

“Artisan Bakes with Bite,” Sarah added, scribbling the tagline under the sketch.

“It’s ridiculous,” Lillian said.

“It’s perfect,” Miles said at the same time.

They looked at each other.

“Fine,” Lillian said. “I like it. A little.”

“I love it,” Miles said. “Unreservedly.”

Sarah grinned, all teeth.

“Good,” she said. “Because I already mocked up an Instagram logo.”

The arguments shifted after that.

They still bickered—over paint colours (she wanted deep teal, he wanted light grey; they compromised on a soft blue that made the room feel bigger), over tile patterns (hexagons versus rectangles, a saga in three acts), over whether to have one big communal table or several smaller ones.

But underneath the bickering sat the certainty of the name, a shared story they could both live inside.

They painted together, rollers squeaking against the walls. Lillian’s strokes were broad and enthusiastic; Miles’ were precise, corners sharp. They ended up speckled with paint, flecks drying on fur. Sarah documented the process with her phone and her sketchbook, catching the moment when Lillian tried to reach a high spot, overbalanced, and Miles steadied her with a paw at her back.

They assembled counters, cursing flat-pack instructions in three languages. They argued about where to put the mixer so that Lillian had enough room to move and still see the door. They measured tables, tested chairs, sat in each one to make sure it received Lillian’s weight with respect and comfort.

“If a chair can’t hold me,” she said, lowering herself into one with experimental caution, “it doesn’t get to live here.”

“Same goes for people,” Miles muttered, adjusting the screw on a wobbly leg.

They hung shelves. They argued about mug capacity. They wired in softly glowing pendant lights that turned the bare walls into something closer to home.

On a late evening near the end of the renovation, Lillian stood alone in the middle of the half-finished bakery. The floor was still bare concrete, but the walls were painted. The counter was in. The ovens, her ovens and his ovens, sat side by side like two solid hearts.

She inhaled.

It still smelled faintly of plaster and paint, but underneath she caught a hint of something else: flour from the test batch of bread she’d baked that afternoon, sugar from the caramel Miles had trialled, the ghost of cardamom she had brought with her like a signature.

Her body was tired in the pleasant, deep way that meant she’d used it fully. Her shoulders ached; her knees reminded her that she was not twenty; her hands bore paint and tiny nicks from wood.

Miles came in from the back, carrying a wrapped package.

“Come outside,” he said.

“If this is another surprise delivery of tiles, I’m going to stage a coup,” she said, but she followed him to the door.

The street was quiet. The sky above Fernwell Lane had settled into that deep city indigo, not entirely dark, lit by streetlamps and the glow from flats above shops. The old Badger’s Crust sign across the way was dark now; the space would become something else soon—a rehearsal room, she told herself. A studio. The memory of it would live here.

Miles set the package down and unwrapped it.

The sign gleamed in the lamplight: THE BADGER & THE HOUND in cream letters on a deep blue background, the ampersand curling like a tail. In one corner, a tiny illustration of a badger and a hound sharing a whisk.

Lillian’s throat closed.

“We can still change it,” he said quickly. “If you hate it. If it feels wrong.”

She couldn’t speak for a moment. She reached out instead, fingertips brushing the painted surface. The wood was smooth, solid under her paw.

“It feels… like us,” she said finally. “Like all the bits. The stubbornness, the spreadsheets, the cardamom, the… whatever it is you do with blowtorches.”

“Sugar work,” he said automatically.

“That,” she said.

Sarah appeared beside them, having materialised from somewhere with the uncanny stealth of adult children. She carried a small step ladder under one arm.

“Are we doing this?” she asked.

Miles nodded.

They worked together to hang the sign.

Sarah held the ladder steady, feet planted wide. Miles climbed, careful with his weight, and bolted the brackets into the brick with the ease of someone who had spent his youth helping his father with DIY projects that were never as simple as they looked. Lillian handed up screws, tools, a damp cloth to wipe away dust.

When he climbed down, the three of them stepped back.

The sign sat above the door, solid and sure.

THE BADGER & THE HOUND.

Lillian felt something in her chest settle into place, like dough reaching the right proof.

“It looks like it’s always been there,” Sarah said quietly.

“It hasn’t,” Lillian said. “We did this. We made this.”

Miles’ paw brushed hers. She curled her fingers around his, the contact easy now, familiar.

“It’s not just a business,” he said.

“It’s a home,” she finished.

They stood there for a long moment, three silhouettes on a quiet street, looking up at the name they’d chosen. Cars passed at the end of the lane, headlights briefly lighting the sign. Somewhere, a fox laughed in a garden.

Down the road, someone would one day point to that sign and say, That’s where the badger and the hound from the telly ended up. That’s where the cinnamon rolls taste like forgiveness and the tarts taste like precision and if you sit there long enough, you start to believe there might be space for you, too.

For now, it was just theirs.

“We should go to bed,” Lillian said eventually. “We have a bakery to open in the morning.”

“We have our bakery to open,” Miles corrected.

She let herself smile, wide and unguarded, letting the word our settle over her like flour.

Together, they turned off the interior lights. The sign glowed softly in the streetlamps, holding its place against the night.
