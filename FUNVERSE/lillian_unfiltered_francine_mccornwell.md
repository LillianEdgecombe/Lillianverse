## Lillian Unfiltered — Season One

### Chapter 1 — The Pitch

The email subject line read:  
**“Community Culture & Music Education — TV Proposal”**, which was exactly the kind of phrase that made Lillian’s fur fluff defensively along her shoulders.

She sat at the kitchen table in Loughton, one broad badger paw wrapped around a mug of dark tea with honey. The late afternoon light came in low and soft through the back windows, making the steam look like stage fog. It smelled of honey, lemon, and the faint metallic tang of Miles’s toolkit, which was still open on the counter where he’d been placating a misbehaving drawer.

“It’s spam,” Miles said, not looking up from the drawer. His deep voice carried easily, even as his paws worked in small, precise movements. “No real project starts with three buzzwords in a row.”

Lillian adjusted her glasses and opened the email anyway.

The production company had a soothing name: **Northern Lantern Media**, which sounded like something that would air documentaries about lakes and otters, not whatever this was. The pitch was earnest and flattering and just a little too enthusiastic, like someone had discovered exclamation marks on sale.

They wanted to make a reality series centred on *community culture and music education*, filmed partly at the Academy, partly in Lillian and Miles’s home. They referenced her work with hybrid students, the band’s legacy in London, and the recent article that had called her “a quiet pillar of the capital’s anthro arts scene”. They used phrases like *authentic storytelling* and *uplifting local voices* and *wholesome representation*.

They also mentioned “light domestic moments” four times.

“I am not a light domestic moment,” Lillian said.

Miles glanced over, brown eyes warm and wary. “No, love. You’re… structural integrity.”

That earned him a small smile. She read on.

There was a proposed episode structure, a respectful but clearly thrilled paragraph about the band, and, tucked neatly between polite sentences, the words: *We’d also love to showcase your home life with your husband Miles.* As if their home life were a garnish. As if it could be portioned out in neat segments between ad breaks.

“They’ve done their research,” she murmured. “They know about Sarah. They know about the Academy board. They even know about the ceramic residency last year.”

Miles tightened a screw, jaw working. “Of course they know. They’ve probably seen your LinkedIn, the Academy website, your YouTube clips from the protests, the three interviews you actually said yes to. That’s their job—collect pieces.”

He eased the drawer open and shut a few times, testing the slide. The wood sighed, compliant again.

Lillian looked from the glowing laptop screen to the familiar kitchen: the sturdy table that had moved with them through three homes, the slightly chipped blue tiles, the wall of magnets and notes. Her belly rested comfortably against the table edge, solid and unbothered. Her body had taken up space in this room long enough that it felt like hers, properly hers. The idea of a camera stepping in and making it into content prickled along her spine.

“They’re offering money,” she said, because it was there, in neat figures. “Enough to cover the new rehearsal room, if the Academy trustees keep dithering.”

Miles closed the toolkit. “And enough to pay a very good therapist for when the whole thing goes sideways.”

From the living room, the muffled sound of Sarah’s latest sculpture playlist drifted in—industrial ambience, the clank and echo of metal, punctuated by a surprisingly tender trumpet line. Lillian thought of Sarah in Manchester, welding mask tilted up, golden retriever ears tied back in a bandana, working on something that looked like a cross between a cathedral and a spine. Her girl, building monuments out of scrap.

Community culture. Music education. Domestic moments.

“We could say no,” Miles said, wiping his paws on a tea towel. “There’s no law that says we have to invite a camera crew in so people can decide whether they like our mugs.”

He said it lightly, but she heard the tension under it—the same thread that had been there when the Academy board had asked her to “consider a more media-forward profile” to attract donors. They were always being asked to be more visible. To represent. To be exemplary and accessible and, ideally, very tidy.

Lillian sipped her tea, letting the honey coat her tongue. She thought of her students, the ones who couldn’t afford private lessons but came alive in the rehearsal rooms. She thought of parents who had never seen a fat badger woman in sequins at a grand piano on television, whose kids might need that image like air.

“What if,” she said slowly, “we could set terms? Proper terms. Non-negotiable ones.”

Miles slid into the chair opposite her, the wood creaking as it accepted his weight. He sat slightly turned, to fit his long back and tail between chair and table, a choreography they had worked out together years ago. He folded his paws, watching her.

“Such as?”

“No shooting in the bedroom,” she began, ticking off on her claws. “No surprise guests. We approve every episode before broadcast. The edit can’t turn my students into pity projects. And if they want Miles, they get the real Miles, not whatever fantasy they’re hoping for.”

“The real Miles is very boring,” he said.

“The real Miles makes the bed feel like it floats,” she replied. “They should be so lucky.”

He huffed, ears flicking back in a pleased, embarrassed way. “They’re not getting the bed. That’s an extra subscription tier.”

She laughed, the sound filling the kitchen. Underneath it, there was a small, tight place in her chest that had never entirely trusted institutions, especially ones that came with contracts and cameras. She thought of the Royal Academy rejection letter in a box somewhere, neatly folded like a curse that never quite stopped humming.

But she also thought of a girl somewhere who might stumble onto a show and see a badger woman like her, unapologetic in her body, her art, her life.

“I want to talk to Sarah,” Lillian said. “And I want our lawyer to look at the contract. But… I’m not saying no.”

Miles exhaled slowly, a long, controlled breath that she recognised from before big gigs and difficult board meetings.

“All right,” he said. “We listen. We set terms. And the moment they cross the line—”

“We kick them out,” Lillian finished.

They held each other’s gaze across the table, paws resting on the wood, not quite touching. The kitchen smelled of honey and oil and the lingering ghost of last night’s garlic. Somewhere in the house, a floorboard settled with a small, decisive click, as if the building itself had voted.

Lillian reached for the laptop again.

“I’ll write back,” she said. “And I’ll make some soup. If we’re inviting trouble in, it’s not going to find this kitchen empty.”

### Chapter 2 — Cameras in the Kitchen

Trouble arrived three weeks later in the form of a white van, three tripods, two boxes of lights, and an assistant producer who apologised to the hydrangeas.

The van reversed gingerly into the short Loughton driveway, tyres crunching on gravel. Lillian watched from the doorway, broad silhouette filling the frame, an apron tied over her dress. The air smelled of damp earth and car exhaust. The sky had the washed-out colour of a rehearsal room wall.

Miles stood behind her, one paw on the doorframe, the other absently smoothing the fur along his muzzle. He was wearing his best cargo trousers and a soft grey shirt that made his shoulders look even broader. Lillian had chosen it; she knew what cameras did to angles.

“They’re early,” he murmured.

“Of course they’re early,” she said. “They want to catch us… unguarded.”

He made a quiet noise that could have meant anything from *I dislike this* to *I will dismantle the van if necessary*. Lillian stepped out onto the threshold, her body meeting the cool air with the small shock it always felt after a warm kitchen.

“Hi!” called the assistant producer, a fox with a clipboard, three pens clipped to her lanyard, and optimism radiating like bad fluorescent lighting. “Lillian? Miles? We’re so excited to be here. And I have to say, your house is just—” she glanced up, visibly delighted “—so visually rich.”

“It’s full of things,” Lillian said. “We’ve been collecting them for twenty years. They’re not props.”

“Exactly!” the fox replied, as if that had been the ideal answer. “We want to honour that.”

Behind her, two camera operators began unloading. One, a tall otter, paused to stare at the garden shed. Lillian watched his eyes travel over the reinforced door and the discreet extra padlock Miles had installed. The shed smelled of clay and sawdust and the faint, metal tang of tools; sometimes she thought it smelled more like Miles than the house did.

“We don’t shoot in the shed,” Miles said, voice calm.

The otter blinked. “Oh. Sure. We just thought—”

“We don’t shoot in the shed,” Lillian repeated, equally calm. “Or the bedroom. Or the bathroom. Those are offline.”

The fox nodded briskly, tapping her clipboard. “Totally understood. We’re focusing on kitchen, living room, Academy, and local community spaces. Those are already cleared with Legal.”

Lillian didn’t relax, but she let herself breathe a fraction deeper. The contract had been clear. Their lawyer had annotated it like a musical score, every clause bracketed and translated. The crew could be here, in these rooms, at scheduled times. They could not follow them to bed, to the doctor, to funerals, to church.

Still, as the first tripod entered the kitchen, the house seemed to pull in around it. The space actually shrank. The big table, which usually sat at a comfortable distance from the counters, suddenly felt too close. The fridge door became a potential collision. The overhead light, previously warm, now revealed every speck of dust the production designer’s eyes latched onto.

“We might just swap that bulb,” the fox said, already reaching up with a practiced stretch. “Get a bit more clarity on the worktops.”

“Careful,” Miles said. “The fitting’s old.”

He moved without thinking, stepping under the light, paws reaching up. His fingers brushed hers as he took the bulb. His body filled the centre of the kitchen, the way he did when he was moving a heavy pan from hob to counter, or when he lifted Sarah’s first sculpture and carried it outside so she could hose it down. The camera operator instinctively lifted his lens.

“We’re not rolling yet,” Lillian said sharply.

The lens lowered. The fox, who would apparently rather die than be rude, produced a smile that looked rehearsed in more ways than one.

“We just like to… get a sense of the space,” she said. “Find the angles. You know.”

Lillian did know. She had spent half her life finding angles—on stage, in meeting rooms, in interviews. She knew what it meant to be arranged. She also knew what it meant to arrange herself, deliberately, as an act of refusal.

“All right,” she said. “Let’s walk through your plan. Then I’ll show you where the tea lives.”

They sat at the kitchen table to go over the schedule. The fox, whose name turned out to be Leila, spread out colour-coded pages. There were segments labelled **LILLIAN AT THE ACADEMY**, **BAND REUNION**, **COMMUNITY CHOIR**, **FAMILY DINNER**. There were time slots, blackout dates, B-roll suggestions (“Miles gardening?” underlined twice).

“We want to start with a simple breakfast scene,” Leila said. “Just you two, talking about the day, making tea, maybe some toast?”

Lillian imagined it, the way the cameras would frame the toast, the butter, the curve of her arm as she reached for the cupboard. She imagined the internet arguing about whether she used too much honey. She imagined comments about Miles’s shoulders, his paws, his tail.

She also imagined her second-year student Layla, who had never seen anyone her size on television who wasn’t a joke, watching Lillian pour tea without apologising for the space she occupied.

“We can do breakfast,” Lillian said. “But not at six in the morning. You get us at nine. We are middle-aged and we rehearse late.”

Leila laughed, tapping her pen. “Nine is great. Authentic sleep schedules.”

“Authenticity apparently comes in time slots,” Miles murmured, too low for the microphones that weren’t yet turned on. Lillian felt the warmth of his thigh against hers under the table and anchored herself there.

By the time the crew had finished their tour, the kitchen had acquired two extra lamps, a matte screen by the window, and a discreet microphone clipped under the shelf. The house’s familiar smells—tea, garlic, clay, pine floor cleaner—were joined by hot metal and the faint chemical tang of gaffer tape.

“We’ll be back tomorrow,” Leila said at the door, “for our first proper day. Just… be yourselves.”

Lillian smiled, slow and real this time. “We’ve had forty-two years of practice.”

As the van pulled away and the driveway settled back into silence, Miles exhaled like he’d been holding his breath since they opened the door.

“The house feels smaller,” he said.

Lillian stepped back into the kitchen. The table was where it had always been. The kettle was still on its familiar ring of limescale. Her favourite mug waited where she’d left it. And yet.

“We’ll find angles of our own,” she said. “If they’re going to look, they’ll look at what we choose.”

She reached up and unplugged one of the new lamps. Just to prove she could.

### Chapter 3 — Authenticity, Apparently

“Okay, so,” Leila said the next morning, standing in the kitchen as if the house were a set she had booked rather than a home she had been invited into, “we really want to capture you being… you. Natural. Authentic.”

Lillian, who had already survived three authenticity-centred initiatives at the Academy and one disastrous Arts Council workshop on “branding your truth”, picked up the teapot and poured.

“And what does that look like, from your side?” she asked.

“Just do what you’d normally do,” Leila said, which was the least useful instruction in the world. “We’ll be flies on the wall.”

There were three cameras, two boom mics, a lighting rig, and an intern trying to pretend they didn’t exist. The wall, Lillian thought, might have opinions about the flies.

She set a mug in front of Miles, who sat at the table, posture slightly straighter than usual. The chair received his weight in a familiar creak. The microphone cable snaked along the floor near his paws, like a small, patient hazard.

“So… what’s on your schedule today?” Leila prompted, with the relentless cheer of someone coaxing a toddler into saying “Mama” on camera.

Lillian took a sip of tea. The heat hit her tongue, the honey settling her nerves in a slow, syrupy way.

“I have second-years at ten,” she said. “They’re doing arrangements of protest songs. Then a meeting with the board about the rehearsal rooms. Then I come home and make soup.”

“That’s great,” Leila said. “Can you say it again, but maybe look at Miles when you say ‘soup’? Just to, like, land on the domestic note?”

Lillian did not look at Miles. She looked directly at the nearest camera instead, her glasses catching a brief flare from the light.

“I have second-years at ten,” she repeated, voice steady. “They’re doing arrangements of protest songs. Then a meeting with the board. Then I come home and make soup, which Miles will eat and pretend is not the best thing that has ever happened to him.”

Miles huffed a laugh, shoulders loosening.

“Accurate,” he said.

Leila’s eyes went bright. “Perfect. That’s perfect.”

The morning went on in a series of small adjustments. Pour the tea again, from a slightly different angle. Could she stand by the window while she answered that question about her students? Could Miles open the fridge as he talked about his work, so they could get ‘a sense of provision’?

“I am not a metaphor for provisioning,” Miles said, rummaging for yoghurt anyway.

“You kind of are,” Lillian murmured. “Sorry, love.”

At the Academy, the cameras multiplied. There were permissions and forms, briefings and no-go zones. Some students volunteered, thrilled; others ducked out of frame with practiced ease. Lillian moved through her day as usual, or as close to usual as the microphones would allow. She corrected bow holds, coaxed shy alto voices, joked about drum patterns with the percussionists.

The cameras tracked her hands. They lingered on the breadth of her back as she leaned over the piano, on the set of her shoulders when a trustee made a comment about “appeal to mainstream audiences”, on the way she smiled without showing teeth when she said, “We are already appealing to our audience. Perhaps the mainstream should expand.”

In the evening, back home, the crew wanted a “simple dinner sequence”.

“Just do what you’d normally do,” Leila said again, as if normal ever involved someone asking you to narrate why you chose carrots.

Lillian chopped vegetables, the knife solid in her paw. The cameras watched her hands, the rhythm of blade on board. Miles set the table, adjusting the chairs for their bodies, the way he always did—his own pushed back a little farther, Lillian’s with the cushions she liked. He placed the cutlery with quiet precision.

“Do you always… do that?” one of the camera operators asked, genuinely curious.

“Yes,” Miles said. “Chairs are systems. They either work for the people using them, or they don’t. We fix them.”

The operator blinked, then nodded, as if this philosophy were both new and obviously correct.

Later, when the crew had finally left and the house exhaled, Lillian and Miles sat at the table with their bowls of soup, the microphones unattached, the lamps unplugged.

“How do you feel?” Miles asked.

Lillian considered. Her feet were sore from standing more deliberately than usual. Her voice had the particular tiredness that came from explaining herself politely all day. But the soup tasted good, and the house smelled like onions and thyme.

“Like I’ve been talked at by a very enthusiastic grant application,” she said. “But nobody cried, and nobody tried to put me on a stool I couldn’t fit on, so… progress.”

Miles reached across the table, brushing his paw against hers.

“We survived day one,” he said. “Authentically.”

She smiled, feeling the word settle differently now. It would need guarding. But so far, they were doing the guarding together.

### Chapter 4 — The Cabinet Incident

The high cabinet over the fridge had always been a minor annoyance. It was where things went to be forgotten—old pudding tins, a novelty teapot, the fondue set they had used exactly twice. It was also, Lillian discovered, the site of their first truly viral moment.

“We’d like to stage a little domestic problem,” Leila said that morning, the word *stage* landing with a soft thud. “Something relatable. A stuck drawer, maybe, or a cupboard you can’t quite reach. Then Miles swoops in and fixes it.”

“Miles does not swoop,” Lillian said. “He plans and stabilises.”

Miles shrugged. “I can swoop if necessary.”

She gave him a look. “You can… arrive deliberately.”

They settled on the high cabinet because it already existed and because Lillian genuinely could not reach it without standing on a stool or asking for help. The crew moved the fondue set to a more photogenic position. The otter cameraman found an angle that showed both the cabinet and the kitchen table, as well as the line of Miles’s back when he reached up.

“We’ll just… run it a few times,” Leila said, almost apologetic.

They started with Lillian.

She stood by the fridge, looking up at the cabinet. The camera watched her eyes, the small squint behind her glasses. She stretched up experimentally, the movement pulling at her dress, her belly pressing against the cold metal of the fridge door. Her paw stopped a good twenty centimetres short of the handle.

“Miles,” she called, turning towards the table where he sat with a mug and a prop newspaper. “Can you get the thing from the stupid cabinet? The one we’ve both agreed is badly designed?”

“Sure,” he said, folding the paper, his voice mild. He stood, the chair creaking, the floorboards handling his weight like an old friend. The camera operators shifted, trying to catch the movement of his shoulders, the way his tail swayed for balance.

He came up behind her, close but not crowding, one paw touching her hip briefly to let her know where he was. Then he reached up, arm extending, back muscles visible even through the soft fabric of his shirt. The cabinet opened obediently.

“There you go,” he said, handing her the fondue set like it weighed nothing.

“My hero,” she said dryly, then couldn’t help the small, genuine up-tilt at the end. It was a scene they had performed a hundred times, without witnesses.

“Beautiful,” Leila said. “Again?”

They did it again. And again. The cabinet became an actor with one line. Miles’s paw on her hip became a mark. The fondue set might as well have been the Holy Grail.

On the fifth take, someone adjusted the camera slightly lower. The shot took in the whole of Miles’s back, the breadth of his shoulders, the way his shirt lifted just enough to show the curve of his lower back above his waistband when he stretched. It included Lillian’s body, solid and relaxed under his touch, the casual intimacy of space negotiated and shared.

“Got it,” the otter murmured, almost to himself.

That night, the production company cut together a teaser. Just a few seconds, for internal use, Leila said. Proof-of-concept, Leila said.

Two weeks later, edited and polished, that same clip leaked onto social media.

It appeared first as a compressed square on an anonymous account, captioned **“Get yourself a man who fixes the stupid cabinet”**. The sound was a little warped, but the image was clear: Lillian stretching, Miles rising behind her, the brief touch at her hip, the effortless reach, the fondue set delivered with a half-smile.

The comments multiplied.

*Who is this husband and where do I get one*  
*The way he just—*  
*THE BACK. MA’AM THE BACK.*  
*This is peak competence porn.*  
*I would marry that drawer.*

Someone looped the clip and set it to a breathy remix of an old Venus in Furs track. Someone else slowed it down and added sparkles. A third user annotated it with analysis of “domestic labour and desirability politics in 21st-century anthro households”.

Miles found out at the grocery store.

He was in the queue, checking his phone, when Brad sent a link with no context except **HAHAHAHAHAHA**. He clicked, watched himself on a tiny screen reach for the cabinet in endless repetition, and felt his ears flatten in slow horror.

By the time he reached the car, his paws were shaking. He sat in the driver’s seat, engine off, phone screen glowing a little too bright. The comments kept loading.

He knew about going viral in the abstract; he ran stores, he read marketing reports. He understood numbers. He did not understand what it felt like to have complete strangers discussing his back muscles and the way he said “there you go” like it was some kind of intimate confession.

He drove home carefully. He parked. He sat in the driveway for a full minute before going inside.

Lillian was at the table, laptop open, the teaser frozen on the screen. Her glasses were pushed up onto her head; her hair, loose, fell around her face in soft waves. She looked up as he came in.

“So,” she said softly. “We appear to have become… content.”

He let out a breath that turned into a laugh halfway through. “I didn’t even fix anything,” he said. “The cabinet was never actually broken.”

“Apparently that doesn’t matter,” she replied. “Apparently you reaching is a cultural event.”

He dropped into the chair opposite her, body heavy with the kind of tired that had nothing to do with his shift.

“I don’t like it,” he said. The words were simple, but they carried years of choosing to stand at the back of photographs, of letting Lillian and Sarah hold the spotlight while he handled logistics.

Lillian turned the laptop so he could see the comment count. It was a number so large it might as well have been fictional.

“I know,” she said. “I’m… angry. And also, a very small, petty part of me is pleased that they’ve finally noticed what I’ve known for twenty years.”

He snorted, ears flicking. “Which part?”

“That my husband is extremely competent,” she said. “And that watching you be kind to gravity is strangely compelling.”

He covered his eyes with one paw. “Lill.”

She reached across the table, resting her other paw over his wrist.

“We can push back,” she said. “We can ask them to take it down. We can threaten legal letters. We can also wait and see if the internet gets bored in forty-eight hours and moves on to a raccoon stealing crisps.”

He thought of the contract. Of the tidy clauses about footage usage. Of Leila’s apologetic text: *We didn’t post it, promise. We’re trying to track the source.*

“What do you want?” he asked, because that was always the centre: not the internet, not the company, but the woman across from him whose life was entangled with his in every cupboard and drawer.

Lillian looked at the frozen frame—at herself, reaching comfortably, at him behind her, steady and unposed in the most posed moment of his life.

“I want you not to hurt,” she said. “And I want us to keep control as much as we can.”

He nodded. “Then we set more boundaries. And we make soup.”

“Always soup,” she agreed.

Outside, unseen, a neighbour’s teenage daughter watched the same clip on her phone and smiled, something loosening in her chest at the sight of a badger woman who did not apologise, and a shepherd man whose tenderness was the point.

### Chapter 5 — Viral Without Consent

By the end of the week, Miles knew three things:

1. His back had been turned into a meme.  
2. The phrase “stupid cabinet husband” was trending in four languages.  
3. Everyone at his stores was pretending not to have seen it.

At the Loughton branch, the teenage cashiers were just a fraction too deferential. The florist kept offering him extra lilies. One of the security guards, a stoic bull terrier, nodded at him with something like respect when he walked past, which was unnerving enough to qualify as a workplace hazard.

“Morning, boss,” the bull terrier said. “Nice… reach.”

Miles, who had spent his entire career cultivating an aura of steady competence and invisibility, felt his ears heat. “Thank you,” he said, because what else was there?

In the staff room, someone had printed out a blurry screenshot of the cabinet clip and taped it to the noticeboard, right next to the rota. Someone else had drawn a little cartoon of a heroic shepherd lifting an entire supermarket aisle with one paw.

He took it down, quietly, and stuck it inside his locker instead. It felt less humiliating there, somehow. More like a weird family photo than a public spectacle.

Back home, the crew wanted to film “Miles reacting to the viral situation.”

“We think it could be a really powerful moment,” Leila said, eyes shining. “Speaking to consent, body image, domestic labour—”

“You’re not putting a ring light on my feelings,” Miles said.

Lillian, standing at the hob, stirred a pot and said, “We talk about it on our terms, or we don’t talk about it at all. You can film us cooking. You can film us tidying. You cannot film Miles having an existential crisis about his lumbar spine.”

Leila bit her lip, then nodded. “Understood.”

They compromised on a quieter scene.

That evening, cameras rolling at a respectful distance, Miles sat at the table with his phone face down in front of him. Lillian ladled soup into bowls, the steam rising in soft clouds. The house smelled of lentils and cumin and something citrusy she refused to identify until he ate it.

“Have you… read any of the comments?” she asked, settling into her chair with the familiar shuffle that let her belly rest comfortably against the table edge.

“Some,” he said.

“And?” she prompted.

He considered. The boom mic hung above them like a curious bat.

“Some of them are very…” he searched for a word that wasn’t *thirsty*, which felt like inviting the entire internet into their bedroom “... complimentary.”

“Complimentary how?” she asked, entirely too innocent.

“In terms of… carpentry appreciation,” he said.

She laughed, full-bodied, making the cutlery rattle.

“Well, you have spent twenty years reinforcing things,” she said. “Beds. Shelves. My self-esteem.”

Her tone shifted on the last one, softening.

“And some of them?” he went on. “Some of them are just… sweet. People saying they’ve never seen a couple like us on screen before. That it makes them feel… possible.”

He swallowed. His throat felt oddly tight.

“It’s weird,” he said. “Knowing my back is someone’s wallpaper. But if one person out there looks at you and thinks, ‘Oh. I can be loved like that,’ then… maybe it’s not entirely awful.”

Lillian reached across, resting her paw on his.

“You’re allowed to hate it,” she said. “Even if it helps someone. Those things don’t cancel each other out.”

He nodded. “I know.”

He did know. But he also knew what it had been like watching Lillian on grainy protest footage, how his own chest had felt when he’d seen her on stage for the first time, singing like the air owed her space. Representation had been a lifeline for him too, even if it had come wrapped in other people’s agendas.

“I just wish,” he said, “that my first big internet moment involved something I actually fixed. Like a collapsing bridge. Or a structurally unsound gazebo.”

“You fixed the cabinet,” she pointed out.

“The cabinet was never broken,” he said automatically.

“You fixed what it symbolised,” she said. “Men who won’t reach.”

There was a collective, almost inaudible intake of breath from the crew. Someone scribbled something on a notepad. Lillian shot them a look that said, *Do not put that on a mug*.

Later, alone in bed, microphones off and doors closed, Miles lay on his back, staring at the ceiling. Lillian turned on her side, fur rustling against the sheets. The mattress dipped and held; he had rebuilt the frame himself to make sure it supported them both without complaint.

“Does your back feel famous?” she asked in the dark.

“It feels like it needs a hot water bottle,” he said.

She chuckled, then grew quiet.

“We can still stop,” she said. “The show. We can pull the plug. Go back to being just us and the stupid cabinet.”

He thought of the Academy’s new scholarship fund, already partly funded by the show’s advance. He thought of Sarah’s texts—*Mum looks amazing. Miles, you look like a hero in a DIY commercial*—and the half-joking, half-serious *I’m going to sculpt this*.

“Let’s see it through,” he said. “But we keep our line. And when we’re done, we get a holiday somewhere with no internet reception.”

“We’ll have to take Sarah,” Lillian said. “She’ll want to document it in charcoal.”

“She can draw the trees,” he said. “The trees are allowed to be famous.”

Lillian shifted closer, resting her head on his chest. He wrapped his arms around her, the bed frame creaking in the comfortable way he had designed.

If the world insisted on watching him reach, he thought, then he would make sure that every reach protected something worth the attention.

### Chapter 6 — The Run

The jog was genuinely his idea.

He had been running, in one form or another, since Trinity—first to clear his head after practice, then to manage the low-level hum of managerial stress. He liked the rhythm of it, the way his paws hit pavement in a steady pattern, the way his lungs negotiated with his legs. Running was a system he understood: input, output, incremental improvement.

“We could film a little bit of it,” Leila said, notebook poised. “Just to show your routine. Balance the cabinet narrative.”

“The cabinet narrative,” he repeated.

“You know,” she said, cheeks colouring, “that clip. We don’t want the audience to fixate on just… that. We want to show… the whole of you.”

He had a strange, brief mental image of the internet being served slices of himself like a cake. He shook it off.

“Fine,” he said. “But you get one route, one time. No drones. And if I say stop, we stop.”

They mic’d him at the front door—clip on his collar, small pack at his waist. The morning was cool and grey, the air smelling of wet leaves and distant toast from neighbouring kitchens. Lillian leaned against the doorframe, watching.

“Don’t let them make you inspirational without your consent,” she said.

“I’ll be aggressively uninspiring,” he promised.

He set off down the familiar route: out of the estate, past the little patch of woodland, through the quiet residential streets where people walked dogs and pushed prams. The camera operator trotted ahead and behind like an overly keen spaniel, trying to keep him framed while not tripping over kerbs.

At first, Miles forgot about the microphone. His body fell into its usual pattern—shoulders loose, arms swinging, breaths counted in fours. The pavement passed under him in a sequence of textures: smooth tarmac, cracked patches, the slight crunch of grit at the edges. His muscles warmed. His thoughts moved into the pleasant, organised hum he only really achieved when in motion or at his workbench.

He thought about stock rotations, about the Academy fundraising gala, about whether the shed roof needed resealing before winter. He thought about the show, about the odd double consciousness of living his life and watching it be recorded. He thought about Lillian’s hand on his arm that morning, steady and grounding.

He did not think about the potential edit of this run until he passed a bus stop and saw himself reflected in the glass—a big German Shepherd man, chest heaving, fur dampening slightly at the neck, a little microphone blinking on his collar. He imagined text overlaid: *He just keeps going*.

He slowed to a walk.

“Everything okay?” the camera operator puffed, catching up.

“Fine,” Miles said. “Just… adjusting pace.”

The crew cut together six minutes of footage into a two-minute segment. Set to gentle piano and the distant sound of his footsteps, the show narrated his work ethic, his discipline, his quiet care for his body. They intercut shots of him running with clips of him in the kitchen, in the stores, in the garden. The voiceover said words like *resilience* and *grounding presence*.

Online, the comments were kinder this time. Less thirsty, more earnest.

*My dad is like this. He never stops. I’m crying.*  
*This is the opposite of toxic fitness content and I’m here for it.*  
*Protect this man at all costs.*

Miles watched the finished segment on the sofa, Lillian’s paw warm against his leg. He felt oddly… split. The man on screen looked like him, moved like him, spoke like him in short interviews about “needing to keep moving so my head doesn’t turn into a storage room with no labels”. But he also looked like an argument the show was making, and he had not written the thesis.

“Do you hate it?” Lillian asked.

He thought.

“Less than the cabinet,” he said. “More than the bit where you made the board squirm with one eyebrow.”

She smiled. “That was good television.”

“That was justice,” he said.

He pressed pause. The screen froze on him mid-step, one paw just about to touch the ground.

“It’s strange,” he said slowly. “Watching myself run. I’ve always thought of it as… maintenance. Not… narrative.”

“Everything is narrative to them,” she said gently. “To us, it’s just Tuesday.”

He leaned his head back against the sofa, eyes on the static image of his own body in motion.

“It’s not… wrong,” he said. “They didn’t lie. They just… organised.”

“We can organise back,” Lillian said. “We can make space for the bits that don’t fit their arc.”

He turned his head to look at her.

“Like what?” he asked.

“Like the days you don’t run,” she said. “The days you stay in bed until ten because your knees hurt and the world can cope without you for ninety extra minutes. The nights you fall asleep in the shed and I have to come out and turn off the lamp.”

He smiled, small and real.

“Those are not very televisual,” he said.

“They’re my favourite episodes,” she replied.

The cameras were not there for that conversation. That one belonged only to them.

### Chapter 7 — Producer Notes

The notes started as emails.

*Could Lillian maybe talk a bit more about her early struggles with body image?*  
*We’d love to explore Miles’ vulnerability around being in the spotlight.*  
*Is there a way to heighten the sense of stakes in the kitchen sequences?*

“What does that even mean?” Miles asked, squinting at the screen. “Stakes in the kitchen? Are we cooking with explosives now?”

“They want arguments,” Lillian said. “Tension. Raised voices. Maybe a plate thrown, if we’re very lucky.”

He looked around at their kitchen—the sturdy table, the well-worn pans, the cupboard of mismatched crockery collected from charity shops and student gifts. The idea of throwing any of it made his fur itch.

“We don’t do that,” he said.

“I know,” she replied. “That’s why they want it. It looks good when something breaks.”

She wrote back polite refusals at first.

*We’re not comfortable manufacturing conflict.*  
*Our tensions are low-volume and mostly about whether the laundry basket constitutes a valid storage solution.*

The notes became more insistent.

*Viewers respond to emotional honesty.*  
*We want to show that even strong couples have cracks.*  
*Could we revisit the miscarriage conversation? It’s powerful.*

Lillian’s claws paused over the keyboard.

She had told them about 2013 in a controlled, careful interview, after a long discussion with Miles and Sarah and the therapist they’d all agreed should be on speed dial during production. She had spoken about not knowing she was pregnant until she wasn’t, about the quiet grief that had settled like dust in the corners of their lives. She had spoken in the past tense, on purpose.

Now, the notes wanted… more. Close-ups. Intercut shots. Music.

“No,” she said aloud. “Absolutely not.”

Miles, across the table, looked up from his spreadsheets. “What’s ‘absolutely not’?”

She turned the laptop so he could see.

His ears went back as he read. The fur along his neck lifted slightly. He breathed in once, deeply, then out.

“We said that was one conversation,” he said. “On our terms.”

“We did,” she said. “And now they want to edit it into a tragic arc between the cabinet and your jog.”

He reached out and closed the laptop gently.

“Then we remind them,” he said.

They did. They reminded Leila in a meeting, and then reminded Leila’s boss on a video call, and then reminded Legal by forwarding annotated sections of the contract. Lillian’s voice stayed calm; Miles’s stayed low. He felt like a structural beam, holding.

“Our pain is not your B-plot,” Lillian said finally, hands flat on the table. “You can keep the interview you already have, as agreed. You cannot mine it for more. If you push, we walk.”

There was a silence on the line that felt like the pause before a bridge either held or cracked.

“We hear you,” the executive producer said at last. “We… lost sight of the line. We’ll back off.”

Leila looked genuinely mortified. “I’m so sorry,” she said afterwards, off-camera. “I should have pushed back for you.”

“It’s not your job to protect us,” Lillian said gently. “It’s ours. But you can help.”

From then on, the notes shifted, slightly. More questions about music. More curiosity about their systems, their routines. Less about tears.

It was exhausting, holding the line, but they held it together. At night, they lay in bed and debriefed like soldiers after a battle.

“How’s your structural integrity?” Lillian would ask.

“Minor hairline fractures,” Miles would say. “Patchable.”

They patched with soup. With quiet. With tiny rebellions, like unplugging lamps and refusing to answer questions in confessional booths if the prompts felt like traps.

Authenticity, apparently, required constant negotiation.

### Chapter 8 — Knife Skills

The knife skills sequence was the crew’s idea and Lillian’s little act of mischief.

“We want to showcase your competence in the kitchen,” Leila said. “It’s such a… sensual space.”

“Absolutely,” Lillian said, very demure. “We’ll do a segment on chopping.”

She made sure Miles was the one with the knife.

On camera, he looked very much like the internet’s favourite cabinet hero, only now he was wielding a chef’s knife with the same calm precision he applied to shelving units. The board was steady under his paws. The vegetables lined up obediently.

“I thought you wanted me to be less of a thirst trap,” he murmured as he diced peppers into perfect squares.

“I want you to be yourself,” she said. “If that accidentally causes a run on high-quality knives, that’s on capitalism, not us.”

She stood beside him, stirring a pot, narrating the recipe. The cameras kept drifting to his paws—the way he curled his fingers, the efficient motion of blade and wrist.

“Have you always cooked like this?” Leila asked, genuinely fascinated.

“I learned because Lillian was working late a lot,” he said. “And because I like knowing exactly how long things take. Boiling an egg is the closest thing we have to a controlled experiment at home.”

“He also prefers being the one holding sharp objects,” Lillian added. “It gives him a sense of control.”

“It gives me a sense that nobody’s going to accidentally slice a paw off while looking at their phone,” he corrected.

The finished sequence was oddly beautiful. The edit intercut close-ups of knife against board with shots of Lillian tasting, of steam curling around their faces, of their bodies moving in practiced choreography around each other in the small kitchen. There was a moment where he reached past her for the salt, hand hovering at her waist for balance, and she leaned back without looking, trusting his arc.

Online, someone wrote a thinkpiece titled **“Knife Skills and Consent: The Intimacy of Domestic Choreography”**. Lillian bookmarked it. Miles refused to read it but listened when she summarised the best lines.

“They say you treat the kitchen like a workshop,” she said. “That your care shows in the way you handle tools. That you move around me like I’m the project you’re never finished with.”

“That makes it sound like I’m sanding you,” he said.

“Emotionally,” she replied.

He shook his head, but his ears were pink.

The crew, emboldened by the positive response, suggested leaning into “the sensuality of competence.” Lillian told them they could film all the chopping they wanted, but the kissing stayed off-screen.

“Think of it as… paywalled content,” she said.

“We don’t have a paywall,” the producer protested.

“Exactly,” she said, and shut the conversation down with a smile.

### Chapter 9 — Confessionals

They had resisted confessionals for weeks.

“It’s a reality staple,” Leila had said, hands folded in pleading. “Just you, in a chair, talking directly to the audience. It gives context. Lets people into your inner world.”

“My inner world is full of half-finished arrangements and anxieties about my students’ posture,” Lillian said. “Nobody needs that on a Tuesday night.”

In the end, they agreed to one session each. For balance. For context. For the inevitable DVD extras.

They set up the confessional space in the front room, against a neutral wall. The crew fussed with cushions—none of their chairs looked “visually dynamic” enough—until Miles dragged in one of the sturdy kitchen chairs and declared it good enough.

“If it holds me, it’ll hold the truth,” he said.

Lillian went first. She sat, adjusted her dress, and stared directly into the camera.

“Hello,” she said. “I’m Lillian. I am here to talk about… whatever they ask, within reason.”

The producer behind the camera chuckled.

They asked about the show, about the cabinet, about music. She answered with care, choosing words like she chose notes. She spoke about representation and boundaries and the strange feeling of watching her life be edited into an arc.

When they asked about her body, she said, “It’s not a plot twist. It’s just my body.”

When they asked about Miles, she smiled.

“He’s the rhythm,” she said. “I’m the melody. The show keeps trying to make him the solo, but we’re a band.”

Later, it was Miles’s turn.

He sat in the same chair, which made a slightly different noise under his weight. He rested his paws on his knees, took one deep breath, and looked at the camera.

“I hate this,” he said.

The producer blinked. “Hate… what, exactly?”

“Being in this chair,” he said. “Talking to a lens. Knowing there’s a whole world behind it that has opinions about things I never thought would be up for voting. I am not… comfortable with any of this.”

There was a pause.

“You don’t have to—” the producer began.

“But,” Miles went on, “I’m here because Lillian said yes. And when she says yes to something, I check the load-bearing walls and see how we can make it safe. That’s my job. That’s… what I do.”

He looked down at his paws, flexing them once, then back at the camera.

“I don’t like being filmed in my own kitchen,” he said. “I don’t like people making comments about my back. I don’t like strangers deciding I’m some kind of standard for… whatever they’re projecting. But I like that some kid out there might see us and think, ‘Oh. I don’t have to shrink to be loved.’”

His voice stayed level, but his ears betrayed the strain.

“So I’m doing this like I do everything else,” he said. “One task at a time. One episode at a time. Making sure the structures hold. And then, when it’s over, I’ll take the cameras down in my head and put them in a neat labelled box.”

The crew was very quiet.

“Is there anything you… like about this process?” the producer asked, a little hesitant.

Miles thought for a long moment.

“The catering is decent,” he said. “And sometimes, when the cameras are here, we remember to sit and talk about things we’d normally just… absorb. That’s not nothing.”

The monologue, when it aired, became a minor cultural artefact. Clips of it circulated with titles like **“Finally, a man who doesn’t want to be on TV”** and **“Consent and competence in reality narratives.”**

People cried in comments. People argued about whether he was performatively humble. People wrote essays.

Miles did not read most of them. He had said what he needed to say. The rest belonged to the noise.

### Chapter 10 — The Line

The line arrived on a Tuesday, disguised as a suggestion.

They were mid-shoot, cameras in the kitchen, lights making the room a little too warm. Lillian was at the hob. Miles was at the sink, rinsing vegetables. The air smelled of garlic and steam and the faint ozone of plugged-in equipment.

Leila approached, tablet in hand, expression tentative.

“So,” she said, “we’ve been looking at the cuts, and the network feels like we’re missing… a turning point.”

“We turned on the lights,” Lillian said. “We signed the contract. We did a jog. How many turning points do they need?”

Leila winced. “They want… conflict. A moment where things get… heated. Not necessarily serious, just—”

“An argument,” Miles said, straightening.

“A… disagreement,” Leila hedged. “Something that shows you’re not perfect. That you have… edges.”

Lillian set down the spoon.

“Everyone has edges,” she said. “We just don’t throw them at each other.”

The director stepped in, sensing the moment.

“We’re not asking you to fake anything,” he said. “Just… maybe lean into something that’s already there. A little tension. You could, for example, have a conversation about how Miles feels about the cabinet clip. Maybe he’s upset you didn’t stop the crew. Maybe you’re frustrated he’s being so sensitive. That kind of thing.”

“That kind of thing has never happened,” Lillian said.

“Right,” the director said. “But it could have. We just need to… heighten reality a bit.”

Something snapped, very quietly, inside her. Not a dramatic break; more like the crack of a long-stressed beam finally saying *enough*.

She reached up and unclipped her microphone.

The movement was small but decisive. The kitchen felt suddenly larger without the little wire tethering her voice to their equipment.

“No,” she said. “We’re done.”

The crew froze. The boom mic hovered.

“Lillian,” Leila began, eyes wide. “We didn’t mean—”

Miles unclipped his microphone too. The soft click of plastic on fabric sounded louder than the hum of the lights.

“You asked us to be ourselves,” he said. “Ourselves do not stage fights for ratings.”

The director looked genuinely alarmed now. “Look, maybe we phrased it badly. We’re not trying to exploit you. We’re just trying to craft a narrative that—”

“The narrative is that we let you into our home,” Lillian said. Her voice was calm, but her fur bristled along her arms, every hair a small exclamation. “We showed you how we live. We showed you our students, our work, our stupid cabinet. You turned my husband into a meme. You poked at old wounds. And now you want us to pretend to hurt each other so your arc feels satisfying.”

She stepped around the island, placing herself between the crew and Miles, not because he needed protection but because she needed to feel her body in that position.

“We have a line,” she said. “You just crossed it.”

There was a moment where time seemed to thin. The humming appliances, the heat from the hob, the faint buzz of a fly against the window—all of it sharpened.

“We can course-correct,” Leila said, voice small. “We can cut that request. We can—”

“No,” Lillian said. “We can. We’re course-correcting now.”

She gestured to the door.

“Thank you for your time,” she said. “We’re ending the shoot. Effective immediately.”

Legal would later call it “invoking the termination clause.” In the kitchen, it was just a badger woman reclaiming her space.

The crew moved slowly at first, stunned. Then Leila, of all people, nodded and started packing up.

“I’m sorry,” she said, as she coiled cables. “We lost sight. I lost sight. You trusted us, and we… wanted more. That’s on us.”

“I know how production pressure feels,” Lillian said, not unkindly. “But it doesn’t trump our life.”

Miles carried out the heavier cases. He apologised to the coffee table when he bumped it, out of habit. The absurdity of that—him apologising to furniture while ending a TV show—almost made Lillian laugh.

When the last tripod left the house and the door closed, the silence was thick. The refrigerator hum sounded like applause.

Lillian leaned her back against the door and slid down until she was sitting on the floor. The cool wood against her spine felt like a blessing. Miles sat beside her, knees drawn up, paws hanging loosely over them.

“We just fired a television show,” he said, sounding dazed.

“We did,” she said. “On camera, even. Shame they can’t use it.”

He let out a breath that turned into a laugh.

“You okay?” he asked.

She thought of all the things they’d let in and all the things they’d kept back. She thought of the kids who had messaged the Academy, the parents who had written letters, the essays, the memes, the memes about the essays. She thought of the line, and the fact that they had seen it, together.

“I am furious,” she said. “And relieved. And hungry.”

He bumped his shoulder gently against hers.

“Soup?” he suggested.

“Soup,” she agreed.

They got up slowly, bodies protesting after sitting on the floor. The kitchen looked suddenly bigger again, as if it too had unclenched.

Lillian tied her apron. Miles opened the fridge. The cameras were gone. The angles were theirs again.

### Chapter 11 — Soup

They invited the crew back once, on their own terms.

Not with cameras. With spoons.

“It feels weird,” Miles said, ladling soup into the large pot. “Feeding people we just kicked out of our house.”

“We didn’t kick them out,” Lillian said. “We kicked their job out. They’re still people.”

He stirred, watching the lentils swirl.

“Is this… closure?” he asked.

“This is hospitality,” she said. “And maybe a tiny bit of closure with garlic.”

Leila arrived first, no clipboard, no lanyard. She looked smaller without her tools. The rest of the crew trickled in after her, hands awkward without equipment to hold.

The house smelled of broth and herbs and warm bread. The table was set with their mismatched bowls. Sarah had sent a quick charcoal sketch from Manchester of Lillian ladling soup into a camera, the lens wide open like a mouth; it sat propped against the fruit bowl, a silent witness.

“Thank you for inviting us,” Leila said, hovering in the doorway.

“Sit,” Lillian said. “Eat. Then we can talk.”

They did. The first few minutes were stiff. People complimented the soup too earnestly. Someone apologised to a chair. But food had its own logic. It warmed paws and tongues, slowing the defensive reflexes.

“We forgot,” one of the camera operators said eventually, staring into his bowl. “That this is your actual life. Not just… material.”

“You’re not the first,” Lillian said. “The Academy board forgets every time I’m in a meeting. The donors forget whenever they ask for more ‘relatable content.’ The internet forgets every time it makes a joke about my dress size. We’re used to reminding people.”

Miles broke a piece of bread in half, the crust crackling.

“But you listened when we reminded you,” he said. “That matters.”

Leila wiped her eyes discreetly.

“I went into this wanting to make… good television,” she said. “Important television. I thought if we showed your lives honestly, it would… help. I didn’t realise how easily ‘honest’ slips into ‘hungry’.”

“It did help,” Lillian said. “Some of it. The cabinet, despite everything, helped. So did the jog. And the knife skills. People wrote to us. My students feel seen. But we get to decide when enough is enough.”

“We’ll finish the series with what we have,” Leila said. “No manufactured fights. No extra grief. Just… what you gave us. If you’ll still let us air it.”

Lillian and Miles exchanged a look.

“With the edits we discussed,” Lillian said. “And with knowing that the last episode ends where we chose to end it.”

Leila nodded. “Deal.”

They shook on it over the soup pot.

Later, when the crew had left and the dishes were stacked in the sink, Miles stood at the window, looking out at the dark garden. The shed was a solid shape against the night. The streetlights painted the hydrangeas in flat, sodium colour.

“Do you ever think about what would have happened if we’d just said no at the start?” he asked.

Lillian dried a bowl, the towel rasping softly against ceramic.

“Sometimes,” she said. “We’d have had fewer emails. You’d still just be my stupid cabinet husband, not everyone’s. Some kid in somewhere would have to wait longer to see someone like us on TV. My students would have one less example to point at when they argue with their parents about what’s possible.”

She set the bowl down, came to stand beside him.

“Would we be happier?” he asked.

She considered.

“We’d be less tired,” she said. “But I don’t know about happier. This was… awful. And useful. It showed us where our boundaries really are, not just where we thought they were. It made us say things out loud we’d been carrying quietly.”

He nodded, leaning his shoulder against hers.

“And now?” he asked.

“Now we finish the washing up,” she said. “And then we go to bed. Without cameras.”

“My favourite part,” he said.

They turned away from the window, leaving the garden to its own small, unfilmed dramas.

### Chapter 12 — Aftermath

The show aired over twelve weeks.

They didn’t have watch parties. They didn’t livetweet. They didn’t sit with bated breath waiting for their faces to appear between adverts for laundry detergent and discount mattresses.

Sometimes, they watched an episode a few days late, together on the sofa, remote in Miles’s paw, ready to pause, rewind, or fast-forward. Sometimes they let friends send them clips. Sometimes they ignored it entirely and went to the pub.

The world, predictably, had opinions.

There were gifs and memes and thinkpieces and fan art. Someone made a lovingly detailed 3D model of their kitchen in a simulation game and posted a downloadable file. Someone else composed a song called “Stupid Cabinet Husband” that was surprisingly tender.

There were also cruel comments, as there always are. Jokes about Lillian’s size, about Miles’s tail, about their age, about the way they moved. They did not read most of those.

“We can’t police the whole internet,” Miles said, one evening when her thumb hovered over the scroll bar. “We can only curate the bits we let into our heads.”

She closed the app and opened a student’s email instead—a shy badger teen who had written five paragraphs about how seeing Lillian on screen had made her wear a dress to school without a cardigan for the first time.

Sarah processed the show in her own way. She watched every episode from her flat in Manchester and sent them sketches: Lillian at the piano, Miles mid-reach, both of them laughing at the table. She sculpted a small, abstract piece titled “Angles” that looked like interlocking cupboards.

“You look like yourselves,” she said on a video call. “Just… with a soundtrack.”

“We are ourselves,” Lillian said. “Just… edited.”

When the finale aired, they didn’t watch it live. They were in the garden, Miles fixing a loose fence panel, Lillian repotting a plant that had been pretending to thrive by the kitchen window but clearly wanted more air.

The evening was cool. The air smelled of soil and cut grass. The shed door stood open, light spilling out.

“Somewhere out there, people are having opinions about our soup,” Lillian said, pushing soil around the roots.

“As long as they don’t ask for the recipe,” Miles replied. “It’s not standardised. I can’t cope with people trying to replicate it precisely.”

Her phone buzzed with messages—friends, students, Leila, the network. She left it on the garden table.

“We did what we could,” she said. “We held the line as best we knew how.”

“We’ll probably see more of ourselves than we’d like for a while,” he said. “In gifs. In articles. In strangers’ faces at the shop.”

“We’ll see more of us in our students,” she said. “In whatever they make after this.”

He looked up at the sky, where the first stars were starting to push through the London haze.

“Do you regret it?” he asked.

She thought for a long moment, paws deep in earth.

“I regret parts,” she said. “I regret that you got turned into an object when you’ve worked so hard to be a person. I regret that it took a TV show for some people to believe fat women can be loved calmly. I regret reading the comments after eleven at night.”

She patted the soil down, firm but not compacted.

“But I don’t regret drawing the line,” she added. “Or showing people that it’s possible to do that on camera.”

He smiled, a slow, tired, satisfied smile.

“We belong where we go,” he said, almost to himself.

It was one of his lines, from years ago, outside a swimming pool. It fit here too, in a garden momentarily free of lenses.

“We do,” she agreed.

They went inside when it got cold. The kitchen greeted them with its familiar clutter, its scuffed floor, its stupid cabinet. The microphones were gone. The lamps were normal. The only red light belonged to the kettle.

Lillian put the kettle on. Miles set out two mugs. The motions were the same as they had been on the first day of filming, and a thousand mornings before that, and all the mornings still to come.

Outside, the world kept watching, talking, making more content. Inside, in the small, warm circle of their house, a badger and a shepherd poured tea, adjusted chairs, and carried on being themselves—off-camera, unfiltered, and very much, deliberately, alive.
