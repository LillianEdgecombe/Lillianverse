# Furlandia: A Tale of Curses, Quests, and Questionable Life Choices

*A Lillianverse Fantasy Story*

---

## Chapter 1: The Bard in the Burrow

It was the sort of morning that smelled of damp thyme, ink, and resignation—three things that, when combined, suggested that the universe was about to become significantly more complicated than anyone had bargained for.

The hill-house squatted beneath a crooked rowan like a particularly stubborn mushroom that had decided to take up residence and charge rent. Its mossy roof twitched with the snores of dozing squirrels, who had long ago established a complex political hierarchy based entirely on acorn hoarding and the ability to look disapproving while upside down.

Inside, in a quilt-drowned armchair framed by towering shelves of sheet music that threatened to achieve sentience through sheer accumulated resentment, Lillian Edgecombe—retired bard, sometime legend, full-time grumbler—attempted to ignore the outside world entirely. This was, she had discovered, considerably more difficult than it sounded, particularly when the outside world had developed opinions about her retirement and wasn't shy about expressing them.

She wore a house-robe made from the flag of a nation that no longer existed, partly because she'd liked the color scheme and partly because she'd been personally responsible for its dissolution during what historians would later call "The Incident with the Singing Teapot." Her belly draped comfortably over her lap—a testament to years of successful retirement and the discovery that lemon scones were, in fact, a perfectly acceptable breakfast, lunch, and dinner when consumed in sufficient quantities.

Her badger stripes had greyed at the edges, but her eyes still held the sharpness of someone who'd once sung a god to sleep and then charged it for the privilege.

"Ballad of the Basilisk Queen," she muttered, flicking through yellowed parchment with the sort of careful disdain usually reserved for tax assessments and love letters from ex-lovers. "Nope. Too much hissing. Ode to My Ex's Missing Hand? Mm. Could use a rewrite. Possibly a sequel."

Outside, something splashed with the sort of deliberate wetness that suggested it was either very large or very determined to be noticed.

She ignored it with the practiced ease of someone who had spent decades perfecting the art of selective hearing.

Then came a rhythmic thump. Thump-splat. Thump-splat. Followed by a baritone croak that sounded suspiciously rehearsed, as if someone had been practicing their dramatic entrance in front of a mirror made of pond scum.

She rose with a grunt that contained within it the entire history of middle age, shuffled to the round window, and squinted through glass that hadn't been cleaned since the last time she'd cared about appearances—which was to say, sometime during the previous century.

Down the crooked stone path hopped a parade of enormous, velvet-caped toads—six of them, bearing a golden scroll on a crimson pillow like the world's most amphibious diplomatic mission. One wore a tiny crown that kept slipping over one bulbous eye. Another had a monocle that served no apparent purpose except to make it look insufferably intellectual. The smallest dragged a ceremonial bell that occasionally rang of its own volition, as if it had developed strong opinions about punctuality.

"Oh gods," Lillian sighed, recognizing the particular species of bureaucratic nightmare that was about to unfold. "Diplomatic amphibians. Again."

The last time she'd dealt with diplomatic amphibians, she'd ended up accidentally married to a frog prince for three days before the paperwork could be sorted out. The divorce had been amicable, but the prenuptial agreement had been written in a language that existed only on Tuesdays, which had made things unnecessarily complicated.

By the time she opened the door—reluctantly, and with a butter knife in one paw because experience had taught her that ceremonial visits often required either spreading jam or defending oneself, and it was best to be prepared for both—they'd arranged themselves in a triangle formation and were mid-chorus of something dreadful in F sharp that sounded like a funeral dirge composed by someone who'd never actually attended a funeral but had strong opinions about what they should sound like.

"Miss Edgecombe of the Thousand Verses!" croaked the lead toad, whose voice gurgled like a clogged fountain trying to achieve enlightenment. "By order of Her Ever-Rumbling Majesty, Queen Amphibia the Thirty-Ninth, we summon thee to court! The Curse of the Withering Voice has returned, and thou art the only bard left who knows the Forbidden Verses!"

Lillian blinked slowly, the way one blinks when presented with information that is simultaneously completely expected and utterly unwelcome. "The curse of the what-now?"

"The Withering Voice, O Honoured One!" The toads spoke in unison, which was either very impressive or very disturbing, depending on one's feelings about amphibian choreography.

"Is that the one that makes everyone sound like boiled turnips, or the one that turns all music into tax law?"

"A silence that devours all song!" the toads cried with the sort of dramatic fervor usually reserved for amateur theatrical productions and religious conversions.

She scratched her chin, where a few grey whiskers had taken up residence like tiny, judgmental hermits. "And you lot think I remember the verses?"

The scroll unfurled with a puff of cinnamon-scented dust that made her sneeze in a way that was both dignified and slightly explosive. At the bottom, written in shimmering ink and barely legible over a spilled wine stain that looked suspiciously like the outline of a small country, was her name. Twice. Underlined. With what appeared to be tiny hearts dotting the i's, which was either very sweet or very ominous.

"I'm retired," she said flatly, with the sort of finality usually reserved for death certificates and restaurant reviews. "That means no more curses, no more quests, and no more being hunted by choir-eating liches. Kindly hop back to Her Amphibiousness and tell her the Bard of the Bloody Ballads is out of service. Permanently. With prejudice."

The toads exchanged glances that somehow managed to convey entire conversations about duty, destiny, and the particular sort of stubbornness that came with being a retired legend who just wanted to eat scones in peace.

The one with the monocle gave a polite cough that sounded like a small pond clearing its throat. "We were told you might say that."

The sky above her burrow cracked.

Not thunder. Something deeper. Something like old paper being torn by invisible hands, or the last note of a song ending too soon because the singer had suddenly remembered they'd left the kettle on. The clouds pulsed violet-black, the color of old bruises and fresh regrets. Birds froze mid-flight, hanging in the air like feathered question marks.

Lillian stared for a moment, watching reality develop what appeared to be a minor case of existential crisis. Then, sighing with the weary inevitability of someone accepting a bill they knew would bankrupt them spiritually as well as financially, she stepped inside, grabbed her travel satchel, and stuffed it with lemon scones, ink, a spare pair of sensible undergarments, and a short sword she hadn't named yet but was considering calling "Regret."

As she shut the door, she muttered, "Buggering bastards. I just repainted."

The toads looked pleased in the way that toads could look pleased, which was to say, slightly less like they were contemplating eating something unpleasant.

## Chapter 2: Sir Miles of Shepherdshire

The tavern was called The Limping Griffin, though the only griffin in sight was the taxidermied horror above the bar, its wing awkwardly nailed to the ceiling and its beak stuffed with old socks in what the proprietor optimistically called "artistic interpretation." It was the sort of establishment where the ale tasted like regret mixed with false hope, the floor stuck to your boots with a combination of spilled drinks and broken dreams, and the locals glared at you for blinking too fast, breathing too loud, or existing in their general vicinity.

In other words, it was exactly the sort of place where a knight might end up when his life had taken several wrong turns and then decided to take up permanent residence in the ditch.

In the center of the room stood a ring of overturned tables, broken stools, and badly repressed shame. And in that ring, stripped to his padded tunic and clanking unevenly in half-armor that had seen better decades, was Sir Miles of Shepherdshire.

He had been, once upon a time, the sort of knight that songs were written about. Now he was the sort of knight that drinking songs were written about, and not the flattering kind.

"Come on, then!" he barked, brandishing a bent longsword like it was a divine relic instead of a piece of metal that had clearly given up on life sometime around the same time he had. "You think you've got the stones to beat me, milk-boy?"

His opponent—a skinny youth in leather breeches and a homemade crest featuring what appeared to be a duck holding a sausage, which raised several questions about heraldic standards in the modern age—trembled visibly. He swung once, missed entirely, and tripped over his own swordbelt with the sort of spectacular incompetence that suggested he'd been practicing.

The crowd booed with the enthusiasm of people who had paid good money to see violence and were being presented with what appeared to be interpretive dance performed by someone who'd never seen dancing before.

Coins clinked as bets were hastily revised downward. Somewhere in the back, a lute string snapped in what might have been protest or might have been mercy.

Miles staggered theatrically, let out a grunt that contained within it the entire history of his disappointments, and collapsed backward into a wine barrel with a splash that sent cheap red wine cascading across the floor like the tears of a particularly dramatic god.

Applause. Scattered, grudging, but applause nonetheless.

He rolled out, covered in syrupy red liquid that made him look like he'd been involved in either a massacre or a very unfortunate cooking accident, and offered a dripping bow that somehow managed to be both dignified and completely ridiculous.

"And that, my fine swine," he announced to the crowd, "is how a knight defends his honor!"

"Oi, your honor left on a barge ten years ago!" someone jeered from the back, where the shadows were thick enough to hide both cowardice and truth.

"Tell her I still fancy her, then!" Miles shot back, which got a laugh that was only slightly cruel.

The crowd laughed, coins exchanged hands with the sort of resigned efficiency that suggested this was a regular occurrence, and the duck-crest lad looked embarrassed to still be standing, as if he'd somehow failed at failing.

From the doorway, cloaked in traveling dust and mild disapproval, Lillian watched the spectacle unfold with the sort of expression usually reserved for watching someone try to eat soup with a fork. She waited until Miles slipped while trying to shake the barrel juice out of his ears, then clapped—slowly, deliberately, with the sort of rhythm that suggested she was keeping time to a funeral march.

"Well," she said, her voice cutting through the tavern noise like a blade through butter, "chivalry isn't dead, but I see it's had a stroke."

Miles froze mid-drip, wine still cascading from his whiskers like the world's least appetizing waterfall. Then slowly, very slowly, he turned.

Even through his ale-slicked fur, even under the ridiculous helmet now slung uselessly at his belt like a metal hat that had given up on being useful, she recognized the old stubborn glint in his eyes. Older, wearier, buried under layers of disappointment and cheap wine, but still there. Still him.

"Edgecombe," he said, his voice hoarse with surprise and something that might have been hope if hope had been left out in the rain for several years. "I thought you were—"

"Dead? Retired? Teaching madrigals to geese?" She stepped into the tavern proper, ignoring the way conversations died around her like flowers in winter. "I tried all three. Retirement stuck. Mostly."

"You've put on weight."

"So have you. Except yours seems to be shame-shaped."

A beat. The tavern watched with the sort of interest usually reserved for public executions and really good street theater. The duck-crest lad began to edge away, apparently deciding that discretion was the better part of not getting caught in whatever was about to happen.

Miles rubbed the back of his neck, leaving a streak of wine that looked like war paint applied by someone who'd never seen a war. "What do you want?"

"A sword. Preferably one that isn't shaped like a question mark. And someone idiot enough to follow me across the continent on a cursed quest that will probably end with us both dead, dishonored, or transformed into something unpleasant."

"That so?"

"It is."

"You want me to help you save the world?"

Lillian tilted her head, considering. "I want you to hit things while I do the actual work. You're still good at that, right? The hitting things part, I mean. The thinking part was always optional."

He snorted, a sound that contained within it the ghost of the man he'd once been. "Depends. What's the pay?"

"Adventure. Potential death. Old wounds reopened both literally and metaphorically. Maybe a footnote in a bard's tale if you don't die too stupidly."

Miles paused, wiping his face with a rag that was mostly fungus and optimism. The tavern held its breath, sensing that something was shifting, that the evening's entertainment was about to become significantly more interesting or significantly more dangerous.

Then he sighed, the sort of sigh that contained within it the weight of years and the lightness of sudden possibility. "When do we leave?"

She tossed him a crusty heel of lemon scone, which he caught with reflexes that suggested the knight was still in there somewhere, buried under the disappointment and wine stains.

"Now."

## Chapter 3: A Map of Teeth and Song

They met the cartographer in the belly of a half-collapsed cathedral, its stained glass bleeding rain onto moldy pews like the tears of saints who'd given up on humanity and taken up interior decorating instead. What remained of the ceiling loomed high above like a ribcage picked clean by time and poor maintenance. The floor pulsed faintly with something that was either very old magic or very new plumbing problems.

In the center, atop a pedestal made from fossilized harp bones that hummed occasionally in B-flat minor, sprawled the Map.

It wasn't parchment. Not exactly. It was stitched from monster hides—shimmering scales that caught light like trapped rainbows, wiry fur that moved when no one was looking, coarse leather that bore the scars of battles fought in places that no longer had names. And teeth. So many teeth, embedded in the surface like tiny white tombstones marking the graves of geography. Some still wiggled when the wind changed direction.

The Map breathed.

"Ah," Lillian muttered, pinching her nose against the smell, which was like a butcher's nightmare crossed with a choirboy's fear and seasoned with just a hint of existential dread. "Smells like Tuesday morning in a morgue."

Sir Miles stepped cautiously closer, his newly acquired sword—a serviceable blade he'd liberated from the tavern's wall display along with a promise to pay for it eventually—held at the ready. "It's looking at me."

"It has no eyes."

"It's still looking at me. I can feel it judging my life choices."

The Map twitched, as if it had heard and agreed with his assessment. Then it opened its mouth—a gnarled slit along its equator that revealed rows of cartographic teeth—and sang.

Not a melody. Not exactly. A sound. Low and wet, like a thousand gargling monks arguing in a forgotten language about the proper pronunciation of despair. A few words emerged like barnacles clinging to a whale's belly:

*"Verse the First lies where silence hungers—  
Hushwood deep, where bards go under.  
Second slumbers in the swamp's old spite—  
Marshes Dire, where day fears night.  
Third in spires of stormglass born—  
Varn's last breath, where gods are torn."*

Then the Map belched, a sound like reality having indigestion. A small fang clattered to the floor and began trying to crawl away.

Miles blinked, the sort of slow blink that suggested his brain was trying to process information it wasn't entirely equipped to handle. "Did that... make sense to you?"

Lillian was quiet. Not in her usual, sardonic way—but still, statue-still, the way she got when she was remembering things she'd tried very hard to forget. Her paws rested lightly on the pedestal, claws twitching faintly over the old hide as if she were reading braille written in nightmares.

"I remember this voice," she said softly, her words barely audible over the cathedral's ambient dripping. "From when I was barely a songling. The verse-maps of the ancient orders. Thought they were gone. Thought they'd been destroyed in the Singing Wars."

"You said you were retired," Miles muttered, but there was no accusation in it, just the weary recognition of someone who'd heard this song before.

"I was. I am. I..." She trailed off, staring at the Map as it shifted and writhed, forming new continents from old scars. "This isn't just about a curse, is it? This is about the old magic. The deep songs. The ones that hold the world together."

"So why're we chasing cursed poetry through places with names like Stormglass and Hushwood instead of letting someone else handle it? Someone younger. Someone with fewer regrets and better knees."

She exhaled, a sound like a weight lifting and another one landing in its place. "Because no one else remembers how to hear it. The old songs, the deep magic—it's all dying. Has been for years. And when it's gone..." She gestured at the Map, which had begun to hum something that sounded like a lullaby written for the end of the world. "When it's gone, everything else goes with it."

The Map sang again, this time quieter—a lullaby fragment, a funeral hum, a love song written in a language that predated words. Its hide shifted, forming a rough outline of their journey. Three glowing points flared like bruises on stretched skin, pulsing with the rhythm of a heart that had seen too much and loved too little.

Miles leaned in, squinting at the cartographic nightmare. "We're starting here," he said, pointing to a swollen node labeled in ancient script that hurt to look at directly: *Skarth's Hollow*. "That's... a week's ride on nothing roads through country that doesn't want to be traveled."

"Four days if we cut through the hill-grave tunnels."

"Those are haunted."

"Only by regret. And possibly the ghost of a tax collector, but he's mostly harmless."

Miles chuckled, a sound that hadn't been used much in recent years and creaked slightly from disuse. "Well then. Shall we get moving, Lady Edgecombe?"

She hesitated, her paw hovering over the Map's surface. When she spoke, her voice was smaller than he'd ever heard it. "I'm not who I was. This isn't just about monsters and curses and the usual nonsense. This... this kind of curse? It eats memory. It forgets you. And I'm barely holding onto myself as is."

For a moment, Miles didn't answer. Then, gently, he reached into his satchel and pulled out a broken tin flute, its surface tarnished and dented but still recognizable.

"Found this," he said quietly. "You threw it at my head once. During that argument about the dragon's hoard and who got to keep the singing sword. I kept it."

She stared at the flute, her eyes wide with something that might have been surprise or might have been the beginning of tears. "You kept it. All these years."

"Seemed important. Didn't know why at the time."

"And now?"

He smiled, the first real smile she'd seen from him since she'd walked into that tavern. "Now I think maybe I was saving it for when you needed to remember who you were."

She took the flute with trembling paws, turning it over in her hands like a prayer made manifest. "Alright," she said finally, her voice stronger now, more like the bard who'd once sung down the walls of Jericho's less famous but equally stubborn cousin. "Let's go make the world remember us."

The Map hissed approvingly, its teeth chattering like applause.

Far above, the cathedral's broken dome flickered—not with lightning, but with something older, watching, waiting to see if the old songs still had power in a world that had forgotten how to listen.

## Chapter 4: The Road to Skarth's Hollow

The road to Skarth's Hollow was the sort of road that existed primarily as a suggestion, a vague notion that someone, somewhere, had once thought it might be nice to have a way to get from one place to another without having to invent teleportation or develop a close personal relationship with a dragon.

It wound through countryside that looked like it had been designed by someone with strong opinions about the aesthetic value of despair. Twisted trees reached toward a sky the color of old pewter, their branches forming shapes that suggested either ancient runes or the sort of graffiti that gets carved into tavern tables by people with more ale than sense. The grass was the particular shade of green that indicated it was probably edible but definitely wouldn't taste good.

Lillian and Miles walked in companionable silence, the sort of silence that develops between people who have shared enough history to fill a small library and enough regrets to stock a moderately sized museum. Their footsteps crunched on gravel that seemed to whisper complaints about the weather, the state of the world, and the general decline of proper road maintenance.

"So," Miles said eventually, because silence, while companionable, could only last so long before someone felt compelled to fill it with words. "The Curse of the Withering Voice. Want to tell me what we're actually dealing with here, or are we going with the traditional approach of stumbling blindly into mortal peril and hoping for the best?"

Lillian adjusted her pack, which contained, among other things, three more lemon scones, a spare set of strings for the flute Miles had returned to her, and a small notebook in which she'd been attempting to write down everything she could remember about the old songs before they slipped away entirely.

"The Withering Voice," she said, "is what happens when the deep magic starts to forget itself. It's not just a curse—it's a symptom. Like a fever, but for reality."

"That's... reassuring."

"It starts small. A song here, a melody there. Bards find they can't remember the words to ballads they've sung a thousand times. Musicians discover their instruments won't hold a tune. Gradually, it spreads. Music becomes noise. Poetry becomes gibberish. Eventually, even speech itself begins to fail."

Miles considered this as they crested a small hill that offered a view of the valley below, where Skarth's Hollow nestled like a secret someone had tried to bury and failed. "And then?"

"And then silence. Complete, absolute silence. Not just the absence of sound, but the absence of the possibility of sound. The world forgets how to sing, and when the world forgets how to sing..." She trailed off, staring at the hollow below.

"It dies?"

"Worse. It becomes boring."

The hollow itself was a collection of buildings that seemed to have been arranged by someone with a deep appreciation for the aesthetic possibilities of mild depression. Thatched roofs sagged like disappointed eyebrows. Windows stared out at the world with the sort of blank expression usually reserved for people who've just been told they owe taxes on their taxes. Smoke rose from chimneys in thin, apologetic wisps.

"Cheerful place," Miles observed.

"It used to be different. Skarth's Hollow was famous for its music festivals. Bards would come from across the continent to compete in the Song Wars. The winner got a golden lyre and the right to compose the official ballad of the harvest season."

"And now?"

"Now it's the sort of place where people go to forget they ever had dreams."

They descended into the hollow as the sun began its reluctant journey toward the horizon, painting the sky in shades of resignation and mild regret. The streets were empty except for a few chickens who looked like they were contemplating existential questions and a dog that appeared to be having a philosophical crisis about the nature of bones.

The inn was called The Silent Lamb, which would have been ominous if it hadn't been so obviously trying too hard. The sign creaked in the wind with a sound like someone clearing their throat apologetically. Inside, the common room was filled with the sort of aggressive quiet that suggested people were working very hard at not making noise.

The innkeeper was a thin man with the sort of mustache that indicated he'd once had ambitions but had settled for facial hair instead. He looked up as they entered, his expression cycling through surprise, suspicion, and resignation before settling on the sort of professional politeness that suggested he'd given up on life but was still committed to customer service.

"Travelers," he said, his voice barely above a whisper. "We don't get many travelers anymore."

"Can't imagine why," Miles muttered, looking around at the common room, where a handful of patrons sat in silence, staring into their ale as if it might contain answers to questions they'd forgotten how to ask.

"Two rooms," Lillian said quietly. "And whatever you have for dinner that doesn't require singing for."

The innkeeper's face went through several expressions before settling on confusion. "Singing for?"

"Old custom. In the musical towns, you used to have to sing for your supper. Literally."

"Oh." The innkeeper looked genuinely puzzled. "We don't... we don't really do music here anymore. Not since..." He trailed off, his eyes going distant.

"Since when?" Lillian prompted gently.

"Since the songs started going wrong. Started sounding like... like screaming. Like the music was in pain." He shuddered. "We stopped singing. All of us. Seemed safer."

Lillian and Miles exchanged glances. This was worse than they'd thought.

"How long ago?" Miles asked.

"Three months. Maybe four. Hard to tell. Days sort of... blend together when there's no music to mark them."

They took their rooms—small, clean, and aggressively silent—and met in the common room for dinner, which consisted of bread that tasted like disappointment and stew that had clearly given up on flavor as a lost cause. Around them, the other patrons ate in silence, their movements careful and deliberate, as if they were afraid that even the clink of a spoon against a bowl might trigger something terrible.

"This is what it looks like," Lillian said quietly, her voice barely audible. "The beginning of the end. When people become so afraid of the wrong kind of music that they stop making any music at all."

"So what do we do?"

She reached into her pack and pulled out the tin flute, its tarnished surface catching the lamplight. "We remind them what the right kind of music sounds like."

Before Miles could stop her, she raised the flute to her lips and played a single, clear note.

The effect was immediate and dramatic. Every person in the common room froze, their eyes wide with terror. The innkeeper dropped a plate, which shattered on the floor with a sound like breaking promises. Someone whimpered.

But the note hung in the air, pure and clean and utterly, completely right. It was the sound of morning breaking over mountains, of children laughing in summer gardens, of lovers meeting after long separation. It was the sound of hope made audible.

Slowly, carefully, Lillian began to play a simple melody. Nothing fancy, nothing complex—just a lullaby her mother had sung to her when she was small and the world was full of possibilities instead of regrets.

One by one, the patrons began to relax. The terror faded from their eyes, replaced by something that might have been wonder. A woman in the corner began to hum along, her voice rusty from disuse but still true. A man near the fire started tapping his foot in time with the melody.

By the time Lillian finished, half the common room was humming along, their voices blending in harmony that was rough and unpracticed but genuine.

The innkeeper was crying.

"I'd forgotten," he whispered. "I'd forgotten how beautiful it could be."

Lillian lowered the flute, her eyes bright with something that might have been tears or might have been starlight. "That's what we're fighting for," she said softly. "Not just the songs themselves, but the memory of why they matter."

Outside, the wind picked up the melody and carried it through the streets of Skarth's Hollow, where windows began to open and faces appeared, drawn by the sound of music that didn't hurt, music that healed instead of harmed.

For the first time in months, the hollow began to sing.

## Chapter 5: The Lake of Quiet Names

They didn't mean to find it, which was perhaps the most honest thing that had happened to them in days.

It wasn't marked on the Map—that breathing, tooth-studded nightmare they'd consulted back in the cathedral—nor mentioned in any bard's tale worth the parchment it was scribbled on. Just a silver crescent glinting through the trees like a dropped coin from some careless god's pocket, hidden in a gully that smelled of pine sap, old magic, and the sort of forgotten songs that made you nostalgic for things you'd never actually experienced.

They stumbled into the clearing near twilight, mud-streaked and blistered, having spent the better part of the day navigating what could charitably be called a path but was more accurately described as "a series of increasingly vindictive suggestions." The march had taken them through bramble-cursed hollows where the thorns whispered insults in dead languages, and up a slope populated by ankle-deep bees—the argumentative kind that held philosophical debates about the nature of pain before administering their stings with the precision of tiny, flying tax assessors.

The lake, by contrast, looked mercifully bee-free. It also looked like the sort of place where sensible people might pause to reconsider their life choices, which was probably why they were drawn to it immediately.

"You take first dip," Lillian rasped, slumping against a mossy log with the grace of a sack of particularly tired potatoes. "I'm too exhausted to look at your parts and be disappointed. Again."

Miles grunted, which was his standard response to most of life's challenges these days. "Fine by me. I'd rather not have an audience while I scream in pain anyway. The acoustics in this place look like they'd make it echo."

He began the complex ritual of removing his armor—a process that involved more grunting, cursing, and creative blasphemy than most religious ceremonies. The dented breastplate came off with a sound like a dying accordion. The gambeson underneath was crusted with sweat, dried blood, and what appeared to be the remains of something that had once been edible but had since achieved a state of aggressive decomposition.

The last strap, naturally, refused to cooperate. It clung to its buckle with the desperate tenacity of someone who knew they were about to be unemployed. Miles solved this problem with a small dagger and a string of profanity that would have made a dock worker blush and a priest reach for his holy water.

Then—bare, patched with scars like a map drawn by someone with a very pessimistic view of geography—he limped into the water with the careful dignity of a man who had learned that pride was a luxury he could no longer afford.

The water wasn't cold. It was cool in the way that childhood lullabies were cool, the way that half-remembered rivers whispered your name when you weren't listening. He waded in until the water touched his ribs, then exhaled with a sound like a cathedral finally allowing itself to deflate after holding its breath for centuries.

Behind him, Lillian watched from beneath her floppy hat, which had achieved the sort of structural integrity that suggested it was held together by willpower and accumulated grime rather than any recognizable textile engineering.

She'd known this body once—in glimpses, in stolen moments, in the golden years before battles had carved their stories across his skin like a particularly violent form of autobiography. Now it looked... not ruined, exactly. Used. Like armor that had been worn too long by someone who'd forgotten how to take it off. But beneath the scars and the weariness, there was still something that made her chest tighten in ways that had nothing to do with the curse they were chasing.

"Quit staring," he called over his shoulder, his voice carrying the sort of mock indignation that suggested he wasn't entirely displeased by the attention. "I've seen goblins gawk with more subtlety."

She rolled her eyes with the practiced ease of someone who'd been perfecting the gesture for decades. "I was admiring the view. The lake's view, you narcissistic old goat. Though I suppose your backside is marginally more interesting than most of the scenery we've encountered lately."

Still, she rose—not with the fluid grace of her younger years, but with the deliberate care of someone who'd learned that getting up was significantly easier than getting back down again.

Her cloak came off in a single motion, followed by the travel-stained tunic, the undergarments that had seen better decades, and the belt that served as a mobile pantry for crumbs, charms, and half a meat pasty that had achieved a state of preservation that bordered on the archaeological. She folded them carefully—not out of vanity, but out of the sort of ritual precision that came from years of living out of a pack and knowing that wrinkled clothes were the difference between looking like a distinguished adventurer and looking like someone who'd been sleeping in ditches.

Her body was softer now, fuller than it had been in the days when she'd been all sharp angles and nervous energy. She had once been wiry as a willow branch, sharp with youth and the sort of restless melody that couldn't sit still. Now she bore the comfortable roundness of years well-lived and songs well-sung, the weight of stories that had settled into her bones like sediment in a riverbed. Her hips ached with the memory of long roads. Her knees whispered mutinous suggestions about retirement and comfortable chairs.

But she stepped into the water with the grace of someone who no longer cared about being seen—only about being remembered.

Miles turned. Not fully—he wasn't quite that brave, or that foolish. Just enough to catch her silhouette against the dying light, the way the water caught the silver in her fur, the careful way she moved as if she were performing a ritual that only she understood.

And for a moment, neither of them moved.

Two bodies. Two stories. Laid bare not for pleasure or desire—though those were there, banked like coals beneath the surface—but for truth. For the simple, terrifying honesty of being seen by someone who remembered who you used to be and chose to stay anyway.

She slid in beside him, the water rising to her shoulders with a small sigh that might have been relief or might have been surrender. She didn't speak, and neither did he. Instead, they stared across the lake at the floating pollen that danced like tiny golden spirits, the midges that wrote their brief stories in the air, the ripples that carried no names but somehow seemed to know theirs anyway.

The silence stretched between them—not uncomfortable, but full. The sort of silence that came after all the words had been said and found wanting, leaving only the essential things behind.

Eventually, Miles said, "This place... it's too quiet."

"That's why I like it," Lillian replied, her voice barely above a whisper, as if speaking too loudly might break whatever spell had settled over the water.

"You always liked quiet things. Except when you were on stage."

"I liked being loud there so I could stay quiet everywhere else. Performance was... armor, I suppose. The louder I was in front of crowds, the more invisible I could be when it mattered."

Silence again, but deeper now. The kind that invited confessions.

Then, after a long pause that felt like a held breath: "You saw my scars."

Lillian nodded, her eyes still fixed on the far shore where fireflies were beginning their nightly dance. "You saw my stomach. And my stretch marks. And the way my left shoulder sits funny since that business with the chimera."

"Neither of us screamed."

"I think that counts as progress."

A half-laugh escaped him—not mocking, not even weary. Just real. The sort of sound that came from someone who'd finally stopped pretending that life was supposed to make sense.

Miles looked down at his hands in the water, watching the way the ripples distorted his reflection. Knuckles broken a dozen times over, one finger that didn't quite bend anymore since a troll had decided to use it as a conversation starter. "Sometimes I forget what I used to look like. Before swords. Before exile. Before I learned that honor was just another word for expensive stupidity."

Lillian, eyes half-lidded, watched the first stars begin to prick through the canopy like pinpricks in dark velvet. "I don't forget. I remember for both of us. That's what bards do—we're professional rememberers. Even when we'd rather not be."

And there it was—not romance, not yet. Not lust either, though that was there too, patient as a cat waiting for the right moment to pounce. Just something raw and old and aching, like a song half-remembered from childhood that you couldn't quite place but that made you homesick for a place you'd never been.

They stayed in the water until their skin puckered like raisins, until the moon came out and painted silver paths across the surface, until the world grew quiet enough that they could hear their own heartbeats echoing in the stillness. Then they emerged, dried off with the sort of careful silence that suggested they were both afraid of breaking whatever fragile thing had grown between them, and lay beside their small fire with nothing between them but breath and possibility and the weight of all the words they weren't quite ready to say.

Nothing happened.

And yet, everything changed.

## Chapter 6: The Hushwood Beckons

They left Skarth's Hollow at dawn, accompanied by the sound of tentative singing drifting from windows and doorways. The innkeeper had packed them extra provisions and refused payment, pressing a small wooden token into Lillian's hand.

"For luck," he'd said. "And... thank you. For reminding us."

The token was carved with a simple musical note, worn smooth by countless fingers. Lillian tucked it into her pocket next to the flute, another small weight to carry forward.

The road to the Hushwood was better maintained than the path to Skarth's Hollow, which was to say it existed as more than a theoretical concept. It wound through farmland where crops grew in neat, orderly rows and farmers waved from their fields with the sort of cheerful determination that suggested they were either genuinely happy or had made a conscious decision to pretend they were until it became true.

"Different here," Miles observed, watching a group of children chase chickens around a farmyard while their mother hung laundry that snapped in the breeze like colorful flags of domestic victory.

"The curse spreads from the center outward," Lillian explained. "Like ripples in a pond. The farther from the source, the less effect it has. For now."

"And the source is?"

"The Hushwood. Where the first of the Forbidden Verses is hidden."

They walked in comfortable silence for a while, the sort of silence that comes from shared purpose rather than shared despair. The sun climbed higher, warming their backs and casting their shadows ahead of them like dark heralds announcing their approach.

It was Miles who first noticed that the birdsong was getting quieter.

Not absent—not yet—but muted, as if the birds were singing through thick cloth or from very far away. The cheerful chatter of sparrows became a whisper. The bold declarations of robins faded to uncertain murmurs.

"We're getting close," Lillian said, though she didn't sound pleased about it.

The farmland gave way to scrubland, then to forest. But this wasn't the sort of forest that invited exploration or promised adventure. This was the sort of forest that suggested you might want to reconsider your life choices and perhaps take up a nice, safe hobby like accounting or professional worrying.

The trees grew close together, their branches intertwining overhead to form a canopy so thick that noon felt like twilight. The undergrowth was sparse, not because nothing could grow there, but because nothing wanted to. Even the weeds seemed apologetic about their existence.

And it was quiet. Not the natural quiet of a forest at rest, but the unnatural quiet of a forest holding its breath.

At the forest's border, an ancient stone slab bore an inscription in letters that seemed to shift and blur when looked at directly:

*"Speak not. Sing not. Cry not. The Beast listens.  
One sound, and you are devoured whole.  
Enter in silence, or enter not at all."*

Lillian stared at the inscription grimly, her thumb brushing the flute in her pocket like a talisman. Miles gave a small, sardonic gesture that somehow managed to convey both "after you" and "this is a terrible idea" simultaneously.

They stepped into the quiet.

Silence in the Hushwood wasn't the absence of noise. It was a presence. Thick, heavy, oppressive. Every breath felt stolen from something that needed it more. Every footstep felt like a blasphemy waiting to happen. The very air seemed to press against their ears, demanding tribute in the form of absolute, perfect quiet.

Miles winced as his armor creaked, the sound seeming to echo forever in the unnatural stillness. He shed the breastplate and packed it tight, wrapping it in cloth to muffle any sound. Lillian did the same with her cloak, tucking away every bell, charm, or item that might betray them to whatever was listening in the deep woods.

But the forest watched.

Each tree had knots shaped like faces—some twisted in warning, some yawning in grief, some screaming in silent agony. Moss crept across trunks in tangled sigils that hurt to look at directly. The air shimmered with hushdust—motes of enchanted stillness that drifted like snow made of compressed silence.

They walked for what felt like hours but might have been minutes or days. Time moved strangely in the Hushwood, flowing like honey mixed with regret. The path—if it could be called a path—wound between trees that seemed to shift when not observed directly, leading them deeper into a silence so complete it had weight.

At one point, Lillian tripped. Her paw hit a branch, and it snapped with a sound no louder than a whisper—

But the forest twitched.

Far off, something answered. Not an echo, but a response. A branch breaking in reply, deliberate and measured. Then another. And another. Getting closer.

The Beast was hunting.

They moved faster now, abandoning caution for speed. Behind them, the sounds of pursuit grew closer—not footsteps, but the deliberate breaking of branches, the careful crushing of leaves, the sound of something large moving through the forest with predatory patience.

The path led them to a clearing shaped like a forgotten chapel, where ancient stones formed a rough circle around a pool of water so still it might have been black glass. In the center of the pool, on a small island of moss-covered stone, stood a single tree.

It was not a large tree, nor particularly impressive. But it sang.

Silently.

The song was visible rather than audible—threads of silver light that wove through the air around the tree's branches, forming patterns that spoke directly to the soul without troubling the ears. It was the first of the Forbidden Verses, the song that held the Hushwood in its grip of enforced silence.

And standing between them and the tree, rising from the pool like a nightmare made of shadow and hunger, was the Beast.

It was difficult to describe the Beast, partly because it seemed to exist in a state of constant flux and partly because looking at it directly caused a sort of mental static that made coherent thought challenging. It might have been a wolf, if wolves were made of compressed darkness and fed exclusively on sound. It might have been a bear, if bears could exist in seventeen dimensions simultaneously. It might have been something else entirely, something for which no words existed because the words themselves had been devoured.

What was certain was that it was large, it was hungry, and it was looking directly at them with eyes like holes punched in reality.

Lillian reached for her flute, then stopped. One note—one single sound—and the Beast would be upon them. But without music, how could they counter the Forbidden Verse? How could they break a curse made of silence with silence itself?

Miles, meanwhile, was having thoughts of his own. Practical thoughts. Violent thoughts. The sort of thoughts that came naturally to someone who had spent years solving problems with the application of sharp metal to soft targets.

He couldn't speak. Couldn't shout a battle cry or call out a warning. But he could move.

The Beast turned toward him as he stepped forward, its attention drawn by the slight sound of his boots on stone. It opened its mouth—a void that seemed to contain the absence of every song that had ever been silenced—and prepared to devour him whole.

That was when Lillian began to sing.

Not with her voice—that would have been suicide. But with her heart, her soul, her very essence. She sang the way she had learned to sing in the deepest, most secret places of her training, where sound was just one way among many to make music.

The song poured out of her in waves of pure intention, visible as threads of gold that wove through the silver of the Forbidden Verse. It was a song of defiance, of hope, of the stubborn refusal to be silenced. It was every lullaby ever sung to a frightened child, every love song ever whispered in the dark, every battle hymn ever raised against despair.

The Beast recoiled, its form wavering like smoke in a strong wind. The Forbidden Verse began to unravel, its silver threads tangling and breaking as Lillian's golden song wound through them.

But the effort was killing her. She could feel pieces of herself being torn away with each note, memories and experiences sacrificed to fuel the song. Her first kiss. The taste of her mother's bread. The sound of rain on summer leaves. All of it feeding the music that would break the curse.

Miles saw her faltering and did the only thing he could think of. He began to sing too.

Not with his voice—he had no training in the deep songs, no knowledge of how to make music without sound. But he had something else. He had memory. He had love. He had the stubborn, bloody-minded determination of someone who had lost everything once and refused to lose it again.

He sang with his sword, each movement a note in a battle-song older than words. He sang with his body, each step a rhythm in a dance of defiance. He sang with his heart, pouring every moment of joy and sorrow and desperate hope into the golden threads that Lillian was weaving.

Together, they sang the Forbidden Verse into silence.

The Beast let out a soundless shriek and dissolved, its form scattering like smoke in a hurricane. The silver threads of the curse snapped and faded, leaving only the natural sounds of the forest—wind in leaves, water lapping at stone, the distant call of birds who had forgotten they were supposed to be afraid.

Lillian collapsed, her face pale and her breathing shallow. Miles caught her before she could hit the ground, his arms strong and steady despite the tremor in his hands.

"Did we...?" she whispered, her voice barely audible.

"We did," he said, and his voice was rough with emotion and exhaustion. "The first verse is broken."

She smiled, a small, tired smile that contained within it the echo of every song she had ever sung. "Two more to go."

In the pool, the tree continued to sing—but now its song was one of gratitude, of freedom, of the joy that comes from being heard after long silence. The Hushwood began to wake around them, filling with the sounds of life returning to a place that had forgotten how to live.

They had won the first battle. But the war was far from over.

## Chapter 7: The Town That Eats Its Dead

They arrived at sunset, when the light had that particular quality of honey mixed with regret and the shadows stretched long enough to hide uncomfortable truths.

Grinvale shimmered in the golden haze like a mirage made of good intentions and questionable architectural choices. Its cottages were too clean, their whitewash so pristine it hurt to look at directly. The hedges were trimmed with mathematical precision, as if someone had taken a ruler to nature and demanded it conform to proper standards of suburban respectability. A little bell above the bakery chimed with metronomic regularity, though no wind stirred the air and the weathervane pointed steadfastly in all directions at once.

Flowers bloomed from every window box with the sort of suspicious perfection that suggested they'd been having stern words with entropy and had won the argument. No wilting. No browning edges. No scent, which was perhaps the most disturbing thing of all—flowers without fragrance were like songs without melody, technically correct but fundamentally wrong.

Miles paused at the town's edge, his hand instinctively moving to his sword hilt. "Either we're dead and this is some sort of bureaucratic afterlife," he said, eyeing the unnaturally straight cobblestones, "or someone's really committed to village upkeep. Disturbingly committed."

Lillian muttered something under her breath that sounded like a prayer to gods who specialized in getting people out of obviously terrible situations. "Let's pray it's the second option. The first would mean we'd have to fill out forms, and I left my good quill at home."

The sign on the town square was carved from wood that gleamed like it had been polished that morning, every morning, for the past century:

*"Grinvale – A Place to Rest (Indefinitely)  
Population: Variable  
Please Wipe Your Feet"*

A woman in a blue bonnet that matched her eyes—or perhaps her eyes had been chosen to match the bonnet, which raised several questions about the nature of identity in small towns—greeted them with a smile so wide it threatened to escape the confines of her face entirely. Her skin had the translucent quality of parchment soaked in milk, and when she moved, she made no sound at all, as if she'd been practicing the art of existing quietly for far too long.

"Well met, travelers!" she cooed, her voice like honey poured over broken glass. "Come in, come in! You must be weary from your journey. Such a long road, and so many unpleasant things along the way. You'll want supper, I'm sure. We've a roast on—the finest in three counties, though admittedly the competition isn't what it used to be. And games afterward, if you're the lively sort. We do so enjoy games."

Miles smiled with the sort of politeness that came from years of dealing with people who might stab you if you were rude. Lillian did not smile at all, which was her way of dealing with people who might stab you regardless of your manners.

They were shown to a house that looked like it had been decorated by someone whose understanding of "cozy" came entirely from books written by people who'd never actually been cozy. Lace curtains hung in windows that reflected nothing, and doilies made of what appeared to be cobweb thread adorned every surface with the sort of aggressive domesticity that suggested the spiders had been having philosophical discussions about interior design.

Their host—Mrs. Pinebloom, who introduced herself with the sort of curtsy that belonged in a different century and possibly a different reality—made tea that smelled of grave earth and chamomile, a combination that should have been impossible but somehow worked in the way that nightmares sometimes made perfect sense until you woke up.

As they drank—or in Lillian's case, held the cup and pretended to drink while actually letting the liquid cool into something that might be less likely to dissolve her tongue—Mrs. Pinebloom asked the usual questions that weren't quite usual: "Where are you from, dears?" "What brings you through our little corner of paradise?" "How long were you planning to stay?" "Would you like to stay forever?"

She didn't blink much. In fact, Lillian realized with growing unease, she didn't blink at all. Neither did anyone else in town, which created the unsettling impression of being watched by a collection of very polite dolls who'd learned to walk and talk but hadn't quite mastered the finer points of being human.

At dusk, the church bell rang thirteen times, which was either a mistake or a very specific sort of invitation. All of Grinvale turned up for dinner in the town hall—a grand old building with a chandelier made from what appeared to be melted candlesticks and fingernail clippings, which raised questions about both the town's aesthetic sensibilities and their approach to personal hygiene.

The long table stretched farther than logic should have allowed, disappearing into shadows that seemed deeper than the room could accommodate. Every dish was perfect. Every roast glistened with juices that caught the light like liquid amber. Every pie steamed invitingly, their crusts golden-brown and decorated with patterns that hurt to look at directly. In the corner, a quartet of skeletons in bow ties played waltzes with the sort of technical precision that suggested they'd been practicing for a very long time and had nothing better to do with eternity.

Miles was halfway through a plate of mutton that tasted like every perfect meal he'd ever imagined before he realized it was still bleeding—not the honest red of fresh meat, but something darker, something that pulsed with its own rhythm. The gravy moved when he wasn't looking at it.

"These people are dead," Lillian whispered across the table, her voice like thunder heard from very far away.

"I know," Miles murmured, cutting another piece of meat that reformed itself as soon as his knife passed through it. "They're just... really good hosts. Disturbingly good hosts."

"Too good. No one's this good at hospitality without an ulterior motive. Usually involving your soul."

Mrs. Pinebloom stood and tapped her glass with a spoon that rang like a funeral bell. The sound echoed longer than it should have, bouncing off walls that seemed to stretch away into darkness.

"We would be honored," she said, her voice carrying to every corner of the impossible room, "if you would consider staying with us. This place remembers your names already—Lillian Edgecombe, Miles of Shepherdshire. Such lovely names. Such heavy names. And in return for your company, you may forget. Pain. Burden. Choice. The weight of all those terrible decisions. You'll never have to run again, never have to fight, never have to lose anything else. Isn't that what you've always wanted?"

The whole room echoed in perfect unison: "Stay with us. Stay with us. Stay with us."

Miles found himself dreaming while awake, the boundaries between thought and reality blurring like watercolors in rain. The roast tasted like his childhood home, like his mother's cooking before the war came and took everything good away. The wine tasted like Lillian's first kiss, sweet and sharp and full of promises that had been broken by time and circumstance and the simple, brutal mathematics of living. He saw visions of himself teaching fencing to ghost-children who laughed without making sound, dancing on spring mornings that never ended, never failing, never bleeding, never disappointing anyone ever again.

"No more regrets," Mrs. Pinebloom said gently, her hand cool as marble on his shoulder. "No more shame. No more wondering what might have been. Just the joy of never having to leave, never having to say goodbye, never having to watch the people you love walk away."

For a moment—a long, dangerous moment—he wanted it. He truly did. The weight of his failures, the ache of old wounds, the constant, grinding exhaustion of being himself—all of it could just... stop.

But across the table, Lillian stared at her untouched plate, trembling like a leaf in a wind that no one else could feel.

"This isn't real," she said, her voice cracking like glass under pressure. "This is hell, dressed up in curtains and pudding and the promise of peace. I've seen this spell before, in the old texts. It feeds you comfort, feeds on comfort. We're not guests here. We're being digested."

Mrs. Pinebloom's smile faltered for just a moment, revealing something underneath that was neither kind nor human. "You always were the difficult one, dear. Even as a child, always asking the wrong questions, always looking for the door when everyone else was content to stay."

The room began to shift around them, walls stretching and contracting like the inside of something vast and hungry. The other diners turned to look at them with eyes that reflected no light, their perfect smiles never wavering even as their faces began to melt like wax left too close to a fire.

To leave Grinvale, Lillian knew from half-remembered lessons and nightmares that felt like prophecy, one must recite the First Sacred Verse—the Verse of Remembrance, the one that breaks false homes and dead spells and the comfortable lies we tell ourselves when the truth becomes too heavy to carry. But it had been lost, hidden away by those who feared its power to shatter illusions.

She searched the church while Miles held off the increasingly insistent hospitality of their hosts. She searched the basement, where portraits of people who had never existed watched her with painted eyes. She searched the tombs, where headstones bore names that changed when she wasn't looking directly at them.

At last, she found the truth in the most obvious place—hidden in plain sight, tattooed inside the mouth of an old ghost who sat in the back pew of the church like a penitent who'd forgotten what he was repenting for. He was a gravedigger named Tolliver, his jaw wired shut with sorrowstring that glowed faintly in the candlelight. When he saw her, he pointed to his lips with fingers that were more bone than flesh and handed her a pair of pliers that felt heavier than they should have.

She nearly dropped them. The weight wasn't physical—it was the weight of choice, of action, of doing what needed to be done even when it would hurt.

In the chapel, under the flickering light of corpse-lanterns that burned with flames the color of old memories, she whispered apologies to a man who could not answer as she pried his mouth open and saw the verse etched in trembling ink across tongue and cheek, written in a language that existed only in the spaces between words.

"First we name the pain," she read, her voice growing stronger with each syllable. "Then we carry it. Then we walk on."

As she spoke, the sky tore open above Grinvale like fabric ripped by invisible hands. Reality bled through the cracks—honest darkness, honest stars, honest cold that bit at exposed skin and reminded them that being alive meant being uncomfortable sometimes.

Miles staggered into the square, his shirt torn and bloody from the dream-knife he'd earned in a dance with one of the villagers-turned-nightmares. His face was pale but his eyes were clear, the false peace finally broken.

"Thought you weren't coming," he gasped, reaching for Lillian with hands that shook only slightly.

"I was," she said, wiping blood from her mouth where the truth had cut her tongue. "I just had to rip the truth out of a corpse first. It's surprisingly difficult to do gently."

Behind them, Grinvale began to collapse in on itself, the perfect facades crumbling to reveal the bones of something much older and hungrier. Mrs. Pinebloom's final scream sounded almost grateful, as if she'd been waiting centuries for someone to finally break the spell that had trapped her in an endless performance of hospitality.

They walked away without looking back, because looking back at places like Grinvale was how you ended up staying forever.

## Chapter 8: The Marshes of Memory

The journey from the Hushwood to the Dire Marshes took them through country that seemed determined to prove that geography could have a sense of humor, and that its sense of humor was both cruel and oddly specific. Rolling hills gave way to flatlands that stretched to the horizon like a green carpet that had been left out in the rain too long. Rivers meandered through the landscape with the sort of aimless wandering that suggested they'd forgotten where they were supposed to be going and were too embarrassed to ask for directions.

Lillian walked with a slight limp now, the price of singing the Forbidden Verse still echoing through her bones. She'd lost more than memories in the Hushwood—she'd lost pieces of herself, fragments of identity that had been sacrificed to break the curse. Sometimes she would reach for a word and find it gone, or try to remember a face and discover only empty space where the memory should have been.

Miles stayed close, watching her with the sort of careful attention usually reserved for unstable explosives or particularly temperamental royalty. He'd taken to humming under his breath—simple tunes, children's songs, anything to fill the silence that seemed to follow them like a faithful dog.

"You don't have to do that," Lillian said as they crested a hill that offered a view of the marshlands ahead, where mist rose from standing water like the ghosts of drowned clouds.

"Do what?"

"Hum. I'm not going to shatter if you stop making noise."

"Maybe I'm not doing it for you," Miles replied, which was both true and not true in the way that most honest statements were. "Maybe I'm doing it because I like the sound of my own voice."

"Your voice is terrible."

"Thank you. I've been working on it."

The Dire Marshes spread before them like a fever dream painted in shades of green and brown and the particular color that water takes on when it's been sitting still for too long and thinking dark thoughts. Twisted trees rose from the water on roots that looked like arthritic fingers, their branches draped with moss that hung like the beards of ancient prophets who'd given up on prophecy and taken up complaining instead.

The air was thick with the smell of decay and possibility, the sort of smell that suggested that life and death were having an ongoing argument and neither was winning. Insects hummed in clouds that moved with purpose and malice, and somewhere in the distance, something large splashed in a way that suggested it was either very happy or very hungry.

"Cheerful place," Miles observed, swatting at a mosquito the size of a small bird. "Really captures that 'abandoned by hope' aesthetic."

"The Dire Marshes have always been like this," Lillian said, consulting the Map, which had taken to making small whimpering sounds whenever they asked it for directions. "Even before the curse. Some places are just naturally inclined toward despair."

"And the second Forbidden Verse is hidden here?"

"In the heart of the marshes, where the old songs go to die."

They found a path of sorts—a series of logs and stones that formed a precarious bridge through the deeper water. It was the sort of path that suggested someone had once cared about making travel possible but had given up halfway through and decided that if people really wanted to cross the marshes, they could figure it out for themselves.

The going was slow and treacherous. The logs were slippery with moss and what might have been algae or might have been something that preferred not to be identified. More than once, one of them slipped and had to be hauled back onto the path by the other, dripping with marsh water and dignity.

It was Miles who first heard the singing.

Not the harsh, discordant sound they'd expected from a cursed place, but something hauntingly beautiful—voices raised in harmony, weaving together in patterns that spoke of loss and longing and the sort of love that persists even after hope has packed its bags and moved to a nicer neighborhood.

"Do you hear that?" he asked, pausing on a relatively stable log to listen.

Lillian tilted her head, her ears twitching. "I hear it. But I don't understand it. The words... they're in a language I should know, but..."

The singing grew louder as they moved deeper into the marshes, and gradually they began to make out individual voices within the chorus. Men and women, young and old, all singing the same song in perfect, heartbreaking harmony.

They found the singers in a clearing where the water was deeper and darker than elsewhere, standing waist-deep in the marsh with their faces turned toward the sky. There were dozens of them, maybe hundreds, all perfectly still except for the rise and fall of their chests as they sang.

They were beautiful in the way that tragic things are beautiful—pale and ethereal, their hair floating around their faces like halos made of water weeds. Their eyes were closed, and their expressions were peaceful, as if they were dreaming of better times.

"The Chorus of the Lost," Lillian whispered, recognition dawning in her voice. "I thought they were just a legend."

"What are they?"

"Bards who came to the marshes seeking the second Forbidden Verse. They found it, but they couldn't leave. The song trapped them here, forced them to sing it over and over for eternity."

As if responding to her words, one of the singers opened her eyes and looked directly at them. Her gaze was ancient and sad and utterly, completely mad.

"New voices," she said, her words somehow audible despite the continuing chorus around her. "Come to join the song. Come to sing with us forever."

"We're not here to join you," Lillian said firmly. "We're here to break the curse."

The singer laughed, a sound like silver bells being dropped into deep water. "Break the curse? Oh, sweet child, you don't understand. We are the curse. We are the second Forbidden Verse, given voice and form and eternal purpose. We sing the song that makes the world forget how to remember."

Around them, the other singers began to turn, their eyes opening one by one to fix on the newcomers. Their song changed, becoming something darker, more insistent. It was a song of forgetting, of letting go, of the sweet relief that comes from abandoning the burden of memory.

Lillian felt it pulling at her, tugging at the edges of her mind like fingers made of music. It would be so easy to step into the water, to join the chorus, to let the song wash away all the pain and loss and regret that she carried. To forget the weight of being the last bard who remembered the old ways.

Miles grabbed her arm, his grip firm and grounding. "Don't listen," he said urgently. "It's not real. It's just another trap."

But even as he spoke, he could feel the song working on him too. It promised forgetfulness, yes, but also peace. An end to the constant struggle, the endless fight against a world that seemed determined to grind him down. He could join the chorus, become part of something larger than himself, something beautiful and eternal.

"We have to sing," Lillian said suddenly, her voice cutting through the hypnotic pull of the chorus. "We have to sing our own song, something stronger than theirs."

"I can't sing," Miles protested. "Not like you can."

"You don't have to sing like me. You just have to sing like you."

She pulled out her flute, the tarnished metal gleaming in the marsh light. But instead of playing it herself, she pressed it into Miles's hands.

"I don't know how—"

"You don't need to know how. You just need to remember why."

Miles looked at the flute, then at Lillian, then at the chorus of lost souls singing their eternal song of forgetting. He thought about the tavern where she'd found him, about the years of disappointment and failure that had led him there. He thought about the moment she'd walked back into his life, offering him a chance to be something more than a washed-up knight drinking himself to death in progressively seedier establishments.

He raised the flute to his lips and played a single, clear note.

It was not a perfect note. It was rough and uncertain, the sound of someone who had never learned to make music but was determined to try anyway. But it was honest, and it was his, and it cut through the chorus like a sword through silk.

Lillian began to sing then, her voice weaving around his simple melody, transforming it into something larger and more complex. She sang of memory, of the importance of holding onto the past even when it hurt. She sang of love that persisted despite loss, of hope that endured despite disappointment, of the stubborn human refusal to give up even when giving up would be easier.

The Chorus of the Lost faltered, their perfect harmony disrupted by the intrusion of imperfect, honest music. Some of them began to weep, tears streaming down their faces as memories they had forgotten began to return. Others covered their ears, trying to block out the sound that was breaking their beautiful, terrible spell.

But the song was stronger than their resistance. Note by note, memory by memory, it pulled them back from the edge of eternal forgetting. The marsh water began to recede, revealing solid ground beneath their feet. The mist cleared, showing them the way forward.

One by one, the members of the chorus stopped singing. They looked around in confusion, as if waking from a long dream, then began to walk toward the edges of the clearing. Some faded away entirely, their long imprisonment finally ended. Others remained solid, blinking in the sudden clarity of returned memory.

The woman who had spoken to them first was among those who remained. She approached slowly, her movements uncertain, as if she was still learning how to exist in a world where she was not compelled to sing.

"Thank you," she said, her voice hoarse from centuries of use. "I had forgotten... I had forgotten my own name."

"What is it?" Lillian asked gently.

The woman smiled, and for the first time in ages, it was a smile of joy rather than madness. "Melody. My name is Melody, and I was a bard once, before I came here seeking songs that should not be sung."

She reached into the marsh water and pulled out something that gleamed like captured starlight—a crystal vial containing what looked like liquid music, notes and melodies swirling in patterns too complex for the eye to follow.

"The second Forbidden Verse," she said, offering it to Lillian. "Take it. Use it. But be careful—it remembers everything, and not all memories are kind."

Lillian accepted the vial with trembling hands, feeling the weight of centuries of accumulated song and sorrow. "What will you do now?"

Melody looked around at the clearing, where the other freed singers were beginning to explore their newfound freedom. "We will remember," she said simply. "We will remember who we were, and we will try to become who we might have been. It's not much, but it's enough."

As they left the marshes behind, following a path that was clearer now, more certain, Lillian clutched the vial containing the second Forbidden Verse. Two down, one to go. But the hardest part still lay ahead—the journey to Varn's Tower, where the third and most dangerous verse waited in the hands of a god who had forgotten how to die.

Behind them, the Dire Marshes began to sing again—but this time, it was a song of hope.

## Chapter 9: The Tower of Varn

The approach to Varn's Tower was marked by a landscape that seemed to have been designed by someone with a deep appreciation for the aesthetic possibilities of apocalyptic despair. The ground was cracked and barren, split by fissures that glowed with an inner light the color of old blood and fresh regrets. The sky above was a roiling mass of storm clouds that moved in patterns too regular to be natural, too chaotic to be comfortable.

The Tower itself rose from the center of this desolation like a finger pointed accusingly at heaven, its black stone surface carved with symbols that hurt to look at directly and seemed to shift when observed peripherally. It was impossibly tall, stretching up beyond the clouds, beyond sight, beyond the comfortable boundaries of three-dimensional space.

"Well," Miles said, adjusting his grip on his sword and trying to project confidence he didn't feel, "it's certainly... imposing."

"Varn always did have a flair for the dramatic," Lillian replied, though her voice was tight with something that might have been fear or might have been anticipation. "Even before he went mad with power and tried to rewrite the fundamental laws of reality using nothing but interpretive dance and aggressive poetry."

"Is that what happened to him?"

"More or less. He was the greatest bard of his generation, maybe of any generation. But he wasn't content with just making music—he wanted to make music that could reshape the world, that could rewrite the very nature of existence. The third Forbidden Verse was his masterpiece, a song so powerful it could unmake creation itself."

"And then?"

"And then he sang it. Once. Just once, to see if it would work." Lillian stared up at the Tower, her expression grim. "It worked. Too well. The song tore a hole in reality, and Varn fell through it. He's been trapped between dimensions ever since, neither fully alive nor properly dead, singing his terrible song over and over for anyone mad enough to listen."

They approached the Tower's base, where a door stood open like a mouth waiting to swallow them. The threshold was carved with a warning in letters that seemed to writhe and shift:

*"Here lies Varn the Magnificent, Bard of the Final Song.  
Enter, and hear the music that ends all music.  
Listen, and learn the silence that devours sound.  
Sing, and join the chorus of the eternally damned."*

"Cheerful," Miles muttered. "Really sets the mood."

Inside, the Tower was larger than it had any right to be, its interior stretching away in directions that didn't exist in any reasonable geometry textbook. Stairs spiraled upward along the walls, defying gravity and common sense in equal measure. The air hummed with barely contained power, and every surface was covered with musical notation written in a script that seemed to move when not observed directly.

They climbed.

The stairs were treacherous, not because they were broken or unstable, but because they existed in a state of constant flux. Sometimes they were stone, sometimes crystal, sometimes what appeared to be crystallized music that chimed softly underfoot. The Tower's interior was filled with the sound of distant singing—not the beautiful harmonies they had encountered in the marshes, but something harsher, more discordant, like the sound of reality being forced into shapes it was never meant to hold.

As they climbed higher, the singing grew louder, and they began to see things that shouldn't exist. Fragments of other worlds drifted past the windows—alien landscapes, impossible cities, creatures that existed in too many dimensions to be properly perceived. The third Forbidden Verse was leaking through the barriers between realities, allowing glimpses of what lay beyond the comfortable boundaries of their own universe.

"How much farther?" Miles asked, pausing to catch his breath on a landing that seemed to be made of compressed starlight.

Lillian consulted the Map, which had taken to making small sobbing sounds and occasionally trying to hide behind her pack. "We're close. The song is getting stronger."

Indeed it was. The discordant melody was now loud enough to feel as well as hear, vibrating through their bones and making their teeth ache. It was a song of unmaking, of dissolution, of the sweet release that comes from letting go of the burden of existence entirely.

They found Varn at the top of the Tower, in a chamber that existed in seventeen dimensions simultaneously and smelled faintly of ozone and existential dread.

He had once been magnificent—tall and proud, with the sort of presence that commanded attention and respect. Now he was something else entirely. His form flickered between states of being, sometimes solid, sometimes translucent, sometimes existing as nothing more than a pattern of light and sound. His eyes were holes in reality, and when he opened his mouth to sing, the universe held its breath.

He was singing the third Forbidden Verse, the song that could unmake creation itself. And he had been singing it, without pause, for over a century.

"Varn," Lillian called out, her voice barely audible over the cosmic discord. "Varn, it's me. It's Lillian."

The singing stopped.

The sudden silence was more deafening than the music had been. Varn turned toward them, his impossible eyes focusing with difficulty on their small, solid forms.

"Lillian?" His voice was like the sound of stars dying, beautiful and terrible and utterly alien. "Little Lillian Edgecombe? But you're so... old."

"Time moves differently for those of us who stayed in one dimension," she replied gently. "Varn, you have to stop singing. The Verse is destroying everything."

He laughed, a sound like galaxies colliding. "Destroying? Oh, my dear child, you misunderstand. I'm not destroying anything. I'm perfecting it. I'm singing the world into a better shape, a more beautiful form. Can't you hear it? Can't you feel how much more elegant reality becomes when freed from the constraints of logic and physics?"

"I can feel it," Lillian said, and there was infinite sadness in her voice. "I can feel the world forgetting how to exist. I can feel music itself dying because you've made it too perfect, too pure. Varn, please. You have to let it go."

"Let it go?" Varn's form solidified slightly, becoming more recognizably human. "Let go of the greatest song ever composed? Let go of the music that could remake the universe in harmony and beauty? Never."

He began to sing again, and reality shuddered around them. The walls of the chamber became transparent, showing the void between dimensions where things that had never been born writhed in eternal hunger. The floor beneath their feet began to dissolve, revealing an abyss that stretched down into the heart of unmaking itself.

Miles stepped forward, his sword raised, though he knew it would be useless against a being who existed partially outside of physical reality. "Lillian, whatever you're going to do, do it now."

She pulled out the two vials they had collected—the first Forbidden Verse from the Hushwood, the second from the Dire Marshes. The liquid music within them swirled and pulsed, responding to the proximity of their sibling song.

"Varn," she called out over the cosmic discord, "listen to me. You're not perfecting the world—you're erasing it. Music isn't meant to be perfect. It's meant to be human. Flawed. Real."

She uncorked the first vial and poured its contents onto the dissolving floor. The liquid music spread like spilled starlight, weaving itself into the fabric of reality and beginning to repair the damage that Varn's song had caused.

"No!" Varn's voice cracked like thunder. "You don't understand! Without perfection, without absolute harmony, music is just noise! Just chaos!"

"Yes," Lillian said, uncorking the second vial. "Beautiful, wonderful, human chaos."

The second Forbidden Verse joined the first, and together they began to weave a counter-melody to Varn's cosmic song. It was not as powerful, not as technically perfect, but it was real in a way that his perfected music could never be.

Varn's song faltered, his perfect harmony disrupted by the intrusion of imperfect, honest music. For a moment, his form solidified completely, and he looked like the man he had once been—brilliant, passionate, but ultimately human.

"I... I can't stop," he whispered, and for the first time in a century, there was fear in his voice. "The song won't let me stop. It's become part of me, part of what I am. If I stop singing, I'll cease to exist."

"Then don't stop," Lillian said gently. "Just... sing something else."

She began to hum—not one of the Forbidden Verses, not some ancient song of power, but a simple lullaby. The same one she had played in the tavern at Skarth's Hollow, the one her mother had sung to her when she was small and the world was full of possibilities.

Miles joined in, his voice rough and untrained but honest. Then, slowly, hesitantly, Varn began to sing along. His cosmic voice, so used to reshaping reality with every note, struggled with the simple melody. But gradually, note by note, he remembered how to sing like a human being instead of a force of nature.

The third Forbidden Verse began to unravel, its perfect, terrible harmony dissolving into something warmer, more forgiving. The Tower shuddered and began to collapse, not in destruction but in relief, like a held breath finally being released.

As the walls crumbled around them, Varn's form began to fade. But he was smiling now, and his eyes were human again.

"Thank you," he whispered as he dissolved into light and music and the memory of songs yet to be sung. "Thank you for reminding me that perfection is the enemy of beauty."

The Tower fell, but gently, like snow settling on a winter landscape. When the dust cleared, they stood in a field of flowers that had not been there moments before, under a sky that was blue and clear and blessedly, wonderfully ordinary.

In Lillian's hand, the third vial materialized—not filled with liquid music this time, but with something that looked like crystallized laughter, bright and joyful and utterly, completely imperfect.

But their work was not yet finished.

## Chapter 10: Varn, City of Endings

They stepped into Varn beneath a twilight sky that refused to set, as if time itself had gotten confused and decided to take a tea break at the most inconvenient possible moment.

The city had no streets anymore—only avenues of marble cracked by time and thought, where the very concept of "walking in a straight line" had been declared philosophically unsound and subsequently banned by municipal ordinance. Buildings leaned, mid-collapse, preserved in the moment before ruin like a photograph of disaster taken by someone with very poor timing. Clock towers melted into vines that told time in colors rather than numbers. Candles floated upside-down in rain that never fell, their flames burning downward in defiance of physics and common sense.

The ground shimmered with paradox. One cobblestone whispered, "You have already failed," in the sort of voice usually reserved for tax assessors and disappointed mothers. Another answered, "You never began," with the philosophical certainty of someone who had given up on linear causality entirely.

And ahead, in the center of the unraveling, rose the Temple of Last Verses—a hollowed amphitheater suspended above a sea of untime, where past and future lapped at the edges in foam that tasted of regret and possibility. The architecture defied description, partly because it kept changing and partly because looking at it too directly caused mild existential crisis.

Lillian felt sick. Not physically—existentially. The sort of nausea that came from realizing that reality was more of a suggestion than a rule, and that someone had been taking liberties with the suggestion.

At the threshold stood a gatekeeper: no face, only hands—dozens of them, turning pages that wrote themselves, holding mirrors that reflected things that weren't there, pointing inward toward truths that no one particularly wanted to face.

Miles drew his sword. It rusted instantly, then unrusted, then briefly became a fish before settling back into being a sword with commitment issues.

"She must speak her own death," said the gatekeeper in a voice like recited prayers spoken by someone who had forgotten what they meant but remembered that they were important. "Only truth unlocks the final verse."

Lillian's knees buckled as the weight of understanding hit her like a particularly vindictive brick.

She knew which truth.

Years ago, long before retirement, long before she had learned that heroism was mostly about showing up when you'd rather be anywhere else, Lillian had stood on the stage of Cindermere Keep during a siege. With voice and spellwoven lute, she had held the line for hours, buying time for refugees to escape through mountain tunnels while the enemy battered at the gates like an extremely persistent and violent door-to-door salesman.

But the king had begged her to play one more song—a hymn of resistance that might rally the army, that might turn the tide, that might save them all.

She had played it.

It had worked. Too well.

Soldiers had charged back into the fray instead of fleeing to fight another day. Civilians had returned to the walls with pitchforks and hope and the sort of desperate courage that came from believing that their sacrifice would mean something.

When the enemy had unleashed their fire-magic, painting the sky in colors that had no names and should never have existed, there had been no survivors on the ramparts.

Only echoes.

Her echoes.

Inside the Temple, time convulsed like a dying animal. Each step forward aged Miles a year, lines appearing on his face like the world's cruelest time-lapse photography. Lillian grew younger, her grey whiskers darkening, her back straightening, until she looked like the bard she had been when the world was simpler and songs were just songs instead of weapons of mass inspiration.

Their reflections walked beside them, sometimes ahead, sometimes behind, occasionally stopping to have arguments about which direction they should be going and whether any of this made sense from a narrative perspective.

At the altar, a shattered harp hovered mid-collapse, its strings vibrating with the memory of music that had never been played and never should be.

The Final Verse was there. She could feel it. Like a thorn in her throat, like a song that wanted to be sung but knew it shouldn't be.

"I sang them to death," she whispered, the words falling into the untime like stones into a bottomless well.

"You saved lives too," Miles said, his voice steady despite the fact that he was currently seventeen years old and trying to hold a sword that was older than he was.

"I lured the others back. I played them a lie wrapped in a beautiful melody."

"No," he said quietly, his young face serious in a way that made her heart ache. "You gave them hope. They chose what to do with it."

She shook her head, tears streaming down cheeks that were smooth and unmarked by the years of guilt she had carried. "I knew they would die. I wanted to believe it meant something."

"And it did."

Lillian stepped onto the altar, feeling the weight of every song she had ever sung, every note that had changed the world in ways both beautiful and terrible.

And sang.

*"We cannot choose which echoes linger.  
But we may choose to let them sound.  
We may sing with the wounded throat,  
And build no monument to silence.  
This is not forgiveness.  
This is remembrance."*

As she sang, the city wept. Not metaphorically—literally. Statues dissolved in tears that tasted of marble and regret. The sky cracked open and rained memory, each drop containing a moment of joy or sorrow that someone had tried to forget. Miles held the gates against things from the untime—warped hounds of regret and false futures that howled with voices like broken promises.

Lillian sang louder, her voice growing stronger with each verse, weaving truth and pain and acceptance into something that was not quite forgiveness but was perhaps something better: understanding.

When the song ended, the sea of untime stilled. The verse etched itself into the air—into the world—like law written in light and sound and the sort of truth that could not be argued with or legislated away.

Lillian collapsed, her voice broken, her memories no longer hidden behind walls of guilt and self-recrimination. She was herself again—old, tired, scarred, but whole.

Miles caught her, his own age restored, his face lined with the years he had lived and the wisdom he had earned.

She sobbed into his chest like a child, all the tears she had never allowed herself to shed finally finding their way to the surface.

"I never told anyone," she whispered.

"You did now," he said, his arms strong around her, his voice gentle. "And I'm still here."

He held her until the untime receded and the Temple faded, stone by stone, like a dream dissolving in the morning light. They left Varn with nothing but each other and the truth, which was perhaps the most valuable treasure of all.

The curse had heard her song.

And it was listening.

## Chapter 11: The Singing Stones

They reached the Singing Stones at dusk, though the sky above had no sun—only auroras bleeding through reality like brushstrokes from a forgotten god who had decided that subtlety was overrated and had gone all-in on cosmic drama.

Twelve monoliths rose from the earth in a perfect ring, each carved with a verse of the Old Songs that predated language and postdated sense. Each pulsed faintly, like breath held too long, like warning given too late.

In the center: a dais of bone-white crystal that hummed with harmonics that made their teeth ache and their souls remember things they had never learned.

The curse waited there.

Not a creature, nor a shadow—but a shape made of absence, a hollow form wrapped in song that was discordant and mocking and beautiful in the way that broken things were beautiful when they caught the light just right. A choir of every ruined thing, every song that had ended too soon, every voice that had been silenced before its time.

It turned toward them. No face. Just echoes of faces, memories of expressions, the ghost of features that had once been human before they had become something else entirely.

It spoke in a dozen overlapping voices—young, old, proud, broken, hopeful, despairing. Some of them were Lillian's. Some were Miles's. Some belonged to people they had never met but whose lives they had touched, whose stories had become part of their own.

"You made me."

"You left me."

"You sang the end into being."

"Now sing it again."

Miles stepped forward, sword drawn, his face set in the sort of expression that suggested he was prepared to fight the concept of despair itself if necessary.

The blade shattered in his hands like spun glass, the pieces falling to the crystal dais with sounds like tiny bells mourning their own destruction.

The curse didn't fight like a beast. It remembered like a god, lashing them with memory and regret and lost futures, showing them every path not taken, every word not spoken, every song not sung. It was the weight of all their choices, all their failures, all their small betrayals and large disappointments given form and voice and the sort of terrible clarity that came from seeing yourself as you truly were.

Lillian heard every false song she had ever performed, every lie wrapped in melody, every half-meant promise set to music. She saw the faces of those who had died because of her songs, and those who had lived, and realized that the weight of both was exactly the same.

As the monoliths began to crack and hum with rising resonance that threatened to tear reality apart at the seams, Miles knelt and struck the earth with his palms, his voice rough with emotion and determination.

"I've nothing left but what I am," he muttered, blood seeping from his knuckles where they had struck stone. "But that's enough."

A radiant shield formed around them—not of light or magic, but of memory. His memory. Not regret, but loyalty. All the quiet things he had never asked for recognition for, all the years of standing by her side not because it was glorious or profitable, but because it was right. All the small acts of love and faithfulness that had never made it into any song but had made all the difference in the world.

He bought her the time.

She stepped onto the crystal dais, blood at her lip where she had bitten it to keep from screaming, voice thin and cracked but still hers, hands trembling but still steady enough to hold the truth.

But she sang.

*"I have walked through grief and fire,  
And made my home in sorrow's choir.  
I do not ask the world to bend—  
I sing it true.  
I sing its end."*

The stones joined her—each one answering with a harmonic of the past, voices of bards long dead adding their strength to hers, creating a chorus that spanned centuries and spoke in languages that had been forgotten before the first human had learned to make fire.

The curse screamed, a harmony too perfect for a lie, too beautiful for hatred, too honest for anything but acceptance.

*"There is no silence that cannot be broken.  
No wound without witness.  
No story without its last breath."*

And Lillian gave it hers.

Light bloomed from the dais—not blinding, not harsh, but gentle. Like dawn after mourning, like the first breath after weeping, like the moment when pain transforms into something that can be carried rather than something that carries you.

The curse unraveled. It didn't vanish—it understood. It saw itself reflected in her song, saw its own pain and anger and loss, and recognized that it was not alone, that suffering shared was suffering halved, that even the deepest wounds could heal if they were witnessed with love.

And it departed, not in defeat but in peace, its voices joining the eternal chorus of the stones before fading into the silence that comes after all songs end.

The Singing Stones cracked and fell silent, their purpose fulfilled, their ancient vigil finally ended.

Lillian collapsed, her strength spent, her voice gone, but her heart full of something that might have been peace or might have been the beginning of it.

Miles caught her as she fell, his arms strong and sure, his presence the only anchor she needed in a world that had suddenly become very large and very quiet and very, very beautiful.

## Epilogue: The Scars and the Song

They found a field near the Singing Stones' ruins, where the grass swayed like waves and the air tasted new, washed clean by the storm of truth that had just passed through the world like a cosmic spring cleaning.

There was no war. No magic. No curse. No destiny calling their names or fate tugging at their sleeves.

Just sky. Stars. And breath.

Miles laid out his cloak and collapsed onto it with a groan that contained within it the entire history of middle age and the particular sort of exhaustion that came from saving the world when you were old enough to know better. His armor was long gone, abandoned somewhere between the Dire Marshes and sanity, his tunic open to the night air, sweat drying on fur matted by exhaustion and wonder and the sort of profound relief that came from realizing you were still alive when you had been fairly certain you wouldn't be.

His body told its own history: a shoulder that never sat right after an argument with a particularly opinionated ogre, ribs that rose uneven from where they had been broken and healed and broken again, a thigh thickened by scar tissue and the sort of use that came from a lifetime of standing between danger and those who couldn't protect themselves.

Lillian curled beside him, her shirt loose and comfortable, her belly pillowed soft from years of comfort and survival and the discovery that happiness, when it came, was best enjoyed with good food and better company. Her thighs dimpled where she had danced too long, walked too far, lived too hard and loved too little until she had learned that it was never too late to start.

Neither flinched from the other. They had moved beyond that, beyond the careful politeness of people who were afraid of being seen, into the comfortable intimacy of those who had chosen to see and be seen and found the experience less terrifying than expected.

She propped her head up on her paw and smiled, the expression transforming her face in ways that made the stars seem brighter and the night seem less vast.

"Let's play a game."

He turned his head, one ear twitching with curiosity and mild suspicion. "A game?"

"I guess the story behind every scar of yours," she said, her voice warm with affection and mischief. "You do the same for... well. All this." She gestured down her torso with theatrical flourish that somehow managed to be both self-deprecating and proud. "Not scars. But still stories."

Miles stared at her for a long moment, his eyes taking in the sight of her—really seeing her, not as the legend or the hero or the bard of a thousand verses, but as Lillian, just Lillian, soft and scarred and beautiful in the way that real things were beautiful.

Then he laughed—deep and worn and full of love, the sort of laughter that came from the heart rather than the throat.

"Deal."

She touched his collarbone first—a thin white line that caught the starlight like a thread of silver. "Sword fight in a tavern. Over a girl who turned out to be a thief."

He grinned, his eyes crinkling at the corners. "Wrong. Wasn't a girl. Was your brother. He swung the chair."

Lillian gasped with mock outrage. "Bastard!"

"You ducked. I didn't."

She swatted him, giggling like a girl half her age, then took his hand and pressed it gently against the curve of her lower belly, where softness had gathered like a comfortable secret.

"Your turn."

He traced the curve reverently, his touch gentle and wondering. "This one... from all the late-night pies you swore you'd share, then didn't."

She snorted with laughter. "Rude. But true."

Next was a jagged scar over his bicep, raised and white against his dark fur.

"Shield broke," she guessed, running her finger along its length.

"Shield exploded," he corrected. "Dwarvish engineering. Never trust a dwarf who says 'it probably won't explode.'"

Then her hips, plush and freckled, marked by the passage of time and the weight of living.

"Years of stage work," he murmured, his voice soft with memory. "Lunges. Leaps. Applause that tasted like sugar and sounded like home."

"Mostly buttered scones," she corrected with a smile that held within it the memory of a thousand small pleasures. "And the occasional meat pie when I was feeling adventurous."

On and on they went, mapping each other's histories in touch and laughter and the sort of gentle honesty that came when all pretense had been stripped away. From his torn ear ("Goblin's revenge for a song that rhymed 'honor' with 'dishonor' one too many times") to the gentle slope of her breast ("The way you carry your song, like a burden made beautiful").

Her touch slowed as the night deepened around them.

His hand lingered, tracing patterns on her skin that had nothing to do with scars and everything to do with wonder.

Neither laughed now. The game had become something else, something deeper and more honest and infinitely more precious.

Their foreheads touched, breath mingling in the space between them.

He exhaled, the sound carrying within it years of longing and hope and the sort of patient love that had learned to wait.

She swallowed, her throat tight with emotion and possibility.

"You sure?" he asked, not pressing, not demanding, just offering.

She nodded, not shy but certain, her eyes bright with tears that had nothing to do with sadness.

"Not because the world needs saving. Not because it's the end?"

"No," she whispered, her voice barely audible over the night sounds around them. "Because I want you. Not the knight. Not the shadow of what you were. You."

He kissed her with the care of someone returning something precious—not claiming it, not taking it, but offering it back transformed by love and time and the sort of patience that came from understanding that some things were worth waiting for.

They undressed with quiet reverence, as if unveiling shrines to gods they had only just learned to worship. Nothing rushed. Nothing demanded. Nothing taken that was not freely given.

They touched the way people do when they finally believe they're allowed to—with wonder and gratitude and the sort of careful joy that came from discovering that happiness, when it arrived, was even better than they had dared to hope.

When their bodies pressed together, neither apologized for softness, for damage, for time. Her belly against his chest. His roughness beneath her paws. His weight. Her weight. The stories between them written in flesh and breath and the sort of truth that could only be spoken in whispers and sighs.

They didn't make love like heroes in songs, all passion and drama and cosmic significance.

They made love like survivors—grateful and gentle and utterly, completely human.

Afterwards, they lay tangled in shared breath and slow heartbeat, their bodies fitting together like pieces of a puzzle that had taken decades to solve.

The stars above whispered new stories, tales of love found late and treasured all the more for its rarity.

And neither was afraid.

The curse was broken. The world was saved. The songs would return, flawed and beautiful and human.

But here, in this field, under these stars, with this person, there was only the quiet music of two hearts learning to beat in harmony.

And that, Lillian thought as sleep began to claim her, was the most beautiful song of all.

## Epilogue: The Song Continues

Six months later, Lillian Edgecombe sat in her hill-house beneath the crooked rowan, but she was no longer alone. The quilt-drowned armchair had been joined by a second chair, slightly less comfortable but considerably more practical, where Miles Ferguson could be found most evenings, mending armor or sharpening swords or simply sitting in companionable silence while she worked on her music.

The house itself had changed. The towering shelves of sheet music were still there, but they no longer threatened to achieve sentience through accumulated resentment. Instead, they hummed quietly to themselves, content in the knowledge that they were being used again, that the songs they contained were being sung and shared and allowed to grow and change as all living music should.

Outside, the world was singing again.

It had taken time for the music to return fully. The Curse of the Withering Voice had left scars, places where songs had been forgotten so completely that they had to be relearned from scratch. But music, Lillian had discovered, was remarkably resilient. Like weeds growing through cracks in pavement, it found a way to flourish even in the most unlikely places.

Skarth's Hollow had become a center of musical learning again, its inn now hosting traveling bards and musicians who came to share their songs and learn new ones. The Dire Marshes had been transformed into a place of pilgrimage, where those who had lost their voices—literally or metaphorically—came to remember how to sing. Even the site where Varn's Tower had stood had become something beautiful: a garden where flowers bloomed in harmony with the wind, creating natural music that changed with the seasons.

"Letter for you," Miles said, entering the sitting room with a piece of parchment that bore the official seal of Queen Amphibia the Thirty-Ninth. "From your amphibious admirers."

Lillian accepted the letter with a smile, breaking the wax seal to reveal an invitation written in the flowery script that diplomatic toads seemed to favor:

*"The Royal Court of the Lily Pad Kingdom requests the honor of your presence at the Festival of Returned Songs, to be held on the first full moon of spring. Your heroic efforts in breaking the Curse of the Withering Voice have not been forgotten, and we would be delighted to honor you with the Order of the Golden Croak, our highest civilian decoration."*

"They want to give me a medal," Lillian said, showing Miles the letter.

"Are you going to accept?"

She considered this, looking out the window at the garden where she had planted herbs and flowers that attracted songbirds. The air was full of their music—not perfect, not technically accomplished, but joyful and alive and utterly, completely real.

"I think," she said finally, "that I've had enough of being a hero for a while. Let someone else save the world. I have songs to write."

Miles smiled, settling into his chair with a cup of tea and a book of engineering principles that he was reading purely for pleasure. "Good. The world can take care of itself for a bit. We've earned some peace."

Outside, the wind picked up one of Lillian's melodies and carried it across the countryside, where it was heard by a young bard just beginning her training, who added her own variations and passed it on to a traveling minstrel, who taught it to a group of children, who sang it while they played, changing it again, making it their own.

And so the song continued, growing and changing and spreading, imperfect and beautiful and utterly, completely human. Which was, Lillian thought as she picked up her flute and began to play, exactly as it should be.

In the distance, thunder rumbled—not the ominous thunder of approaching doom, but the comfortable thunder of a summer storm bringing rain to thirsty gardens. And beneath it, barely audible but unmistakably there, the world sang along.

The music had returned. Flawed, chaotic, and more beautiful than any perfect song could ever be.

And in a hill-house beneath a crooked rowan, two old friends sat together in comfortable silence, listening to the symphony of an imperfect world learning to sing again.

*The End*

*Author's Note: This tale of Furlandia draws upon the wit and social observation of Terry Pratchett, the complex character development and political intrigue of George R.R. Martin, and the gothic humor and necromantic complexity of Tamsyn Muir. Like all the best songs, it is imperfect, human, and hopefully worth the singing.*
