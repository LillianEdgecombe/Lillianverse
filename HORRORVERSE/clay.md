Clay


My pottery studio is glowing with the breezy early morning sunlight, the kind that feels syrupy yet cold. The light pouring down from the high window coats the shelves and the wheel like molasses. On days like this, it feels good to stay here: the world stays outside, my life stays inside. Like a cradle.
The bowl slips before I even feel my elbow hit it. A dull bump, then a heavy tumble — and the stoneware shatters against the hardwood floor in what sounds like breaking teeth. One shard leaps up and taps my left lens. I hear a soft crystalline tick and I immediately pull my glasses off.
A hairline fracture slices the lens like a frost crack on a window. And the world beyond my snout becomes a blurry mass, like watching a watercolor by a bored version of William Turner. “Bugger.”
Before the dread has a chance to settle, my phone buzzes.
Miles.
I sigh, feeling my tense muscles unclench.
I swipe, prop it against a jar of brushes, and his muzzle fills the screen — boxy office lighting behind him, paperwork stacked high, tie loosened in that “I swear I’m fine” way that isn’t fooling anyone. Not me, at least. I can see him very well even without my glasses. “Lils! Still in the cathedral?” he asks.
“Still worshipping at the altar of clay,” I say, raising my mug of tea. “Your god-tier wife just broke her glasses.”
He winces. “Again? That’s — what — the third time this year?”
“The clay fights back,” I reply.
“Well, tell it to pick on someone its own consistency,” he says. “Soft, pliable, dramatic under heat.”
“Oh, that’s definitely you, love.”
He gives a wounded gasp, then laughs. The studio feels warmer with his voice inside it. I miss him, and I feel glad for this sensation.
“Also,” I say, leaning out of frame to grab the parcel, “look what arrived from Southampton.” I heft the small black bucket into view. It's heavier than I thought initially.
Miles squints. "Uh... what am I looking at?"
“Deep-sea clay!" I reply. "Isn’t it amazing?” I lift the lid. “Straight from the abyssal plain somewhere off Ireland. Ten thousand feet down.”
"How did you..."
"Remember my friend at that centre in Southampton? They came back last week, and she kept for me these ten pounds of mud."
“Please don’t make food-contact items out of whatever eldritch sludge that is.”
“I’m making coffee cups.”
“Lils!”
“One for you, one for me, one for Sarah,” I grin.
He scrubs a hand down his face. “I’m gonna drink out of a haunted ocean? Brilliant. Then I know who to blame when I'm going to develop tentacles out of my eyes.”
“You love me.”
“God help me, I do. Listen — I’ve got to run. Crisis with the Kensington branch refrigeration. But text if you want me to swing by early, alright?”
“I’ll be fine,” I say.
"Don't get eaten by ancient gods, please."
"I'll do my best, honey."
He smiles — the real one, the soft one — and the call ends. Silence returns like something slipping back into place.
I put the glasses down. The crack across the left lens catches the light like a thin, closed eye.


The Cups

The deep-sea mud is colder than regular clay. Not normal cold — animal cold. Like plunging my paws in a bucket full of sticky snakes. It sticks to my fingers as if tasting me.
I tell myself I’m being dramatic, but the picture just stuck in my mind and it won't go away. Yikes.
I divide the mud into three equal lumps and throw each one into a cup shape. My cup ends up slightly crooked. Miles’s is sturdy, thick-walled. Sarah’s is delicate, sharper at the rim and tall. All three feel… warm by the time I set them down to dry. Body-warm, like someone’s been holding them tightly.  Overall, it's a nice sensation. It feels like love.
I pull my hands back and rub my fingers against my apron, but the warmth clings.
There’s still plenty of clay left. Enough for figurines.
I might sculpt something pleasant. Birds, maybe. Little houses. Woodland animals, why not? They would look fine on my desk at the academy.
But the moment I sit at the workbench, my mind pushes elsewhere — toward faces I haven’t seen in years. Faces that once looked at me with contempt so casual it felt like breathing.
Powell.
My fast-food manager from 'The Hungry Hound' in 2003. The way his face wrinkled when he spoke with me about my 'performance metrics', about 'customer complaints.' When he sided with a customer who called me 'animal.'
My chest tightens. I shouldn’t sculpt him. But my hands are already shaping the mud. I can feel his arms, his face growing from the blob of clay I'm working. He comes out squat and square, half-a-feet tall, a little more grotesque than he was in life. It feels good. Too good. Like vengeance.
My mind quickly goes to the next one. The Royal Academy dean, the one who told me I had a 'distinctive presence' but my features would be 'distracting.' A polite way to say that they didn't want badgers in their exclusive academy. Well, guess what, moron? I'm the head of my own academy right now. I sculpt his nose, his tight mouth, his smug eyes, and put the figurine next to that of Powell. Two small assholes looking at me from the table.
Then more.
Faces that accused.
Faces that dismissed.
Faces that didn’t even look at me long enough to see the person beneath the fur.
By the time I stop, I’ve made eight figurines. All of them staring at me with tiny, hateful faces.
I laugh under my breath. “That’s enough self-therapy for one day.”
I turn to wash my hands.
I hear something shifting behind me. Soft. Wet.


The First Movement

At first I tell myself it’s the light. The golden haze thickening. The exhaustion.
But then something touches my ankle. Just a tap.
I jerk back, knocking into the pottery wheel.
I see something at my feet. It's hard to understand without my glasses, but it's grey-ish and small, has... arms and legs.
Powell lies on its side at my feet, arm reaching toward me.
I freeze.
My heart races.
Slowly, I kneel and pick him up.
The clay feels both warm and cold. And soft, wet, as if sweating, even though it should have begun to firm.
As I hold the figurine in a shaky paw, his mouth opens just slightly. By itself. I feel my throat clenching.
And I hear it: “You’re difficult, Edgecombe.”
My heart slams against my ribs.
I drop him. He hits the floor with a wet thud and rolls.
I stand. Too fast. The room tilts.
Another voice speaks. Thin, prim, nasal:
“You have a striking presence, dear.”
My stomach flips violently. I need to puke. I gasp for air.
I turn toward the workbench.
Every figurine has turned to face me.
"But your features might be distracting for the other students," the voice keeps telling me.
All eight.
Their tiny mouths open.
And the noise that comes out is a layered chorus of every insult, every dismissal, every casual cruelty.
“You’re too much.”
“Several customers have mentioned feeling uncomfortable with direct service from anthro staff.”
“Who wants to listen to a fat badger? You aren't credible!”
“You make people uneasy.”
“Stop,” I whisper.
“Ma'am, the dress is absolutely unflattering on your figure. May I suggest something looser?”
“I'm not sure you're not... infectious. You know, the virus.”
“You’re difficult, Edgecombe.”
"Please," I cry. My head throbs. I shudder.
But the chorus keeps going — louder now, overlapping, building, the study’s huge interior amplifying them until the air itself seems to vibrate.
I back away until my spine hits a shelf.
My breath comes shallow. The room feels smaller. Heavier. Like the weight of the sea pressing down.


The Attack

The figurines move.
Not fluidly. Not humanly.
They jerk. They stutter. They skitter. It's like watching a stop-motion movie with all-too-real special effects, except that the special effects are trying to kill me and mock me.
Powell drags himself toward me first, clay fingers scrabbling at the hardwood. Little clay fingers that can move, bloody hell. Behind him, the other figures follow with unnatural little hops, their mouths yawning wider as their overlapping insults rise into a shrill, swelling mass:
“Not enough.”
“Too much.”
“Too fat."
“Distracting.”
“Aggressive.”
“Animal.”
“Difficult.”
“DIFFICULT.”
“Stay away,” I choke.
They don’t.
The first one reaches my foot and clamps onto my ankle with fingers that tighten like drying cement. The second grabs my calf. The third lunges and strikes my shin with its tiny, vicious head. It has teeth. Teeth like unnumerable little needles that pierce my dungarees and bite my flesh.
I scream.
Their voices sharpen.
“You made people uncomfortable.”
I kick, but the clay holds fast — soft enough to mold, strong enough to anchor.
Their mouths open wider. 
“Stop!” I scream at them. I feel tears swelling in my eyes, from the pain I feel on my body and in my soul. Why are they doing this to me?
I feel like the girl I used to be... is it 2003? No, it's not.
It's hard to remember. I'm married, I'm mother, I'm successful... I'm not that kid... or am I still?
They sting. Their words... hurt.
They clamber up my legs. Dig into my fur. Press their clay faces against my skin, rubbing against me as if trying to absorb something — or leave something behind.
Every touch feels hungry. Every voice feels like a punch.
“You don’t belong.”
“You never did.”


The Glasses

My hand scrabbles blindly along the shelf behind me. I grab something.
The broken glasses.
I jam them onto my face with shaking fingers. The left lens fractures the world into a kaleidoscope of wrong angles. The right shows the trembling reality.
Through the broken lens, the figurines’ bodies warp, stretching, taking up distorted version of the real people who hurt me. Shadows of my haters, shadows of my pains.
My brain tries to hold both truths.
"You're not real," I try to utter, but my voice isn't collaborating. "You're not here."
"You're difficult, Edgecombe."
"...yes. I am," I admit. "And... I will always be..."
Something in the barn screams.
"...I am enough," I manage to say. "I am." I start crying. "For myself," I sob. And it's true. I am enough. "For my family." I am enough for me. For them.
The figurines recoil, bodies spasming, voices warping into static. As if they didn't want to hear the truth.
I wrench myself free, stumbling backwards until my back hits the main door. It stings, but the physical pain grounds me.
This is my reality. I'm not their prey.
I turn myself around and slam the door open, then I run outside.
I let the sunlight wash over me, feeling the cold air brush my hair and my fur. I fall on my knees, panting, gasping for air, before turning my head towards the study.
The short building seems to swell slightly, like a lung trying to breathe.
Then everything stops. I can't see it, but... I feel it.
Silence.
I try to stand on my feet and get back inside, one trembling step at a time.
The figurines have collapsed into inert lumps on the floor.
My breath shakes out of me in shuddering gasps.
The glasses fall from my face and shatter completely on the floor.


Aftermath

I wake up. Did I sleep?
When did I-?
I'm sitting on the floor. Upright. More or less.
Clay smudged up my arms, blood dried on my cheek.
“Lils—? What happened? Jesus—your hands—”
Miles.
I look at him.
His face swims in and out of clarity without my glasses.
The light behind him looks too bright.
“I broke my glasses,” I whisper.
He helps me to my feet.
Scoops up the remains of the frames.
“Lils… both lenses are... utterly destroyed. What did you do, jump on them?” he says. But I feel the fear beneath the joke.
“I don’t remember.”
And I don’t.
I remember sound.
Voices.
Clay gripping me like something with intent.
But not the moment everything stopped.
Not the moment the glasses died.
Miles steadies me and leads me outside.
I keep my eyes fixed ahead, away from any reflective surface.
We ride home by his car. At home, he cleans my cuts. He brings me my spare glasses. He kisses me on the cheek and prepares tea for me.
I put them on.
The world sharpens.
Everything looks normal.
I sigh. It's over.
Is it?
Will it?
