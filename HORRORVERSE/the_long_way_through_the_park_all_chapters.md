# The Long Way Through the Park — Chapter 1: Stiffness

Asim first noticed the stiffness at his desk, not on a run and not on a stage.

The office chairs at the streaming support centre were meant to swivel and yield, some ergonomic committee’s idea of mercy, but his had developed a stubborn notch in the backrest. Every time he leaned away from the screen, something inside the mechanism caught and pushed back against his spine, a small refusal that grew louder with each hour of the late shift.

He shifted again, rolling his shoulders until his blazer pulled tight across his chest. The headset cushioned his ears in their worn faux leather, warm and slightly damp from eight hours of other people’s problems. On-screen, a chat window blinked with a producer in Manchester insisting that their upload had “just disappeared for no reason”. In the corner of the monitor, a corporate wellness email pulsed unread: TAKE A MINDFUL MOMENT. YOUR BODY WILL THANK YOU.

His body was not thanking him. His knees ached from staying bent at the same angle, his lower back throbbed with a dull, undramatic pain. When the floor manager called a break, the other agents stood with little groans and stretches; Asim pushed away from the desk and felt something resist, as if the chair wanted to keep him seated.

He laughed under his breath and forced himself upright. Pins and needles flared down his thighs. The office was a low hum of fluorescent light and distant printers, the air stale with coffee and microwaved pasta. Outside the sealed windows, the Westway roared, a constant tide of headlights sweeping towards the estates.

On his way to the tiny kitchen, he caught his reflection in the dark glass. African Wild Dog markings dulled by the office lighting, shirt collar slightly askew, tie loosened a notch, headset still hanging round his neck like an apology. His tail, cramped against the back of the chair all evening, twitched once and then stayed low. His throat felt dry in a way he recognised from bad rehearsals, the muscles at the base of his tongue tired from using his voice for apologies instead of songs.

“You’re walking like an old man,” Toby, his boyfriend, had said that morning, watching him wrestle his feet into trainers by the door. “Maybe you should let me book you in with my physio. Your body’s your instrument, remember.”

“I don’t need a physio,” Asim had protested, breath already a little tight from leaning down. “I need fewer escalations and a better chair. I sing for hours, remember? I’ll stretch at soundcheck.”

Now, making instant coffee while the vending machine shuddered, he thought of Toby on a shoot at the surgery last month, cheerful among the dog-eared leaflets and the laminated posters about weight and blood pressure, rearranging them so they’d look less miserable in the campaign photos. Toby loved that work, the way design could take people’s fear and turn it into something clear. Asim loved that about him, the steadiness, the way he could put his paws on someone’s shoulders and talk them down from panic even when the brief was only fonts and colour palettes.

He wrapped both hands around the hot paper cup, feeling the warmth seep into his fingers. When he straightened, something in his lower back protested again, a dry pinch. It wasn’t fear, not yet. Just annoyance, embarrassment at the thought of groaning the way his older uncles did when standing up from the sofa.

On the walk home along Harrow Road, the city had that late-spring chill that insinuated itself through his shirt. He lengthened his stride automatically to keep warm, but his knees complained at the change. The pavements were uneven, old tarmac patched and repatched, puddles catching the orange glow of the streetlights. Buses hissed at stops, spilling out people with tired eyes and lanyards.

In the estate courtyard, someone had propped open the main door with a parcel. The smell of fried onions drifted from the second floor, mingling with bleach and the faint mineral damp of the concrete stairwell. Asim climbed slowly, one paw on the rail to pull his weight up. The stiffness travelled differently now, a band across his hips.

Inside their flat, Toby was on the sofa in joggers and an oversized T-shirt, watching some medical drama with the sound down and the subtitles racing along the bottom of the screen. He looked up immediately, ears pricking.

“You’re limping,” he said, remote already in hand to pause the show.

“I am not limping,” Asim replied, dropping his backpack by the door. The move sent a small shock up his back that he tried not to wince at. “I am… favouring one side.”

Toby snorted. “That’s what limping is, babe.”

Asim rolled his eyes and crossed the room, each step negotiated. The flat was small but theirs: sofa sagging in the middle, second-hand coffee table with ring marks, a guitar propped by the radiator with his folded prayer mat tucked between it and the wall, a stack of community flyers on the shoe rack—Stray support meeting, tenants’ association, mutual aid food drop-offs. On the kitchen counter, a Tupperware of something fragrant and tomato-based waited with two plates.

“You’ve been sitting in that chair again,” Toby said, watching the way Asim eased himself down onto the sofa, careful to lower rather than flop. “You need to stretch more. Move in between calls.”

“We’re not supposed to leave our stations when we’re in queue,” Asim said. “Besides, the queue never ends.”

Toby shifted closer and rested a paw on his thigh. The touch was warm, familiar, an anchor. “Your body doesn’t care about the queue.”

“Tell that to my manager,” Asim muttered, but the line lacked heat.

They ate in companionable silence, the TV show playing again with low sound. Doctors in unnaturally crisp scrubs rushed down corridors, barking orders, saving lives in forty-three minutes plus adverts. Asim’s back ached and then settled, a background hum. His voice, his real work, stayed in his throat like an unsung note.

By the time they crawled into bed, the stiffness had sunk deeper. When he lay on his side, it flared along his spine. When he rolled onto his back, his knees complained. Toby, half-asleep, draped an arm across his chest and mumbled, “You should let me help. Proper stretches. Or… something.”

Asim stared at the ceiling, tracing the hairline cracks in the plaster. Somewhere under the floor, a neighbour’s washing machine thumped into a spin cycle. Outside, a siren wailed, rose, faded.

He shifted again, searching for a position where his body did not argue. It was only discomfort, he told himself. Everyone’s joints started talking more in their late thirties. It was normal. It had nothing to do with fear, with the unspoken panic that had lived in his chest since the past few years of bad headlines and worse policies, since the protests and the counter-protests, since the subtle tightening around the borders of every life he cared about.

He closed his eyes and tried to breathe past the stiffness, counting the seconds of each inhale like a metronome. Tomorrow would be work again, another shift under the fluorescent lights. He would complain to Toby, joke about being old, promise to do some stretches.

He did not yet know that the problem was not that he could not move.

It was that, very soon, he would not be able to stop.

# The Long Way Through the Park — Chapter 2: The Present

The gift arrived on a Tuesday, wrapped in brown paper and Tobias-level enthusiasm.

Asim found it on the kitchen table when he stumbled out of the bedroom, still half in the grip of a dream about dropped calls and a studio session that wouldn’t end. The flat was full of morning light, that thin London brightness that made everything look honest and a little unforgiving. Outside, buses grumbled along the main road; someone in the courtyard was arguing in Polish over a mobile.

Toby stood by the kettle, tail flicking in a quick, pleased rhythm. He was still in his work clothes from the graphic design studio, pale blue shirt rolled to the elbows, yesterday’s ink smudged faintly on his fingers where he’d been making adjustments to a poster. His fur was flattened in one patch where his headphones had sat late into the night while he exported files.

“Open it,” he said, nodding at the parcel. “Before your brain boots fully and you start checking emails.”

“I don’t check emails before coffee,” Asim protested, but his hand was already on the paper. The stiffness in his back flared when he bent, a reminder of yesterday’s shift, of the countless days before that. He ignored it and peeled the tape back carefully, the way his mother had taught him when paper was to be saved and reused.

Inside lay a small, matte-black box with a glossy logo he recognized from adverts on the Tube: a stylised runner, mid-stride, headphones blazing neon. The slogan was embossed on the lid—RUNYOURCITY.

He looked up. “Toby…”

“Before you say anything,” Toby said quickly, setting two mugs on the counter, “this is not a hint. Or a criticism. Or a ‘new year, new you’ thing, even though it is, technically, a new year and you are, technically, you.”

“Good to know my identity is confirmed,” Asim said dryly, but his throat had gone tight. Gifts still did that sometimes. After the years when presents meant strings—polite obligation, a phone call afterwards in the right tone, evidence of compliance—it was strange to be given things with no condition except his existence. His parents still sent Eid money addressed only to him, as if Toby were a lodger rather than his boyfriend; there were no cards that said both their names in the same careful script.

“It’s just…” Toby hesitated, searching for a path through the sentence. “You complain about your back and your knees and the chair and the buses, and then you sit all day at work and then you sit at home because you’re exhausted. And movement helps. We both know that. But I also know if I say ‘go for a run’ you’ll dig your claws in and say you’re not that kind of anthro.”

Asim opened the box. Inside, nestled in moulded cardboard, were wireless earbuds in a smooth charging case and a glossy card with a QR code. The text underneath promised “personalized coaching, adaptive audio, real-time motivation”. A trial code was scrawled in ballpoint at the bottom, the writing clearly human.

“So,” Toby went on, “I thought—what if it’s not about miles or pace or any of that rubbish. What if it’s just… someone in your ear who isn’t a customer, who reminds you to breathe and unclench your jaw and maybe move faster than a shuffle sometimes.” He scratched the back of his neck, ears dipping. “I know you miss walking home from gigs. The feeling of the city moving with you.”

That landed. For a moment, Asim was back in 2013, spilling out of The Basement with his bandmates, sweat drying under his shirt, the air outside thick with cigarette smoke and night buses and possibility. The ten years since then had rearranged themselves into something smaller: pandemic closures, shuttered venues, the slow bleed of austerity. The city was still there, but it had stopped belonging to them in quite the same way.

He turned the earbuds case over in his paw. It was surprisingly heavy, reassuring. “How much did this cost?”

“Less than the physio,” Toby said. “More than a packet of crisps.”

“Tobias,” Asim warned.

“I got a discount through a health-campaign client,” Toby admitted. “Corporate wellness partnership. They want us to recommend it to people who sit all day and complain about their backs but refuse to do physio exercises.”

“So I’m a case study now,” Asim said, but there was no real bite. The idea of something structured, some ritual that was not just the commute between screens, tugged at him.

Toby stepped closer, resting his hips against the table. “You don’t have to like running,” he said. “You don’t have to become one of those people who post times and split graphs and pictures of their shoes. But I’ve seen how you are after we walk along the canal on Sundays. How your shoulders drop, how you stop grinding your teeth. I just… I want that for you more than once a week.”

Asim swallowed. “You’re sure this isn’t about…”

“About what?” Toby frowned, genuinely puzzled.

He gestured vaguely at his own body. Roundness around the belly that hadn’t been there in 2013, the way his shirts pulled tighter across his chest, the softness at the base of his neck where fur gathered when he slumped. “About me needing to take up less space.”

Understanding clicked. Toby’s expression shifted, went soft. “Absolutely not,” he said firmly. “You can take up as much space as you like. I like your space. I want more of your space, ideally on me.” He leaned in and pressed a kiss to Asim’s jaw. “This is about your back not seizing up every time you stand up from the sofa. And about you getting to feel your body doing something it’s good at, instead of just enduring chairs.”

The earnestness embarrassed and warmed Asim in equal measure. He made a noncommittal noise, the one he used when agreeing without wanting to be seen as easy.

“I’ll try it,” he said, which, between them, meant yes.

Toby’s tail thumped once against the cupboard. “Good. There’s an app. Of course there’s an app. I already set up your account—”

“You what?”

“—with your permission in advance, which I am now receiving retroactively.” Toby grinned, unrepentant. “It syncs with your phone. You choose a coach voice and a difficulty level and whether you want music or ambient sound or spooky birdsong. They started a new series this month—urban routes, history of the parks, that kind of thing. Thought it might tempt you.”

Asim imagined himself trotting through Hyde Park—or at least along the outer edge, where he wouldn’t have to dodge as many tourists—listening to some smooth-voiced narrator talk about Victorian bandstands and protest marches. The image was both ridiculous and… not unpleasant.

“First run after work?” Toby asked. “I can meet you at Marble Arch with water and a smug expression.”

“Let’s not get ahead of ourselves,” Asim said. His back twinged as if in agreement. “We’ll see how bad today’s queue is.”

But when he left the flat an hour later, earbuds case in his pocket next to his Oyster card, he felt something quieter than excitement and more dangerous than indifference. A tiny shift in the day’s shape. A small, hopeful glitch in the routine.

On the bus, he downloaded the app over patchy 4G and watched the progress bar inch across the cracked screen. The interface was all clean lines and aspirational photos of people who looked like they slept eight hours a night. When it asked for his goals, he hesitated, then tapped “feel better in my body” instead of “run a race”.

The app responded with a cheerful chime: GREAT CHOICE. LET’S TAKE THE LONG WAY HOME.

Asim snorted, sliding the phone back into his pocket as the bus lurched past a row of shuttered shops and a payday lender glowing blue in the grey morning. He told himself he was doing this to humour Toby, to have something to report over dinner.

He agreed too quickly, he thought later. But by then, the voice in his ear had already started counting down.
# The Long Way Through the Park — Chapter 3: First Run

The first run began at the edge of the office car park, between a lamppost and a skip that always smelled faintly of fried chicken and printer toner.

Asim stood there in his work trousers and an old band T-shirt, the one with the faded Venus in Furs logo, feeling vaguely ridiculous. His trainers were clean in the way that said they had been bought more with optimism than use. Cars pulled out of the lot in tired succession, their drivers avoiding eye contact with the anthro loitering by the bins.

He popped open the earbuds case. A tiny green light blinked awake. The buds fitted into his ears with a soft click, muting the world to a padded hush. The app on his phone chimed.

WELCOME BACK, ASIM, the screen read in cheerful font. TODAY: EPISODE 1 — START WHERE YOU ARE.

“That’s very philosophical for a Tuesday,” he murmured.

He selected it before he could change his mind. A three-two-one countdown flashed. Then a voice slid into his ears—low, androgynous, with that professional warmth that could sell anything from cereal to mortgages.

“Hi, I’m your coach,” it said. “We’re going to take this at your pace. Literally. Today’s about getting used to the rhythm. No pressure. No performance. Just you, your city, and the sound of your own breath.”

He took a few tentative steps, phone zipped into the inside pocket of his blazer. The voice guided him through a minute of standing still, noticing where his weight fell on his feet, how his shoulders sat in their sockets.

“Notice any stiffness,” it said. “Any resistance. We’re not judging. We’re just paying attention.”

His back throbbed in time with his heartbeat. His knees felt like they had forgotten how to be straight. He shifted, embarrassed by the intimacy of the inventory.

“Good,” the voice said, as if responding to his discomfort. “Now we’re going to start with a gentle walk. No need to hurry. Just find a pace where your body feels… curious.”

Curious was not the word he would have chosen for the way his thighs complained, but he set off anyway, crossing the car park and turning onto the main road. The evening had settled into that particular London grey-blue, the sky low over the tower blocks. Buses growled past, the smell of diesel and hot brakes mixing with takeaway steam from a chicken shop.

He was acutely aware of being seen. Every reflective surface—the dark windows of office buildings, the glass of bus shelters, the screen of his own phone—seemed to throw his image back at him. Big anthro body, awkward arm swing, ears pricked too high, tail not sure whether to tuck or wag.

“If you’re feeling self-conscious,” the coach’s voice said, uncannily well-timed, “you’re not alone. Most people feel weird the first time they move differently in public. Here’s the thing: London’s seen stranger things than you walking home. Let the city carry some of that attention.”

He snorted, caught between irritation and reluctant amusement. Ahead, the pavement funneled past a row of shops—a vape place, a newsagent, a betting shop with its windows full of screens. Two teenagers in puffer jackets leaned against a shutter, phones cupped in their hands like secret fires. They glanced up as he approached, then back down, disinterested.

The voice kept talking, an easy patter about rooting his feet, rolling his shoulders, loosening his jaw. After five minutes, a soft chime sounded. “If you’d like,” it said, “you can try a slow jog. Think of it as a slightly accelerated walk. If that feels like nonsense, keep walking. You’re still doing the work.”

He hesitated at the crossing, watching the traffic lights cycle through their colours. His body hummed with a wired, restless energy he usually poured into late-night scrolling. He thought of Toby’s face that morning, earnest with hope. He thought of the queue, the stiff chair, the way his legs had tingled when he stood.

“Fine,” he muttered. “Slightly accelerated walk.”

The green man lit up. He stepped off the kerb and let his stride lengthen, letting momentum pull him forward. His heart responded instantly, picking up speed. His breath went from comfortable to noticeable within seconds, chest tight under his T-shirt.

The route the app suggested wasn’t complicated: down to the canal, along the towpath, then up towards Paddington and on to Hyde Park if he felt like it. Familiar geography, rearranged. As he trotted down the ramp to the water, the air changed. It cooled, dampened, took on a tang of algae and metal.

On the towpath, cyclists glided by with curt bells, commuters in suits marched with their heads down, a jogger in expensive gear overtook him without a glance. Asim’s footfalls thudded heavier than theirs, a four-beat pattern of pads on tarmac. His knees protested, but there was a strange relief in the protest. At least this pain felt earned.

“Nice,” the voice said in his ear. “Check in with your shoulders. Are they up around your ears? Let them drop. Good. Notice the rhythm of your steps. You don’t have to change it. Just listen.”

He listened. The slap of his feet, the rasp of his breath, the soft hiss of bikes on wet concrete. Above, trains rattled over viaducts. The canal water lay dark and flat, reflecting fragments of sky and graffiti. A group of kids lounged on the opposite bank, speakers leaking tinny drill beats into the dusk.

When he reached the bridge near Little Venice, the app chimed again. “If you’re near an entrance to a park or quieter street,” the coach said, “this is a good place to turn towards it. Not because parks are ‘better’—every street has its own beauty—but because variety keeps the body and mind awake.”

He could have stayed on the canal, followed it all the way to his estate. Instead, without quite deciding to, he turned up towards Edgware Road, letting the city shuffle its deck. The pavements here were wider, the air crowded with the smell of shawarma and shisha, exhaust and perfume. Arabic signs glowed above shopfronts. A kid ran past with a football under his arm, shouting to a friend.

Asim’s chest burned. Sweat dampened the fur at his neck and lower back. He became aware of how people stepped aside—or didn’t—as he approached, the microcalculations of shared space. A woman with a pushchair glanced up, saw him, and angled without breaking stride. A man in a suit walked straight towards him until the last second, forcing Asim into the gutter. He swallowed his irritation, saving his breath.

“You might be getting tired,” the voice said. “That’s okay. You can walk whenever you like. There’s no prize for suffering.”

He slowed automatically, the transition from jog to walk awkward, like changing gear in a car that had forgotten how to clutch. His lungs flooded with cool air. The burning in his thighs receded to a dull roar.

“Notice how quickly your body recovers,” the coach said. “You’re more adaptable than you think.”

By the time he reached Marble Arch, the episode was winding down. “That’s enough for today,” the voice said. “You did it. You moved. You listened. Next time we’ll build on this. For now, find a way to mark the end of this run. A stretch. A drink of water. A view.”

He stopped under the arch itself, traffic circling around in impatient loops. Beyond, Hyde Park’s winter trees spiked against the sky, branches bare and intricate. The park gates stood open, dark beyond them.

He checked his phone. The app cheerfully informed him that he’d covered just under three kilometres in twenty-five minutes, with generous pauses. The map showed his route in neon yellow, a hesitant curve through the city.

He had expected to feel ashamed at the numbers, at how short the distance was, how slow the pace. Instead, unexpectedly, he felt… something like pride. The stiffness in his back had eased, replaced by a general hum of fatigue that was almost pleasant.

His phone buzzed with a message from Toby.

How’s my favourite case study? Still alive?

Asim took a photo of Hyde Park’s gates, the last of the daylight catching on the ironwork, and sent it with a single word.

Moving.

On the way home, earbuds back in, he replayed the cool-down section of the episode. The voice talked about recovery, about how rest and effort were part of the same pattern. Asim leaned against the bus window and watched the city slide past in fragments—off-licences, kebab shops, lit windows of upstairs flats where other people were cooking, arguing, existing.

He finished sooner than he’d expected. The run, the episode, the sense of beginning. He told himself it had been almost easy.

The real difficulty, he thought, would be doing it again.

# The Long Way Through the Park — Chapter 4: Episodes, Not Miles

By the end of the week, Asim was measuring time in voices rather than minutes.

“I’ll be back after two episodes,” he told Toby on Thursday, toeing his trainers on by the door. “Three if the queue was bad.”

“So, four,” Toby said, glancing up from the kitchen table where he was sorting through a fan of print proofs, margins covered in notes. “Given the state you came home in.”

“Three,” Asim insisted, though his shoulders were still tight from the afternoon’s escalation. A customer in Surrey had called him everything but his name because their streaming box kept buffering. The headset’s plastic had dug into the base of his ears. Even now, with it hanging on its hook by the router, he almost felt it there, a ghost pressure.

On the app, Episode 3 blinked, ready. ROUTES AND RUTS, the title said. DURATION: 32 MINUTES. He liked the bluntness of that: a promise of an ending.

He slipped the earbuds in and stepped out into the corridor, the coach’s voice greeting him as he descended the concrete stairwell. “Welcome back,” it said. “Today we’re paying attention to patterns—how your body gets used to certain routes and speeds, and how you can gently interrupt that.”

Outside, the estate courtyard smelled of wet tarmac and the faint sweetness of someone’s laundry detergent. Children’s bikes were abandoned against the low walls, their plastic wheels beaded with rain. Asim started with a walk to the gate, letting his body warm, his breath settle into something he could build on.

He found, after a few runs, that he preferred leaving from home rather than from work. The streets here were messier, more familiar, full of the clutter of his everyday life—corner shop, bus stop, takeaway with the inconsistent chips. The app suggested a range of routes; he ignored most of them, carving his own path through the grid.

Still, certain turns repeated themselves. A left at the laundrette, where the smell of steam and detergent billowed out onto the pavement. A right by the closed pub with its peeling sign and boarded windows. Up past the primary school railings, where posters about healthy lunches flapped in the wind. His body began to anticipate these points, his feet angling before his brain finished deciding.

“Notice the moments you don’t have to think about the next step,” the coach murmured as he trotted past a row of wheelie bins. “Habits are efficient. They carry us when we’re tired. But they can also become invisible. Today, let’s see if we can become aware of your autopilot.”

He became aware of it when he realized that, without planning to, he’d taken the same turn towards the canal for three days in a row. The curve of the towpath had imprinted itself on his muscles; his knees felt the slight tilt before his eyes registered the water.

The app’s map afterwards showed his routes in neon strands. They looked like scribbles at first, but patterns emerged: a cluster around his estate, a loop along the canal, a more tentative line towards Hyde Park. The distance totals meant less to him than the episodes. Episode 3: done. Episode 4: a day when his legs had felt heavy but his breath easy. Episode 5: the one where it had started raining halfway through and he’d run faster, laughing at the indignity of wet fur.

Some evenings, the city blurred. After hours on the phones, the streets could feel like an extension of the queue—faces, needs, demands. The coach’s voice cut through that, a thread he could follow. It narrated not just his movements but his attention.

“What can you hear that’s more than traffic?” it asked one night as he passed under the Westway, concrete pillars rising like tree trunks around him. He listened and picked out the rhythm of a train over tracks, the call of a vendor at a food truck, the drip of water from a leaking gutter.

“What can you smell?” it asked another time near Paddington, and he catalogued diesel, coffee, rain on hot tarmac, the faint metallic tang from the canal.

His body began to anticipate the coach’s questions. After a week, he found himself noticing things before being prompted: the way his right foot landed slightly heavier than his left; the micro-tilts of the pavement where tree roots pushed up through the concrete; the slight easing in his back around the ten-minute mark, like a knot unspooling.

With that awareness came a strange sense of safety. As if the map of his own body on the city was something he could rely on.

Routes repeated. He took the same shortcuts without thinking: through the alley behind the supermarket where the delivery trucks idled; across the zebra crossing by the library; down the side street where a mural of a fox watched over parked cars. His body began to predict the sequence of footsteps and turns the way he once predicted chord progressions.

He didn’t think of it as compulsion. Not yet. It was just how you learned a place—by walking it until your feet could do it for you.

“You might notice that your mind wanders on familiar routes,” the coach said in Episode 5, as he cut along a back road towards the park. “That’s okay. Let it. But every so often, come back to your breath. Back to your feet. Back to now.”

He came back to now when buses roared too close, when a cyclist flashed past with a curse, when a car window rolled down and someone shouted something crude about his fur and his body and the way it shook when he ran. Those moments yanked him out of the rhythm, dropped him back into the old scripts—threat assessment, de-escalation, the calculation of how much space he was allowed.

Then the episode would end, and the voice would say, “Nice work. You showed up. That matters.”

He came home flushed and damp, tail loose with exhausted satisfaction. Toby met him at the door with a towel and a glass of water, eyes scanning him for injuries with the same focus he used when checking a layout for mistakes.

“Episodes, not miles,” Asim said, half-joking, when Toby asked how far he’d gone.

“Whatever gets you out there,” Toby replied. “As long as you come back.”

Asim always came back. The routes looped, curved, returned. The city unfolded and refolded around his familiar landmarks. He trusted that as long as he moved within those patterns—canal, side streets, the park on its edges—there would be an end point he recognized: their estate, their door, Toby’s face.

It never occurred to him that a route could keep stretching without getting further away.
# The Long Way Through the Park — Chapter 5: The Nice Loop

The app called it “The Nice Loop”.

Episode 7 loaded with a small animation of trees and a lake. ROUTE: HYDE PARK SHORT CUT, it said. DURATION: 28–32 MINUTES, DEPENDING ON PACE. The description promised “gentle paths, city history, and a calm loop that brings you back where you started.”

“They’ve clearly never tried to cross Bayswater Road at rush hour,” Asim muttered, standing at the pedestrian island with traffic surging around him. His breath misted faintly in the cool evening air. The sky over the park was the colour of dishwater.

The coach’s voice slid into his ears. “Today we’re running with the trees,” it said. “London’s lungs. We’ll use Hyde Park as a way to soften the city edges.”

He’d been inside the park countless times—picnics with the band when that was still a thing, protests that started at Speakers’ Corner, late-night walks when he couldn’t sleep. Hyde Park was never soft. It was flat and exposed, full of tourists and police horses and unpredictable cyclists. But there were quieter corners if you knew where to look, and the idea of cutting through instead of skirting around appealed to him. A long diagonal instead of a rectangle of roads.

The traffic light flicked red. He crossed with the herd, dropping into a slow jog as soon as his feet hit the path. The park opened around him, big sky and bare branches, the city noise muffled as if someone had turned down a fader.

“Notice the change underfoot,” the coach said. “Tarmac to gravel. Gravel to dirt. Your body knows the difference.”

It did. The grit of the path gave slightly with each step, gentler on his knees. The smell changed too—less exhaust, more wet earth and leaf mould, a faint whiff of horse. The Serpentine lay to his right, a sheet of dark water broken by the occasional duck. Joggers in neon tights and serious expressions ran past, their headphones glowing under woollen hats.

The route the app suggested was simple: enter near Marble Arch, follow the outer path towards Speaker’s Corner, loop down past the Serpentine, then cut back across to the starting point. A neat oval. A promise of return.

He settled into a rhythm, breath syncing with the voice’s cues. “Two minutes easy, one minute steadier,” it said. “Think of it as waves. Effort rising, falling. You’re not fighting the park; you’re running with it.”

He snorted at the idea of being in harmony with Hyde Park’s squirrel population, but the intervals did make sense. His legs warmed, his back loosened. At the top of a small rise, he could see over the trees to the office towers beyond, their windows lit like grids on a server rack.

Benches punctuated the path at regular intervals—dark metal and slats, some with memorial plaques. He’d never paid them much attention before. Today, they seemed like markers in a code he didn’t yet know how to read. One had fresh flowers tied to it; another, a takeaway coffee cup abandoned on the seat. A lamppost leaned slightly off-true beside an older oak, the roots pushing up against the path’s edge.

One reply, from a user with no profile picture, was short.

DON’T LET IT SET YOUR PACE, it read. AND DON’T STOP INSIDE.

His fingers hovered over the keyboard. He wanted to respond. To ask, “Inside what?” To say, “I didn’t stop. That’s the problem.” To demand, “How do you know?”

He didn’t reply. The thread was three years old. Whatever conversation had happened had already happened. Still, the words lodged in his mind, joining the flat voice’s instructions in an unwanted chorus.

At home, he watched his own sleep data glitch. The night after the run, his tracker recorded his sleep as “restless,” full of micro-wakings. It did not record the moment he surfaced gasping from a dream of running on treadmills that spun under his feet without ever moving him forward, watched by rows of blank-faced silhouettes.

The graphs and charts were all so confident. Smooth curves, neat peaks. The numbers knew nothing of the smell of bog rot or the feel of roots under his skin.

He tried, once, to tell Lillian about it over a video call. She listened, eyes narrowing in the way they did when she was turning something over and deciding whether it was material for a song.

“So the park ate you,” she said eventually. “And then spat you out in an underpass.”

“That’s one reading,” he said dryly.

She sipped her tea. “London does that,” she said. “In more ways than one.” She paused. “You should write it down.”

“I am, apparently,” he said, gesturing at his phone. “In code and maps and heart rate graphs.”

“No,” she said. “In your words. Before the data decides what happened.”

The idea made him uneasy. He’d always resisted turning his own worst experiences into art. It felt too close to extraction. But he also knew how stories hardened around whatever records existed first.

That night, instead of opening the app, he opened a blank document. He typed: The day the coach’s voice cut out in Hyde Park, time stalled at 19:11 and the ground remembered it used to be a bog. The sentence looked melodramatic and inadequate and, in places, not technically accurate. He left it anyway.

He did not go for a run.

His body, contrary to his fears, did not fall apart without three weekly episodes. His back complained more. His knees ached differently. The restless energy he’d been burning off at night had nowhere to go. It started to manifest as irritability in calls, as impatience in meetings, as a gnawing itch under his skin.

“You need to move,” Toby said gently one evening, watching him pace the kitchen while pasta boiled. “Not because of the app. Because of you.”

“I know,” Asim said. “I just… don’t trust the routes right now.”

“We’ll make our own,” Toby said. “No parks. No loops. Straight lines only.”

“London doesn’t do straight lines,” Asim said, but the idea appealed.

He closed the running app. He didn’t delete it. Not yet. Some part of him wanted the record there, inaccurate as it was. A glitch in the archive, a small rebellion against neat metrics.

The data, unbothered, continued to accrue. Steps counted as he walked to the Tube. Time-on-task logged as he handled calls. Sleep cycles recorded his tossing and turning. Somewhere in the aggregated mass of numbers, his anomaly was a blip small enough to ignore.

He knew better than to mistake that for insignificance.

# The Long Way Through the Park — Chapter 19: 404

The episode didn’t exist anymore.

A week after the run, on a quiet Sunday afternoon when the rain made venturing outside unappealing, Asim opened the app out of a grim sort of curiosity. His body still ached in places; his knees complained on stairs; he hadn’t run since. But the part of him that had spent years troubleshooting networks wanted to poke the system that had misbehaved.

He navigated to the episode list. The URBAN LOOP series was still there, glossy thumbnails of cityscapes overlaid with motivational slogans. Episode 17: CITY INTERVALS. Episode 18: URBAN LOOP. Episode 19: NIGHT CIRCUIT.

He tapped Episode 18.

A message popped up, white text on a grey background.

CONTENT TEMPORARILY UNAVAILABLE.

PLEASE CHOOSE ANOTHER EPISODE.

He stared at it. Tapped again. Same message. There was no further explanation—no “under maintenance,” no “licensing issue,” no “sorry about that.” Just absence, blandly presented.

“Huh,” he said.

“What?” Toby asked from the sofa, where he was half-watching an old crime drama.

“The episode’s gone,” Asim said. “The one from that night.”

Toby paused the TV. “Gone how? Deleted?”

“Unavailable,” Asim said. “Like a 404. Everything else plays. Just this one doesn’t.”

He tapped Episode 17; it loaded immediately, the coach’s familiar voice greeting him. He backed out before it could say more than his name. Episode 19 started too, jaunty and unaware.

Episode 18 remained stubbornly absent, an empty slot in a grid.

For a moment, he wondered if this was targeted. If some background process had flagged his run as anomalous and quarantined the content on his account. He logged out, logged back in. Same result. He opened the app on his work phone—same message. He searched online.

On the app’s subreddit, a few people grumbled about a “missing episode” in the URBAN LOOP series. Most assumed a licensing issue. A pinned post from the company’s community manager apologised for “temporary unavailability of certain content due to a technical update” and promised a fix “as soon as possible.”

No one mentioned parks stretching or paths woven under their feet. No one described silence that ate sound.

He scrolled further. A handful of older threads mentioned weird GPS traces in Hyde Park. One user posted a screenshot of their route that night—an endless spiral that never seemed to leave the park’s interior. The comments were mostly jokes about running in circles.

One reply, again from a user with no profile picture, read simply: IT DOES THAT SOMETIMES. DON’T WORRY ABOUT IT.

Asim’s hands tightened around his phone.

“If you delete the app,” Toby said carefully, “you don’t have to wonder what it’s doing.”

“It’s not about that,” Asim said. “It’s… evidence.”

“Of what?” Toby asked. “That their QA is rubbish?”

“That I didn’t imagine it,” Asim said. “Or that if I did, it’s a shared hallucination with their codebase.”

He knew the dangers of reading too much into corporate decisions. Companies pulled content all the time for reasons that had nothing to do with the experiences people attached to it. A bug, a complaint, a rebranding. Episode 18’s absence might mean nothing beyond an overworked dev toggling the wrong flag.

Still, the timing itched at him. The neatness of it. The way his own record of that night now pointed to a piece of content you could not replay.

He thought of broken links elsewhere. News articles taken down under legal threat. Tweets deleted after backlash. Government PDFs quietly updated, the original wording lost unless you’d archived it. Absence as a form of denial.

“You could write a review,” Toby suggested, half-teasing. “‘Went for a run, fell out of linear time, zero stars.’”

“They’d offer me a free month,” Asim said. “And a mindfulness track about accepting uncertainty.”

He closed the app. The icon remained on his home screen, a small square of aggressive optimism.

Later that night, lying in bed with the city’s sounds muffled by double glazing, he tried to recall the coach’s exact last words before the cut. The sentence had been mid-flow, something about the sounds of the city softening. His memory wanted to fill in the end in that predictable cadence: “… as you find your own rhythm,” or “… as you tune into your breath.”

But when he let the scene play in his head, the cut came earlier than any of those script-complete options. A clean severing. As if the voice had been interrupted, not faded out.

“Maybe it hit something it wasn’t meant to,” he mused.

“Who, the coach?” Toby asked sleepily.

“The algorithm,” Asim said. “Whatever routes they built. They mapped the park, laid their own logic over it. But the ground had other ideas.”

He thought of undersea cables and shipping routes, of old myths about things in the deep that didn’t care about human charts. Cities layered their own lines on top—roads, tunnels, tube lines—but the older networks, of water and stone and rot, remained underneath.

Someone had told him once that Hyde Park had been a marsh before it was a royal hunting ground. Drained, landscaped, managed. The bog didn’t stop being itself just because someone put a path through it.

“404,” he murmured into the dark. “Not found. Or not yours to find.”

He slept, eventually, and dreamed of login screens that asked not for passwords but for consent to be path.

In the morning, the app’s icon was still there. He moved it to a folder labelled ARCHIVE and slid it to the last page of his home screen, out of sight but not erased.

He did not need it to remember.

# The Long Way Through the Park — Chapter 20: Narrowing

He rerouted without fully deciding to.

On the map, it looked simple. Instead of cutting through Hyde Park on his walks and errands, Asim took the streets around it. Bayswater Road, Park Lane, the maze of side streets near Marble Arch. Lines bent to accommodate a rectangular absence.

The first time he did it, a week after the collapse, he framed it as pragmatism.

“Tube’s a mess,” Toby said that morning, peering at his phone before work. “Signal failure at Edgware Road again. You might as well walk to Paddington; it’ll be faster.”

“I’ll go round the park,” Asim said. The words came out before he’d even thought them through.

He traced the route in his head: down their usual roads, up past the Lebanese bakeries and vape shops, then along the long, loud edge of Hyde Park, buses snarling, tourists wheeling suitcases. No need to enter. No need to risk whatever layers lay under the curated grass.

The city obliged. The pavements were crowded but predictable. Street vendors, buskers, people with selfie sticks, office workers in trainers. Sound washed over him—horns, engines, snatches of conversation. He could feel his body reacting to it, tensing in old ways, but the familiarity was reassuring. The noise meant he was back in human systems, however flawed.

At Speaker’s Corner, he paused at the railings. Beyond, the park stretched, green and open. Joggers dotted the paths, their bright clothes moving in steady lines. Dog walkers. Families. No warped canopy, no woven path. Just Hyde Park, performing normality.

He felt a physical flinch at the idea of stepping through the gate.

“No,” he said quietly, and kept walking.

The avoidance spread. If someone at work suggested a lunchtime walk “in the park to clear our heads,” he demurred with an excuse about a call. When Lillian floated the idea of meeting near the Serpentine for a catch-up, he steered them to a café in Soho instead. He declined protests that planned to gather at Speakers’ Corner, volunteering for logistics elsewhere.

He still ran, eventually. The itch under his skin, the restlessness of his muscles, built to a point where sitting more felt unbearable. But he kept to concrete routes. Along Edgware Road to Kilburn. Down the canal in the opposite direction, away from the park, towards Alperton’s industrial sprawl. Around estates and along ring roads, where the geometry was dictated by property developers and traffic engineers rather than landscaped whims.

He ran without headphones.

At first, the absence of the coach felt like a missing limb. No countdowns, no prompts, no soothing script about curiosity and breath. Instead, he counted his own inhales and exhales. Four steps in, four steps out. He let bus engines and sirens and overheard arguments fill the space where the voice had been.

The city felt narrower. Not because there was less of it, but because parts of it were now off-limits. Routes he’d taken for granted became no-go zones. He flinched at casual mentions of Hyde Park, the way he used to flinch at unexpected references to Birmingham.

When friends suggested “We’ll take the Hyde Park shortcut,” he corrected them automatically. “It’s not a loop,” he said once, the words slipping out before he could catch them. “It doesn’t resolve.”

The friend—a human drummer he’d been depping with—blinked. “Okay,” he said slowly. “We can go round.”

Asim wanted to explain, to caution, to sketch out the contours of the wrongness he’d experienced. Instead, he shrugged. “Bad memories,” he said. It wasn’t untrue.

London, in 2023, had narrowed in other ways too. Protests met with heavier police presence. New laws about public order and noise. Friends leaving the city for cheaper rents, for better air, for places where their bodies felt slightly less surveilled.

His own world had been contracting for years—by economics, by policy, by the slow creep of fatigue. The park incident felt both like an outlier and like a culmination, the physical manifestation of a pattern he’d felt for a decade: spaces that looked open but weren’t, paths that punished stopping.

He adapted. It was what he did. He found pockets of air where he could—a quiet bench by the canal, a side street where the planes overhead sounded distant. He carved new loops that never quite closed, out-and-backs that let him see where he was going and where he’d return.

When people in the community mentioned starting Couch to 5K, he bit back the urge to warn them about route choices. He didn’t want to be the guy who told everyone the park was haunted. London did that to itself without his help.

Instead, he offered practical advice. “Stay aware of your body,” he said. “Don’t let an app push you past what feels right. Pause if you need to. Stopping isn’t failure.”

He meant it more broadly than anyone realised.

The memory of the woven path faded at the edges, softened by time, but the core remained: the sense of being used as a moving part in someone else’s system. The knowledge that he’d refused, at the end, to be repurposed.

In the flat, his running shoes lived by the door again—new ones, clean, soles still unscuffed. The old pair, after thorough washing, had gone out with the rubbish. He’d watched the bin lorry take them, a small, irrational satisfaction blooming as metal jaws crushed them indiscriminately.

“You’re glaring at the bin,” Toby observed one morning.

“We had a disagreement,” Asim said.

“Who won?”

“Jury’s out,” Asim said. “Ask me next time the city tries to sell me a shortcut.”

He still dreamt of trees sometimes. Not the warped canopy of that night, but the ordinary plane trees of his estate, their roots lifting the paving stones. In the dreams, he stood at the edge of the park, looking in. The paths stretched and narrowed and shifted. He did not enter.

He woke with his heart pounding and the taste of damp earth in his mouth. Then he rolled over, listened to the muffled hum of buses on Harrow Road, and let the city’s noise narrow the gap between worlds.
