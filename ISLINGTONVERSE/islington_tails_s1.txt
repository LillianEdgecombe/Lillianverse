EPISODE 1
The Mystery of the Vanishing Tupperware

It began, as so many domestic crises do, in the middle of an otherwise ordinary Thursday morning, with the sort of silence that only pointedly wasn't ordinary.
Lillian stood in the glow of the open refrigerator, wrapped in a silk robe that pulled slightly tight across her middle. Her left paw rested on the curve of her broad hip, one claw tapping against the side of the door, following a tune of her own. Her striped snout was inches from the shelves, eyes narrowed, searching. Somewhere behind her, the house creaked—perhaps in protest at the early hour, or at the quiet fury radiating into the kitchen from her large badger-shaped presence. 
The blue Tupperware was missing.
Not just any container—the blue Tupperware. The big one. The one that had traveled across Europe with her during her busking years. The one that still bore a gouge on its lid from an ill-fated trip through airport security in Lisbon. The one that last night had been filled by Miles with a lovingly prepared, structurally ambitious roast beef sandwich, meant to be Lillian’s lunch for a long day of private lessons and rehearsals.
Now it was gone.
“Miles,” she called out, voice rich and thunderous even at half-volume, “where’s my sandwich?”
From the garage came a brief clatter, a yelp, and then the sound of cautious, muffled footsteps.
Miles appeared a moment later, already in his grease-smeared apron, holding a pair of pliers and looking rather like someone who’d just been caught stealing time from God. He blinked behind his fogged protection goggles, trying not to look at her cleavage—which, given the angle and the way her robe had shifted, was more generous than usual.
“I put it in the fridge,” he said, approaching slowly, like a man who’d once offered a solution and lived to regret it. “Top shelf. Right side. Same place I always put your lunch.”
Lillian turned slightly, casting a wide shadow across the kitchen tiles. “It’s not there.”
Miles approached her and peered inside the fridge. He frowned. Then frowned harder.

“It’s... really not there,” he commented, to fill the growing, icy void between them.
She gave him a long, patient stare. “You think I made up a sandwich and gaslit myself about it?”
“No, no, never. Not with that particular sandwich. That was.. an architectural feat,” he said, proud of his creation like a modern Frankenstein.
She closed the fridge with a decisive clunk. “This is the third one this week.”
“I know,” he muttered, rubbing his forehead. “First it was the leftover curry. Then the shepherd's pie.”
“And now roast beef.” Her voice dropped to a whisper. “I swear, if Ramon’s been sneaking in again...”
“I changed the locks after the bread pudding incident.”
They both paused, eyes narrowing.
This wasn’t a one-off. A pattern was forming—food, vanishing overnight, no trace. No sauce stains. No crumbs. No suspicious smells. And most damning of all: no dirty Tupperware returned.
Sarah, their adopted daughter, was already up and sketching at the dining table, her sleek golden fur tucked into a hoodie three sizes too big. She said nothing, eyes fixed on her sketchbook.
“Mozzarella wouldn’t do this,” Miles mumbled, referring to the scruffy stray white cat who had started loitering around their back garden.
“He’s clever,” Lillian agreed. “But lacks opposable thumbs."
Miles grunted and rubbed his chin. “There’s only one thing for it.”
“Reinforced locks?”
“No.” He grinned. “Surveillance.”

That night, Miles dusted off an old motion-detecting micro-camera from his failed home security phase. After twenty minutes of fidgeting, programming, and accidentally recording his own left ear, he managed to mount the device in a discreet corner of the kitchen, high above a sideboard, aimed directly at the fridge and half-hidden by a jar of pickles that had to be kept away from prying hands.

He sat on the couch, right at Lillian's side. “Operation Sandwich Stakeout is a go,” he declared, tapping his tablet screen.
Lillian reclined on the couch with a heavy, pleased sigh, a blanket draped across her thighs. “If this turns out to be Penny, I’ll make her eat the couch.”
“Penny hates our couch,” he muttered.
“My thoughts exactly.”
At precisely 2:03 a.m., the tablet beeped loudly, waking them both up in the darkness of the room. Movement was caught on camera: the mystery unraveled.
Sarah crept into the kitchen like a whisper, moving with the fluidity of someone who had grown up in silence. She opened the fridge gently, extracted the missing blue Tupperware, and popped it open with practiced ease. Then, with remarkable calm, she wrapped the sandwich in a napkin, grabbed her hoodie, and slipped through the back door into the London night.
Lillian watched with arms folded across her ample chest, brows furrowed. Miles looked surprised—but not angry. Just puzzled, concerned. Even impressed, slightly, by the stealth of it.
“Why didn’t she just tell us?” he murmured.
Lillian exhaled. “We'll find out.”

Next morning, Sarah was up, sitting at the breakfast table with a cup of tea steaming between her paws. She looked like she hadn’t slept, but met their gaze calmly when they approached her.
“Mozzarella?” Lillian asked gently, taking the seat beside her. The wooden chair creaked audibly under her weight, as if greeting her with familiarity.
Sarah hesitated. She looked at both before replying. “He was starving. Like... you could see his bones.”
“And you thought feeding him without telling us was better than asking?” Miles asked, curious, not accusatory.
“I thought you'd say no.” She shrugged. “We don’t know where he came from. He might be sick.”
Lillian reached out and touched Sarah’s shoulder with the same tenderness she used when handling old records. “You did the right thing, sweetheart. Just not the right way.”
“We’ll take him to the vet,” Miles added. “Make sure he’s okay. And if he’s not... well, we’ll figure it out.”
Sarah looked up, eyes wide and just a little watery. “You’re not mad?”
Lillian smiled. “Mad? You saved a life with roast beef. That’s pretty on-brand for this family.”
Sarah laughed. It was short, shy—but real.

Later that afternoon, Mozzarella was curled on a blanket in the sunniest corner of the house. The vet had confirmed he was underweight, but healthy. Sarah had painted him a placemat. Miles had rigged him a tiny heated box in the corner of the laundry room. And Lillian, with her characteristic flair, declared him an official dependent and demanded he be included in all family group texts.
The blue Tupperware, slightly scratched but otherwise intact, returned the following day. Clean. Tucked neatly into the dishwasher.
And from then on, no one ever questioned a disappearing lunch again.
 
EPISODE 2
The Forbidden Shelf

There was a shelf in the living room of that Islington house, at the 37b of Leconfield road; a shelf that no one was allowed to touch, under the threat of melodramatic cries and/or punitive, prolonged silences.
It wasn’t locked. It didn’t gleam or buzz or radiate any strange aura. It wasn’t radioactive or alien in substance or spirit. Honestly, it looked like any other stretch of white-painted wood in the living room, nestled between a houseplant with droopy leaves which looked like it was always that close to dying and a bookshelf stacked with books about obscure folk instruments. But in this house, where every room was a kingdom and every drawer a time capsule, the Forbidden Shelf had power.
It belonged, wholly and unconditionally, to Lillian.
Sarah, in the naïveté of her youth, had asked about it once—not with words, but with a long, speculative stare in her eyes and a sketchpad in her hands. She’d sat across the room and drawn it from memory later that evening, giving it exaggerated shadows and teeth, like a beast lying in wait. She never mentioned it again. But the shelf lingered in her thoughts.
That Saturday, with Lillian out teaching a weekend vocal masterclass and Miles engrossed in rebuilding a coffee grinder in the garage, Sarah wandered into the living room with the sort of innocent aimlessness that, in teenagers, always masks intent.
She studied the shelf.
It wasn’t even that high—at most chest-level for her. The objects were arranged with deceptive randomness: an old silver locket, a cracked ceramic frog, a teeny chandelier with three candles, a concert badge from the 90s, and a single pink bootie, far too small to belong to anyone in the current household.
She wasn’t going to touch anything. She just wanted to see. Touching with her mind.
Then she heard it.
A creak. A deep, familiar groan of floorboards.
Miles.
She darted back to the couch, picked up the nearest magazine, and held it like a shield in front of her. Upside down.
The German Shepherd entered the living room with the dazed look of someone emerging from a mechanical fugue, covered in grease and holding an espresso mug that probably hadn’t seen soap since Tuesday.
“Morning,” he said, plopping on the couch beside her. It dipped heavily beneath him—despite being leaner than his wife, Miles had the dense build of a man—of a dog—who had wrestled with engine blocks most of his adult life.
“Morning,” Sarah replied dryly, eyes still on the shelf.
He followed her gaze attentively, then sighed. “Thinking about the shelf?”
“No,” Sarah replied, pretending to turn a page showing the photo of an upturned minimalist living room.
He sipped his coffee. “That’s a yes.”
Sarah fidgeted. “I didn’t touch anything.”
“I know. Because you’d be dead otherwise,” he replied casually. “And we wouldn’t be speaking right now.”
That drew a short laugh from her. “She ever tell you what’s on it?” she asked, finally putting the architecture and interior design magazine back on the short table beside the couch.
Miles scratched behind one ear, then leaned back, one arm draped over the back of the couch, the other still grasping the now-empty coffee mug. “Not in words. Not really. I figured it out after a while by myself.”
“Is it a shrine?”
“Sort of. It’s… Lillian’s private timeline. Each object’s from a moment no one else gets to claim. Before me. Before you. Just… hers.”
Sarah squinted. “But why keep it out in the open?”
Miles smiled gently. “So she remembers. And so we know it’s there. So we all know she had a past before all of us.”
Sarah was quiet for a while.
Miles let her sit with it, letting the ticking clock and occasional cat-scratch fill the silence.
“She never had kids,” Sarah said eventually. “Not until me.”
“Nope.”
“She ever wish she did?”
Miles looked up at the ceiling. “She wished she had you. That’s different.”
Sarah’s jaw twitched. “So you never talked about it?”
“It never happened,” said Miles, shrugging. “Until you, at least.”
She turned her gaze toward the sketch of the shelf, which she had left half-finished on the side table the night before. “I remember when I came to live with you. She told me I don’t have to earn my place here,” she muttered.
“You don’t. You already have your place.”
“But I still want to. Sometimes.”
“Why?”
This time, Sarah shrugged. “Because you took me in when I had nothing. When I was nothing. And… I keep thinking I want to… give back something.”
He leaned in, nudging her with his shoulder. “Then do it by living. By being weird. By being yourself. By drawing haunted bookshelves. That’s more than enough.”
The front door opened.
And then, like the rising tide of some ancient ocean, Lillian entered.
She was resplendent in a flowing lapis blouse that clung in all the wrong—and therefore right—places, her full hips sashaying across the entrance with authority. The buttons across her chest strained slightly as she moved, a soft testament to the power her body held. The moment she stepped inside, the temperature of the room shifted: warmer, brighter, louder.
She toed off her shoes and gave a hum of satisfaction. “I smell… grease. And teenage guilt.”
Sarah went crimson. Miles casually slipped the sketch of the shelf under a book.
“Everything all right?” Lillian asked, setting her bag down and undoing the top button of her blouse with a sigh. Her breasts relaxed, finally happy to move a little bit beneath her blouse.
“Fine,” Sarah chirped.
“Absolutely,” Miles added, just a little too quickly.
Lillian narrowed her eyes. Then, with theatrical suspicion, she crossed to the shelf. Her wide figure momentarily blocked the sunlight through the window, casting the collection in shadow. She adjusted a small candle that had tilted and smiled.
Then she turned back to them, beaming.
“Good. Because if anyone had touched this shelf…” Her grin turned wicked. “I would have sung every embarrassing lullaby I know and dedicated it to the culprit. In public. Amplified.”
Sarah let out a nervous laugh. Miles made the sign of the cross. They both knew she always meant her threats.

Later that night, Sarah slipped into the living room again. The shelf stood undisturbed, humming in its own quiet way. She didn’t touch it. But she did add something.
Tucked just behind the ceramic frog, so only Lillian would notice it, was a tiny drawing: three figures seated on a couch. A massive badger, a tall shepherd, and a wiry golden retriever. All smiling.
 
EPISODE 3
Baking Bad

It started, as usual in that house, with a craving. Specifically, Lillian’s craving.
“I want something obscene,” she said, voice reverberating through the kitchen like a cathedral bell. “Sticky. Warm. Dangerous.”
Miles, sitting at a table still engrossed in his laptop, looked up from his spreadsheet, blinking. “You mean… like murder?”
She rolled her eyes, hips swaying as she crossed the kitchen, opening cupboards with theatrical disgust. “I mean brownies, Miles. Brownies. Real ones. Thick with hazelnuts and chocolate, dark chocolate. Not this… bland nutritional paste you call breakfast,” she added, spitting out the last word as if it was acrid and holding a bland-looking slice of pie Miles had left on the counter.
“I put cinnamon in that paste,” he muttered, offended.
Sarah, sitting at the kitchen table beside Miles with a sketchpad and earbuds, didn’t even look up. “She wants chocolate. You’ve lost her. Give in.”
Lillian huffed and leaned forward on the counter, her ponderous body drawing gravity and attention like a white dwarf. The edge of the marble groaned. “I’ll bake them myself if I must. But you remember what happened last time.”
Miles gulped. Indeed, he did.
The last time Lillian had “baked,” they'd needed a fire extinguisher, two apologies to neighbors, and a forensic chemist to identify what remained in the pan—a feat that earned the chemist a single-name paper in a prestigious academic journal. Lillian was a genius on stage, a goddess in heels, and a sensualist par excellence. But, in the kitchen, she was a force of pure entropy, something not even the topmost theoretical physicists could really grasp.
“I’ll bake them,” Miles said quickly, standing.
“You will?” she beamed.
He nodded. “On one condition.”
She arched a brow. “Is it sexual?”
“No.”
“Disappointing.”
“You let Sarah help.”
Both badger and retriever turned to him with identical expressions of betrayal.
“She’s an artist,” Lillian protested. “She decorates. She doesn’t do—”
“—measurements,” Sarah finished. “Or ‘precise’ things. Like heating or mixing quantities.”
“Which,” Lillian said dramatically, “is why she takes after me.”
But Miles stood firm. “If you want obscene brownies, you’ll need both precision and flair. Chemistry and chaos. Order and art.”
There was a long pause.
“…Fine, I’ll allow it,” Lillian said at last, tossing her scarf over one shoulder with queenly drama. “But if the brownies taste like tax returns, I’m blaming you.”
Ten minutes later, the kitchen had become a war zone.
Sarah wore a bandana and gloves, eyeing the mixing bowl like it might talk back. Lillian stood by the oven, reading the recipe aloud as though she were announcing laws for a military curfew. Miles moved between them, organizing, soothing, and occasionally body-blocking them from turning a mild disagreement about cocoa into a full musical number.
“Do not substitute baking powder for baking soda!” he cried, intercepting Sarah’s paw mid-reach.
“But the tin packaging is cuter,” she argued.
“And deadlier,” he replied, swapping it for the correct one.
Meanwhile, Lillian was waging a losing war against the pan size.
“This thing’s a coffin,” she said, pointing at the pan Miles was suggesting to use. Its handle fit this paw, meaning it was big enough to cook a turkey. “It’ll drown the batter.”
“It’s the standard 9x13,” Miles said patiently.
“9x13 yards, maybe. Also, I am not standard.”
“No one here is.”
By the time the batter was poured—thick, dark, and glistening—Lillian’s hair was frizzed, Sarah’s sleeves were soaked in butter, and Miles looked ten years older.
The oven closed with a collective sigh of relief.
They collapsed at the table, staring at each other with the haunted eyes of veterans.
And then, the scent began to rise.
Warmth. Sugar. Darkness. A decadent cloud of nostalgia and temptation. It filled every room, wrapping around them like a hug from a rubenesque angel.
“I hate to say this,” Sarah whispered. “But… we might’ve nailed it.”
Lillian sniffed the air and made a reverent noise. “I could weep.”
And then came the knock: Ramon and Rosalia—humans, neighbors, bearers of fine wine, gossip, and unwanted opinions. Always welcome, never leaving on their own.
“I knew you were baking,” Ramon sang, gliding into the room like a sequined gust of wind. “The entire street smells like chocolate and chaos.”
Rosalia followed, muttering something in Italian about "the scent of temptation and probable carcinogens."
They were handed slices without ceremony.
Ramon took one bite and stopped mid-moan. “Oh. Oh, this should be illegal.”
Sarah blinked. “You like it?”
“Like it?” he gasped. “This is what I want to taste on my deathbed.”
Even Rosalia, usually unflappable, nodded once. “It’s… excellent.”
The room fell into a rare silence as everyone chewed. A silence so thick it could be sliced.
Then Lillian, cheeks dimpled, leaned her head on Miles’ shoulder and murmured, “You’re brilliant, darling. And you’ve tamed two generations of chaos into something edible.”
He smiled and kissed the top of her head. “You’re my favorite chemical reaction.”
Sarah rolled her eyes, but her tail wagged slightly.
She slipped back into her chair, took a fresh bite, and quietly pulled out her sketchpad. A new idea was blooming both in her heart and her mind—The Alchemy of Family: One Batch at a Time.
 
EPISODE 4
Cleavage and Other Natural Disasters

The dress shimmered like envy.
Emerald green, satin, sleeveless. A neckline that plummeted like a dare. A thigh slit that whispered things Lillian would only tell Miles under the bedsheet. She held it up in front of the bathroom mirror, squinting.
“It still fits,” she said aloud. Then, after a pause: “It will fit.”
Behind the closed door, she maneuvered her way into the fabric. The lining clung like an old grudge. The zipper staged a protest somewhere around her ribcage. The seams stretched in pain.
From the bedroom, Miles called gently. “Want a hand?”
“No," she whispered. "I want physics to comply.”
The zipper surrendered after a long war made of wiggles, inhales and scandalous poppings. The victorious Lillian regarded herself in the mirror: full bust commanding center stage, hips wide and proud, the satin clinging to every rise and curve like ivy on a monument. Her rounded glasses slid down her nose. She pushed them back and tried to exit the bathroom with a proud step.
The doorframe disagreed.
Her hips thudded against its sides. The doorframe protested, squeaking and threatening to sequester her. “Doors weren’t built for greatness,” she muttered, twisting sideways and shimmying through. While passing through the doorway, her hair brushed against the frame with a majestic, theatrical flourish.
She descended the stairs in slow, deliberate steps. Miles stood in the hallway, fixing the cuffs of his shirt. When he looked up, his mouth fell open slightly.
“Bloody hell,” he murmured, blinking.
“You like it?”
He nodded, slow and reverent. “That dress should come with a warning label. Or an air raid siren.”
“Flatterer.” But she couldn’t keep her tail tip from curling in pleasure.
Sarah, sitting on the sofa in a slouchy jumper and overalls, gave Lillian a theatrical once-over. “You’re gonna murder someone with that neckline,” she said, half-impressed, half-horrified.
“I dress for myself, dear,” Lillian replied. “Collateral damage is part of the experience.”

Ramon and Rosalia booked a table at a cozyposh place in Kensigton, founded by one of their long-time friends – a “lucky Bernese who thinks he’s the main character in your story just because he hit a jackpot once,” as once Rosalia described him. The black marble walls were lined up with pretentious modern art pieces and parquet flooring made the place look halfway between the reception of Hell and a museum.
Sarah was shrinking in her hoodie. She hadn’t spoken a single word since turning the corner of the building.
Lillian noticed it. “Are you alright, pup?” she cooed.
Sarah shrugged. “I used to come nearby. Before… you. Begging.” She shook her head. “I don’t feel… I’m fitting here.”
“D’you want to go away?” Miles suggested, after sharing a stare with his wife. Sarah shook her head, again. “It’d be okay. No pressure.”
“It’s… R&R are your friends. I don’t want to… be a nuisance.”
“You’re no nuisance. They’re friends: they’ll understand.”
Lillian passed an arm around Sarah’s shoulders, moving her towards her breasts. “And if they don’t, maybe we need to change our definition of ‘friendship’.”
Sarah met Lillian’s gaze. Strong, sturdy, unmoving, a mountain shielded by a thick layer of glass.
She felt safe enough to nod and go ahead. Inside, Ramon and Rosalia were already seated, waving from a corner booth lined up with chandeliers and dishes. 
They slid into the booth—well, most of them. Lillian required a little more maneuvering to position herself without harming her dress – “I’m not going to ruin this sculpture for a steak.” While finally sitting down, her bust knocked a fork clean off the table. “Oops,” she said, catching it on the fly. “Weapons-grade, apparently,” she commented, adjusting her neckline.
As dinner progressed, so did the wine. Conversation flowed from work gossip to holiday plans to whether badgers could legally own boats. Lillian, warm and laughing, leaned forward to cut a particularly stubborn scallop.
A candle leaned too.
One gentle whoosh—and the tiny flame sputtered and died, snuffed out in a breathless second by the rounded swell of her cleavage, like a gust of wind putting out a wildfire.
A moment of stunned silence followed. Everyone stared at the wick.
Then Lillian sat back and said, perfectly calm, “My body just extinguished fire.”
The table exploded with laughter. Miles leaned in close, eyes wide with admiration.
“You’re incredible.”
She raised an eyebrow. “I know.” But she blushed anyway.

Later that night, she sat on the edge of their bed, heels off, the emerald dress folded beside her in a gentle pool of shimmer and regret. She stared at it in the lamplight, her fingers brushing its hem.
“It used to fit better,” she said softly.
Miles emerged from the bathroom in pyjama pants, towel around his neck. He saw her expression, caught the undertone of her words and said nothing for a moment. Just crossed the room, sat beside her, and took her hand.
“It still does,” he said. “Differently. Powerfully.”
Lillian exhaled, long and quiet. She let her body relax against his for a moment, the warmth of his palm resting over hers. She didn’t need the mirror to know her outline. She could feel it in every doorframe, every button, every pair of tights that surrendered too soon. Age didn’t arrive with a trumpet. It crept in like new habits: how she reached for chairs, how long she stood at gallery openings, how her thighs kissed with every step.
But that night her body had extinguished fire.
She smiled faintly. “Then I suppose the world can adjust around me.”
Miles pressed a kiss to her knuckles. “It always has.”

In the room next door, Sarah sat cross-legged on her bed, sketchbook open, pencil dancing in tight, clever strokes. Her tongue poked slightly from the corner of her mouth as she shaded.
On the page: Lillian, full-figured and radiant in green satin, a tiny cartoon flame sputtering out between her breasts. Above it, in dramatic lettering:
“Beware: Fire Hazard.”
Sarah snorted, then added a speech bubble from Miles.
“We’ll need a permit just to leave the house!”
She tucked it into her folder with a smile and turned off the light.
 
EPISODE 5
Pancakes and hearts

The pancake lying in Miles’ dish was shaped like a heart.
Miles stared down at it, flipping the spatula in confusion. "I didn't—how did it…?"
From the other side of the kitchen island, Lillian folded her arms and gave him a look. "Trying to summon a confession?" she asked in a joke, her brow arched high above her glasses.
Sarah, still groggy and dressed in an oversized hoodie that might've once belonged to Miles, slid into a chair. She glanced at the pancake, then at her parents, then sighed.
"Fine," she muttered. "I like someone. Maybe."
The silence was instant and total. Lillian’s fork stopped mid-air. The kettle clicked off in the background like it, too, needed a moment.
Then—clatter. Miles dropped the syrup bottle.
"You okay there, darling?" Lillian asked without turning her head.
"I'm great," he said, too quickly, wiping his hands. "I'm great. I love heart-shaped pancakes. I love… growth."
Lillian ignored him and leaned toward Sarah, her tone softening. "Are they kind to you?"
Sarah blinked. "Yeah. He's quiet. Like Dad. But he draws. Really well. Like… scary well."
Lillian's smile curved slow and sly. "Ah. Art. The gateway drug."
"Lillian," Miles warned her while sitting down at the island.
"Shush," she said sweetly, passing a finger on his lips without even looking at him.
Sarah picked at her pancake, suddenly pretending to be fascinated by the syrup patterns. "His name's Oliver. He's in my art class. He sits in the back corner by the window and never talks to anyone, but his sketches are... they're like photographs, but better. Like, he sees things the rest of us miss."
"And does he see you?" Lillian asked gently.

A flush crept up Sarah's neck. "Yesterday he asked if I wanted to work on a project together. Something about… urban landscapes. I said yes before I could think about it."
Miles, who had been quietly hyperventilating into his coffee mug, looked up. "A project. That's... educational. Invaluable."
"Dad."
"What? Projects are good. Supervised. Public."
"Miles," Lillian said with dangerous sweetness, "if you don't stop being weird about this, I'm going to tell Sarah about your poetry phase."
Sarah's ears perked up. "Poetry phase?"
"There was no poetry phase," Miles said quickly, trying to defuse the conversation.
"'Ode to a Carburetor,'" Lillian recited dramatically, one hand on her forehead, the other busy gesturing. "'Your pistons move my heart—'"
"LILLIAN."
Sarah giggled despite herself, and some of the tension melted from her shoulders.

That afternoon, the house smelled of jasmine and floor polish. Lillian vacuumed the front hallway twice, waging war against her (non-existent) enemies of cleanliness. Miles had arranged the cushions by color theory, after spending the morning studying the effects of light patterns on the brain. Sarah paced upstairs in her room, muttering, changed outfits three times before returning to her first choice: casual corduroys and a shirt that said "I Bite" in friendly letters.
The doorbell rang.
Lillian opened the door with a dramatic flourish and her most intimidating hostess smile. Oliver was a red squirrel. Slim, but not a twig. A bit twitchy. His shirt hung off one shoulder and his tail flicked nervously every few seconds. He clutched a sketchpad to his chest like armor. "You must be the artist," she said, loud. "Come in. I don't bite." Then glanced back and shouted, "Usually!"
Oliver's eyes went wide, but he stepped inside with the careful politeness of someone who'd been raised by librarians. Particularly polite and scared librarians.
Miles welcomed him in the living room, measuring the boy with calm calculation as if he was an engine he had to fix. His eyes scanned shoes, body language, handshake pressure, even the brand of pencil poking from the sketchpad. "I'm Mr. Ferguson," he said evenly. "Would you like some tea, juice, or to explain your intentions toward my daughter?"
"MILES," Lillian barked, scandalized.
The squirrel nearly dropped the sketchpad. "J-Juice, please."
They sat awkwardly, Sarah and Oliver perched on one end of the sofa like students waiting for a final exam. Lillian, all theatrical poise, served tea with the ceremony of a diplomatic summit. Miles brought out juice with the solemnity of a peace offering.
Conversation took time. Oliver didn't say much, but his eyes moved constantly—cataloging the way light fell across Lillian's reading glasses, the precise angle of Miles's suspicious lean, the nervous energy radiating from Sarah like heat from a radiator.
"So," Lillian said, settling into her armchair with the grace of a settling mountain, "Sarah tells us you're collaborating on a project."
Oliver nodded, suddenly excited and more in control. "Urban landscapes. We're documenting how the city changes when you look at it from different perspectives. Sarah has this way of seeing... she notices things," he added, looking at her.
Sarah's tail gave a tiny wag.
"What kind of things?" Miles asked, his tone carefully neutral.
"Like how the shadows between buildings make patterns that look like lace. Or how the rust stains on that old bridge form shapes that could be constellations." Oliver's voice grew stronger as he talked about art. "She draws the poetry in ordinary things."
Lillian and Miles exchanged a look. This boy saw their daughter. Really saw her.
As Sarah described a funny story from school—something about a janitor, a mop, and a runaway frog—Oliver sketched on a folded sheet of paper with rapid, deft strokes. His pencil moved like it was dancing, capturing not just the scene but the feeling of it.
When he left, he handed the sketch to Miles with shy formality.
After the front door closed and Sarah retreated to her room with a dreamy expression that made Miles want to check all the locks twice, he sat on their bed and unfolded the paper.
It was a sketch of their living room. Not just the furniture, but the feel of it—Lillian's abandoned knitting on the armrest, the slight lean in Sarah's coffee mug, the way the sunlight hit the curtains. Everything in sharp graphite lines, drawn with startling tenderness. But what made Miles's breath catch was the way Oliver had drawn them: Lillian as a warm, protective presence, Miles as a steady anchor, and Sarah as the bright center that held it all together.
He held it up wordlessly.
Lillian looked over his shoulder. "He noticed my mug has a crack. That's... oddly sweet."
"He's observant," Miles murmured. "Too observant. But... he sees her. That matters."
"And he's terrified of you," Lillian added with amusement, pointing at the thick strokes Oliver employed to outline Miles in the drawing.
"Which shows excellent judgment."

Upstairs, Sarah paced her room, replaying every moment of the afternoon. The way Oliver had looked at her when she talked about the bridge rust. How he'd laughed at her frog story—not politely, but like he genuinely found her funny. The careful way he'd said goodbye, like he was already planning their next conversation.
She cracked open their bedroom door, eyes wide and uncertain. "Did I do okay?" she whispered.
Miles looked up from the sketch. "You did better than okay."

The Mozzarella Conspiracy

EPISODE 11
The Box

It was late when Miles knocked on Sarah's door. No answer. Just the sound of pencil scratches inside—raw, restless, the kind that left graphite on fingers and shadows under eyes.
He'd been standing there for ten minutes, working up the courage. The fight from earlier still hung in the air like smoke, acrid and lingering. Words that couldn't be taken back. Hurt that couldn't be easily smoothed over, even with the help of Lillian’s coddling, because it ran too deep.

"I didn't throw it away," he said quietly, not expecting a reply. "But I shouldn't've snapped. I just…" He paused, choosing his words carefully. "I'm not good at arguing. My dad used to bark and shut down. I guess I inherited both."
The pencil sounds stopped.
Miles took a breath. "I never thought your art was rubbish. You know that, right? That sketch of your mum and me at the market? It made me cry. I was slicing onions at the time, but still. Wasn't just the onions, I have to confess it."

"I'm sorry too," she said, voice low and rough. "I shouldn't've said what I did. You're not—" She faltered, looking down at her feet. "I mean, I know you're not just… some guy."
Miles smiled gently. "Takes more than a yelling match to scare me off. Yet… yeah, bugger off, that scared me. Not you yelling, but what you said, because… it made me feel wrong. In the wrong place. But I’m happy we’re here now."

Her eyes shimmered, the corner of her mouth twitching up before she silently closed the door, one paw over her chest, the other wiping away the softest tear.

"She called me Dad," he said, still marveling at it. “And she felt it. I felt it. Not as… not as her usual way, you know.”

"But you didn't," Lillian said, setting down her brush and turning to face him. "You made her a box. You listened. You showed up. You made her know her things are precious for you, and made something to make her feel safe. That's what fathers do."

The Unbearable Lightness of Laundry

Her face was a mixture of excitement and terror, the kind of expression that suggested she was either about to reveal a masterpiece or spontaneously combust from nervous energy.
Lillian, still in her favorite polka-dot house dress with a smear of flour on her muzzle from the morning's baking session, blinked owlishly at the elaborate setup. The kitchen timer was still ticking in the background, counting down the minutes until the sourdough would be ready to come out of the oven.  "Do I need to sit?" she asked, looking around for a designated viewing spot. "Is there a flash? Should I hold a cabbage? I feel like I should be holding something significant."
"No," Sarah laughed, the sound slightly breathless with nerves. "Just… don't laugh, okay? Promise me you won't laugh. This is… this is important."
Miles wandered in from the kitchen, drying his paws on a tea towel and looking pleasantly domestic in his weekend clothes—the soft jumper with the hole in the elbow that Lillian kept threatening to mend and he kept refusing to let her touch. "What's all this then? Is it the IKEA furniture assembly situation again? Because I've told you, it’s not your fault. Those instructions are written by people who hate happiness."
Sarah took a deep breath, her hands trembling slightly as she reached for the cloth. "It's… it's the thing I've been working on for weeks. The final project for my advanced studio class. The one that's worth forty percent of my grade and possibly my entire future as an artist." She paused, looking back at them with eyes that were bright but fragile, like glass ornaments that might shatter if handled too roughly. "I drew what I saw. What I have. What matters."
She tugged at the cloth. It didn’t budge. She closed her eyes and took a deep breath before tugging again. The cloth dropped from the canvas like a curtain falling at the end of a play.
Silence bloomed in the room like a held breath, thick and expectant and full of possibility.
It was them.
Not photorealistic—Sarah had never been interested in mere technical reproduction—but true in a way that photographs could never capture, true in the way that only art can be when it looks past the surface and finds the soul underneath.
Lillian stood at the canvas's heart, full-bodied and radiant, wrapped in a cloak of deep indigo that seemed to move and breathe with its own life. The fabric curled around her like smoke, like music made visible, like the physical manifestation of her voice when she sang. Her shoulders were broad and proud, her belly full and captivating, her hips defiant monuments to beauty. She wore her long black hair down, cascading over her shoulders like a waterfall of midnight, and her glasses glinted with joy, catching light that seemed to come from within. In one paw, she held a wine glass filled with something that sparkled like liquid starlight. In the other, a treble clef dipped in gold and silver stars, as if she had reached into the night sky and plucked music from the cosmos itself.
Beside her, Miles stood like a lighthouse—steady, reliable, essential. His stance was firm but gentle, one paw holding a toolbox that looked well-used and beloved, the other a pen poised thoughtfully at his lips as if he were in the middle of solving some complex equation or writing a love letter to the universe. His tail curved gently behind him, and his eyes—those kind, patient eyes that had seen Lillian through twelve years of marriage—were warm and fixed on her with the kind of love that doesn't need words.
And Sarah—smaller in the composition, yet at the real center of the composition, standing close to both her parents—glowed in firelight tones, wearing Lillian's oversized denim jacket like armor, like a talisman, like a promise that she would always carry a piece of home with her wherever she went. A sketchbook peeked from her satchel, and her paw was outstretched, holding a single red thread that connected them all, weaving through the composition like a lifeline.
"I drew what I saw," Sarah said softly, her voice barely above a whisper. "What I have. What I want to remember when I'm old and everything else has faded."
Lillian covered her mouth with both hands, her eyes welling with tears that came too quickly to stop. She wiped them clumsily with her sleeve, not caring about the flour smear or the fact that her mascara was probably creating abstract art on her cheeks. "Sweetheart," she said, her voice catching on the word. She was trying to tell more, but words appeared to form and vanish on her tongue, destroyed by a joy so pure she couldn’t define.
Miles had to clear his throat twice before he could speak, his eyes suspiciously bright as he studied the painting with the intensity of someone memorizing every detail. "You made us immortal," he said finally. "You made us into something that will last."
Sarah smiled crookedly, the expression transforming her face from nervous artist to confident creator. "That's what art's for, right? To make the temporary permanent. To say 'this mattered, this was beautiful, this was worth preserving.' And it’s my family that’s worth preserving."
They hung it above the couch with the ceremony it deserved, Lillian guiding the hammer with the precision of a conductor directing an orchestra, Miles balancing the level with the patience of someone who understood that some things were worth getting exactly right, Sarah directing them both with theatrical flair and an artist's eye for composition. When it was finally set, perfectly positioned to catch the afternoon light, they stepped back in reverent silence.
The sunlight hit the canvas just right, illuminating Lillian's indigo cloak and making it shimmer like breath on a winter morning, like the surface of deep water, like magic made manifest.
They didn't say much more after that.
Instead, they made tea—proper tea, with the good china that usually only came out for special occasions. They put on music—Lillian's favorite Puccini, the aria that always made her cry in the best possible way. They sat beneath the painting like royalty in their own private palace, like people who had suddenly realized they were living in a work of art themselves.
Mozzarella, with the impeccable timing that only cats possess, jumped onto the couch and immediately knocked over a coaster, sending it clattering to the floor with a sound that was both jarring and somehow perfect—a reminder that even in moments of transcendence, life continues with all its small, beautiful imperfections.
Sarah pulled out another blank canvas from behind the piano, setting it up on the easel. Lillian raised an eyebrow, her curiosity piqued. "Already planning your next masterpiece? The paint's barely dry on this one."
Sarah just shrugged, but there was something in her expression—a contentment, a sense of purpose, a recognition that she had found her calling in the act of making the invisible visible. "Well… we've still got a lot of life to paint. A lot of moments worth preserving. A lot of love worth documenting."
Miles reached out, resting a paw on both of theirs, creating a small circle of connection beneath the painted version of themselves. "Then we better make it beautiful," he said. "We better make it worth painting."

And with the smell of bread rising again in the kitchen, filling the house with the promise of nourishment and comfort, a cat purring at their feet like a small engine of contentment, and sun flickering gold against the canvas above them like a benediction, they made space—between the couch cushions, on the wall, in their lives, in their hearts—for whatever came next.
Because that's what families do, Lillian thought, looking up at their painted selves. They make space for each other. They make room for growth and change and all the beautiful chaos of being human. They create art out of the ordinary moments, and they hang it on the wall where everyone can see it, and they say: this is us. This is what we built together. This is what love looks like when it's not afraid to take up space.
