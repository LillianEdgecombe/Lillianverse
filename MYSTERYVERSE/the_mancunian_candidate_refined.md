# The Mancunian Candidate — Refined Edition
*A domestic comedy-thriller in the Lillianverse*

> Long-form version in 5 chapters (~1500–2000 words each), based on the original outline.

## Chapter 1: Teakettles and Trigger Phrases

The teakettle began to sing precisely eight seconds before boiling, which is the sort of thing that would trouble a physicist but merely made Lillian Edgecombe tilt her striped muzzle and listen. European badgers are very good at listening; it is what happens when your ancestors spent an unreasonable amount of time underground with nothing to do but eavesdrop on worms, roots, and the neighbours’ plumbing.

Lillian stood at the hob in her Loughton kitchen, a solid, rounded presence framed by steam and pale autumn light. She was an anthro European badger in her early forties—about one-sixty in height, three-hundred-and-something pounds in unapologetic reality—with the kind of broad shoulders that made even heavy coats hang like capes. Her face carried the classic badger mask: two bold white stripes running from her dark, rounded ears down either side of her blunt black nose, separated by a dusky blaze that deepened when she frowned.

This morning she wore a soft black jersey dress that skimmed her curves, practical leggings, and no shoes; her black-and-white paws were dusted with flour from the bread she had put in earlier. Long black hair, streaked at the temples with silver she refused to call “going grey,” was twisted up into an imperfect bun, held in place by a pencil that had once contained marking notes for a student’s sonata.

The kitchen—like the rest of the little semi near Epping Forest—had clearly lost the battle against being a showroom and won the war of being lived in. Mismatched mugs hung from hooks. The fridge door carried a collage: Sarah’s early sketches, gig posters, a photo of Lillian on stage in a gold-embroidered gown, mouth open mid-note, badger stripes thrown into relief by spotlight. A faint line of clay dust tracked in from the back door, pointing toward the shed where her pottery wheel lived among shelves of drying bowls.

Outside, rain combed the garden, turning last week’s leaves into a slick rust carpet. The air through the cracked window smelled of damp soil and woodsmoke; inside, the kitchen was warm with the scent of rising bread, dark tea, and the muted varnish of the upright piano in the next room.

The kettle’s pre-boil murmur tripped again: four quick pulses, a pause, an almost jaunty lift before sliding back into a normal whistle.

“Don’t start getting ideas,” Lillian told it. “We’ve enough musicians in this house without the appliances developing a backbeat.”

She reached for the teapot—fat, chipped, beloved—when pawsteps sounded in the hall. A familiar scent arrived half a second before the man: soap, engine oil, and the faint cedar of wardrobe hangers. Then Miles Ferguson padded into the kitchen, shaking a few stray drops of rain from his fur.

Miles was a German Shepherd anthro, also forty-two, built like someone had taken the ideal blueprint for “reliable” and given it fur. Broad-shouldered, long-backed, with a natural forward lean that made every corridor look like a job he was about to tackle. His coat was short and dense, sable along his back deepening to almost black at the ridge, fading to tan at his throat, muzzle, and along his strong forearms. His ears were high and mobile, pricking and canting as he took in the sounds of the house; his warm brown eyes had that particular shepherd mixture of kindness and vigilance.

He wore a flannel shirt in soft navy and grey, sleeves rolled to just below the elbow, the fabric stretched across the curve of his shoulders. Dark trousers, scuffed but clean boots. Around his wrist, his battered smartwatch; around the other, the faint ghost of a drumstick callus from years of practice that never fully went away.

He stepped in close and dipped his head to kiss the top of her skull between her ears, black nose bumping momentarily against her white scalp stripe. “Morning, love,” he said, voice deep and unhurried.

“You’re up indecently early for a man whose calendar claims it’s Sunday,” Lillian replied, not taking her eyes off the heat under the kettle. Her accent, still more Edinburgh than Essex despite twenty years in London, wrapped itself around the vowels and made morning sound like something you could hold.

“Quarterlies,” Miles said, and the word came out like a weather report. “Sir Basil wants a ‘pre-summit touch base’. Apparently the British shopping soul is at a crossroads.”

“Poor thing,” Lillian said. “Has anyone offered it a cup of tea?”

Miles huffed a laugh and opened the fridge with the serious air of a man approaching a known but solvable problem. Shelves of leftovers, vegetables, cheeses, and jars regarded him. He regarded them back.

“You’ve rearranged Condiments again,” he observed.

“I put things back where they live,” she said. “It is not my fault that your taxonomy contradicts where my paw reaches when I’m mid-chorus.”

“If we group by brand—”

“No.”

“—loyalty tier,” he finished, because he was constitutionally incapable of leaving a system half-suggested.

She turned slightly, enough that the white stripe along her cheek caught the light. “Brand loyalty tier should only apply to bands, biscuits, and possibly spouses, not salad dressing.”

He wagged his tail once, humour easing the line of his shoulders. “It’s efficient,” he said.

“So’s a guillotine,” Lillian said. “And democratic?”

“Now you’re just being inflammatory.”

He began moving jars anyway, in that careful, precise way of his: mayo, mustard, chutneys. Lillian watched for half a beat, then surrendered the argument to ritual and poured boiling water over tea leaves, the dark swirl in the pot as comforting as an old key in an old lock.

The kitchen radio, parked beside the bread bin, chattered amiably about delays on the Central line and an upcoming weather front. Under the announcer’s patter, a bed of brand-safe synth chords hummed. It was the sort of thing that was supposed to be invisible to the ear.

This morning, something in it clicked.

Lillian’s musician brain picked out a pattern the way her claws picked out clay seams: four notes, evenly spaced, a little rise-and-fall motif that wasn’t part of the advertised melody at all. It slid under the announcer’s voice like a cat under a settee, invisible but there.

Miles’s paw stilled on a jar of Dijon. His ears went rigid, turning fractionally toward the speaker. The fur along his neck lifted the tiniest amount, barely enough for anyone who wasn’t his wife to see.

Very softly, as if the sound were being pulled out of him on a string, he hummed: “Shop smart—shop bright—FreshMart every night…”

The kettle hit full whistle. The sound collided with the last line of the jingle and turned into pure noise.

“Miles?” Lillian put the pot down more abruptly than she meant to. She turned. His eyes had that fogged, inward look she recognized from the first half-second before a panic attack, or from the moment in the wings before a performer walked on stage and remembered that there were people out there.

She stepped across the narrow kitchen, weight sure and balanced on the balls of her broad feet despite the slick tile. Her paw closed around his forearm—warm fur, solid muscle, pulse a little faster than baseline.

“Hey,” she said, low. “Shepherd. Come back.”

He blinked. The glassy sheen cleared. His ears twitched, then rotated back toward her. “Sorry,” he said at once. “I was—” He stopped, listening to the end of his own sentence. “Was I… singing?”

“You were,” she said. “Technically. We shall not call it that in public.”

His muzzle creased with embarrassment, the tan fur there ruffling slightly. “Oh hell. That thing.”

The thing in question had been born in the early 2000s, in a flat on Mandela Street where the radiators clanged in E minor and the neighbours banged on the ceiling in F sharp. Lillian, Miles, and the rest of *Venus in Furs* had churned out demo jingles for quick cash between gigs. One, a parody of corporate pep, had lodged stubbornly in their collective memory. It had also, regrettably, landed in FreshMart’s slush pile and been briefly, disastrously, considered.

“You haven’t gone near that tune in twenty years,” Lillian said. She turned the gas down under the kettle, more out of respect for physics than necessity. “Why is it coming out of your mouth now, of all mornings?”

Miles rubbed the back of his neck, claws faintly catching in the fur. “We’ve been talking about ‘sonic branding’ all week,” he said. “Nigel’s lot from Wetherby Creative want to ‘harmonize the in-store experience.’ Basil keeps saying ‘sonic identity’ like it’s a Hogwarts house.”

“And they dug up your old shame-tape,” Lillian said.

“Maybe,” he said. “Probably they generated something new. They like algorithms. They don’t need drummers anymore.”

She poured out two mugs of tea, the dark liquid catching the light. Her own had honey and no milk; his had just enough milk to soften the edge, two sugars because he refused to call it a treat. She handed him his, watching the way his big paws wrapped around the ceramic, claws tapping once against the rim.

“Band rehearsal tonight,” she said, more to check his reflexes than to convey information. “Milla says if you’re not there, she’s defecting to the cellists’ union.”

He smiled with both mouth and eyes this time, the way he did when he was truly present. “Wouldn’t dare. They’ve got better lawyers than we do.”

They drank in silence for a minute. The radio moved on to more palatable fare; the odd little four-note motif vanished into the wallpaper of sound. Outside, a gust rattled raindrops down the glass.

“You’re sure you’re all right?” Lillian asked quietly.

“Overworked,” Miles said. “Not broken.” His tail twitched once, the faint, betraying sweep of a dog who wanted to reassure and was not entirely sure he was succeeding. “It’s probably nothing. Just… suggestions in the air.”

When you love someone long enough, you become fluent in the ways they lie to themselves. Lillian heard the hollow under probably nothing and, being a sensible badger, did not argue with it outright. She filed it away beside other useful facts, like “Sarah pretends she isn’t nervous before a show” and “the second oven shelf runs hotter on the left.”

Miles rinsed his mug, put it carefully in the rack, and closed the fridge with a double tap of his knuckles on the door—a ritual check that it had properly sealed and, perhaps, that the world was, for the moment, ordered.

He shrugged into his jacket from the hook by the door. The fabric settled neatly along his back, catching for a moment on the angle of his tail before he adjusted it. At the threshold he paused, looking back at her with that half-smile he only wore at home.

“Text me if the kettle starts auditioning for the band,” he said.

“Text me if your CEO starts speaking in rhyme,” she replied. “Either way, bring back your brain in one piece.”

He gave her a little two-fingered salute with his paw—the private echo of a drummer’s count-in—and left. The front door closed with its usual click. The house inhaled, rebalanced around one absence and one presence.

Lillian poured herself a second mug and moved into the living room. The piano waited under the front window, its wood warm in the morning light. She ran her paw along its edge, claws making the faintest shick-shick. In the mirror above, her reflection looked back: round badger face, tired but content, dress a little flour-dusted, dignity entirely intact.

She sat and let her paws find a simple progression. The notes filled the room, honest and deliberate. No scaffolds. No tricks. Just sound moving air, the way it should.

Still, in the back of her mind, four notes echoed stubbornly. Shop smart. Shop bright.

“We will see about that,” she murmured to the empty room, and shifted into a minor key.

## Chapter 2: Sonic Identity

By the time Miles reached FreshMart’s regional headquarters in Stratford, the rain had downgraded from Biblical to merely petty. It pecked at the windscreen of his hybrid in rapid little beaks, beading on the glass in a way that reminded him—annoyingly—of a certain brand of FreshMart mineral water.

The car park behind the glass-and-steel building was a study in managed mundanity. Reserved spaces bore tasteful plaques: **SIR BASIL CRANDALL, CBE**; **NIGEL WETHERBY – CREATIVE PARTNER**; **REGIONAL OPERATIONS – NORTH & EAST**; and so on. The logo—an aggressively cheerful green leaf, mid-bounce—loomed over everything from a billboard by the entrance, accompanied by the current slogan: **FRESHMART: FEEL IT IN YOUR BONES.**

Miles’s shepherd ears tilted back ever so slightly at that. Nobody had asked the bones.

He pulled into his usual unreserved slot, killed the engine, and sat for a moment, paws on the wheel. The cabin smelled faintly of wet fur and pine air freshener, overlaying the permanent undertone of supermarket: plastic, fruit esters, cardboard.

Shop smart—shop bright—

He caught the thought before it finished, jaw tightening. His claws flexed against the steering wheel, the leather creaking in protest.

“No,” he told himself quietly, because sometimes engineers needed verbal asserts. “We are not doing that.”

He closed his eyes briefly, pictured Lillian in the kitchen, flour on her dress, the calm weight of her paw on his forearm.

Shepherd. Come back.

He opened his eyes. The intrusive rhythm receded to the mental background noise where old advert jingles and teenage embarrassments lived. He took a steadying breath, grabbed his satchel from the passenger seat, and stepped out into the drizzle.

Inside, the building was aggressively neutral. The foyer smelled of glass cleaner and the sort of air freshener that claimed to be “mountain breeze” and delivered “plastic pine.” A human receptionist in a FreshMart-branded polo glanced up, smiled with professional wattage, and returned to her screen. Behind her, a looping video played: happy multispieces families pushing trolleys through aisles bathed in warm light, the FreshMart leaf bouncing in the corner.

Faintly, the house sound system purred out what was supposed to be inoffensive corporate ambience: soft synth pads, gentle piano, the occasional aspirational chord change.

Four notes stepped out of the wash like a stranger into a spotlight. Miles’s ears swivelled before he could stop them.

Shop smart—

He swallowed, forced his paws to keep moving. The lift doors sighed open with a noise that always made him think of a cymbal brushed lightly, and he stepped inside. On the mirrored wall, his reflection looked like what he was meant to be: a middle-aged German Shepherd in a rain-damp flannel shirt, tie slightly crooked, fur neatly trimmed, broad shoulders filling the space. Reliable. Reasonable. The kind of anthro you wanted managing your grocery logistics, not having an existential crisis about background music.

He straightened his tie. “Quarterlies,” he reminded himself. “Spreadsheets. Nigel’s PowerPoint.” Reality, such as it was.

The lift carried him up to the sixth floor: REGIONAL OPERATIONS & CUSTOMER EXPERIENCE. The doors slid open on a familiar landscape of grey carpet, glass-partitioned offices, and clusters of desks where humans, dogs, foxes, badgers, cats, and the odd avian hunched over screens. The click-clack of keyboards, the burr of phones, the scent of too many takeaways and not enough fresh air.

“Morning, Miles!” called Anusha from Customer Metrics, a small jackal anthro whose earrings always matched her spreadsheets. “Sir Basil’s already in. He’s on his second espresso and his third ‘big picture.’”

“Dangerous combination,” Miles said, managing a smile. “If he starts drawing on the windows again, text me and I’ll trigger a fire drill.”

Anusha laughed, slender tail swishing. “Nigel’s with him. They’re in the Blue Room.”

Of course they were. The Blue Room, FreshMart’s prized “innovation space,” featured writable walls, beanbags nobody over thirty actually used, and a sound system worth more than Miles’s first car.

He detoured to his own office first, a modest glass box with a view of the DLR and, on clear days, the distant smudge of the City. He shrugged off his jacket, hung it on the back of his chair, and smoothed the fur along his forearms where the fabric had left a ridge. The ghost of last night’s drum practice tugged briefly at his wrists. He flexed his paws, feeling the familiar itching memory of sticks.

On his desk, his inbox blinked with unread messages. A dozen were flagged from Nigel’s people. Subject lines: **Sonic Identity – Store Trial Wave 1**. **URGENT: Audio Mix Feedback**. **CONFIDENTIAL: Behavioural Response Deck**.

Miles stared at that last one for a heartbeat longer than he meant to. Behavioural response. The words sat there, apparently harmless.

He clicked his tongue softly against the back of his teeth, a drummer’s equivalent of a metronome. One-two-three-four. Right. Blue Room.

Nigel Wetherby was already talking when Miles arrived.

“—it’s not just about what they *hear*,” the fox was saying, standing in front of the enormous screen as if it were a mirror designed solely to reflect his opinions. “It’s about what they *feel*. We’re not selling bread and milk, Basil. We’re selling narrative. We’re selling who they get to be when they pick up that trolley.”

Nigel was a red fox in his late thirties, narrow and sleek in a navy suit tailored so precisely it might have been poured onto him and then allowed to set. His fur was a glossy rust, the white at his throat impossibly crisp, his black paws tipped with carefully manicured claws. His muzzle tapered to an elegant point that suggested, even at rest, that he was thinking three moves ahead in a game you didn’t realise you were playing. His ears were adorned with tiny silver cuffs, carefully calibrated to whisper “creative, but expensive” rather than “band guitarist.”

Sir Basil Crandall lounged—or as near as his physiology allowed—on a high stool nearby. He was human, mid-fifties, well-fed in the way of men who had long ago stopped pushing their own trolleys. His hair, expensively maintained in a colour one shade too eager to be natural, lay in a careful wave. His suit was lighter than Nigel’s and more rumpled, as if it had recently lost a wrestle with an enthusiastic PA. He clutched a tiny coffee cup as if it were a lifeline.

“…bones,” Basil was saying. “I like the bone thing. It’s primal. You feel good food in your bones. My grandmother said that.”

“Exactly,” Nigel nodded smoothly. “And we’ve taken that and translated it into a soundscape that speaks directly to the subconscious shopping psyche.”

At that, Miles’s ears flattened a fraction, an involuntary centimetre drop.

Nigel noticed. Of course he did. “Ah, Miles,” he said, turning toward him with a smile sharp enough to slice lemons. “Our resident wizard of aisles. Come in, come in. We were just talking about you.”

“Always a worrying opener,” Miles said dryly. He stepped into the room, tail giving a polite, neutral swish. “Morning, Sir Basil. Nigel.”

“Miles!” Basil beamed. “How’s my favourite drum‑playing grocery genius?”

“Keeping the stock on the shelves and the beat in four‑four, sir,” Miles said. He took a seat on one of the less preposterous chairs, his broad frame making it look immediately more practical than designed.

Nigel clicked a button on the remote. The screen behind him flashed to a slide bearing the title: **FRESHMART SONIC IDENTITY: PHASE ONE TRIALS.** Below, a graph with cheery colours swooped upwards.

“We’ve rolled out the new in‑store audio package to six trial locations,” Nigel said. “Manchester Piccadilly, Stockport, Loughton, Croydon, Reading, and that one where the escalator keeps breaking.”

“Walthamstow,” Miles supplied.

“Yes, that one.” Nigel waved a paw dismissively. “Early data shows a statistically significant uplift in dwell time in key promotional zones, increased basket size, and self‑reported ‘positive feelings’ in exit surveys.”

“It makes them feel good,” Basil translated. “Happy shoppers, happy shareholders.”

On the screen, a short video clip began to play: a FreshMart store, shot from the viewpoint of a trolley. The camera glided past produce displays, end‑caps piled with snacks, a cheerful in‑store bakery. Over it all, a wash of sound: pads, piano, a subtle, rising motif of four notes repeating at intervals that were just irregular enough not to be obvious.

Miles’s heart did a small, annoyed stutter‑step.

Shop smart—shop bright—FreshMart every night.

Except the words weren’t there. The notes were. The rhythm was. The bones of the old jingle lay under a disguise of tastefulness.

“We worked with a terrific composer,” Nigel said, as if he’d personally escorted the muse into the studio. “We fed in some classic retail sonic triggers, a few clever little psychoacoustic cues—”

“Psychoacoustic?” Miles repeated, unable to stop himself.

“Just meaning how sound affects behaviour,” Nigel said smoothly. “Perfectly benign. Music makes people happy, happy people buy more, et cetera, et cetera. Nothing sinister. No hypno‑spirals.” He twirled a paw in the air as if drawing one.

Miles smiled dutifully, but the word lodged: behaviour.

“And you wanted me here because…?” he prompted.

“Because you know our stores,” Basil said, leaning forward enough that his tie swung precariously near his coffee. “You know the staff, the customers, the flow. We want you to be our boots on the ground for this. Ears on the ground. Paws on the ground. You know what I mean.” He waved vaguely at Miles’s feet.

“We’ve got the quantitative data,” Nigel said. “What we need is qualitative. Field notes. Does the new sonic identity *feel* right? Does it fit with the brand narrative we’re trying to build? Any… anomalies.” His eyes glittered just a little on the last word.

An image flashed behind Miles’s eyes: his own paws, frozen on a jar of Dijon; Lillian’s paw closing around his forearm.

“Anomalies?” he echoed.

“You know,” Nigel said airily. “Unexpected behaviour. People standing still too long in front of the tinned tomatoes. Staff humming the motifs in the break room. That sort of thing. Any reports of irritation, discomfort, the opposite of what we want.” He folded his paws in front of him, claws tapping once against one another in a quiet little click. “We’re not monsters. If something’s off, we want to know.”

“We’re a family brand,” Basil added. “Families don’t, you know, brainwash each other.” He chuckled, as if at his own joke.

Miles’s fur prickled along the back of his neck. No hypno‑spirals. Families don’t brainwash each other.

He thought of Lillian’s upright piano, the honest, deliberate notes filling their living room. He thought of Sarah, sketchbook on her knees, brow furrowed when she listened to a tricky passage.

“So you want me to visit the trial stores,” he said slowly. “Observe. Talk to managers. See how it’s landing.”

“Precisely,” Nigel said. “And because you have… shall we say… a musical background, you’re uniquely placed to notice anything interesting in the mix.”

Miles’s tail gave an involuntary flick. “My student bands would be very flattered to hear that described as a ‘background,’” he said.

“Every little helps,” Nigel replied, without apparent irony. “You’ll be meeting our audio partner later this week, but for now, start with Loughton, since it’s convenient, and Manchester Piccadilly, since it’s a flagship. We want to make sure the heartland feels it in their bones.”

Bones again. Miles nodded. “All right,” he said. “I’ll go out this afternoon. Talk to the store teams. Listen.”

“Splendid,” Basil said. “Knew we could count on you, Miles. You’ve always had your paw on the pulse.”

There was a brief moment in which every metaphor in the room seemed determined to sit on his chest.

He forced a genuine‑enough smile. “That’s what you pay me for, sir.”

Nigel clicked to the next slide, full of graphs and heat maps. Miles watched, or appeared to. When the meeting concluded, Basil swept out with the air of a man who had personally reinvented shopping, Nigel close behind, already tapping ideas into his phone.

Miles lingered, alone in the Blue Room, as the auto‑shutdown dimmed the big screen.

The speakers in the ceiling hummed faintly, like bees behind a wall. From somewhere, the in‑house loop resumed, soft and inoffensive.

Four notes surfaced, serene and inevitable.

Shop smart—shop bright—

His paw twitched toward his ear, as if he could physically turn the sound down. He took a slow breath through his nose, counting in fours. One‑two‑three‑four. One‑two‑three‑four.

It’s just a jingle, he told himself. You wrote worse for that betting app. Remember the one with the whistling ferret?

The memory, embarrassingly, made him snort. The tension eased a fraction.

“Behavioural response,” he muttered. “Right.”

He picked up the printed briefing Nigel had left on the table. The cover page bore the slogan in big, friendly letters. Inside, bullet points and buzzwords marched in tight formation. **Micro‑targeted affective priming.** **Subconscious mood scaffolding.** **Choice‑architecture augmentation.**

Miles’s ears tipped back. Somewhere under the jargon, something old and uncomfortable shifted.

He closed the folder, tucked it under his arm, and went to book his train to Manchester.

---

Loughton FreshMart at midday on a Sunday was its own sort of liturgy. Sliding doors whooshed open to admit worshippers in parkas and hoodies, carrying reusable bags, dragging children, pushing trolleys with one temperamental wheel each.

The scent here was immediately different: onion and orange, bakery bread and cleaning fluid, crowd‑sweat and the faint fur‑musk of half the local anthros. Bright banners proclaimed WEEKEND VALUE across fresh produce and meal deals.

Miles paused just inside the threshold, beyond the spray of damp footprints, and listened.

The sound system here was better than it had been a month ago; he’d signed off on the upgrade himself. The new mix flowed out in warm, curated waves. A gentle synth pad underpinned a piano line designed to be forgotten as soon as you stepped back into the car park.

Except.

There. Four notes, rising and falling in that familiar contour, woven through the background like a watermark.

His hackles lifted just enough to notice. He rolled his shoulders back, forcing the fur flat.

“Miles!”

He turned. Zara, the store’s assistant manager, trotted toward him from the customer service desk, striped tail flicking behind her. She was a zebra anthro in her early thirties, compact and kinetic, her black‑and‑white fur pattern broken only by a FreshMart lanyard and a pair of neon green trainers that violated three separate uniform guidelines. Her short mane was braided back with practical elastics, but a few strands rebelled, falling over one dark eye.

“Didn’t know we were being audited,” she said, grinning. “If I’d known, I’d have bribed the trolleys to behave.”

“No audit,” Miles said. “Just your friendly neighbourhood operations manager, out for a Sunday stroll.”

“On a Sunday?” She gave him a look that could have written a performance review. “Pull the other hoof, it’s got bells on. Is this about the music?”

He blinked. “Why do you ask?”

Zara jerked her chin toward the ceiling speakers. “Head office finally noticed we exist. We’ve had this new playlist thing for a week. Customers keep humming it in the fruit aisle.”

As if on cue, a human woman pushing a trolley drifted past. Under her breath, just audible to a shepherd’s ears, she murmured, “—feel it in your boooones…,” then stopped, frowned in faint confusion, and shook her head.

“See?” Zara said. “It’s like the world’s least threatening earworm. I’m not complaining; sales in bakery are up and nobody’s tried to strangle the self‑checkout this morning, but it’s weird.”

“Weird how?” Miles asked. He took out his notebook, an actual paper one, because some habits didn’t translate to apps.

Zara considered, chewing lightly on the end of a pen she’d produced from nowhere. “People seem… calmer. Slower, but in a good way. They browse more. Normally, you get this edge on a Sunday—everyone’s trying to cram a week of life admin into three hours. You can smell the stress. This week? It’s like someone turned the volume down on the collective panic.”

Her ears flicked back. “I don’t hate it. I just want to know what the catch is.”

Miles jotted that down. *Calmer. More browsing. Possible catch.*

“Any complaints?” he asked.

“Couple of older customers said the music made them feel ‘floaty,’” Zara said. “One badger gentleman—Mr. Patel, you’d like him, he’s got opinions about sourdough—said it made him think of a song he couldn’t place. He came back twice to the same aisle, like he was trying to remember the words.”

Miles’s paw tightened slightly on his pen. “Which aisle?”

“Household cleaning,” she said promptly. “As if Fairy Liquid was about to break into a solo.”

He nodded, scribbling. “Staff?”

“Jess on tills keeps whistling the tune,” Zara said. “I’m this close to banning humming in the break room.” She held up two striped fingers, very close together. “You want to ask her?”

Miles did. Jess was a young collie anthro with a perpetually worried brow and immaculate uniform. When he approached her lane, she straightened, ears perked in automatic customer‑service alert.

“Mr. Ferguson,” she said. “Do I need to put the ‘lane closing’ sign up?”

“Only if you fancy a break,” he said. “I just wanted your impression of the new music.”

Jess’s ears dipped, then rose again. “Oh. It’s… nice?” she ventured. “Feels sort of… cosy. Like when you’re little and someone’s making toast in the morning.” She frowned, dismissing her own simile. “Sorry, that’s not very helpful.”

“It is,” Miles said. “Do you notice it?”

“Not really, until it stops,” she said. “The other day, we had a glitch and it cut out for a few seconds. Everyone in the queue went sort of… twitchy. Like when the heating cuts in at night and wakes you up, but you didn’t realise it was what woke you.”

He added that to his notes. *Silence causes discomfort.*

“And you’ve been whistling it?” he asked lightly.

Jess flushed under her facial fur, ears tipping back. “Have I? Sorry. It’s just… you know when you get something stuck? It’s like that. I don’t even realise I’m doing it.”

“Join the club,” Miles said dryly. “Try not to drive yourself mad. Or the customers.”

He did a full circuit of the store. At the fresh produce, a squirrel kit bounced in the trolley seat, paws drumming an offbeat on the handle as their mother loaded apples. The kit was singing something under their breath, tail flicking in time.

“—shop bright, shop bright—”

Miles’s stomach dropped a centimetre.

“That’s catchy, isn’t it?” the mother said, noticing his glance. She was a deer anthro, antlers tipped with little plastic guards so she wouldn’t spear the stack of avocados by accident. “They picked it up on the telly, I think. Or in here. I swear they know your tunes better than their times tables.”

“We do aim for memorable,” Miles said, throat suddenly dry.

At the back of the store, by the warehouse doors, he found a corner with slightly less speaker coverage. He leaned against a stack of flour bags and closed his eyes, letting the sound wash over him.

The motif repeated at regular but not entirely predictable intervals, tucked into different instrumental voices. Sometimes it was piano, sometimes a higher synth, once in a faint glockenspiel line that made the fur along his arms lift.

He tested his pulse. Slightly elevated. Not panic, but not baseline either.

“Behavioural response,” he murmured. “Congratulations, Nigel.”

His phone buzzed. A message from Lillian: **[Band’s threatening mutiny if you nick my drummer for corporate cult duty. You alive?]**

He looked around the humming aisles, the calm shoppers, the quiet whirr of trolleys. He thought about the way he’d zoned out at the radio in their own kitchen.

**[Alive,]** he typed back. **[Investigating. Will report later. Tell them the cult doesn’t have biscuits.]**

Her reply arrived almost immediately. **[Bring your brain home with you. We’re short one drummer and one husband as it is.]**

His tail gave a small, private wag. The world tilted back toward normal by a degree and a half.

---

Two days later, Manchester Piccadilly FreshMart felt like walking into a different chapter of the same book.

The store sat just off the main station concourse, an anchor of light and relative calm amid the endless announcements, suitcase wheels, and coffee queues. City grime and train diesel drifted through the sliding doors every time they opened, overlaying the baseline scent of fruit, bread, meat, fur, scales, feathers.

Miles stood just inside the entrance, paws in his jacket pockets, and listened.

The same mix. The same motif. Only here, with the relentless flow of travellers, it landed differently. Where Loughton had felt like someone turning down the panic, Piccadilly felt more like someone smoothing the edges off urgency.

Shoppers who should have been in a hurry dawdled slightly longer by meal deal fridges. A fox in a business suit stood in front of the ready meals for a full minute, expression vague, fingers tapping on the trolley handle in perfect time to the unseen four‑note phrase.

“You all right there, mate?” a staffer called from the end of the aisle.

The fox blinked, shook himself, and laughed. “Yeah. Just… drifted off. Long day.” He pushed on, faster now, as if making up time he hadn’t realised he’d lost.

The store manager, a stout beaver anthro named Colin, met Miles by the customer service desk. His brown fur was flecked with grey around the muzzle, and his hi‑vis vest strained slightly over a generous middle.

“Was wondering when they’d send someone,” Colin said without preamble. “You here about the music?”

“Among other things,” Miles said. “You’ve noticed something.”

Colin rubbed the back of his thick neck, flattening the fur there. “Don’t get me wrong, it’s fine. Better than the old stuff. Staff like it. Customers’ve been… nicer. We’ve had fewer arguments over yellow‑sticker reductions this week than any time since Christmas 2012.”

“That sounds like a win,” Miles said, taking out his notebook again.

“It does,” Colin agreed. “But there’s this… oddness. You know me, Miles. I’ve been walking these aisles since before contactless. You get a feel. And this past week, every time I do a floor walk, there’s this moment where I realise I’ve been standing still for… I don’t know. Thirty seconds? A minute? Just listening.” He snorted. “Listening to lift music. Me.”

“What do you feel when that happens?” Miles asked.

Colin considered. “Not bad. Not good. Just… away. Like when you’re driving the ring road and your brain goes somewhere else and then you’re at your turnoff and you don’t remember the last three roundabouts.”

Miles’s fur prickled. “Right,” he said quietly. “Anyone complained?”

“Couple of customers said they lost track of time,” Colin said. “One lad missed his train because he ‘got into a groove’ in the crisp aisle.” He made air quotes with his thick paws. “He tried to blame us. I told him we don’t yet control the national rail timetable.”

Yet hung in the air like a joke that might not be one.

They walked the store together. Miles watched faces, ears, tails. Species told their own micro‑stories: a lynx pausing by the magazine rack, tufted ears flicking in time to an unheard beat; a human child tugging on their dad’s sleeve in the sweets aisle, chanting a half‑remembered line about bones; a coyote teenager stacking shelves, hips swaying in a rhythm just off the store’s tempo.

At the end of frozen foods, they nearly collided with a display unit someone had thoughtfully parked in the middle of the aisle: a floor‑standing cardboard tower laden with FreshMart‑branded headphones.

**FEEL IT DEEPER,** the sign proclaimed. **EXPERIENCE FRESHMART’S SONIC IDENTITY IN HD. INTRODUCTORY OFFER £14.99.**

“When did those go up?” Miles asked.

Colin scratched his chin with blunt claws. “Yesterday. Nigel’s team sent them. Said it was brand synergy. Got a box in the back as well, with a tablet thing playing the mix on loop. Staff keep sneaking in for five minutes on break. Says it helps them ‘reset.’”

Miles’s stomach made a small, uneasy twist. “And what do you think?”

“I think if it keeps them from throttling a customer over fourteen different kinds of oat milk, I’m not going to complain,” Colin said. “But I’d like to know what’s in it, exactly. You know?”

So would I, Miles thought.

He made his notes, thanked Colin, and stepped out into the station concourse again.

Here, the store’s carefully curated sonic world met the chaos of trains, announcements, buskers, and the tannoy’s merciless reverb. A busker—a young golden retriever anthro with a cheap guitar and an honest, open muzzle—was playing an Oasis cover near the entrance. The chord progression tangled, for a second, with the four‑note motif still echoing in Miles’s skull.

Shop smart. Shop bright.

He shoved his paws deeper into his jacket pockets until his claws bit his own palms.

On the platform, as he waited for his train back south, he pulled out Nigel’s briefing again. **BEHAVIOURAL RESPONSE. SUBCONSCIOUS MOOD SCAFFOLDING.**

Lillian’s voice floated up in his memory, dry as good gin. *No scaffolds. No tricks. Just sound moving air, the way it should.*

He stared at the page, at the graphs and the smiling stock‑photo shoppers.

For the first time since he’d taken the job at FreshMart, Miles Ferguson—engineer of aisles, tamer of supply chains—felt an unease that had nothing to do with delivery schedules.

He did not yet have the words for it. But in the back of his mind, four notes would not let him go.

Shop smart—shop bright—

He closed the folder, watched the incoming train’s lights appear down the track, and promised himself that when he got home, he would sit at Lillian’s piano and try to play the damn thing out of his system.

For the first time, he wondered if it would be allowed to leave.

## Chapter 3: The Sculpture and the Spell

The first thing Lillian noticed when she got home that Tuesday was that the house was humming.

Not metaphorically. Physically.

She stood in the narrow hall, shutting the front door behind her with the toe of one broad paw, and listened. Rain ticked on the letterbox, distant traffic shushed along the road, a neighbour’s television bled laughter through the party wall.

Underneath it all, the house had acquired a tone.

Not a note she could name. It was more like the feeling of a note, the way a room held the aftertaste of a chord once the piano lid came down. A pressure in the air, a gentle insistence along her whiskers.

“Sarah?” she called.

“In here!” came the answering shout from the back of the house, followed by the unmistakable crash of something ceramic being moved without adequate respect.

Lillian set her satchel down on the little hall table, hung her coat on its hook—badger‑sized, reinforced, purchased after a humiliating incident with a flimsy IKEA unit—and padded down the corridor. The scent of acrylic paint mingled with dinner smells: onions softening in a pan, garlic, something tomato‑based. Over all of it lay the faint tang of wet dog fur, graphite, and the citrus cleaner Miles favoured for the kitchen counters.

The hum grew stronger as she approached the dining room, which, on weekdays, was less for dining and more for whatever project their daughter had colonised it with.

Sarah Edgecombe‑Ferguson, golden retriever anthro, seventeen, artistic menace, was in the middle of the table. Not sitting on it—Lillian’s lectures on furniture respect had at least sunk in that far—but surrounded by drifts of paper, sketchbooks, and three different types of pencil. Her golden fur caught the overhead light in warm ripples, ears tucked back as she frowned at the page, tongue poking out of one side of her muzzle in that unconscious expression of concentration she had had since she was small.

On the wall behind her, the big pinboard that usually held school timetables and gig flyers had been requisitioned. It now displayed a sprawling collage: staff lines, hand‑drawn waveforms, scribbled annotations, and what looked suspiciously like supermarket floor plans.

“Well,” Lillian said, leaning on the doorframe. “Either you’ve finally decided to reorganise your life, or you’ve started a conspiracy wall.”

Sarah startled, fur fluffing, then relaxed when she saw who it was. “Mum! You’re back. Don’t touch anything. It’s in a… precarious balance.” She gestured with a pencil in each paw, creating an invisible diagram in the air.

“So not a life plan, then,” Lillian said. “Smells like pasta. Also indecision. Where’s your father?”

“Kitchen,” Sarah said. “He’s communing with the sauce. And trying not to think about graphs.”

That tracked. Lillian pushed off the doorframe, detouring to the kitchen long enough to kiss Miles on the cheek and taste tomato and anxiety on his fur. His ears flicked once in greeting, warm brown eyes softening when he saw her.

“Hey,” he murmured.

“Hey yourself,” she replied. “We’ll talk after we eat. Your child appears to be staging a musical coup in the dining room.”

“She’s been at it since I got back,” he said. “Said not to look until it was ‘coherent.’”

“Dread word.” Lillian patted his arm. “Don’t overcook the penne. I’m going to risk eye contact with the genius.”

Back in the dining room, she circled the table, claws clicking softly on the worn wood floor. Up close, the hum resolved into something more specific: the air seemed to hold four phantom steps of melody, repeated just often enough for her brain to anticipate them.

Shop smart. Shop bright.

No lyrics now, only contour. Up, up, down, hold.

“You’re humming,” she observed.

Sarah blinked, then clamped her jaws shut. “Sorry.” The apology sounded automatic, like someone hitting a familiar chord without thinking.

“Don’t be,” Lillian said. “Just tell me what we’re looking at before my brain slides out of my ears.”

Sarah sat back, ears angling self‑consciously. She wore a paint‑smudged hoodie over leggings that had once been black and were now a mottled archive of previous projects. A graphite streak bisected the gold fur along her right cheek, giving her the look of someone who had gone into battle with a pencil and lost.

“Okay,” she said, taking a breath. “So. You know how you and Dad always say music is just maths that learned table manners?”

“We say that because it is true,” Lillian said. “Do not tell the brass section, they’ll start believing they can count.”

“Right.” Sarah jabbed at the largest sheet pinned to the board, a series of rising and falling lines over a crude treble staff. “So I’ve been hearing this thing everywhere. On the radio, in the shop, even in that ad they keep playing before my art history videos. At first I thought it was just one of those catchy corporate earworms. But then…” She trailed off, flipping open a sketchbook and spreading it flat.

Inside, page after page showed the same motif in different guises. Four linked shapes, sometimes as notes, sometimes as abstract curves, sometimes as thick, gestural blocks of graphite.

“It’s the same phrase,” Sarah said. “Four notes, always the same interval pattern, but they keep changing the way it’s dressed up. Different instruments, different keys, different tempos. But the skeleton’s the same.”

Lillian felt the fur along her arms lift. “Go on,” she said lightly.

“At first I thought I was imagining it,” Sarah said. “Like when you get obsessed with a shape and see it everywhere. But then I started mapping where it shows up.” She pointed to the collage of floor plans. “Look. Loughton FreshMart. The motif repeats every… what, sixty seconds? Ninety? But the speakers are placed so you hit a fresh iteration just as you round certain corners. Bakery. Household cleaning. The end of the crisps aisle.” She tapped each location with a pencil tip.

“You’ve been timing it,” Lillian said.

“I’ve been *feeling* it,” Sarah corrected. “I sketched my route through the store like I do for galleries. It’s like there’s an invisible sculpture made of sound in the aisles. You walk through it and it wraps round you.”

She flipped to another page. This one held a perspective drawing of an aisle, trolleys parked at odd angles, shoppers rendered as loose, gestural figures. Above and around them, the four‑note motif was drawn as a ribbon of light, looping from speaker to speaker.

“See?” Sarah said, eyes bright now. “They think they’ve made background music. But they’ve actually built… I don’t know. A shape. A space. A spell.”

There was a time, Lillian reflected, when that last word would have been pure teenage melodrama. Today, it landed with a different weight.

She crossed to the pinboard, claws clicking softly on the cork as she traced one of the staff lines. Her badger eyes, sharp in low light, tracked the notes Sarah had written: tonic, minor third, fifth, suspended second. Familiar intervals.

“You’ve transcribed it,” she said.

“As best I can,” Sarah said. “It’s annoyingly bland, which makes it hard to nail down. But the contour’s always this.” She sketched four quick marks in the air. “Up, up, down, hold. Like… like when Dad used to count us into that stupid jingle you wrote at Trinity.”

Lillian’s paw stilled. “You remember that?”

Sarah snorted. “Mum, you two played it non‑stop for a month. You nearly summoned the neighbours’ complaints committee.”

“They objected to the cowbell,” Lillian said. “Phil went through a phase.”

“Anyway,” Sarah said, impatient now. “It’s *that* shape. I’m sure of it. But stretched and twisted and hidden inside other bits. Like… you know those pictures where there’s a face hidden in the branches if you squint?”

“Pareidolia,” Lillian said automatically. “The brain’s need to make meaning out of noise.”

“Exactly. Only this time, the noise is doing it on purpose.”

There was a clatter in the kitchen, followed by Miles’s voice and the soft curse of someone who had just dropped a wooden spoon. Lillian listened to the brief domestic chaos with half an ear. Her musician’s brain was busy turning Sarah’s drawings into sound.

Up, up, down, hold.

Shop smart. Shop bright.

She had heard dozens of advert jingles in her life, had been paid to write more than a few. Most of them clung for a week, then sloughed off. This one had settled in like an old lodger.

“How do you feel when you hear it?” she asked.

Sarah shrugged, ears flicking. “Depends. In the store, it makes everything feel… slower? Softer round the edges. Like someone put a slight blur filter on reality. On the radio, it just makes me want to change the station. It’s like my brain knows it’s being nudged and gets grumpy.”

“Any headaches? Dizziness?” Lillian asked, the ex‑teacher and current overprotective parent surfacing.

“No more than usual when I think about A‑levels,” Sarah said dryly. “Why?”

Because your father lost thirty seconds of himself in our kitchen, Lillian thought. Because Nigel Wetherby says behaviour as if it’s a hobby.

Aloud, she said, “Because I am your mother and I have a contractual obligation to worry.”

Sarah smiled, then went serious again. “Dad’s been weird about it,” she said. “He pretends he isn’t, but every time it comes on, his ears do that thing.” She twitched her own, mimicking the fractional flattening. “Did something happen at work?”

Lillian considered lying. Decided against it. “He heard it at home the other day and zoned out for a moment,” she said. “Said it was just stress.”

“Yeah, and I ‘just’ scroll my phone for three hours when I’m avoiding a painting,” Sarah said. “What if it’s more than that?”

“That,” Lillian said, “is what we are going to find out.”

Sarah’s tail thumped once against the back of her chair, sending a cascade of eraser shavings to the floor. “So you believe me?”

“I believed you when you were six and told me there was a monster living in the cupboard under the stairs,” Lillian said. “You were right. It was called ‘our filing system.’”

“You reorganised it,” Sarah pointed out.

“Exactly,” Lillian said. “We don’t ignore monsters. We give them labels and better shelving.”

The timer on the cooker beeped. From the kitchen, Miles called, “Pasta in three minutes! Whoever set up the dining room like a detective show, clear enough space for plates!”

“That would be you,” Lillian told Sarah.

“Art waits for no bolognese,” Sarah muttered, but she began shuffling papers anyway, carefully consolidating piles without disturbing their order.

Lillian helped, her broad paws surprisingly deft. As she stacked sketchbooks, her claws brushed graphite and ink, little ghosts of lines. The hum in the room swelled, as if pleased to have been noticed.

“After dinner,” she said, “you and I are going to the piano.”

Sarah’s ears perked. “To play it?”

“To see what happens when *we* play it,” Lillian said. “On our terms.”

---

The upright piano had acquired a small crowd.

After bolognese (Miles’s sauce: excellent; Miles’s appetite: distracted), they relocated to the living room. Miles took the armchair, large shepherd form folded into it with the uneasy grace of someone trying to look relaxed and failing. Sarah sprawled on the rug, sketchbook balanced on her knees, tail flicking absently. Lillian settled on the piano bench, the wood comfortably creaking under her weight.

Outside, the rain had given way to a soft, persistent drizzle. Inside, lamplight pooled on the piano’s varnish, catching the worn spots where years of paws had rested.

Lillian flexed her fingers, black‑and‑white paws hovering over the keys. “All right,” she said. “Sarah, count me in.”

Sarah hummed the four‑note contour under her breath, then said, “One, two, three, four.”

Lillian played.

It was deceptively simple on the keys. Four notes: up a minor third, up a major second, down a perfect fourth, hold. She let them ring, let the room catch their shape.

Miles’s ears flicked. His tail gave an involuntary twitch.

“That’s it,” Sarah said quietly. “That’s the bone of it.”

Lillian repeated it, this time embedding it in a slightly fuller progression. I–vi–IV–V, the world’s most overused chord loop, the kind that had sold everything from toothpaste to insurance.

Up, up, down, hold.

Shop smart. Shop bright.

Her own fur prickled. There was a familiarity here that went beyond melody. It wasn’t just that she and Miles had once written a parody jingle with that contour. It was the way the phrase seemed to slot into a groove in the brain, as if it had always been there, waiting for someone to hang notes on it.

“Feels like chewing gum,” Sarah said suddenly.

“Pardon?” Lillian asked, fingers still moving.

“In your head,” Sarah said. “Like it stretches and sticks and you can’t quite swallow it.” She scribbled rapidly, sketching the phrase as a series of interlocking loops.

Miles shifted in his chair. “It feels like waiting for something,” he said. “Like there’s a next bit I should remember, and if I just…” He trailed off, eyes going slightly distant.

Lillian stopped playing at once.

The silence hit the room like a small, sharp shock. Sarah’s ears flattened. Miles blinked, then exhaled, shoulders dropping a fraction.

“Sorry,” he said, rubbing at his muzzle. “Did it again, didn’t I.”

“For about three seconds,” Lillian said. “Where did you go?”

He hesitated. “Nowhere. Just… inside the tune.”

“What were you thinking?” she pressed gently.

“That I should… go down this aisle,” he said slowly, frowning as he groped for the thought. “Pick up that product. Follow the arrows. Stupid, I know. Like one of those flow diagrams I make for delivery schedules, but for… shopping.”

Sarah’s pencil stopped moving. “Mum,” she said. “The sculpture. It’s not just in space. It’s… in him.”

“Thank you, darling, that’s not ominous at all,” Miles muttered.

Lillian ignored the quip. Her mind was spinning in a different key now, one composed of audiology lectures, half‑remembered neuroscience articles, and a healthy dose of Scottish suspicion.

“We know certain patterns are sticky,” she said, more to herself than to the room. “Hooks. Earworms. Repetition, interval preference. Pop songs live off it. Advertisers pay good money for it. That’s not new.”

“This is more than sticky,” Sarah said. “It’s… structural.”

Lillian nodded. “And our fox friend at Wetherby Creative talks about *behavioural response* and ‘subconscious mood scaffolding’ as if he’s ordering coffee.”

Miles’s ears tipped back. “You talked to Nigel?”

“Not yet,” Lillian said. “But you did.”

He grimaced. “Blue Room presentation. Graphs. Heat maps. The phrase ‘choice‑architecture augmentation’ was used without anyone being arrested.”

“That’s not architecture, that’s puppetry,” Lillian said.

Sarah looked between them. “So you think they’re doing it on purpose? Not just, like, accidentally inventing a cursed ringtone?”

“Nobody ‘accidentally’ rolls out a national sonic identity,” Lillian said. “At best, they don’t care what else it does as long as it sells biscuits. At worst…” She let the sentence hang.

Miles swallowed. His paws, resting on his knees, flexed once, claws dimpling his trousers. “At worst, they’re using sound as a script,” he said quietly. “A way to write behaviour into people without them noticing.”

The word script hung in the air like a key just out of reach.

Lillian played the phrase again, this time very softly, almost under her breath. Sarah watched her paws as if they were writing in the air. Miles stared at the middle distance.

“Can we break it?” Sarah asked.

Lillian looked at her. “Break what?”

“The spell,” Sarah said. “If it’s a structure, there must be a way to… unbuild it. De‑tune it. Like when you change one colour in a pattern and the whole thing stops working.”

Trust her daughter to approach mass behavioural manipulation as an art installation.

“Maybe,” Lillian said slowly. “If we understand what it’s doing. The intervals it favours, the way it repeats, where it sits in the frequency range. There are studies on music and suggestibility. I’ve read some. Mostly to argue with them, but still.”

“We could run an experiment,” Sarah said, eyes lighting. “Record it. Play variations. See how people react.”

“We are not using your classmates as unwitting test subjects,” Lillian said at once.

“I was going to say the band,” Sarah said innocently.

“Absolutely not,” Miles and Lillian said together.

They exchanged a look. In that shared glance lay twenty‑plus years of gig nights, studio sessions, and the kind of co‑conspiratorial intimacy that came from having once written a jingle so catchy it made a brand manager cry.

“We need more data,” Lillian said at last. “Not just feelings. Observations. Patterns. You’ve started that.” She nodded toward the pinboard. “We build on it. Quietly.”

“Quietly as in ‘don’t tip off Dad’s employers that we think they’re casting spells through the PA’?” Sarah said.

“Exactly that,” Lillian said. “Miles, love, you keep doing what they’ve asked—visit stores, talk to managers. But this time, listen with this in mind. Where the motif happens. What people do just before and just after. Sarah and I will map what we can from this end.”

Miles rubbed his muzzle again, fur rasping under his claws. “You’re suggesting we investigate my employer for subliminal mind‑tuning,” he said.

“I’m suggesting we make sure the people who write our direct debits aren’t also writing our brains,” Lillian said calmly. “If it turns out to be nothing worse than Nigel being up himself about semiotics, we can all go back to shouting at the television in peace.”

Sarah snorted. “As if.”

“And if it’s more?” Miles asked.

Lillian looked at him, at the line of worry between his brows that hadn’t been there in their twenties. At the way his tail lay still along the chair, the usual easy wag stilled by something heavier.

“Then,” she said, “we do what we always do. We outplay them.”

She let her paws settle on the keys again, this time not on the jingle, but on a different progression entirely. Something older, in a mode that had comforted badgers in burrows long before supermarkets existed. A tune her grandmother had hummed while kneading bread.

The house’s hum shifted, for a moment, to meet it. The four‑note motif still lurked at the edges of her hearing, but it no longer felt like the only shape in the room.

Sarah relaxed fractionally, shoulders dropping. “That one’s better,” she said.

“A counter‑spell,” Lillian said lightly.

“Is that how this works now?” Miles asked. “We have spells and counter‑spells?”

“It’s how music has always worked,” Lillian said. “Ask anyone who’s ever been marched to war behind a drum.”

Miles closed his eyes. “I just wanted to make sure people knew where the tinned tomatoes were,” he said. “How did we get here?”

“Capitalism,” Sarah said promptly.

“Art school answer,” Lillian said. “Accurate, but incomplete.”

She let the last chord fade, then turned on the bench to face them both fully.

“We keep this between us for now,” she said. “No panicking, no wild accusations, no posting conspiracy threads on whatever cursed platforms you haunt.” She aimed that last part at Sarah.

Sarah held up her paws. “I’m not stupid, Mum. If this is real, shouting about it on the internet is the worst thing we could do.”

“Good,” Lillian said. “We observe. We listen. We gather.”

“And then?” Miles asked.

“And then,” she said, “if we find proof that Wetherby & Co. are doing more than nudging people toward the biscuits, we go and put a very large, very heavy spanner in their works.”

“Metaphorically,” Miles said quickly.

“Of course,” Lillian said, in a tone that had made entire brass sections reconsider their life choices.

---

Later, when the washing‑up was done and Sarah had retreated to her room with a podcast and a stack of sketchbooks, Lillian and Miles lay in bed with the light off.

The house’s night sounds folded around them: the soft tick of cooling pipes, a fox barking in the distance, a late train’s rumble. Even here, in the quiet, she could feel the ghost of that four‑note shape lurking at the edges of her awareness.

“You’re awake,” Miles said softly into the dark.

“So are you,” she replied.

He shifted, fur brushing the sheet, and she felt his paw find hers under the duvet. His pads were warm, claws carefully angled away. She squeezed back.

“Do you think I’m overreacting?” he asked. “About the music. The zoning out. The… feeling.”

“I think you are an engineer,” Lillian said, staring at the dim suggestion of the ceiling. “Engineers don’t like black boxes. When someone hands them a system and says ‘don’t worry about what’s inside,’ they worry.”

“Nigel would say it’s just clever branding,” Miles said. “That everyone’s doing it. That if we don’t, someone else will.”

“Nigel would also put glitter on a moral hazard and call it experiential,” Lillian said.

Miles huffed a laugh, then sobered. “If they *are* doing something… more… why use *my* tune?”

“Because it works,” Lillian said simply. “Because it stuck in some junior creative’s brain twenty years ago and never left. Because somebody in a meeting said, ‘remember that demo from that band with the badger singer and the cute drummer?’ and everyone nodded and then an algorithm chewed it up and spat it back out in four million data‑optimised variations.”

He was quiet for a long moment. “Feels like a violation,” he said at last.

“It is,” she said. “Of you. Of the people listening. Of what music is for.”

“What *is* music for, then?” he asked, half teasing, half not.

She considered. “Telling the truth sideways,” she said. “Carrying people when words give out. Marking time. Making sense of chaos. Making more chaos, sometimes. *Not* turning people into better‑behaved shoppers without their consent.”

He squeezed her paw. “You always did have a way with mission statements.”

“Put it on a mug,” she said.

They lay there, paws joined, listening to the night.

At the edge of her hearing, Lillian fancied she could distinguish between sounds that belonged to the world and sounds that had been added to it. A siren, distant and real. The faint thump of next door’s teenager’s bass through the wall. The imagined echo of four notes that had followed them from kitchen to store to piano.

Somewhere in Manchester, she thought, Nigel Wetherby likely slept well, dreaming in charts.

“We’ll figure it out,” she said into the dark, more to the pattern itself than to the man.

Beside her, Miles’s breathing deepened. Whether he believed her or was simply too tired to argue, she couldn’t tell.

Lillian Edgecombe closed her eyes, summoned the feel of her grandmother’s tune, and let it play her down into sleep like a counter‑charm.

The house hummed on, halfway between spell and shelter.

## Chapter 4: The Brand Summit

The FreshMart Brand Summit was held in a converted warehouse in Manchester’s Northern Quarter, the sort of space that had once stored things like cotton and now stored things like concepts.

On the morning of the launch, rain turned the cobbles outside into a glistening grid. Vans off‑loaded crates and flight cases, human and anthro crew in black T‑shirts darting like purposeful shadows. Inside, the raw brick walls had been softened with enormous banners bearing the FreshMart leaf and phrases like **NOURISHING TOMORROW, TODAY** and **FEEL IT IN YOUR BONES** in a font that looked scientifically proven to reassure shareholders.

Up in the rafters, a state‑of‑the‑art sound system hung like a nest of shiny black bats.

Lillian Edgecombe stopped just inside the entrance and felt every hair along her arms lift.

“Subtle,” she murmured.

Beside her, Sarah shoved her hands further into the pockets of her oversized denim jacket. Her golden retriever ears were half‑flattened against the usual Northern Quarter soundscape of espresso machines and earnest conversations, now overlaid by the conference’s pre‑show playlist.

“Feels like walking into someone’s brain,” Sarah said. “But, like, if their brain had catering.”

The air hummed. Not loudly; that was the point. A wash of pads, tasteful percussion, the occasional glitter of piano. And under it, woven through like a watermark on expensive paper, the now‑familiar four‑note motif. Up, up, down, hold.

Shop smart. Shop bright.

“Remember,” Lillian said softly, adjusting the strap of her satchel where it cut into her shoulder, “we are here as guests of honour.”

“Dad’s the guest of honour,” Sarah said. “We’re… support.”

“Every headliner needs a rhythm section,” Lillian said. “And a stagehand with opinions.”

Sarah huffed a laugh, tail giving an anxious flick behind her knees. Her badge, printed that morning at registration, read **SARAH EDGEOMBE‑FERGUSON – FAMILY / CREATIVE** in block capitals. It was already speckled with paint fingerprints from her hoodie sleeve.

Lillian’s badge simply read **LILLIAN EDGECOMBE – FAMILY / MUSIC ACADEMY**. She had resisted the temptation to add *and witch* in biro.

Across the space, near the stage, Miles stood talking to a pair of regional managers. Even at a distance, Lillian could read his posture: shoulders rolled back by conscious effort, ears pricked, tail still in that careful, managerial way that signalled confidence to nervous subordinates and did not at all say *my employers may be using my old jingle to rearrange reality*.

He caught sight of them, excused himself, and crossed the floor. The crowd parted instinctively around him; a large German Shepherd with determined purpose and a lanyard had that effect.

“You made it,” he said, relief warming his voice as he leaned down to brush his muzzle briefly against Lillian’s cheek. His fur smelled of hotel shampoo and the faint, metallic tang of anxiety.

“We took the train with the influencers,” Sarah said. “If I hear the phrase ‘brand journey’ one more time, I’m defecting to Aldi.”

Miles winced in sympathy. “Nigel’s presentation is heavy on the journeys,” he said. “Sorry.”

“We’re not here for Nigel,” Lillian said. “We’re here for you. And for the bats.” She nodded upward at the suspended speakers.

Miles followed her gaze. “They’ve brought in an audio firm from Berlin,” he said. “Nigel keeps dropping their name like a designer label. Apparently the system can dynamically adapt to crowd noise and behaviour in real time.”

“Of course it can,” Lillian said. “Wouldn’t want anyone escaping.”

He gave her a look that was half amusement, half plea. “Please don’t hex my employer in the first ten minutes,” he said under his breath.

“I make no promises about Nigel,” she replied. “Basil I will spare. For now.”

Sarah glanced between them, then at the stage. “Where do you need us?”

“Audience,” Miles said. “Front third if you can manage it. Nigel thinks you’re here to make me look relatable.”

“We’ll do our best to look like a focus group,” Sarah said gravely.

He squeezed her shoulder, briefly, claws careful not to snag her jacket. “I’m on in the second segment, after Nigel does his theatre,” he said to Lillian. “Store walk‑through and ‘real‑world testimony.’ Then Basil does the visionary bit. Then Q&A.”

“Anything we should know?” Lillian asked.

He hesitated. His ears tipped back, then forward again. “They’ve built a demo environment in the back,” he said quietly. “Mini supermarket. They’re going to bring some delegates through in small groups to ‘experience the sonic identity.’ Nigel asked if I’d lead one of the walk‑throughs. He said it would be ‘authentic.’”

The word sat between them like something left on the pavement by an untrained dog.

“We’ll be listening,” Lillian said.

Miles nodded once, sharply, as if bracing himself, then straightened his tie. “I’ve got to go do a mic check,” he said. “Try not to get recruited by any rival brands while I’m gone.”

“Depends what their biscuits are like,” Sarah said.

He snorted, then headed for the stage, tail swinging with rehearsed ease.

By the time the lights dimmed and the first slide appeared on the screen, the warehouse was full. Retail executives in suits, store managers in branded fleeces, marketing people in aggressively interesting glasses; humans, dogs, foxes, deer, cats, the odd dragonet from Finance whose scales glimmered under the downlights. The smell of coffee, perfume, fur, and anticipation hung over the rows of chairs.

Nigel Wetherby walked onstage to polite applause, every hair of his rust‑red fur in place. His navy suit caught the light just enough to suggest subtle expense. His tail, neatly groomed, made a modest, media‑trained sway behind him.

“Good morning, FreshMart family!” he called, as if addressing a stadium.

“Drink every time he says ‘family,’” Sarah whispered.

“You’d be unconscious by slide three,” Lillian murmured back.

On the huge screen behind Nigel, the words **SONIC IDENTITY** appeared over a stylised waveform. The house mix swelled slightly, then smoothed out, as if bowing to its master of ceremonies.

Nigel launched into his patter. He talked about cluttered marketplaces and overwhelmed consumers. He talked about the tyranny of choice, the paradox of abundance, the lonely shopper in aisle seven searching for meaning in the tinned tomatoes. He used phrases like “emotional wayfinding” and “affective resonance.” He quoted a study about how tempo affected trolley speed.

Lillian listened with half an ear. The other half was tuned to the room.

At key beats in Nigel’s speech, the four‑note motif surfaced in the background track. Sometimes on chimes, sometimes on a low synth, sometimes implied in the harmony rather than stated outright. Each time, she saw a ripple run through the crowd: a collective lean forward, a softening of shoulders, a slight stilling of fidgeting paws.

Sarah was sketching furiously, pen scratching quietly over paper. On her notepad, rows of tiny chairs marched across the page, each occupant rendered as a quick gesture; above them, the motif traced arcs that connected them in an invisible lattice.

“We’re not just in the grocery business,” Nigel said, pacing the width of the stage with practised ease. “We’re in the *feeling* business. When your customers step into a FreshMart, they’re not just shopping for dinner. They’re shopping for comfort. For safety. For identity. Our new sonic identity ensures that, from the moment they cross the threshold, they know—deep down, in their bones—that they’re home.”

On the word bones, the motif chimed, clear as a teaspoon on glass.

Shop smart. Shop bright.

The crowd let out a soft, collective sigh.

“You feel that?” Nigel said, smiling. “That’s not an accident.”

“No,” Lillian murmured. “I don’t suppose it is.”

He advanced his slide deck: heatmaps of dwell times, graphs of basket sizes, quotes from customers about how “relaxed” they felt in trial stores. Short video clips showed shoppers in various locations, all moving with the same curiously unhurried gait.

Then the video changed.

This time, the camera followed a single figure: a middle‑aged fox in a tan trenchcoat, entering a FreshMart. As he walked, glowing arrows appeared overlaid on the floor, guiding him through the aisles. Each time he reached a junction, the four‑note motif twinkled, and his path curved smoothly toward an endcap or a promotional display.

“We call this ‘choice architecture,’” Nigel said. “By shaping the soundscape, we reduce friction. We *gently* nudge customers toward better decisions—for them and for us.”

“Better for *whom*?” Sarah hissed under her breath.

Lillian’s claws pricked her own palms.

“Of course,” Nigel said, spreading his paws in a parody of humility, “this isn’t about control. We’re not making anyone do anything they don’t want to do. We’re simply giving them a soundtrack that supports the choices they already wish to make.”

The four‑note motif glided under the word soundtrack like a knife under skin.

Lillian thought of Miles in their kitchen, eyes fogged, humming words he hadn’t sung in twenty years.

Next, Nigel called Miles onstage.

“None of this would be possible,” he said, “without the hard work of our operations teams on the ground. People who understand that sound isn’t just something you pipe in—it’s something you *engineer.* Please welcome our very own maestro of the aisles, Miles Ferguson.”

Applause rose. Miles stepped into the lights, fur catching a faint sheen. His flannel shirt had been upgraded to a crisp white one under a charcoal blazer, but he still looked solidly himself: broad‑shouldered, steady, paws wrapped around the handheld mic with the same surety he’d once wrapped them around drumsticks.

“Morning,” he said, ears angling forward. His voice, when it came through the speakers, was deeper than Nigel’s, less polished, more grounded. “I’m Miles. I manage operations for the North and East region, which is a fancy way of saying I make sure the beans get to the shelves and the lights stay on.”

Polite chuckles.

Nigel beamed at him like a proud puppeteer. “Miles, you’ve been visiting our trial stores. What have you seen?”

Miles adjusted his grip on the mic. Lillian saw the tiny flick of his tail, the set of his jaw. He had decided something.

“On paper,” he said, “I’ve seen what Nigel’s shown you. Longer dwell times. Bigger baskets. Fewer complaints at the self‑checkout.”

The audience nodded. That, they understood.

“On the floor,” Miles went on, “I’ve seen something else.”

Lillian’s breath caught. Nigel’s tail stilled.

“I’ve seen customers who usually rush through on their lunch break actually stop and breathe,” Miles said. “I’ve seen staff who were on the edge of burnout say they feel a little calmer on shift. I’ve seen a kid in Loughton come in with his grandad and sing along to the motif while they picked out apples. It made the grandad cry. In a good way.”

The room softened. This was safe territory: heart‑warming anecdotes.

“I’ve also,” Miles said, and here his ears tipped back just a fraction, “caught myself standing in the middle of an aisle, not quite remembering how I got there.”

The room shifted. A ripple of unease ran through the third row and leapt to the back.

Nigel laughed lightly, stepping in. “We’ve all been there, haven’t we?” he said. “You go in for milk, you come out with three bags and no idea what happened in between.”

“True,” Miles said. “But usually that’s because I got distracted by the endcap of biscuits. This felt… different.”

On the word different, the house mix dipped, almost inaudibly. Lillian’s ears, trained in a thousand rehearsal rooms, picked it at once. The four‑note motif slid in under the silence like someone smoothing a tablecloth over a spill.

Miles blinked, just once. Then he smiled.

“That said,” he continued, “we’ve been monitoring staff well‑being and customer feedback closely. So far, the response has been overwhelmingly positive. People say the store feels like a calmer space. In a world as noisy as ours, that’s not nothing.”

Lillian’s claws dug into her own knees. She recognised the tonal shift. He had wanted to push further and had… not.

How much of that, she wondered, was self‑censorship—and how much was the script?

Nigel nodded, seizing the baton. “Exactly,” he said. “Thank you, Miles. It’s about creating environments that support better experiences. And experiences create loyalty.”

The motif twinkled on loyalty.

They dismissed Miles to more applause. He walked off into the wings, shoulders tight.

Sir Basil took the stage next, to talk about vision and legacy. He spoke in warm generalities about nourishing communities and sustainable futures, about FreshMart as a “third place” between home and work. He misquoted a philosopher. He almost pronounced “psychoacoustic” correctly on the third attempt.

Lillian barely heard him. Beside her, Sarah had stopped sketching and was staring at the ceiling speakers as if willing them to confess.

At last, Nigel returned to announce the practical demonstration.

“We’ve talked about the data,” he said. “We’ve talked about the feelings. Now we want you to *experience* the sonic identity for yourselves.”

On cue, a section of the back wall parted to reveal a corridor lit in FreshMart green. Above it, a sign: **EXPERIENCE ZONE A.**

“In a moment,” Nigel said, “we’ll invite you to join guided walk‑throughs of our prototype environment. Small groups, tailored tours. Some of you will go with our store design team. Some with our marketing folks. And one lucky group,” he flashed a practiced grin, “will get to be led by Miles himself.”

The crowd murmured, amused.

“While those sessions are running,” Nigel went on, “the rest of you are invited to explore our innovation gallery, meet our partners, and enjoy the refreshments. Please drink responsibly and think ambitiously.”

Polite laughter.

“Group assignments will appear on the screens in the foyer,” he concluded. “Follow the arrows. FreshMart will do the rest.”

The four‑note motif chimed, and the house lights came up.

---

“We’re going in,” Sarah said.

They stood by one of the foyer screens, watching names flicker into place under group headings. **EXPERIENCE ZONE A – GROUP 3 – GUIDE: MILES FERGUSON.** Beneath it, a list of delegates from various regions. At the bottom:

**LILLIAN EDGECOMBE – FAMILY / MUSIC ACADEMY**

**SARAH EDGEOMBE‑FERGUSON – FAMILY / CREATIVE**

“Nigel really can’t resist theatre,” Lillian said.

“Is this good for us or bad?” Sarah asked.

“That,” Lillian said, “is what we are here to find out.”

They joined the small knot of Group 3 participants gathering by the corridor entrance. A human woman from Marketing adjusted her scarf and chatted about brand touchpoints with a fox from Merchandising. A tall otter in an impeccably cut suit kept checking his watch. Name badges bobbed, reflecting the overhead light.

Miles appeared, now without his blazer, sleeves rolled, as if to signal this was Practical Time. He gave Lillian and Sarah a brief, private look that said more than the words he used.

“Right,” he said to the group. “Thanks for coming along. This is our prototype environment. Think of it as a lab where we’ve brought together layout, lighting, and the new sonic identity to see how they behave together. No clipboards required today—just your impressions.”

He led them down the corridor. As they walked, the air thickened with the soundscape.

The prototype store wasn’t large—a handful of aisles, a condensed version of reality. Shelves were fully stocked, labels facing front with military precision. The lighting was warm, calculated to within an inch of its life. Somewhere, an artificial bread smell puffed through vents.

The speakers were discreet, tucked into corners and disguised as ceiling fixtures. Their presence was obvious only if you knew to look. Lillian knew to look.

The four‑note motif was everywhere.

Not constant; that would have been too simple. It came and went, slipping in at junctions, surfacing at the end of aisles, woven into different instrumental colours. Here, a soft marimba; there, a muted trumpet; there again, a high synth that tickled the edges of hearing.

“As you move through the space,” Miles said, “pay attention to how you feel at key points. Do you feel rushed? Calm? Overloaded? Barely notice anything? There are no wrong answers.”

Lillian pushed a trolley because that was what one did in these spaces, a large badger in a comfortable black dress and boots, moving at half speed. Sarah walked beside her, sketchbook in one paw, pencil in the other, eyes everywhere.

At the end of the first aisle, the motif chimed. The group, almost as one, curved toward a display of FreshMart‑branded ready meals.

“You’ll notice we’ve placed key promotions where natural decision points occur,” Miles said, as if narrating a nature documentary. “The sound supports that.”

Lillian watched her own paws on the trolley handle. They had twitched, micro‑movement, preparing to turn before she’d decided to. She forced them to keep going straight. The effort felt like stepping out of a river’s current.

In the household goods section, Sarah stopped dead.

“Mum,” she whispered. “Look.”

On the wall above the cleaning products, a decorative installation had been hung for the summit: a series of translucent panels printed with abstract patterns, back‑lit in FreshMart green. At first glance, it was corporate art—safe, pleasing, forgettable.

To anyone who had spent the last week drawing the motif, it was something else.

“They’ve turned it into visual branding,” Sarah said, voice tight. “The four‑note shape. Over and over. You could trace the intervals with your finger.”

She did, paw hovering in the air. Up, up, down, hold.

Lillian’s muzzle tightened. “Of course they have,” she said. “Multi‑channel reinforcement. If you can’t hear it, you can see it.”

The Marketing woman behind them snapped a photo on her phone. “Love how the graphics echo the audio,” she said. “It really lands the story.”

“It certainly lands something,” Lillian said.

They rounded the next aisle. Miles was talking about basket composition and wayfinding. His voice had taken on that slightly distant tone she recognised from committee meetings: present, but running a secondary process in the background.

As they approached the snacks endcap, the motif surfaced again, this time on a bright little glockenspiel line. Lillian watched the otter in the suit. His paw, which had been empty, reached out and picked up a packet of crisps almost before his eyes had settled on it.

He blinked, looked down at the packet, and laughed. “Looks like it works,” he said.

“Or you like crisps,” Lillian said mildly.

He shrugged. “Same difference.” He dropped the packet into the trolley as if that settled the matter.

At the self‑checkout area, the music softened, the motif moving into a low, soothing register.

“We’ve tuned this zone to reduce stress,” Miles said. “Lower tempo, warmer harmonies.”

The Marketing woman nodded appreciatively. “People hate this part,” she said. “Anything that keeps them from throwing avocados at the machine is a win.”

“Do you ever worry,” Lillian asked, voice light, “that you’re making it a little *too* pleasant?”

Heads turned toward her. Miles’s ears twitched.

“In what sense?” asked the otter, curious.

“In the sense that people are being guided very… smoothly,” Lillian said. “Almost as if someone’s written a choreography in their heads without asking.”

“It’s just nudging,” the Marketing woman said. “We’re not forcing anyone. We’re helping them.”

“Like putting handrails on a staircase,” the fox from Merchandising added.

“Or indicators on a car,” another delegate said.

“Or instructions on a fire exit,” someone else suggested.

The analogies piled up, each more reasonable than the last. The motif chimed again, subtly, and the tension in the group dissipated.

Sarah shot Lillian a look that said *see?*

Miles cleared his throat. “At the end of the day,” he said, “our job is to make it easier for customers to get what they need. We’re just trying to take some friction out of the system.”

His words were company‑approved. His eyes, when they met Lillian’s for a fraction of a second, were not.

The tour ended back in the foyer. Feedback tablets waited on high tables, asking participants to rate their experience on a scale of one to five leaves. Delegates dutifully tapped answers, some humming under their breath.

“Well,” Sarah said, once they were out of earshot, “that was horrifying.”

“On a spectrum of horrors,” Lillian said, “I’d call it… insidious.”

“Are we sure we can’t just burn it down?” Sarah asked.

“The warehouse or capitalism?” Lillian said.

“Yes,” Sarah said.

Lillian’s striped muzzle twitched. “We’ll start with something smaller,” she said. “Like a chord progression.”

She looked back at the prototype entrance, where another group was filing in behind a different guide. Above them, the sign glowed green. Somewhere inside, the four‑note motif was looping, patiently, writing pathways into anyone who walked through.

“They’re going to roll this out everywhere,” Sarah said quietly.

“Not if we can help it,” Lillian said.

“Do we have a plan?”

“We have a hypothesis and a piano,” Lillian said. “That’s how revolutions start in this family.”

The rest of the summit blurred: break‑out sessions, networking, canapés shaped like miniature sandwiches. Nigel did a fireside chat about “the future of experiential retail.” Basil shook hands and said things about “leveraging potential.”

Lillian spoke politely to people who asked about the music academy, fielded questions about youth engagement, endured three separate conversations with men who insisted on telling her about their favourite jazz albums.

All the while, the motif played on.

By late afternoon, the formal programme wound down. Delegates began to drift toward the exits, lanyards askew, clutching gift bags containing FreshMart‑branded notebooks and, inevitably, a pair of the sonic identity headphones Colin had mentioned.

In a corner of the foyer, a small cluster had formed around a display plinth. On it, under a clear acrylic case, sat a sleek white device roughly the size of a lunchbox.

**FRESHMART HOME HUB – PILOT**, read the sign. **BRING THE FEELING HOME.**

“Oh, brilliant,” Sarah said. “They’re making it portable.”

“Of course they are,” Lillian said.

The device, according to the placard, was a smart speaker pre‑loaded with the FreshMart sonic identity package. It promised to “transform your kitchen into a zone of calm productivity” and “sync seamlessly with your in‑store experience.”

“It’ll talk to your loyalty card,” said a man nearby, voice full of admiration. “Imagine the cross‑promotion opportunities.”

Lillian imagined them and did not like what she saw.

“They want it in people’s houses,” Sarah said. “In their literal bones.”

“Which means,” Lillian said, “that whatever we do next has to happen before the hub does.”

Sarah swallowed. “Mum,” she said quietly, “what if it’s already too late? It’s in the adverts, the stores, the summit. People are humming it on trains. Maybe it’s just… in the water now.”

Lillian looked at her daughter—golden fur, graphite streaks, eyes that saw patterns where others saw wallpaper. Then she looked across the room at Miles, who was standing with Nigel and Basil, listening while the fox gestured at a chart on a tablet.

Miles’s ears were angled politely forward. His tail hung in that carefully neutral line that meant he was bracing.

“It’s not too late,” Lillian said. “Not while people can still hear the difference between what’s being done *to* them and what they choose for themselves.”

Sarah’s ears tipped. “And if they can’t?”

“Then we teach them,” Lillian said. “With whatever tools we have.”

She watched Nigel tap the tablet, watched Miles nod. The motif swelled in the background, sweet and insistent.

“Come on,” she said to Sarah. “We need to steal your father for a bit before they plug him directly into the PA.”

They crossed the room. Up close, Nigel’s fur looked even more carefully maintained than onstage, each hair lying in compliant alignment. Basil’s tie had migrated half an inch off‑centre.

“Lillian!” Basil said warmly when he saw her. “Marvellous to finally meet the famous badger behind the man.”

“I prefer to think of us as a three‑piece,” Lillian said. “This is Sarah.”

“The artist!” Nigel said, eyes bright with professional curiosity. “Miles has told us all about your work. Loved your portfolio site.”

Sarah blinked. “You looked at my portfolio?” she said.

“We like to keep tabs on creative talent in the family,” Nigel said smoothly. “You see patterns others miss. That’s valuable.”

Lillian did not like the way he said patterns.

“We were just talking about next phases,” Basil said. “Rolling out the sonic identity to more stores. The hub project. Very exciting.”

“I’m sure,” Lillian said. “I’m afraid we’re going to steal Miles for a bit. Family debrief.”

Nigel’s ears tilted, curiosity and calculation flickering together. “Of course,” he said. “Don’t keep him all to yourself. The brand needs him too.”

“We had him first,” Sarah said, too sweetly.

They extracted Miles and found a quiet corner behind a pillar, out of the main acoustic hotspot.

“You nearly did it,” Lillian said without preamble. “Onstage. You nearly told them.”

Miles rubbed the back of his neck, claws raspling his fur. “I wanted to,” he said. “Then it hit. The phrase. The room. Everything sort of… slid.”

“You felt it?” Sarah asked.

“Like fog,” he said. “I had the words lined up and then… nothing. Just the approved script.”

He looked between them, ears tipped back. “If I can’t even finish a sentence about this in a room full of my colleagues, how are we supposed to fight it?”

“By choosing a different room,” Lillian said. “And a different audience. Nigel built this space. He controls the mix, the visuals, the cues. We don’t argue with a fox in his own burrow.”

“So where do we fight?” Sarah asked.

“Somewhere the soundscape is ours,” Lillian said. “Somewhere we can set the tempo.”

She glanced at her watch, at the station timetable app on her phone. “We go home tonight,” she said. “We get the band. We get your students. We get anyone who’ll listen. And we teach them to hear it.”

“A counter‑concert,” Sarah said, eyes lighting.

“A workshop,” Lillian said. “Concert sounds too much like fun. This will be education.”

Miles’s tail gave a faint, hopeful wag. “You think that’ll be enough?”

“It’ll be a start,” Lillian said. “If Nigel wants to wage a campaign in the subconscious, we’ll give people armour.”

She looked back at the stage, at the speakers hanging like bats.

In the background, the four‑note motif played on, unaware that anyone had begun to hear it as anything other than harmless.

“Besides,” she added, “I’ve never been able to resist sabotaging a bad arrangement.”

Sarah grinned. Miles managed a small, tired smile in return.

Outside, beyond the warehouse doors, Manchester waited: damp, grey, full of buses and kebab shops and real, uncurated noise. It would, Lillian thought, be a good place to start breaking a spell.

## Chapter 5: The Mancunian Candidate

The poster on the music academy’s noticeboard read:

**CAN YOU HEAR IT?**

Underneath, in Lillian’s neat, assertive paw‑writing:

*A community workshop on music, mood, and how not to let jingles live rent‑free in your head.*

And, much smaller at the bottom in Sarah’s untidy scrawl: *Also there will be biscuits.*

“Bribery,” Lillian had said when she saw that addition.

“Engagement strategy,” Sarah had replied.

Now, on a damp Thursday evening, the academy’s main rehearsal room hummed with people. Students with instrument cases, local parents in work clothes, a couple of Miles’s colleagues from the Loughton store, a smattering of curious townies who had seen the social media post. Humans and anthros, fur and scales, feathers and hair, all shedding umbrellas and dripping onto the scuffed wooden floor.

Fold‑out chairs formed a loose semicircle around the upright piano. The band’s gear—drum kit, amps, music stands—sat pushed back slightly, as if waiting their turn. On a table by the door, a tray of biscuits did brisk trade.

“Feels like an intervention,” muttered Milla, the band’s lynx guitarist, ears flicking as she tuned quietly in the corner. “Did you invite us here to tell us we’re flat?”

“You’re in tune, darling,” Lillian said. “Reality isn’t.”

“Cool,” Milla said. “No pressure then.”

Miles stood by the tea urn, sleeves rolled, pouring cups for anyone who wanted one. His flannel shirt was back, blazer abandoned. He looked more at home here than he had in the summit warehouse, though a residual tightness lingered along his shoulders.

“You ready?” Lillian asked him quietly as she passed.

He considered. “I’m as ready as I’m likely to be to publicly pick apart my own brain,” he said. “So yes.”

Sarah perched on the edge of the piano bench, sketchbook open on her knees. On the first page, she had drawn a rough outline of the room with little blank ovals where people sat, ready to be turned into tiny figures later. Above them, an empty space waited for whatever shape the evening would reveal.

Lillian stepped to the front and let the low murmur settle.

“Evening,” she said. Her voice, amplified only by her stripes and the acoustics, carried to the back row. “Thank you all for coming out when you could have stayed home arguing with your kettles.”

Soft laughter.

“Some of you know me as the terrifying badger who makes you play scales,” she went on. “Some of you know me as the terrifying badger who insists you face the violas in school orchestra. Some of you know me because you buy tinned tomatoes from my husband. However you got here, welcome.”

She let the room breathe.

“Tonight is not a concert,” she said. “It’s a lesson. Specifically, a lesson in how sound can be used to move you around without your consent—and how not to let it.”

There was a rustle of interest. One of the teenage drummers in the back sat up straighter.

“We are not,” she added, “going to tell you that all adverts are evil and you must only listen to seventeenth‑century choral music henceforth. That would be hypocritical; I have written my share of jingles, many of them for yoghurt. We *are* going to show you one particular pattern that is currently slithering through your radios and supermarkets, so that the next time you hear it, you recognise it. Once you can hear something, it has less power to sneak up on you.”

She nodded to Sarah. “Our resident visualiser will assist.”

Sarah hopped off the bench and moved to the whiteboard, uncapping a marker.

“First,” Lillian said, “we’re going to play you a tune. Don’t think too hard. Just listen, and afterwards, tell us how it made you feel.”

She sat at the piano and played the four‑note motif, naked and unadorned, a few times. Then she wove it into that familiar, bland chord loop the brand had favoured, exactly as she’d heard it in the stores.

Up, up, down, hold.

Shop smart. Shop bright.

The air in the room seemed to lean in.

When she stopped, a few people blinked as if waking.

“Reactions?” she asked.

“Feels like an advert,” said one of the older parents—a badger gentleman Lillian recognised from Loughton, Mr. Patel of the sourdough opinions.

“Comfortable,” said a human woman from the choir. “Like background telly.”

“Annoying, but in a way where you don’t notice it until it’s gone,” said a teenage fox with a bass guitar.

One of Miles’s colleagues, a rabbit from produce, frowned. “That’s… that’s in the shop,” she said slowly. “Isn’t it? That’s the thing they’ve been playing.”

“It is,” Miles said. He stepped forward, paws loosely clasped. “I should know. I helped put it there.”

Murmurs.

“We’re not here to shame anyone,” Lillian cut in. “Least of all my shepherd. We’re here because this particular thing has started doing more than it says on the tin.”

Sarah turned to the board and drew four simple shapes on a staff: up, up, down, hold. Then she drew them again as a curve, then again as a sort of stepped wave.

“This is the skeleton,” she said. “You’ve heard it on the radio, in shops, probably in a few videos online. They keep changing its clothes—different instruments, tempos, backing—but underneath, it’s always this shape.”

She sketched it again, faster.

“Once you know this,” she said, tapping the drawing, “you start seeing it everywhere.”

“Like when you learn a new word and then it’s suddenly in every article you read,” someone offered.

“Exactly,” Sarah said. “Only this word is writing you back.”

Lillian watched the room’s faces: furred brows furrowing, human mouths tightening, ears angling forward. Recognition flickered in more than a few eyes.

“Now,” she said, “we’re going to teach you to whistle it wrong.”

There was a beat of silence, then laughter.

“If this phrase is a path, we’re going to put potholes in it,” she went on. “Disrupt the pattern just enough that, when your brain goes to follow it automatically, it trips. That’s one way to break a spell: change the words.”

She played the motif again, but this time she altered one interval—flattened the second note, shifted the third. The contour was almost the same, but the expectation failed to land.

It felt, deliciously, like missing a step on purpose.

“Hear that?” she asked.

The drummer in the back raised a paw. “It’s wrong,” he said.

“Why?”

He frowned. “Dunno. Just… is.”

“Because your brain learned the other one,” Lillian said. “Without your permission. What we’re doing now is teaching it an alternative. Sarah?”

Sarah turned to the whiteboard again and drew the altered contour in a different colour.

“This is Version B,” she said. “Your get‑out‑of‑jingle card. If you catch yourself humming the original, you can switch to this. Annoy the script.”

“Isn’t that just replacing one earworm with another?” someone asked.

“Yes,” Lillian said. “But this one belongs to us.”

They spent ten minutes whistling and humming, the room filling with slightly off versions of the phrase. It was chaotic and, to Lillian’s ear, deeply satisfying.

At the back, Miles leaned against the wall, paws in his pockets, watching. When his turn came, he hummed Version B with such determination that Sarah looked up and grinned.

“Next,” Lillian said, “we’re going to talk about silence.”

She played the motif again, then cut it off mid‑phrase, leaving the last note unsaid.

Everyone leaned forward.

“That gap you just felt,” she said, “is your brain begging for completion. Advertisers love that. Brains are completion machines. What happens if we… don’t?”

She let the incompleteness hang, then deliberately moved into a different tune entirely—her grandmother’s bread‑kneading melody, simple and steady and older than any brand.

The room’s shoulders dropped. Someone sighed.

“You can always choose to walk away from a pattern,” she said. “Into another one you trust more.”

She saw a few people nod, slow and thoughtful.

“All this might sound abstract,” Miles said, stepping forward again. “So let me make it personal.”

He took a breath. Lillian watched his ears; they tipped back, then forward. The house speakers in this room were old, cheap, and safely under her control. There was no hidden mix to undercut him.

“A few weeks ago,” he said, “I found myself singing this tune in our kitchen without knowing why. I’d written a joke version of it twenty years ago with the band, back when Lillian and I were students. We never sold it. We binned it. Or thought we did. Somehow, it crawled out of the bin and into FreshMart’s new sonic identity.”

There was a murmur at that, half sympathy, half horror.

“Since they rolled it out,” he went on, “I’ve had moments in stores where I lost track of time. Where I felt like I was walking a route someone else had already decided. At the summit last week, I tried to talk about that onstage. I… couldn’t.”

He flexed his paws, claws catching lightly in his own fur.

“Something about the space, the sound, the way the phrase kept sliding in—it was like fog. My brain went from what I wanted to say to what was safe for the brand, and I only noticed afterwards.”

He looked around the room, meeting eyes.

“I don’t like the idea that a tune can make me skip my own thoughts,” he said simply. “So I’m here to ask you to help me make enough noise that it can’t do that quietly anymore.”

Lillian felt something shift in the room—a collective aligning. This was why she had wanted him to speak here, not in Nigel’s bat‑nest: no scripted soundtrack, no corporate slides. Just a shepherd admitting he didn’t want strings on his paws.

“If you work at FreshMart,” she said, “I am not asking you to quit on the spot. Please don’t; the mortgage company would object. I *am* asking you to notice. When you’re on shift and the music makes you feel floaty, write it down. When customers hum, clock it. When you get home and find yourself whistling in the kitchen, stop and ask, ‘Is this mine?’ If it isn’t, change the tune.”

She tapped Sarah’s altered contour on the board.

“And if you don’t work there,” she added, “do the same when you’re in any space that is very keen for you to buy things. Or vote for things. Or believe things. If the room has a soundtrack, ask who wrote it.”

There was a low murmur of assent.

“We’re going to finish the formal bit now,” she said. “Then we’ll play some proper music and you can eat more biscuits. But before we do, I want you to try something when you go shopping next. When you hear that four‑note motif”—she played it again, briefly—“I want you to imagine it wearing a ridiculous hat. Picture Nigel Wetherby falling over a stack of loo roll every time it plays. Or imagine it in the wrong key. Anything that breaks the spell.”

“Mock the spell,” Sarah said. “Very British of us.”

“We weaponise sarcasm in this family,” Lillian said.

There were smiles now. Good. Sarcasm was a kind of magic too.

She closed the workshop with a short piece that used the motif as raw material but mangled it beyond recognition—modulating, reharmonising, twisting it until it became something new and slightly rude. The band joined in, Milla adding sly guitar commentary, the drummer throwing in a cowbell purely on principle.

By the time they finished, the four‑note phrase felt less like a commandment and more like a joke shared among co‑conspirators.

---

The fallout, as it turned out, was not cinematic.

Nobody stormed Nigel’s office brandishing pitchforks and staff discount cards. Sir Basil did not break down on live television and confess to weaponised muzak. There were no dramatic injunctions.

Instead, there were emails.

Staff at trial stores began sending polite, carefully worded feedback up the chain. *Some colleagues report feeling disoriented when the new audio mix loops for extended periods.* *Customers in Loughton and Piccadilly have commented on ‘losing track of time’ in aisles.* *Suggestion: reduce repetition of core motif; consider more varied playlists.*

Customer surveys, which had previously asked only about cleanliness and value, acquired a new optional box: *How did the in‑store music make you feel today?* The answers were mostly “fine” and “nice,” but enough people wrote “weird” or “floaty” or “like I was being steered” that someone in Compliance raised an eyebrow.

An article appeared in a niche but well‑read trade publication: **ARE WE OVER‑TUNING THE CUSTOMER? THE ETHICS OF SONIC BRANDING.** It quoted “anonymous sources” from regional operations and linked, coyly, to academic studies about subliminal influence and informed consent.

Lillian read it over toast one morning and snorted. “Anonymous sources my striped backside,” she said. “You have very distinctive turns of phrase, love.”

Miles, ears tilting in embarrassment, muttered something about corporate responsibility.

At head office, Nigel fought back, of course. There were memos about “misinterpretations” and “fear‑based resistance to innovation.” He insisted the data still looked good, that any negative feedback could be addressed with “tweaks.”

But Basil, for all his obliviousness, was not entirely a fool. Nor was Legal.

The most aggressive elements of the rollout were quietly shelved. The Home Hub pilot was “paused pending further research.” The motif stayed, but it was diluted, its edges blunted, its placement less surgical. Other, less sticky tracks joined the rotation. In some stores, managers simply turned the volume down a notch when they felt the room get that floaty, far‑away look.

You could still hear the four notes, if you knew how to listen. They just no longer had the field to themselves.

“So we didn’t slay the dragon,” Sarah said one afternoon, trailing Lillian through the Loughton aisles with a sketchbook tucked under her arm. “We just made it share the cave.”

“Dragons are rarely slain with one shout,” Lillian said. “Usually you have to keep nagging.”

She paused by the tinned tomatoes, listening. The motif surfaced faintly under a bland pop song, no longer the spine of the soundscape but a vertebra among many.

Her hackles didn’t rise this time. That felt like progress.

They met Mr. Patel by the bakery. He was humming something under his breath.

“Still at it?” Lillian asked lightly.

He blinked, then chuckled. “Caught myself,” he said. “But I changed it.” He hummed Version B, the deliberately wrong variant they’d taught in the workshop.

Lillian’s stripes brightened. “Very good,” she said. “Ten points to whichever house resists mind control today.”

“I’ve been teaching it to the grandchildren,” he said conspiratorially. “They think it’s a game. Every time the ‘shop bright’ bit comes on, we mess it up. Very annoying for the advert, I imagine.”

“May it be so,” Lillian said.

---

At home, the kettle still sang eight seconds before boiling. The radio still chirped out its chatter. Adverts still tried to sell them insurance, soap, holidays.

Sometimes, the FreshMart motif slipped into the mix. When it did, three pairs of ears in the Edgecombe‑Ferguson household twitched in near unison.

“Hat,” Sarah would say, and they’d all picture Nigel Wetherby in something undignified.

“Version B,” Miles would hum, stubbornly flatting the interval.

Lillian would switch to her grandmother’s tune, overlaying the phrase with something older and truer until it lost its teeth.

The house’s hum had changed, too. It still held echoes of the jingle—it would have been dishonest to pretend otherwise—but now those echoes rubbed up against scales, jazz progressions, punk riffs from Sarah’s latest obsession, the rhythmic thump of Miles’s drums vibrating faintly through the floorboards when he practised.

There were other projects now as well. Sarah had turned her conspiracy wall into an art series: installations that mapped sound motifs as light and shadow, exhibited at a student show under the title **Subconscious Aisles**. People stood in front of them, frowning thoughtfully, and some came away humming under their breath in new patterns.

Lillian built a short module into her academy syllabus: “Music and Manipulation.” Her students, a mix of earnest teens and late‑blooming adults, argued cheerfully about what counted as influence and what counted as coercion. She considered that a sign of health.

Miles, for his part, did what he had always done: kept the beans moving, the lights on, the aisles clear. But he added something new to his role. When store managers grumbled about the music making them feel odd, he listened properly. When head office sent down a new mix, he played it at home first, with Lillian and Sarah and the band, and they poked at it like suspicious badgers around a new hole.

He also, quietly, drew firmer lines.

“If we need music to make people stay in the crisp aisle,” he told Nigel in a meeting one day, “maybe the problem isn’t the crisps.”

Nigel had stared at him for a long second, then smiled a little too sharply. “You’re becoming quite the philosopher, Miles,” he’d said.

“Occupational hazard,” Miles had replied. “Too much time with a badger.”

Afterwards, in the car park, he had leaned against his hybrid and taken several deep breaths, counting one‑two‑three‑four like a drummer keeping himself on beat.

The motif had whispered at the edge of his hearing. He’d hummed Version B back at it until it slunk away.

---

One evening, a few months after the summit, they sat around the kitchen table after band rehearsal, mugs and instrument cases scattered like the aftermath of a small, joyful storm.

Milla lounged sideways in her chair, lynx tail hanging over one arm. Phil, the eternally chaotic ferret bassist, had commandeered the biscuit tin. The new drummer—a stoat who had joined when Miles’s work schedule got too tight—tapped idly on the table edge.

“We should just write our own anthem,” Phil said through a mouthful of digestive. “Drown them out.”

“With what distribution budget?” Lillian asked. “We do not, sadly, own forty‑seven supermarkets and a psychometric database.”

“Yet,” said Sarah.

Milla glanced at Miles. “You all right, shepherd?” she asked. “You went a bit quiet during that last bridge.”

Miles toyed with his mug, claws ticking lightly on the ceramic. “Just thinking,” he said.

“Dangerous hobby,” Lillian said.

He smiled sideways at her. “About choice,” he said. “About how easy it is to think you’re making one when actually you’re just following the arrows on the floor.”

Phil swallowed. “Is this about my solo?”

“No, Phil,” Lillian said. “Your solo is its own crime.”

Laughter loosened the room.

“I spent a long time,” Miles said, “priding myself on designing systems that made people’s lives easier. Shorter queues. Better stock. Fewer empty shelves. That still matters. But I don’t want to be the bloke who also quietly designs the bits that make it harder for them to notice when someone’s nudging.”

He looked at Lillian. “You said once that my job is to keep the aisles honest.”

“I’m very wise,” she said.

“You are,” he said. “So I’m going to try and do that. From the inside. As long as I can. And if that stops being possible…” He shrugged, ears flicking. “We’ll cross that aisle when we come to it.”

Lillian reached across the table and covered his paw with hers. Their claws, black and cream, meshed like the teeth of a well‑made gear.

“You’re not a candidate,” she said softly. “You’re not a weapon. You’re an engineer and a drummer and my favourite person to argue about condiments with. If anyone tries to write a script over that, they’ll have to get through me first.”

“And me,” Sarah said, lifting her sketchbook like a shield.

“And me,” said Milla, brandishing a guitar pick.

“And presumably Phil, once he’s finished those biscuits,” Lillian added.

Phil, crumbs on his whiskers, raised a paw. “Down with fascist muzak,” he said earnestly.

The room dissolved into laughter again. It wasn’t much, against the weight of algorithms and brand strategies and quietly humming speakers in a thousand ceilings. But it was theirs.

Later, when everyone had gone and the house had settled back into its night‑time creaks, Lillian and Miles stood together at the kitchen window, looking out at the dark shape of the garden.

Somewhere faintly, from a neighbour’s television or a late‑night advert on the radio, the four‑note motif drifted in, softened by distance and plaster.

Lillian felt Miles tense beside her, just a fraction. She slid her paw into his, the familiar weight of him anchoring her as much as she anchored him.

Quietly, she hummed Version B.

He joined her on the second repetition, their voices—a badger’s alto, a shepherd’s baritone—twining in deliberate disharmony with the distant jingle.

When they finished, the motif outside kept playing, oblivious. But inside the kitchen, the air belonged to them.

“Do you think it’ll ever stop?” Miles asked. “The… trying to get into people’s bones?”

“No,” Lillian said honestly. “Not while there’s profit in it.”

He huffed a soft, resigned laugh.

“But,” she added, “bones are stubborn things. They remember dances and marches and weddings and funerals. They remember who taught them which tunes mattered. We can’t stop people writing new spells. We can make sure our people know how to read them—and how to write their own.”

He squeezed her paw. “Sounds like a curriculum,” he said.

“Everything is a curriculum if you’re nosy enough,” she replied.

They stood there a while longer, listening. The kettle, on the hob, gave a little anticipatory murmur.

“Tea?” Miles asked.

“Always,” Lillian said.

He moved away to set the kettle properly to boil. Eight seconds before it did, it began to sing.

Lillian smiled, hearing in its little rising whistle not an instruction, not a script, but a familiar household voice saying the same thing it had said every morning of their shared life.

She tilted her striped muzzle, listened, and let herself hum back—not the brand’s four notes, not even Version B, but something older and entirely theirs.

The Mancunian candidate, as Nigel might once have conceived of Miles, never quite materialised. Instead, Manchester—and Loughton, and every other place their music reached—ended up with something far more awkward for the brands to handle.

People who listened on purpose.
