# Goldstar
## A Lillianverse Mystery Novella

*Influences: Kathy Reichs (forensic realism), Michael Connelly (police procedural grit), Jeffery Deaver (clockwork plotting), Tana French/Denise Mina (psychological tension, UK social edge)*

---

## Part I: The Involuntary Witness

### Chapter 1: Opening Note

The last chord of "Blackbird" still hung in the smoky air of The Lamb and Flag when Lillian Edgecombe noticed the silence. Not the usual post-song pause—this was different. Heavier. The kind of quiet that settles over a room when bad news arrives uninvited.

She lowered her guitar, her badger ears twitching at the sudden shift in atmosphere. The pub's usual Friday night crowd—a mix of humans and anthros from Camden's music scene—had their eyes fixed on the television mounted above the bar. The volume was low, but the red banner scrolling across the bottom of the BBC News broadcast was clear enough: *ANTHRO MUSICIAN FOUND DEAD IN MANCHESTER*.

"Turn it up, Dave," called someone from the back.

The barman reached for the remote, and the newsreader's voice filled the room: "...twenty-eight-year-old Marcus Thornfield, a well-known folk singer in Manchester's anthro community, was discovered this morning in his Chorlton flat. Police are treating the death as suspicious..."

Lillian's stomach dropped. She knew Marcus—not well, but enough. They'd shared a stage at the Northern Folk Festival two summers ago. A red fox with a voice like honey and whiskey, passionate about preserving traditional anthro songs that predated the Integration.

The camera cut to footage of Marcus performing, his russet fur gleaming under stage lights, his voice carrying an old ballad about the first anthros who'd emerged from the deep forests. Then back to the newsroom, where the presenter's expression carried that particular gravity reserved for stories involving anthros.

"...no arrests have been made at this time. This marks the third suspicious death of an anthro community figure in the North West this month..."

Third? Lillian hadn't heard about the others. The anthro community's informal networks usually carried news faster than the BBC, but lately, those networks had been... quieter. More careful.

She packed her guitar with mechanical precision, muscle memory taking over while her mind raced. Around her, conversations resumed in hushed tones. She caught fragments—speculation, worry, the kind of coded language anthros used when they felt exposed.

The walk home through Camden's winding streets gave her time to process. Marcus had been young, talented, and completely dedicated to preserving the old songs. Who would want to hurt someone like that? And why were anthro community figures suddenly dying?

Her phone buzzed with messages from other musicians, all asking the same questions she was asking herself. The anthro music community was small enough that everyone knew everyone, at least by reputation. Marcus's death would ripple through it like a stone thrown into still water.

By the time she reached her terraced house in Kentish Town, Lillian had made a decision she didn't fully understand yet. Whatever was happening to people like Marcus, she needed to know about it. The alternative—pretending it wasn't happening, hoping it would pass—felt like a betrayal of everything she'd spent her career fighting for.

Miles was waiting up for her, concern written across his human features. "Saw the news," he said, pulling her into a hug. "You knew him?"

"A little." She breathed in his familiar scent, letting it ground her. "Miles, I think something's happening. Something bigger than just one death."

"What do you mean?"

"The news said three deaths. Three anthro community figures in one month." She pulled back to look at him. "That's not coincidence."

Miles's expression darkened. He'd been part of her life long enough to understand the undercurrents of anthro society, the ways prejudice could manifest in violence. "What are you thinking?"

"I'm thinking," Lillian said slowly, "that someone is targeting people like me."

### Chapter 2: Police Knock

The knock came at half past seven on Saturday morning, sharp and official. Lillian was in the kitchen, making coffee and trying to shake off the previous night's unease, when the sound cut through the quiet house like a blade.

Miles appeared in the doorway, hair still mussed from sleep, wearing the expression he got when his protective instincts kicked in. "I'll get it."

"No." Lillian set down her mug. "It's probably for me."

She knew that knock. Every anthro did—the particular rhythm of authority, measured and insistent. Through the frosted glass of their front door, she could make out two figures. Police.

Detective Inspector Sarah Chen was younger than Lillian had expected, maybe mid-thirties, with sharp eyes and the kind of tired posture that spoke of too many early mornings and late nights. Her partner, Detective Sergeant James Morrison, was older, broader, with the weathered look of someone who'd seen enough to be permanently skeptical of everything.

"Ms. Edgecombe?" Chen held up her warrant card. "I'm DI Chen, this is DS Morrison. We're investigating the death of Marcus Thornfield. We understand you knew him?"

"Not well," Lillian said, stepping aside to let them in. "We performed together once or twice. Why?"

"We found your contact details in his phone," Morrison said, pulling out a notebook. His voice carried a slight Scottish accent, roughened by years of cigarettes. "Recent calls, actually. Three in the past month."

Lillian's mind raced. Three calls? She remembered one—Marcus asking about a traditional song, something about the melody. But three?

"Could we sit down?" Chen asked. "We have some questions about Mr. Thornfield's... cultural activities."

They settled in the living room, where morning light filtered through the windows, illuminating the family photos on the mantelpiece—Lillian and Miles on their wedding day, Sarah's art school graduation, the three of them on holiday in Scotland. Normal life, documented and displayed.

"Cultural activities?" Lillian repeated.

"Mr. Thornfield was involved in preserving anthro folk traditions," Chen said. "Songs, stories, that sort of thing. We understand you do similar work?"

"I teach music. I perform. Sometimes I research historical songs." Lillian kept her voice level. "It's not exactly underground activity."

Morrison looked up from his notebook. "No one's suggesting it is, love. But we need to understand the community Mr. Thornfield was part of. Who he knew, who might have had issues with his work."

"Issues?" The word came out sharper than Lillian intended.

"Ms. Edgecombe," Chen leaned forward slightly, "Marcus Thornfield wasn't just killed. He was... displayed. Arranged. Someone wanted to make a statement."

The coffee in Lillian's stomach turned to acid. "What kind of statement?"

The detectives exchanged a look. Morrison closed his notebook with a soft snap.

"We can't share details of an ongoing investigation," Chen said. "But we're trying to understand if there's a pattern. If other people in your community might be at risk."

"Other people like me, you mean."

"We're just being thorough."

Miles appeared in the doorway, carrying a tray with coffee and biscuits—his default response to any crisis. "Officers? Can I get you anything?"

"Thank you, Mr...?"

"Ferguson. Miles Ferguson. Lillian's husband." He set the tray down with practiced ease, but Lillian caught the slight tremor in his hands.

Chen accepted a cup of coffee, wrapping her hands around it as if seeking warmth. "Mr. Ferguson, has your wife received any unusual communications recently? Phone calls, emails, letters?"

"No," Miles said, but his eyes found Lillian's. "Nothing like that. Why would you ask that?"

"We're exploring all possibilities," Morrison said, his Scottish burr more pronounced now. "Ms. Edgecombe, can you think of anyone who might have objected to Mr. Thornfield's work? Anyone in the community who might have had disagreements with him?"

Lillian almost laughed. "Marcus was one of the gentlest people I knew. He spent his time recording elderly anthros singing lullabies their grandmothers taught them. Who could object to that?"

"You'd be surprised," Chen said quietly, "what people can find threatening."

The words hung in the air like smoke. Lillian understood the implication—that preserving anthro culture could be seen as a political act, that visibility could be dangerous.

"There's something else," Chen continued. "We found evidence at the scene that suggests the killer has knowledge of anthro cultural practices. Detailed knowledge."

"What kind of evidence?"

"I can't be specific, but... whoever did this understood traditional funeral rites. They arranged the scene according to old customs."

Lillian felt the blood drain from her face. "That's... that's not possible. Those practices are barely documented. You'd have to be a serious scholar to know them in detail."

"Or have access to someone who was," Morrison said, his pen poised over his notebook again.

The implication was clear. The killer was either from within the anthro community or had inside knowledge. Either possibility was terrifying.

"We'd like you to look at some photographs," Chen said, reaching into her briefcase. "See if the arrangements mean anything specific to you."

"I don't think—"

"Please. It could help us catch whoever did this."

The photographs were clinical, black and white, but they still made Lillian's hands shake. Marcus lay in his flat, positioned with careful precision—head pointing east, hands folded, personal items arranged in a spiral pattern around his body. It was a perfect recreation of pre-Integration funeral rites, the kind practiced in secret during the early days when anthros had to hide their customs from human authorities.

"This is... this is exactly right," Lillian whispered. "Every detail. Whoever did this knew our traditions better than most anthros do."

"Could you make a list?" Chen asked. "People who might have this level of knowledge?"

"It would be a very short list. Maybe a dozen scholars in the entire country. And most of them are..." She paused, realizing what she was about to say.

"Most of them are what?"

"Elderly. The people who remember these practices firsthand are in their seventies and eighties. The younger scholars like me, we know about them from books and interviews, but this level of detail..."

She stared at the photographs again, noting the precise angles, the careful spacing. This wasn't academic knowledge—this was lived knowledge, passed down through generations.

"There's something else," Chen said, producing another photograph. "We found this on the body."

It was a close-up of Marcus's forehead, where someone had placed a small golden sticker—a five-pointed star, the kind teachers used to reward good work.

"That's not part of any tradition I know," Lillian said, her voice barely above a whisper.

"We think it's a signature," Morrison said. "The killer's calling card."

As the detectives prepared to leave, Chen handed Lillian a business card. "If you think of anything—anything at all—please call. And Ms. Edgecombe? Be careful. Until we know what we're dealing with, it might be wise to... keep a low profile."

After they left, Lillian sat in her living room, staring at the business card. Keep a low profile. It was advice she'd been given her entire life, the default setting for anthros who wanted to avoid trouble.

Marcus had kept a low profile too, and it hadn't saved him.

### Chapter 3: First Look

The crime scene photographs haunted Lillian for the rest of the weekend. She found herself returning to them again and again, studying the careful arrangement of Marcus's body, the precise placement of his personal belongings. The killer hadn't just murdered him—they had transformed his death into a ritual, a performance of cultural knowledge that felt both reverent and deeply wrong.

On Monday morning, her phone rang. DI Chen's voice was crisp, professional. "Ms. Edgecombe? We'd like you to come in. There are some additional details we'd like your opinion on."

The police station in Camden was a study in institutional beige—fluorescent lights, worn carpeting, the smell of instant coffee and stress. Chen led her through a maze of desks to a small conference room where Morrison was waiting with a stack of files and a laptop.

"Thank you for coming," Chen said, closing the door behind them. "We've had some developments."

She opened the laptop, revealing a digital crime scene reconstruction. The flat appeared on screen in three dimensions, every detail captured and catalogued. Lillian could see Marcus's body from multiple angles, the spiral arrangement of his possessions, the careful positioning of furniture.

"We've been analyzing the scene more thoroughly," Morrison said, his Scottish accent softer in the confined space. "The arrangement isn't random. Everything follows a specific pattern."

He clicked through the images, highlighting different elements. "The body faces east, toward the sunrise. The personal items—his guitar, his notebooks, his collection of traditional instruments—they're arranged in a counterclockwise spiral, starting from his head."

"That's the traditional pattern for honoring a cultural keeper," Lillian said quietly. "Someone who preserves songs, stories, traditions. It's... it's how we would have honored him if he'd died naturally."

"But there's more," Chen said. She pulled up another image—a close-up of Marcus's hands. "His fists were closed, but there was something inside them."

The photograph showed Marcus's palms, opened post-mortem. In each hand was a small object—in the left, a piece of sheet music, handwritten in the old notation style. In the right, a small wooden flute, carved in the traditional manner.

"These weren't his," Lillian said immediately. "Marcus played guitar and sang. He didn't play flute, and he couldn't read the old notation. He learned everything by ear."

"So the killer brought these items specifically for the ritual," Morrison said.

"It's not a ritual," Lillian corrected, her voice sharp. "It's a mockery. Whoever did this knows our customs well enough to replicate them, but they're using that knowledge to... to make a point."

"What kind of point?"

Lillian stared at the screen, her mind working through the implications. "In traditional funeral rites, we place objects in the deceased's hands that represent their life's work, their contribution to the community. But these objects... they're generic. Symbolic rather than personal."

She paused, a terrible thought forming. "It's like the killer is saying that all anthro cultural work is the same. Interchangeable. That Marcus wasn't an individual with his own talents and passions—he was just another 'cultural keeper' to be... processed."

The room fell silent. Chen and Morrison exchanged glances.

"There's something else," Chen said finally. "The gold star. We've had it analyzed."

She produced an evidence bag containing the small sticker. Under the harsh fluorescent lights, it looked even more incongruous—a cheerful, childish reward placed on a murder victim's forehead.

"It's a standard teacher's reward sticker," Morrison said. "The kind you can buy in any stationery shop. But there's something interesting about the placement."

He pulled up another photograph, this one showing Marcus's forehead in detail. The star was positioned precisely in the center, perfectly aligned.

"The killer used tweezers," Chen said. "There are tiny marks around the edges where they gripped it. They wanted it placed exactly right."

"Like grading a paper," Lillian murmured.

"What?"

"Teachers use these stars to grade work. One star for poor, five stars for excellent." She looked up at the detectives. "How many points does this star have?"

Morrison counted. "Five."

"So Marcus got full marks," Lillian said, her voice hollow. "The killer thinks he did a good job preserving anthro culture. Good enough to deserve... this."

The implications were staggering. If the killer was grading victims based on their cultural contributions, then other anthros—scholars, musicians, artists—were all potential targets. And the more successful they were, the higher their "grade" would be.

"We need to warn people," Lillian said. "The community needs to know what's happening."

"We can't release details of an ongoing investigation," Chen said. "But we can increase patrols in areas with high anthro populations. Issue general safety warnings."

"That won't be enough. You don't understand—the anthro community is already scared. We've been targets before. If people think there's a serial killer specifically hunting cultural figures..."

"What would happen?" Morrison asked.

Lillian thought about the musicians she knew, the scholars and artists who formed the backbone of anthro cultural preservation. Many of them were already living on the margins, their work undervalued and underfunded. If they went into hiding, if they stopped performing and teaching and creating, an entire cultural tradition could disappear.

"They'll go underground," she said. "Stop performing publicly, stop teaching, stop preserving the traditions that make us who we are. The killer won't need to murder anyone else—they'll have achieved their goal just by creating fear."

Chen leaned back in her chair, processing this. "So what do you suggest?"

"Let me help," Lillian said. "I know this community. I understand the cultural context. I can help you identify potential targets, understand the killer's methodology."

"Ms. Edgecombe, this is a police investigation—"

"And you need someone who understands what you're investigating," Lillian interrupted. "You've already admitted you don't know enough about anthro culture to interpret the crime scene properly. How many more people are going to die while you're learning?"

Morrison looked at Chen, who was staring at the crime scene photos with a troubled expression.

"There would be conditions," Chen said finally. "You'd be a consultant only. No direct involvement in the investigation. And if we think you're in danger, we pull you out immediately."

"Agreed."

"And your family needs to be protected. If the killer sees you as helping us..."

Lillian's stomach clenched. She hadn't thought about that. By involving herself in the investigation, she might be putting Miles and Sarah at risk.

The alternative—letting the killer continue to hunt people like Marcus, like her—was worse.

"What do you need me to do?"

### Chapter 4: Threat Spreads

The news broke on Tuesday morning, splashed across the front page of the *Daily Mail* with the subtlety of a sledgehammer: "ANTHRO DEATH CULT: Police Investigate Ritual Killings in Manchester."

Lillian stared at the headline over her morning coffee, her blood running cold. The article was a masterpiece of inflammatory journalism, describing Marcus's death in lurid detail while speculating about "ancient anthro practices" and "primitive rituals" that might be connected to the killing.

"Bloody hell," Miles muttered, reading over her shoulder. "They've made it sound like anthros are killing each other in some kind of religious ceremony."

The article quoted "anonymous police sources" describing the crime scene arrangement, the traditional funeral positioning, the objects placed in Marcus's hands. However, the context was completely wrong—instead of recognizing these as elements imposed by the killer, the paper presented them as evidence of anthro involvement in the murder.

*Police sources confirm that the victim was found arranged according to ancient anthro burial customs, with traditional objects placed in his hands and his body positioned to face the rising sun. The ritualistic nature of the killing has led investigators to explore connections to anthro religious practices and possible cult activity within the community.*

"This is a disaster," Lillian said, her hands shaking as she set down the paper. "They're going to blame us for our own murders."

Her phone was already ringing. The first call was from her department head at the university, Dr. Patricia Whitmore, her voice tight with concern.

"Lillian, I've seen the news. Are you all right? Do you need to take some time off?"

"I'm fine, Patricia. But this story—it's completely wrong. The killer isn't anthro. The ritual elements were imposed by someone else."

"How can you be sure?"

Lillian paused, realizing she couldn't explain without revealing her involvement in the investigation. "I just... I know our community. This isn't how we operate."

"Well, regardless, I think it might be wise to cancel your public lectures for a while. Until things settle down."

After Patricia hung up, the calls kept coming. Students, colleagues, friends from the music community—all asking the same questions, all carrying the same undercurrent of fear. By noon, three universities had canceled anthro cultural events scheduled for the coming weeks. Two music venues had quietly removed anthro performers from their lineups.

The killer hadn't needed to murder anyone else. The media was doing their work for them.

Miles found her in the garden that afternoon, sitting on the bench they'd built together the previous summer, staring at her phone as message after message arrived.

"How bad is it?" he asked, settling beside her.

"The Folklore Society has suspended all public meetings indefinitely. The Traditional Music Festival has been canceled. Three of my graduate students have asked to switch to different research topics." She looked up at him, her eyes bright with unshed tears. "Twenty-four hours, Miles. It took twenty-four hours for them to destroy everything we've built."

"It's not destroyed. It's just... paused."

"Is it? When this is over, when they catch the killer, do you think people will come back? Do you think the venues will book anthro performers again? Do you think the universities will support anthro cultural studies?"

Miles didn't answer, because they both knew the truth. Fear was easier to spread than trust was to rebuild.

"There's something else," Lillian said, showing him her phone. "I've been getting messages."

The texts had started arriving around lunchtime, all from unknown numbers:

*Saw the news. Maybe it's time you people learned to keep quiet.*

*Traditional practices, eh? How traditional is murder?*

*Go back to the forests where you belong.*

"Jesus," Miles breathed. "Have you told the police?"

"What's the point? They're not direct threats, just... opinions. And there are dozens of them."

She scrolled through the messages, each one a small cut, a reminder that no matter how integrated anthros became, they were always one news story away from being outsiders again.

Her phone rang. Chen's number.

"Ms. Edgecombe? We need to talk. Can you come in?"

"Is it about the media coverage?"

"Among other things. There's been a development."

An hour later, Lillian sat in the same conference room, but the atmosphere was different. Tenser. Chen looked like she hadn't slept, and Morrison's usual calm demeanor had been replaced by barely controlled frustration.

"The leak came from inside the department," Chen said without preamble. "Someone with access to the crime scene photos and forensic reports."

"Why would someone do that?"

"Money, most likely. The *Mail* pays well for exclusive information." Chen's voice was bitter. "Or maybe they genuinely believe the anthro community is involved."

Morrison leaned forward. "The problem is, the media attention has made our job ten times harder. We've got protesters outside anthro community centers, vigilante groups 'investigating' anthro cultural figures, and a killer who's probably enjoying every minute of it."

"There's more," Chen said. "We've identified two more potential victims. Both found dead in the past week, both arranged in similar ritualistic patterns."

Lillian's stomach dropped. "Who?"

"Dr. Elena Vasquez, a historian at Liverpool University. Specialized in pre-Integration anthro social structures. And Professor James Riverwind, an oral history specialist from Birmingham."

The names hit Lillian like physical blows. She knew both of them—Elena from academic conferences, James from collaborative research projects. Good people, dedicated scholars, now dead because someone had decided their work made them targets.

"Same killer?" she asked, though she already knew the answer.

"Same methodology, same attention to cultural detail. But there are differences." Chen pulled up new crime scene photos on her laptop. "Dr. Vasquez had four gold stars on her forehead. Professor Riverwind had six."

"The killer is grading them," Lillian said, her voice hollow. "Rating their contributions to anthro culture."

"It gets worse," Morrison said. "We found evidence that the killer has been researching potential victims for months. Detailed files on dozens of anthro cultural figures—their work, their schedules, their personal lives."

"Am I on the list?"

The detectives exchanged glances. "Yes," Chen said finally. "You are."

The room seemed to tilt around Lillian. She gripped the edge of the table, fighting off a wave of nausea.

"How detailed?"

"Very. The killer knows where you work, where you live, your research interests, your performance schedule. They've been watching you, studying you."

"For how long?"

"At least six months. Maybe longer."

Lillian thought about the past six months—the lectures she'd given, the concerts she'd performed, the research trips she'd taken. Had the killer been there, in the audience, taking notes? Had they followed her home, learned her routines, planned her death while she went about her normal life?

"We're going to put you under protection," Chen said. "Round-the-clock surveillance, safe house if necessary."

"No."

"Ms. Edgecombe—"

"No. If I disappear, if I stop working, the killer wins. They'll have silenced another voice, removed another cultural keeper from the community."

"They'll have kept you alive," Morrison said bluntly.

"For how long? Until they kill everyone else on their list? Until there's no anthro culture left to preserve?" Lillian stood up, pacing to the window. "You don't understand. This isn't just about individual murders. It's about destroying an entire way of life."

She turned back to face them. "The killer thinks they're preserving anthro culture by turning us into museum pieces. But culture isn't preserved in death—it's preserved in living, breathing, creating people. Every person they kill, every person they scare into silence, is a piece of our heritage lost forever."

"So what do you propose?" Chen asked.

"We catch them. We use their obsession with me against them. We make them come to us."

"Absolutely not," Morrison said. "We're not using you as bait."

"You're not using me as anything. I'm volunteering."

The room fell silent. Outside, London continued its daily rhythm, unaware that in a small conference room in Camden, a decision was being made that would determine whether an entire culture lived or died.

"It's too dangerous," Chen said finally.

"Everything is dangerous now," Lillian replied. "The question is whether we're going to let fear win."

## Part II: The Reluctant Detective

### Chapter 5: Deeper In

The safe house was a nondescript terraced house in Hampstead, the kind of place that blended invisibly into London's residential landscape. Lillian sat in the living room, surrounded by police protection officers and feeling like a prisoner in her own life.

It had been three days since the meeting at the police station, three days of arguments and negotiations and careful planning. In the end, they'd reached a compromise—Lillian would continue her normal activities under heavy surveillance, acting as both consultant and potential bait for the killer.

Miles paced by the window, his anxiety radiating through the room like heat. "This is insane," he said for the dozenth time. "You're putting yourself in the path of a serial killer."

"I'm already in his path," Lillian replied. "At least this way, we have some control over the situation."

Sarah sat curled in an armchair, her art supplies scattered around her as she worked on a sketch. She'd been quieter than usual since learning about the threat, processing the information in her own way.

"Mum," she said without looking up from her drawing, "are you scared?"

The question hung in the air. Lillian considered lying, offering reassurance, but Sarah deserved honesty.

"Yes," she said. "I'm terrified. But I'm more scared of what happens if we don't stop him."

Chen arrived at noon with Morrison and a stack of files. They'd set up a temporary command center in the dining room, complete with computers, phones, and crime scene photographs that made the house feel less like a home and more like a war room.

"We've made some progress," Chen said, spreading photos across the table. "The killer left more evidence at the Birmingham scene."

The photographs showed Professor Riverwind's office, transformed into another ritualistic display. The elderly otter had been positioned behind his desk, surrounded by his life's work—recordings of traditional anthro stories, transcripts of interviews with community elders, artifacts from pre-Integration settlements.

"Six stars," Morrison said, pointing to the victim's forehead. "The highest rating yet."

"James was one of the most respected oral historians in the country," Lillian said, studying the images. "If the killer is grading based on cultural contribution, six stars makes sense."

"There's something else," Chen said. She produced a clear evidence bag containing a handwritten note. "This was left on the victim's desk."

Lillian read the note aloud: "*Professor Riverwind dedicated his life to preserving the oral traditions of the anthro community. His recordings and transcripts represent irreplaceable cultural knowledge. This display honors his contributions and ensures that his work will be remembered. He has earned his place among the treasures of his people.*"

"It's signed with a gold star," Morrison added.

The note was written in neat, academic handwriting—the kind Lillian recognized from years of grading student papers. Precise, controlled, educated.

"The killer thinks they're doing us a favor," she said, her voice barely above a whisper.

"What do you mean?"

"They genuinely believe they're honoring these people. Preserving their contributions for posterity." She looked up at the detectives. "This isn't hatred. It's... it's twisted love."

The realization was somehow more disturbing than simple malice would have been. A killer motivated by hatred could be understood, predicted, caught. But someone who murdered out of misguided reverence, who saw death as a form of cultural preservation—that was something else entirely.

"We've been analyzing the handwriting," Chen said. "It's consistent across all three crime scenes. Same pen, same paper, same careful formation of letters."

"Any matches in your databases?"

"Nothing yet. But we're working on it."

Morrison pulled out another file. "We've also been looking at the cultural knowledge displayed at each scene. The level of detail, the specific traditions referenced."

He spread out a chart showing the ritualistic elements found at each crime scene—the positioning of bodies, the arrangement of objects, the specific cultural practices referenced.

"Whoever's doing this has access to information that's not widely available," he said. "Some of these funeral rites haven't been practiced in decades. They're documented in academic papers, archived interviews, museum collections."

"So we're looking for someone with academic access," Lillian said.

"Or someone who's been studying anthro culture for years. Building up knowledge, preparing for this."

The thought sent a chill through Lillian. The killer hadn't just decided to target anthro cultural figures on a whim—they'd been planning this for years, studying their victims, learning their traditions, preparing to turn murder into a twisted form of cultural preservation.

"There's something else," Chen said. "We've been monitoring online forums, social media groups, anywhere the killer might be communicating or researching."

She opened her laptop, showing a series of forum posts and academic discussion groups. "We found several accounts that have been asking detailed questions about anthro funeral rites, traditional practices, cultural preservation methods."

The posts were scattered across different platforms, different usernames, but they all showed the same pattern—someone systematically gathering information about anthro culture, particularly death rituals and memorial practices.

"Can you trace them?"

"We're trying. Most use VPNs, anonymous email addresses. But there's one account that's been more active recently." Chen clicked to a university forum dedicated to anthropological research. "Someone calling themselves 'CulturalKeeper' has been posting extensively about the importance of preserving anthro traditions."

Lillian read through the posts, her unease growing with each message. The language was academic, respectful, but there was an underlying obsession that made her skin crawl.

*The anthro community is losing its cultural identity through integration with human society. Traditional practices are being forgotten, diluted, commercialized. We need to find ways to preserve the pure essence of anthro culture before it's lost forever.*

*Death is the ultimate form of preservation. In death, cultural figures become permanent, unchanging, perfect representations of their contributions. They can never be corrupted, never forget their traditions, never compromise their heritage.*

*The most valuable members of the anthro community are those who dedicate their lives to cultural preservation. They deserve to be honored, remembered, made permanent.*

"Jesus," Lillian breathed. "This is him, isn't it?"

"We think so. The posts started about eight months ago, right around the time we think the killer began planning the murders."

Morrison leaned forward. "The interesting thing is, some of the posts reference specific academic papers, research projects, conference presentations. The killer has been following anthro cultural studies very closely."

"Following my work, you mean."

"Among others. But yes, several posts reference your research specifically."

Lillian felt sick. The killer had been watching her work, studying her contributions to anthro cultural preservation, deciding whether she deserved to be "honored" with death.

"We need to draw him out," she said.

"How?"

"I give a public lecture. Something high-profile, well-publicized. Make myself an irresistible target."

"Absolutely not," Miles said from across the room. "I won't let you paint a target on your back."

"The target's already there," Lillian replied. "At least this way, we choose the time and place."

Chen and Morrison exchanged glances. "It could work," Chen said slowly. "If we control the environment, have enough backup..."

"No," Miles said firmly. "I won't be part of this."

The room fell silent. Lillian looked at her husband, seeing the fear and love and desperation in his eyes. She understood his position—he wanted to protect her, to keep her safe, to pretend that hiding would make the danger go away.

She also knew it wouldn't work.

"Miles," she said gently, "I'm going to do this with or without your support. I'd rather do it with."

"And if something goes wrong? If the killer gets to you before the police can stop him?"

"Then at least I'll have tried to stop him from killing anyone else."

Miles stared at her for a long moment, then turned and walked out of the room. Lillian heard the front door slam, followed by the sound of his car starting.

"He'll come around," Sarah said quietly from her corner. "He's just scared."

"We're all scared," Lillian replied.

Chen cleared her throat. "If we're going to do this, we need to plan it carefully. Choose the right venue, the right topic, make sure we have enough security."

"I have an idea," Lillian said. "There's a conference next month—the International Symposium on Cultural Preservation. It's being held at the Royal Festival Hall."

"That's a big venue. Hard to secure."

"But it's also the perfect bait. The killer won't be able to resist a conference specifically about cultural preservation, especially if I'm giving the keynote address."

Morrison was taking notes. "What would you talk about?"

Lillian smiled grimly. "The importance of keeping cultural traditions alive. The danger of turning culture into museum pieces. The difference between preservation and death."

"You want to taunt him."

"I want to make him angry enough to make a mistake."

Chen nodded slowly. "It could work. But we'll need weeks to prepare, coordinate with venue security, plan backup strategies."

"Then we'd better get started."

As the detectives began making phone calls and planning logistics, Lillian sat back in her chair, staring at the crime scene photographs spread across the table. Somewhere in London, the killer was planning their next move, choosing their next victim, preparing another twisted tribute to anthro culture.

She was going to stop them, even if it killed her.

### Chapter 6: The Bait

The venue arrived in another letter two days later: The Royal Festival Hall, South Bank. A public lecture on "Musical Archaeology and Cultural Preservation" scheduled for the following Thursday evening. Tickets were already on sale through the venue's website, advertised as part of their "Voices of Heritage" series.

Lillian stared at the letter, her hands trembling slightly. The killer had anticipated her plan, had somehow known she would try to draw them out with a public appearance. They were always one step ahead, always in control.

"How did they know?" she asked Chen, who was studying the letter with a magnifying glass.

"Either they're monitoring our communications, or they're closer to the investigation than we thought."

The possibility that the killer had inside information was terrifying. It meant nowhere was safe, no plan was secure. They were playing a game where the rules kept changing, and their opponent knew their moves before they made them.

"We could cancel," Morrison suggested. "Find another approach."

"No." Lillian's voice was firm. "This is what we wanted—a controlled environment, a specific time and place. The fact that they chose it instead of us doesn't change the fundamentals."

Chen nodded. "We'll have the entire venue under surveillance. Armed officers in the audience, snipers on the surrounding buildings, backup teams at every exit."

"And if something goes wrong?"

"Then we'll be ready."

The week leading up to the lecture passed in a blur of security briefings and rehearsals. Lillian practiced her presentation while police officers ran through evacuation procedures and contingency plans. The Royal Festival Hall was mapped and measured, every entrance and exit catalogued, every potential hiding spot identified.

Miles had returned the day after their argument, apologetic but still worried. He threw himself into the security preparations with the intensity of someone trying to control an uncontrollable situation.

"I've been thinking," he said on Wednesday evening as they sat in the safe house living room. "Maybe I should be there. In the audience."

"Absolutely not."

"If something happens, if the killer gets past the police—"

"Then you being there won't help. It'll just give me something else to worry about."

Sarah looked up from her sketchbook. "I want to come too."

"No," both parents said simultaneously.

"But—"

"No," Lillian repeated. "This is dangerous enough without putting you at risk."

"I'm already at risk," Sarah said quietly. "We all are. At least if I'm there, we're together."

The words hung in the air like a challenge. Sarah was right—the killer's obsession with Lillian put the entire family in danger. Hiding wouldn't change that.

"The venue will be secure," Chen said. "If your family wants to attend, we can arrange special seating, extra protection."

Lillian looked at Miles and Sarah, seeing the determination in their faces. They were going to be part of this whether she liked it or not.

"All right," she said finally. "But you follow every security instruction, no matter how paranoid it seems."

Thursday evening arrived gray and drizzling, typical London weather that matched Lillian's mood. She stood in the green room behind the Royal Festival Hall's main auditorium, listening to the sound of the audience settling into their seats.

"How many?" she asked Chen, who was monitoring the security feeds on a tablet.

"About eight hundred. Mix of academics, music enthusiasts, general public. We've identified and cleared everyone who bought tickets in advance."

"And the ones who bought tickets at the door?"

"Extra screening, metal detectors, bag searches. If the killer's in there, we'll spot them."

Morrison appeared in the doorway. "Five minutes, Ms. Edgecombe. You ready?"

Lillian nodded, though she felt anything but ready. Her presentation was loaded on the laptop, her notes were organized, her wireless microphone was clipped to her jacket. Everything was as prepared as it could be.

"Remember," Chen said, "stick to the planned presentation. Don't improvise, don't go off-script. We need to control as many variables as possible."

"And if I see him? If I recognize someone in the audience?"

"You keep talking. We'll handle it."

The walk from the green room to the stage felt like a mile. Lillian could hear the murmur of conversation from the auditorium, the rustle of programs, the occasional cough. Normal sounds of a normal academic lecture, except for the knowledge that somewhere in that crowd might be someone who wanted to kill her.

The stage lights were bright, making it difficult to see individual faces in the audience. Lillian found Miles and Sarah in the third row, surrounded by plainclothes officers who looked like academics but moved like soldiers.

"Good evening," she began, her voice carrying clearly through the auditorium's excellent acoustics. "Thank you for coming to what I hope will be an illuminating discussion about the preservation of cultural heritage."

She clicked to her first slide—a photograph of Marcus Thornfield performing at the Northern Folk Festival. The image drew a murmur from the audience; Marcus's death had been widely reported, and many people recognized him.

"This is Marcus Thornfield," Lillian continued. "A talented musician who dedicated his life to preserving traditional anthro songs. He was murdered three weeks ago, along with several other cultural figures, by someone who claims to be honoring their contributions to our community."

The audience was completely silent now, hanging on every word.

"The killer believes that death is a form of preservation—that by murdering cultural figures, they're somehow making their contributions permanent, unchanging, perfect." Lillian clicked to the next slide, showing a traditional anthro funeral arrangement. "They arrange their victims according to ancient funeral rites, as if death were a form of cultural celebration."

She paused, scanning the audience. In the darkness beyond the stage lights, she could make out shapes, silhouettes, but no individual faces.

"But culture isn't preserved in death," she continued. "Culture lives in the breath of a singer, the hands of a craftsperson, the memory of a storyteller. When you kill the people who carry culture, you don't preserve it—you destroy it."

A movement in the balcony caught her eye. Someone standing up, moving toward the aisle. Security would be tracking them, but Lillian felt her heart rate spike.

"The killer thinks they understand anthro culture, but they've missed the most fundamental truth—that culture is about life, not death. About growth and change and adaptation, not static preservation."

The figure in the balcony was moving down the stairs now, heading toward the main floor. Lillian could see security officers shifting position, but they weren't moving to intercept. Probably just someone leaving early.

"Traditional funeral rites aren't about preserving the dead," Lillian said, clicking to another slide. "They're about helping the living process grief, maintain connections, continue the cultural traditions that the deceased helped to preserve."

The figure had reached the main floor and was walking down the center aisle. In the stage lights, Lillian couldn't make out details, but something about their movement seemed purposeful, determined.

"When someone murders a cultural figure and arranges them according to funeral rites, they're not honoring tradition—they're perverting it. They're taking something sacred and turning it into something obscene."

The figure was halfway down the aisle now, and Lillian could see that it was a man, middle-aged, wearing a dark suit. He was walking steadily toward the stage, ignoring the people he passed.

"Culture preservation isn't about creating museum pieces," Lillian continued, her voice steady despite her racing heart. "It's about keeping traditions alive, relevant, meaningful to new generations."

The man had reached the front of the auditorium. He stopped at the edge of the stage area, looking up at Lillian with an expression she couldn't read in the bright lights.

"The killer claims to love anthro culture, but love doesn't murder. Love doesn't turn people into displays. Love doesn't silence the voices it claims to honor."

The man reached into his jacket, and Lillian saw security officers moving, but everything seemed to be happening in slow motion.

"True cultural preservation," she said, her voice carrying clearly through the auditorium, "happens when we keep our traditions alive, not when we turn them into monuments to the dead."

The man pulled something from his jacket—not a weapon, but a small object that glinted in the stage lights. A gold star.

"Dr. Edgecombe," he called out, his voice carrying clearly in the sudden silence. "You've earned this."

Security officers were moving now, converging on the man from multiple directions. But he wasn't trying to escape. He stood calmly, holding the gold star, waiting for them to reach him.

"You understand," he said as the officers surrounded him. "You understand what I'm trying to do."

Lillian stared down at him, this ordinary-looking man who had murdered her friends and colleagues, who had turned their deaths into twisted tributes to cultural preservation.

"I understand," she said quietly, "that you're completely insane."

As the officers led him away, Lillian realized she was shaking. The auditorium was in chaos, people standing and talking and trying to see what was happening. But it was over. The killer had been caught, the threat had ended.

She looked out at the audience, found Miles and Sarah in the crowd. They were safe. Everyone was safe.

The killer had been wrong about everything—about culture, about preservation, about the value of life versus death. But he'd been right about one thing.

Lillian had earned her stars.

## Part III: The Reckoning

### Chapter 7: Second Body

The call came at 4:17 AM on Thursday morning, dragging Lillian from uneasy dreams into a reality that was somehow worse. Chen's voice was tight with exhaustion and something that might have been fear.

"We have another body. Brixton. Can you meet us there?"

Lillian was dressed and out the door within ten minutes, leaving Miles with a hastily scribbled note and a promise to call as soon as she could. The streets of London were empty at this hour, populated only by night shift workers heading home and early commuters beginning their day. The city felt different in the pre-dawn darkness—more vulnerable, more exposed.

The crime scene was in a converted warehouse that housed several artist studios. Police cars lined the street, their blue lights painting the brick walls in shifting patterns of color. Lillian showed her consultant ID to the constable manning the perimeter and was directed to a loading dock where Chen and Morrison were waiting.

"Victim is Rebecca Thornfield," Chen said without preamble. "Wolf, thirty-four, ran a traditional crafts workshop. Found by her assistant when she came in early to prepare for a class."

Lillian's legs nearly gave out. "Rebecca? I know her. I spoke to her just yesterday."

"About what?"

"The media coverage. She was scared, said someone had vandalized her shop. I told her not to let them win." The irony was bitter as poison. "I told her to keep the workshop open."

Chen's expression softened slightly. "This isn't your fault."

"Isn't it? If I hadn't encouraged her to stay visible, to keep working..."

"The killer would have found her anyway. Come on. We need your expertise."

The workshop was a testament to Rebecca's dedication to preserving traditional anthro crafts. Looms lined one wall, their half-finished tapestries still stretched on the frames. Workbenches held tools for woodcarving, metalwork, pottery—all the skills that had been passed down through generations of anthro artisans.

And in the center of it all, Rebecca lay arranged with the same meticulous care as the previous victims.

"Same positioning," Morrison said, his voice carefully neutral. "Head east, hands folded, personal items in a spiral pattern."

But there were differences. Rebecca's tools had been arranged around her body with even greater precision than the previous scenes. Each implement was placed according to its traditional use, creating a complex mandala of craft knowledge around her still form.

"This is more elaborate," Lillian said, forcing herself to study the scene professionally. "The killer spent more time here, took greater care with the arrangement."

"Why?" Chen asked.

Lillian moved closer, noting the specific tools that had been chosen, their placement relative to Rebecca's body. "Rebecca wasn't just a craftsperson—she was a teacher. She passed on knowledge that would otherwise be lost. The killer is acknowledging that."

"How many stars?" Morrison asked.

Lillian looked at Rebecca's forehead, where six gold stars had been carefully placed in a perfect hexagonal pattern. "Six. One more than Marcus."

"So the killer considers her more valuable?"

"More comprehensive, maybe. Marcus preserved songs, but Rebecca preserved entire craft traditions. Weaving, carving, metalwork—skills that take decades to master."

Chen was taking notes, but her expression was troubled. "The escalation is accelerating. Three stars, four stars, five stars, now six. And the time between murders is decreasing."

"The killer's getting more confident," Morrison said. "Or more desperate."

Lillian studied the crime scene photos that the forensics team was taking, trying to understand the killer's mindset. "There's something else. Look at how the tools are arranged."

She pointed to the pattern around Rebecca's body. "It's not just decorative. It's instructional. Each tool is positioned to show its proper use, its relationship to the others. The killer is creating a teaching display."

"Teaching what?"

"Traditional craft techniques. This whole scene is like a museum exhibit—'The Life and Work of Rebecca Thornfield, Master Craftswoman.'"

The realization made her stomach turn. The killer wasn't just murdering people—they were turning them into educational displays, preserving their knowledge in the most twisted way possible.

"We found something else," Chen said, leading her to a workbench near the back of the studio. "A note. Left where Rebecca's assistant would be sure to find it."

The note was handwritten in neat, academic script: *Rebecca Thornfield dedicated her life to preserving the traditional crafts of the anthro community. Her knowledge of weaving, woodcarving, and metalwork represented centuries of cultural wisdom. This display honors her contributions and ensures that her expertise will not be forgotten. She has earned her place among the cultural treasures of her people.*

"It's signed," Morrison said. "With a gold star."

Lillian stared at the note, her mind reeling. "The killer thinks they're doing us a favor. They genuinely believe they're preserving our culture by murdering our cultural figures."

"Preserving them how?"

"By making them permanent. By turning them into monuments to their own expertise." She looked around the workshop, seeing it now through the killer's eyes. "Rebecca was mortal, fallible. She might have forgotten techniques, made mistakes, failed to pass on knowledge. But now she's frozen in time, surrounded by the tools of her trade, a perfect representation of traditional craft mastery."

"That's insane."

"It's academic," Lillian corrected. "The killer thinks like a scholar, a curator. They're building a collection."

Chen's phone buzzed. She answered it, listened for a moment, then hung up with a grim expression.

"That was the lab. They found something in Rebecca's hands."

"What?"

"A business card. Yours."

The words hit Lillian like a physical blow. "Mine?"

"Your university business card. The killer placed it in her left hand, along with a small piece of traditional weaving."

Lillian felt the world tilt around her. The killer wasn't just targeting anthro cultural figures—they were specifically targeting people connected to her. Marcus had called her. Rebecca had spoken to her just yesterday. The killer was working their way through her network, her community, her friends.

"I need to call Miles," she said, her voice barely above a whisper.

"Already done. We've got units heading to your house and Sarah's university. They'll be safe."

But Lillian knew that safety was an illusion. The killer had made this personal, turned her into both consultant and target. Everyone she cared about, everyone she'd ever worked with, was now at risk.

And somewhere in London, the killer was already planning their next move, their next "preservation" project.

The only question was who would be next to earn their stars.

### Chapter 8: Personal Stakes

The package arrived at Lillian's university office on Friday morning, delivered by courier with no return address. Her secretary, Mrs. Patterson, brought it in with the morning mail, setting it on the desk with the casual efficiency of someone who'd been handling academic correspondence for thirty years.

"Came by special delivery," she said, then paused at the door. "Dr. Edgecombe? Are you all right? You look pale."

Lillian stared at the package—a small brown box, her name and address written in the same neat handwriting she'd seen on the killer's notes. "I'm fine, Mrs. Patterson. Could you... could you ask security to come up?"

"Security? Is something wrong?"

"Just a precaution."

After Mrs. Patterson left, Lillian sat staring at the package, afraid to touch it. The killer had her business address, knew her schedule, had been watching her closely enough to time the delivery perfectly.

Campus security arrived within minutes—two officers who looked more suited to handling drunk students than potential bombs. Lillian explained the situation as best she could without revealing details of the investigation.

"We should call the police," the senior officer said after examining the package. "This could be dangerous."

"Already done," Lillian said, showing him Chen's number on her phone.

The police arrived twenty minutes later—Chen, Morrison, and a bomb disposal expert who looked like he'd rather be anywhere else. They cleared the office, set up a perimeter, and carefully X-rayed the package.

"No explosives," the expert reported. "Looks like papers, maybe a small object."

They opened it in the hallway, using remote tools and protective equipment that seemed excessive for such a small package. Inside was a manila folder containing photographs and a handwritten letter.

The photographs were crime scene images—not the official police photos, but personal shots taken by the killer. Marcus in his flat, Rebecca in her workshop, both arranged with the same ritualistic precision Lillian had seen in the official documentation.

But these photos were different. They showed details the police had missed, angles that revealed the killer's perspective, the care they'd taken in arranging each scene.

The letter was written on university letterhead—not Lillian's university, but the logo was familiar. She recognized it after a moment: University College London, where she'd given a guest lecture six months ago.

*Dr. Edgecombe,*

*I hope you appreciate the care I've taken with your colleagues. Each arrangement was designed to honor their contributions to anthro cultural preservation, to ensure that their knowledge would not be lost or forgotten.*

*Marcus Thornfield: Five stars for his dedication to preserving traditional songs. His voice carried the music of our ancestors, and now it will never be silenced by age or illness.*

*Rebecca Thornfield: Six stars for her mastery of traditional crafts. Her hands held the skills of generations, and now they will never lose their dexterity or forget their training.*

*I know you understand what I'm trying to accomplish. Your own work on cultural preservation shows that you recognize the importance of maintaining our heritage. You've spent your career fighting against the dilution and commercialization of anthro traditions.*

*But you've been working within a flawed system, trying to preserve living culture in an academic environment that treats it as historical artifact. I'm offering a better solution—permanent preservation, perfect and unchanging.*

*You've earned seven stars, Dr. Edgecombe. Your contributions to anthro cultural studies, your performances, your research—all of it deserves to be preserved forever.*

*I'll be in touch soon to arrange your final performance.*

*With deepest respect,*
*A Fellow Cultural Preservationist*

The letter was signed with a gold star sticker, just like the ones found on the victims.

"Seven stars," Morrison said grimly. "Higher than anyone else."

"The killer sees you as the ultimate prize," Chen added. "The most valuable target."

Lillian stared at the letter, her hands shaking. The killer's twisted logic was becoming clearer—they saw themselves as a curator, collecting the most valuable examples of anthro cultural preservation. And they'd decided she was the crown jewel of their collection.

"We need to get you somewhere safe," Chen said. "Somewhere the killer can't reach you."

"No." Lillian's voice was firm despite her fear. "Running won't solve this. The killer will just move on to other targets, other people I care about."

"Ms. Edgecombe—"

"Look at the photographs," Lillian interrupted, pointing to the crime scene images. "Look at the care, the attention to detail. This isn't random violence—it's obsession. The killer won't stop until they get what they want."

"Which is you."

"Which is me. So let's give it to them."

Morrison frowned. "What are you suggesting?"

"A trap. We use the killer's obsession against them. Make them come to us on our terms."

"Absolutely not," Chen said. "We're not using you as bait."

"You're not using me as anything. I'm volunteering."

The argument continued for an hour, moving from the university hallway to a secure conference room where they could speak freely. Lillian laid out her plan—a public lecture, heavily advertised, designed to draw the killer out into the open.

"It's too dangerous," Chen insisted. "Too many variables we can't control."

"Every day I'm alive is dangerous now," Lillian replied. "At least this way, we choose the time and place."

In the end, they reached a compromise. Lillian would continue her normal activities under heavy surveillance, acting as both consultant and potential bait. If the killer made contact, if they tried to approach her, the police would be ready.

But as Lillian sat in her office that afternoon, surrounded by plainclothes officers and trying to grade papers as if her life were normal, she couldn't shake the feeling that the killer was still one step ahead.

The letter had been delivered to her office, despite all the security measures. The killer knew where she worked, where she lived, probably where she shopped and ate and spent her free time.

They were being watched, studied, evaluated like a specimen in the killer's twisted collection.

And somewhere in London, the killer was planning her final performance.

### Chapter 9: Community Meeting

The emergency meeting was held at the Camden Community Centre on a rainy Tuesday evening, three weeks after Rebecca's murder. Word had spread through the anthro community's informal networks—phone calls, text messages, whispered conversations in shops and cafes. By seven o'clock, nearly two hundred people had gathered in the centre's main hall.

Lillian sat in the front row, flanked by plainclothes police officers who looked uncomfortable in the crowd of worried anthros. She'd argued against attending, knowing her presence would make her a target, but Chen had insisted.

"If the killer is part of the community, they'll be here," Chen had said. "We need to see who shows up, how they react to you."

The meeting was chaired by Thomas Webb, a elderly badger who'd been a community leader since before the Integration. His voice carried the weight of decades as he called for order.

"We're here because our community is under attack," he began, his words echoing in the packed hall. "Three of our cultural leaders have been murdered in the past month. The police say they're investigating, but we need to talk about how we protect ourselves."

A murmur ran through the crowd—fear, anger, frustration mixing in equal measure.

"The media is calling it a death cult," continued a voice from the back. Lillian turned to see Elena Vasquez, a historian from Liverpool University. "They're making it sound like we're killing each other in some kind of ritual madness."

"Because that's easier than admitting someone is hunting us," replied another voice. James Riverwind, an oral history specialist from Birmingham, stood up in the middle of the hall. "The police want to treat this as random violence, but we know better."

Lillian felt a chill. Both Elena and James were exactly the kind of cultural figures the killer targeted—scholars, preservationists, people dedicated to maintaining anthro traditions. If the killer was in the audience, they were looking at potential victims.

"What do you suggest we do?" Thomas asked. "Cancel all public events? Stop teaching, stop performing, stop being visible?"

"Maybe that's exactly what we should do," said a young fox from the back. "Go underground until this maniac is caught."

"And let them win?" Elena's voice was sharp with anger. "Let them silence our entire community through fear?"

The debate that followed was heated, passionate, and exactly what Lillian had expected. The anthro community had always been divided between those who favored integration and visibility and those who preferred to maintain separation and privacy. The murders had brought those divisions into sharp focus.

"We can't let fear dictate our choices," James said, his voice carrying clearly through the hall. "Our ancestors hid in the forests for centuries, preserving our culture in secret. We didn't come out of hiding just to go back into it at the first sign of trouble."

"Our ancestors weren't being hunted by serial killers," the young fox replied.

"Weren't they?" Elena stood up, her historian's perspective adding weight to her words. "We've always been targets. The Integration didn't end prejudice—it just made it more subtle."

Lillian found herself nodding. Elena was right—anthros had always lived with the threat of violence, the knowledge that their visibility made them vulnerable. The current murders were just a more focused version of the same old hatred.

"The difference," Thomas said, "is that this killer claims to be honoring us. They're not motivated by hatred—they're motivated by some twisted form of love."

"Which makes them more dangerous," Lillian said, speaking for the first time. The hall fell silent, all eyes turning to her. "Hatred is predictable. You can defend against it, fight it, overcome it. But obsession... obsession doesn't follow rational rules."

"Dr. Edgecombe," Elena said, "you've been working with the police. What can you tell us about the investigation?"

Lillian glanced at the plainclothes officers, who nodded slightly. "The killer has extensive knowledge of our cultural practices. They're arranging the victims according to traditional funeral rites, placing objects that represent their life's work, treating each murder as a form of cultural preservation."

"So they're one of us," James said grimly.

"Or someone who's studied us extensively. The level of detail, the specific traditions referenced—it's not casual knowledge."

A new voice spoke from the side of the hall—Dr. Marcus Whitfield, a psychology professor from King's College. "Has anyone considered that the killer might be trying to provoke exactly this kind of response?"

All eyes turned to him. Whitfield was human, one of the few non-anthros in attendance, but he'd been studying anthro psychology for decades.

"Think about it," he continued. "The killer targets cultural figures, arranges them according to traditional rites, leaves notes about preservation. They're not just murdering people—they're making a statement about anthro culture."

"What kind of statement?" Thomas asked.

"That it's dying. That it needs to be preserved before it's lost forever. That the only way to maintain pure anthro culture is to freeze it in time, turn it into museum pieces."

The hall was completely silent now, everyone processing the implications.

"The killer isn't trying to destroy anthro culture," Whitfield said. "They're trying to save it. In the most twisted way possible."

"But that's insane," Elena protested.

"Is it? Look around this room. How many of you have children who speak the old languages? How many practice the traditional crafts? How many know the funeral rites that the killer is replicating?"

The silence that followed was answer enough. Integration had brought many benefits, but it had also led to cultural dilution. The old ways were being forgotten, replaced by hybrid traditions that blended anthro and human customs.

"The killer sees this as cultural death," Whitfield continued. "They're trying to preserve what they see as the pure essence of anthro culture before it disappears entirely."

"By murdering the people who are actually preserving it," Lillian said, her voice bitter.

"Because they think living culture is corrupted culture. Only in death can traditions remain pure, unchanging, perfect."

The meeting continued for another hour, but the energy had shifted. Whitfield's analysis had given everyone something to think about, a new way of understanding the killer's motivation.

As people began to leave, Lillian noticed several individuals approaching Elena and James, engaging them in quiet conversations. Both scholars were exactly the type the killer targeted—prominent, knowledgeable, dedicated to cultural preservation.

She made a mental note to warn Chen about them, to suggest increased surveillance. If the killer was in the audience tonight, they'd just identified their next targets.

Walking to her car, flanked by police protection, Lillian couldn't shake the feeling that they were being watched. The killer had been studying the anthro community for months, learning their habits, their gathering places, their leaders.

Tonight's meeting had been a perfect opportunity for reconnaissance.

And somewhere in the crowd of worried faces and passionate voices, the killer had been taking notes.

### Chapter 10: Miles Intervenes

Miles found Lillian on the farmhouse porch at dawn, still clutching her phone, her eyes red from crying and lack of sleep. The Cotswolds stretched out before them in the morning light, beautiful and serene and completely meaningless in the face of what was happening in London.

"Another message?" he asked, settling beside her on the wooden bench.

She showed him the phone. The latest text had arrived an hour ago: *Thomas Webb was even more passionate about cultural preservation than I expected. Seven stars seemed appropriate for someone so dedicated to maintaining our musical heritage. His archive will make a lovely memorial.*

"Jesus," Miles breathed. "He's killed Webb?"

"Probably. The police haven't called yet, but..." Lillian's voice broke. "This is my fault. If I hadn't worked with them, if I hadn't made that bloody list, if I hadn't gone to that meeting..."

"Stop." Miles took the phone from her hands, set it aside, and pulled her against him. "This is not your fault. None of it."

"Isn't it? The killer is targeting people connected to me. My friends, my colleagues, people who trusted me."

"The killer is targeting people because he's a psychopath. You're not responsible for his choices."

But Lillian couldn't shake the feeling that she was somehow complicit in the murders, that her involvement with the police had made everything worse. The killer was using her as a focal point, a way to terrorize the entire anthro community.

"I have to go back," she said.

"What?"

"To London. I have to stop this."

Miles pulled back to look at her. "Lil, you can't be serious. That's exactly what he wants. You go back, you're playing right into his hands."

"And if I stay here, he keeps killing people. How many more have to die while I hide in the countryside?"

"As many as it takes to keep you alive."

The words came out harsh, desperate. Miles ran his hands through his hair, his composure finally cracking.

"I can't lose you," he said. "I won't. I don't care about the investigation, I don't care about catching the killer, I don't care about any of it. I just want you safe."

"And I want my friends to stop dying."

They stared at each other across an impossible divide. Miles saw only the woman he loved, the person he'd sworn to protect. Lillian saw the weight of responsibility, the knowledge that her community was being destroyed while she sat in safety.

"There has to be another way," Miles said finally.

"What way? The police have been trying to catch him for weeks. He's always one step ahead, always anticipating their moves."

"Then maybe it's time to stop playing by his rules."

Lillian looked at him sharply. "What do you mean?"

Miles was quiet for a long moment, staring out at the rolling hills. When he spoke, his voice was different—harder, more determined.

"The killer is obsessed with you, right? He sees you as the ultimate prize, the crown jewel of his collection."

"Yes."

"And he's been escalating, getting more desperate, taking bigger risks."

"Where are you going with this?"

Miles turned to face her. "What if we give him what he wants? Not really, but... what if we make him think he's won?"

"I don't understand."

"A fake death. We stage your murder, make it look like the killer got to you. Then we wait for him to claim credit, to reveal himself."

Lillian stared at him. "That's... that's actually not a terrible idea."

"It's a terrible idea," Miles said quickly. "I hate it. But it might be the only way to draw him out."

"The police would never agree to it."

"Then we don't tell them. We do it ourselves."

The plan they hatched over the next hour was simple in concept, complex in execution. They would stage Lillian's death in a way that looked like the killer's work—traditional funeral arrangement, gold stars, the whole ritualistic display. Then they would wait for the killer to take credit, to reveal himself through his need to claim the ultimate prize.

"It's dangerous," Miles said as they worked out the details. "If something goes wrong, if the killer realizes it's fake..."

"Then we'll deal with it," Lillian replied. "Together."

They spent the rest of the morning making preparations—researching funeral rites, gathering props, planning the logistics. It felt surreal, planning her own fake death while the real killer was somewhere in London, probably planning the genuine article.

But it also felt like taking control, like finally being proactive instead of reactive.

"There's one problem," Miles said as they reviewed their plan. "The police will investigate. They'll discover the body is fake."

"Not if we don't use a body."

"What?"

"We stage the scene, but no body. Make it look like the killer took you away, like he's planning some grand finale."

Miles nodded slowly. "That could work. The killer's been escalating, getting more elaborate. Taking his ultimate prize somewhere special would fit the pattern."

"And it gives us time to set up surveillance, to wait for him to make contact."

They called Chen from the farmhouse landline, using a voice modulator Miles had ordered online. The message was simple: "Dr. Edgecombe has been taken. Check her office."

Then they drove back to London, taking back roads and avoiding traffic cameras, arriving at the university after dark.

Breaking into Lillian's office was easier than expected—Miles had learned a few things during his years as a journalist, including how to pick simple locks. They arranged the scene carefully, following the killer's established pattern but adding their own touches.

Lillian's desk was cleared, her papers arranged in neat stacks. Her computer was left open to a document about traditional funeral rites. A gold star sticker was placed on her keyboard—seven stars, just as the killer had promised.

But there was no body, no blood, no sign of violence. Just the suggestion that someone had been taken, removed for purposes unknown.

"Think it'll work?" Miles asked as they surveyed their handiwork.

"It has to," Lillian replied.

They left through the fire exit, avoiding the security cameras, and drove to a safe house Miles had rented under a false name. Then they waited.

The call came six hours later—not to the police, but to the BBC newsroom where Miles had contacts. The voice was electronically disguised, but the message was clear:

"Dr. Lillian Edgecombe has been preserved for posterity. Her final performance will take place tomorrow evening at the Royal Festival Hall. The public is invited to witness the culmination of my work."

Miles and Lillian looked at each other across the small flat.

"He took the bait," Miles said.

"Now we just have to survive the performance."

### Chapter 11: The Trap

The Royal Festival Hall stood like a concrete monument against the gray London sky, its brutalist architecture somehow appropriate for what was about to unfold. Lillian crouched in the back of an unmarked police van, watching the building through tinted windows as the sun set over the Thames.

"Still think this is insane," Chen said for the dozenth time, adjusting her earpiece. "We should have more backup, more surveillance, more everything."

"Too much backup and he'll spot it," Lillian replied. "The killer's been planning this for months. He'll know if something's wrong."

Morrison checked his weapon for the third time in ten minutes. "And if he realizes it's a trap before we can take him?"

"Then we improvise."

The plan was simple in theory, complex in execution. Lillian would enter the Festival Hall through the stage door, making herself visible to the killer's surveillance. The building would appear empty except for essential security, but police officers would be positioned throughout, ready to move when the killer revealed himself.

"Remember," Chen said, "your job is to stay alive long enough for us to identify and apprehend the suspect. Don't try to be a hero."

"Wouldn't dream of it."

But Lillian knew that once she was inside the building, once the killer made his move, all the careful planning would go out the window. She would be alone with a murderer who saw her death as the culmination of his life's work.

Miles squeezed her hand. "You don't have to do this."

"Yes, I do."

"I could go instead. Wear a disguise, pretend to be you."

"He'd know. The killer has been studying me for months. He knows how I move, how I speak, probably how I breathe."

The radio crackled. "All units in position. Target building is secure."

Chen looked at Lillian. "Last chance to change your mind."

"I'm not changing my mind."

The walk from the van to the stage door felt like a mile. Lillian was acutely aware of every shadow, every window, every potential hiding place where the killer might be watching. Her skin crawled with the sensation of being observed, studied, evaluated.

The stage door was unlocked, as arranged. Lillian stepped into the familiar backstage area, remembering her last performance here—a concert of traditional anthro songs that now seemed like a lifetime ago.

"I'm inside," she whispered into her microphone.

"Copy that. We have you on visual."

The main auditorium was dark, empty seats stretching away into shadows. But the stage was lit, a single spotlight creating a circle of brightness in the vast space.

And in the center of that circle, someone had arranged a display.

Lillian's breath caught as she approached the stage. It was a perfect recreation of a traditional anthro memorial—flowers arranged in spiral patterns, candles burning in ceremonial holders, photographs of the killer's victims arranged like a gallery of the honored dead.

Marcus, Rebecca, Thomas, Elena, James—all the cultural figures who had died for the killer's twisted vision of preservation. Their faces smiled out from the photographs, frozen in moments of life and creativity.

"He's been busy," Lillian murmured.

"What do you see?" Chen's voice in her earpiece.

"A shrine. He's turned the stage into a memorial for his victims."

At the center of the display was an empty space—a gap in the pattern where one more photograph would complete the arrangement. Lillian's photograph, she realized. The final piece of the killer's collection.

"Dr. Edgecombe."

The voice came from the darkness beyond the stage lights. Calm, educated, almost gentle.

"I'm here," Lillian called back.

"Please, come onto the stage. Stand in the center of the memorial."

Lillian climbed the steps slowly, her heart hammering against her ribs. The memorial was even more elaborate up close—each element carefully chosen and positioned, each photograph accompanied by objects representing the victim's work.

"You've done beautiful work," she said, hoping to keep the killer talking.

"Thank you. I've tried to honor each of them appropriately."

"Why?" Lillian asked. "Why did they have to die?"

A figure emerged from the wings—middle-aged, unremarkable, wearing a dark suit that made him blend into the shadows. Dr. Marcus Whitfield, the psychology professor who had spoken at the community meeting.

"Because living culture is dying culture," he said, stepping into the light. "Every day they lived, every day they taught and performed and adapted their traditions, they were corrupting the pure essence of anthro heritage."

"That's not corruption," Lillian said. "That's evolution. That's how culture stays alive."

"No." Whitfield's voice was patient, as if explaining something to a slow student. "Culture stays alive through preservation. Through maintaining the original forms, the traditional practices, the authentic expressions."

He gestured to the memorial around them. "Look at what I've created. Marcus will never forget a traditional song. Rebecca will never lose her skill with traditional crafts. Thomas will never compromise his musical heritage. They are perfect now, preserved forever."

"They're dead."

"They're eternal."

Lillian could hear movement in the darkness beyond the stage—police officers getting into position. But Whitfield seemed oblivious to everything except his twisted vision.

"You understand," he continued. "Your research, your performances, your dedication to preserving anthro culture—you know how precious these traditions are. How easily they can be lost."

"I know they're lost when the people who carry them are murdered."

"Are they? Or are they preserved in their purest form?"

Whitfield reached into his jacket and pulled out a small object—a gold star sticker, larger than the others, with seven points instead of five.

"Seven stars," he said. "The highest honor I can bestow. You've earned this through decades of dedication to cultural preservation."

"I've earned it by staying alive and keeping the culture alive with me."

"No." Whitfield stepped closer, the star gleaming in the stage lights. "You've earned it by becoming the perfect example of what anthro culture can be. And now you'll be preserved forever, a permanent testament to the beauty and wisdom of your people."

"Dr. Whitfield," Lillian said, backing away slowly, "you're not preserving anything. You're destroying it."

"I'm saving it from destruction. From dilution, commercialization, corruption. From the slow death of integration and compromise."

He was close enough now that Lillian could see the madness in his eyes—not the wild madness of a raving lunatic, but the cold, calculated madness of someone who had convinced himself that murder was scholarship.

"The others didn't understand at first," he continued. "They fought, they pleaded, they tried to convince me I was wrong. But in the end, they all accepted their honor. They all realized that preservation was better than corruption."

"And if I don't accept it?"

"You will. Because you're a scholar. You understand the importance of maintaining cultural integrity."

Whitfield raised the star, preparing to place it on Lillian's forehead. In the darkness beyond the stage, she could hear the soft sounds of police officers moving into position.

"Dr. Whitfield," she said, "there's something you need to know."

"What?"

"I'm not dead."

The confusion in his eyes lasted only a moment before understanding dawned. He spun around, seeing the police officers emerging from the shadows, weapons drawn.

"You tricked me," he said, his voice filled with something that might have been admiration.

"I preserved myself," Lillian replied. "The way living culture is supposed to be preserved—through intelligence, adaptation, and survival."

As the police closed in, Whitfield looked around the memorial he had created, at the photographs of his victims, at the elaborate display of his twisted scholarship.

"They were perfect," he said quietly. "I made them perfect."

"You made them dead," Lillian said. "There's a difference."

The arrest was anticlimactic after the buildup—Whitfield offered no resistance, seemed almost relieved to be caught. As they led him away, he looked back at Lillian one last time.

"Seven stars," he said. "You earned seven stars."

"I earned the right to keep living," she replied.

And as the police cleared the scene and the memorial was dismantled, Lillian stood on the empty stage, surrounded by the ghosts of her murdered friends, and began to plan how to honor them properly.

Not through death, but through life.

Not through preservation, but through continuation.

Not through stars, but through songs that would never end.

## Epilogue: After the Stars

*Six months later*

The memorial concert was held on a warm evening in June, in the same community center where the emergency meeting had taken place. But this time, the atmosphere was different—not fearful, but celebratory. Not about death, but about life.

Lillian stood on the small stage, her guitar in her hands, looking out at the faces in the audience. Many were familiar—friends, colleagues, students who had come to honor the memory of those who had been lost. Others were new—people who had heard about the concert, about the community, about the importance of keeping culture alive.

"We're here tonight," she began, "to remember Marcus Thornfield, Rebecca Thornfield, Thomas Webb, Elena Vasquez, and James Riverwind. Not as victims, but as the cultural keepers they were in life."

She played Marcus's favorite song, the one he had been working on when he died—a traditional ballad about the first anthros who had emerged from the deep forests. Her voice carried the melody he had loved, keeping it alive in the way he would have wanted.

Rebecca's apprentices demonstrated traditional weaving techniques, their hands moving with the skill she had taught them. Thomas's choir performed one of his arrangements, their voices blending in harmonies he had crafted. Elena's students presented research on pre-Integration social structures, continuing the work she had started. James's oral history project was expanded, with new interviews and recordings that honored his methodology.

Each tribute was a living memorial, a way of keeping the victims' contributions alive and growing rather than frozen in time.

Miles sat in the front row with Sarah, both of them beaming with pride. The investigation had taken its toll on their family, but it had also brought them closer together, reminded them of what was truly important.

Chen and Morrison were there too, off-duty but still watchful. The case had been closed, Whitfield was in prison awaiting trial, but the scars remained. The anthro community would be more cautious now, more aware of the dangers that came with visibility.

But they would not hide.

As the concert continued, Lillian thought about Whitfield's twisted logic, his belief that culture could only be preserved through death. He had been wrong about everything—about preservation, about culture, about the value of life versus the permanence of death.

Culture wasn't a museum piece to be protected behind glass. It was a living, breathing, evolving thing that grew stronger through sharing, through adaptation, through the messy, imperfect process of being passed from one generation to the next.

The killer had tried to turn cultural figures into stars—perfect, unchanging, dead. But real culture wasn't about perfection. It was about continuation, about the endless human need to create and share and connect.

As the evening wound down, as the last songs were sung and the last stories told, Lillian felt something she hadn't experienced in months: hope.

The anthro community had been wounded, but it had survived. The traditions had been threatened, but they endured. The culture lived on, not in the killer's twisted memorial, but in the voices and hands and hearts of the people who carried it forward.

Later, as they walked home through the quiet streets of Camden, Miles took her hand.

"Good concert," he said.

"It felt right. Like something Marcus and the others would have approved of."

"Living memorial instead of a dead one."

"Exactly."

They walked in comfortable silence for a while, past the shops and cafes and venues where anthro culture continued to thrive. The community had been shaken, but it had not been broken.

"Lil," Miles said as they reached their front door, "what are you going to do now? With the investigation over, with Whitfield in custody?"

Lillian considered the question. She could go back to her normal life—teaching, researching, performing. She could try to forget the past few months, to pretend that none of it had happened.

Or she could do something more.

"I'm thinking about starting a foundation," she said. "Something to support anthro cultural figures, to help them stay visible and safe. To make sure what happened to Marcus and the others never happens again."

"That sounds like a lot of work."

"The best work usually is."

Miles smiled. "Then I guess we'd better get started."

As they went inside, Lillian thought about the gold stars Whitfield had used to grade his victims. Seven stars for her, he had said. The highest honor he could bestow.

But she had earned something better than stars. She had earned the right to keep living, to keep creating, to keep the culture alive in all its messy, imperfect, beautiful complexity.

And that was worth more than all the stars in the sky.

## Author's Note

*Goldstar* explores themes of cultural preservation, the difference between living tradition and static museum pieces, and the dangerous obsession with "purity" that can lead to violence. The story examines how communities respond to threats, how fear can be used as a weapon, and how the best way to honor the dead is to continue their work among the living.

The character of Dr. Marcus Whitfield represents the academic mindset taken to its most extreme conclusion—the belief that culture can be "preserved" through documentation and display, rather than through the messy, evolving process of being lived and shared. His murders are not acts of hatred, but of misguided love—a twisted attempt to create perfect, unchanging examples of anthro cultural achievement.

Lillian's journey from reluctant consultant to active participant reflects the way ordinary people can be drawn into extraordinary circumstances, and how the choice between safety and responsibility is never simple. Her decision to risk her life to stop the killer is not heroic in the traditional sense—it's practical, born from the understanding that hiding from danger doesn't make it go away.

The story also examines the role of media in shaping public perception, the way fear can be weaponized against marginalized communities, and the importance of solidarity in the face of threats. The anthro community's response to the murders—the debates about visibility versus safety, the tension between integration and separation—reflects real-world discussions about how minority communities navigate hostile environments.

Ultimately, *Goldstar* is about the difference between preservation and continuation, between honoring the dead and serving the living, between the static perfection of death and the chaotic beauty of life. Culture is not a museum piece to be protected—it's a living thing that grows stronger through sharing, adaptation, and the endless human need to create meaning through connection.

The gold stars that give the story its title represent the killer's attempt to grade and categorize cultural contributions, to reduce complex human lives to simple ratings. But real culture can't be graded or rated or preserved in amber. It can only be lived, shared, and passed on to the next generation of voices waiting to add their own songs to the endless symphony of human expression.
