# Operation Albatross

## 1. The Opera Bluff  
Staatsoper Unter den Linden, East Berlin — March 1987, 21:14

The curtain went up on Verdi and came down on the handoff.

Lillian Edgecombe adjusted the collar of her sapphire coat and watched the courier three rows below pretend to love opera. He didn’t. His paws were too tight on the rolled programme, his ears too stiff, the way people got when they thought death might be sitting behind them.

She’d set the pattern: seat K17, transfer during the interval crush, programme swapped for programme. Neat. Denied drama. Berlin liked its intrigue quiet.

So she dropped her compact.

Chrome on brass. A sharp clatter, one, two, three ricochets down the aisle before it landed in the lap of a couple who were trying very hard not to look like Stasi. The overture swallowed the first gasp, not the second.

“Entschuldigung,” she said in German, smooth and apologetic. Gloved paw, small bow, practised smile.

Too loud. Too many eyes.

The courier snapped. He stood, programme crushed in his fist, and bolted up the aisle towards the rear exit.

Perfect, she thought.

Ten seconds later a man stepped out of the side‑door shadows, already moving to intercept. Charcoal coat, short‑cropped fur, ears pricked. German Shepherd. Military set to his shoulders that Berlin never quite scraped off.

Miles.

Lillian rose as if heading for the bar and slid into the corridor behind the auditorium. The music was a wall at her back, all brass and threat. Out here it was just carpet and bad paint and the sound of a courier discovering the staff door was locked.

He hit it with his shoulder, bounced, swore.

“Mein Gott—” he started, then saw her.

“Change of plan,” she murmured. One paw took the rolled programme from him, the other produced a key she’d never intended him to use and flicked it down the nearest ventilation grille. The clink was tiny but final.

The door behind him opened without a sound. Miles Delacroix came in like a closing argument, coat flaring, one paw already under the lapel where he kept his sidearm.

Lillian turned to meet him, the picture of mild surprise.

“Ah,” she said. “There you are.”

He did not lower the gun.

“Do I want to know what the hell that was?” he growled in English. The accent on the vowels said West. The distrust on his muzzle said East, old habits that hadn’t forgotten which whispered in which stairwell.

“A small mistake,” she said. “First night in Berlin. I got nervous. I dropped something.”

His eyes didn’t blink. “You flushed a courier through a house packed with Stasi watchers and at least one Soviet rezident handler. That’s not nerves. That’s theatre.”

“You’re at the opera,” she said. “Enjoy the staging.”

His tail flicked once. “Who are you?”

She took half a step towards him, into the range where perfume and intention travelled together. “On paper? Cultural attaché. British Embassy. I drink sherry, I admire Schiller, I complain about the plumbing.”

“And off paper?”

She smiled. “Off paper, I’m the reason your courier still has a pulse. For the next five minutes.”

He weighed it; the gun didn’t move. That was one thing she’d noted in his file—the way he held lines. Ex‑Stasi, turned by the Americans, now wearing a CIA badge like it itched.

“You’re MI6,” he said at last.

“Officially?” she replied. “No. Officially, London doesn’t do things like this. Unofficially, I’m your new partner.”

He swore in a language the Personnel file hadn’t covered and rubbed the bridge of his muzzle with his free paw.

“God help me,” he muttered.

“He’s busy,” Lillian said. “You’re stuck with me.”

Behind them, the orchestra swelled, the singers onstage climbing towards an illusion of fate. In the corridor, three people stood very still with a programme that was heavier than it looked.

Berlin, she thought, was still very good at music.

It was worse at trust.

## 2. Helmstedt, Night Crossing  
Inner German Border — March 1987, 03:27

Snow fell like ash. Drift, settle, erase.

The Citroën DS shuddered its way along the iron‑lined road, heating system humming at a low, useless tremor. Headlights carved a tunnel through the white; beyond that, the border was just darkness and memory.

Miles drove with his paws locked on the wheel. He wore his trench coat buttoned to the throat, but tension climbed further than fabric. Beside him, Lillian sat with her own coat neat, scarf tucked properly, boot crossed over boot. She could feel his silence like static.

“You ever talk,” he muttered at last, “or do you just break things and smile?”

“Silence is underrated in tense situations,” she said. “Especially when microphones get bored easily.”

“We’re crossing into West Germany with documents the KGB would skin us for,” he said. “I’d like to know whether the woman who nearly got my asset shot at the opera has anything in her head besides quips.”

She opened her bag. His hand twitched towards his weapon; she ignored it and pulled out a thermos.

“Tea?” she asked.

Suspicion. Irritation. The usual.

“I’m not drinking MI6 tea,” he said.

“How tragic,” she said, and poured for herself. Steam rose in the dark, bergamot cutting through petrol and cold.

They drove in silence for another mile. The border lights grew brighter, twin smears in the snow.

“I read your file,” he said suddenly. “Cambridge. Physics. Behavioural psychology. Linguistics. Top of your class. You could have been anything. So you chose… this?”

She watched the road ahead, the way the tyres found ruts carved by heavier vehicles.

“In another life,” she said, “I’d be teaching students how to model risk curves in nuclear power plants. Instead, I get to see the people who sign the risk forms. You learn more about a weapon from the hand that holds it than from the equation.”

“That’s not an answer,” he said.

She let that stand. Answers were expensive. Then she turned her head, watched his profile in the green dash glow.

“You were Stasi,” she said. “Files don’t like to say it, but they can’t quite hide it. Leipzig. Internal surveillance. Now CIA. Did they give you a medal or just better cigarettes?”

His jaw clenched. “They gave me a choice,” he said. “I took it.”

“And now you’re driving ex‑Soviet documents across a frozen border with someone you don’t trust,” she said. “How’s that working out?”

“I don’t need to trust you,” he said. “I need you to do what you’re told.”

“I don’t do what I’m told,” Lillian said. “I do what makes the pattern make sense.”

The radio crackled, a clipped voice in German and then English.

“Checkpoint ahead. Papers ready. Be sharp.”

Miles drew in a breath. “Just follow my lead.”

“Lead away,” she said. “I’m right behind you.”

The checkpoint rose out of the snow like something abandoned from an older war: guard towers, cranes, the geometry of suspicion. Concrete blocks turned traffic into diagrams. Floodlights painted everything in colourless theatre.

Lillian watched the guards move, the choreography of bureaucracy. Who checked passports. Who lingered on licence plates. Who watched the watchers. Systems had a rhythm. You could learn it. Or you could let it crush you.

As the Citroën rolled to a stop, she flexed her fingers under her gloves and smiled at the nearest gun.

There were worse places to be than between empires.

At least here, everyone was honest about the walls.

## 3. Honeymoon Cover  
Leipzig, East Germany — Safehouse Apartment

The wallpaper was state socialism at its most passive‑aggressive: pale green flowers that had given up halfway through blooming. Miles stood in the middle of the living room, arms folded, taking in the sagging sofa and the low table and the television that pretended to receive only authorised signals.

He looked, Lillian thought, like a dog who’d been told the only way out of the building was in a bow tie.

She emerged from the bedroom in a house robe she’d found in the wardrobe, belted tight, hair pulled back into something sensible. Glasses on. Domestic. Married.

“You didn’t have to commit this hard,” he said.

“I always commit,” she said, brushing a stray thread from his shoulder. “It’s half the job.”

“We’re here forty‑eight hours,” he said. “We intercept a microfilm courier in the consular crowd, we get out. We don’t need… props.”

She picked up the framed photo from the shelf: the two of them on a beach, sun behind them, his arm around her shoulders. The forgery was good enough that she felt the ghost of sand between her toes.

“Apparently we spent a week in Corsica last summer,” she said. “You look almost relaxed in this one. You should try it sometime.”

He scowled. “You snore.”

“I do not snore,” she said. “I breathe assertively. You, on the other hand, grind your teeth like you’re interrogating them.”

He almost smiled. Almost.

They cooked in the tiny kitchen, bumping elbows. Soup from a tin, bread from a rationed loaf. The bug in the overhead light hummed softly to itself. Somewhere in the ceiling plaster, wires carried their performance upstairs to a bored Stasi operator who was being paid to listen to the sound of spoons.

“Two days of this?” Miles said quietly, under the clink of cutlery. “Playing house?”

“Could be worse,” Lillian said. “They could have given us children.”

On the sofa later, shoulders touching, they watched the evening news talk about agricultural targets and comrades. The real show was across the street: a diplomatic building with curtains that twitched slightly too often.

“You ever think about doing something else?” he asked suddenly, voice low, eyes still on the television.

“All the time,” she said. “I’d be writing papers on game theory. Running tutorials. Baking lemon tarts for office Christmas parties. You?”

He thought about it. “I’d be dead,” he said.

Silence. Not cold, for once. Just weight.

Three knocks at the door. A pause. One knock.

The signal.

They were no longer just pretending. They were on.

## 4. A Guest at the Table  
Leipzig Safehouse

The knock came again while Lillian was setting the roasted trout on the table. Miles wiped his paws on a dish towel, frown deepening. Nobody on their side was due.

He opened the door with the chain still on.

“Herr Delacroix!” The man on the landing beamed, holding a cheap bouquet of tulips like a flag. Pale hair, sharp glasses, coat that tried too hard to look civilian. “You left your paperwork at the consulate.”

“Paperwork,” Miles repeated. Neutral tone. Tail still.

Lillian appeared at his shoulder, smile polite, paw resting lightly on the stranger’s arm. Framing, for the benefit of any microphone.

“And you are?” she asked.

“Kaspar,” he said. “Just Kaspar.”

Of course it was. Men like this always had one‑word names, like brands or knives.

“You must be the attaché from London,” he added, eyes lingering too long.

“Only on paper,” she said.

They let him in. They had to. The tulips sat on the counter like a joke no one quite wanted to explain.

At the table, Miles poured wine. Lillian steered conversation through safe topics like a pianist working scales: books, architecture, the difficulty of finding decent coffee in Leipzig. Kaspar smiled, nodded, agreed too readily. His gaze flicked to the bugged light, to the photos on the shelf, back to Miles.

“Agent Delacroix,” he said once, too smoothly.

Miles’s paw tightened on the bottle. Lillian’s paw brushed his knee under the table. Warning.

“I’m a trade liaison,” Miles said. “Paperwork and polite lies. Nothing as glamorous as agents.”

Kaspar laughed. “Then I must be confused.” His glasses flashed. “It happens.”

Later, as he drifted near the bookshelf, pretending to admire their fake holiday, Lillian stepped in close enough that he had to tilt his head back a fraction.

“You’ve made your point,” she said quietly. “Tell your friends in Normannenstraße they’re late. The party started without them.”

His smile slipped. “You’re not just an attaché.”

“No,” she said. “I’m the one with the cake knife.”

He left with the same smile he’d brought, but the bouquet stayed behind. Tulips, she thought, were terrible at listening devices.

When the door closed, Miles exhaled.

“You knew he was Stasi,” he said.

“He files his claws,” she said. “And wears knock‑off cologne that only comes through one procurement officer. MI6 has been reading their post.”

He poured another glass, more for something to do with his hands than the taste.

“What are you really doing here, Lillian?” he asked. “MI6 doesn’t throw Cambridge prizes into Leipzig for a routine courier.”

She met his eyes. “Discovering,” she said, “whether the Americans’ favourite defector is worth my time.”

He laughed once, without warmth. “Congratulations,” he said. “You’ve picked the losing team.”

## 5. Guts and Ghosts  
CIA Outpost, East Berlin — Weeks Later

The outpost sat under an old train station, where the halogen lights flickered as if they resented being in the twentieth century. Pipes rattled. Dust turned every breath into archaeology.

Miles pushed through the metal door and stopped.

Lillian sat on the camp bed by the boiler, blouse ripped at the shoulder, bandage darkened at the edges. Her fur was matted where blood had dried. She held a cigarette she hadn’t lit.

“What happened?” he asked. The question was too big for the room.

She looked up, then down again.

“Someone rewired my equipment,” she said. “I plugged in the transmitter. It exploded. My handler didn’t show up to watch.”

He moved closer, paw careful on her collar as he checked the dressing. Shrapnel, small and sharp. Designed to scare. Or to miss by just enough to look like mercy.

“MI6?” he asked.

She shook her head. “If London wanted me dead, I’d be quieter,” she said. “They think I’ve gone freelance. Your people?”

“Langley thinks you turned me,” he said. “Vienna pulled my Stasi file out of a drawer and waved it around. Anything I say now is contaminated by what I used to be.”

She laughed once, harsh. “We’re both radioactive.”

The radio on the shelf hissed softly between stations. Above them, a train rumbled past, shaking rust down onto the floor like snow.

“Someone’s playing all three sides,” she said. “Stasi, CIA, MI6. Maybe Moscow for dessert.”

“Four,” he said. “There’s a group calling itself Die Spindel. The Spindle. Former Stasi, former KGB, whoever could see this coming and didn’t like their pension prospects. They’ve been rebuilding networks under everyone’s nose.”

She lifted her head. “That’s what my handler was tracking,” she said. “Not just documents. Patterns. Nuclear targeting lists, biotech briefs. They’re hoarding leverage.”

“And killing anyone who gets close,” Miles said. “Including you.”

She didn’t argue.

Something heavy hit the corridor outside. A footstep. Another. The cheap light buzzed like a hornet.

Miles drew his gun. Lillian’s paw went to hers, slower only because of the bandage.

Whatever game they thought they’d been playing, it was about to change again.

## 6. The Drop That Wasn’t  
West Berlin, near Friedrichstraße — 02:14

The tenement stairwell smelled of mold and burnt coffee. Wallpaper peeled under Lillian’s glove like old secrets.

Apartment 3C was supposed to hold a courier pouch under a loose board. Miles had said the whole thing stank. She had agreed, privately. Then she’d gone anyway.

At the landing she stopped and listened.

Bootsteps above. Two sets. Heavy. No attempt at stealth because they didn’t need one.

She swore, checked the pistol in her coat, and nudged the door open.

Dark room. Dust in the air. An armchair that had seen better regimes. Ash in a cold grate. No pouch under the nearest board.

Just a tape recorder on the floor, red light blinking.

She turned for the corridor.

Floodlight. White, brutal, through the window. German orders barked through a bullhorn.

“MI6 agent! Nicht bewegen!”

She dropped, rolled. Glass exploded above her, bullets stitching plaster where her head had been. She fired twice through the window frame, no time to see if she hit anything, and sprinted towards the hall.

Another door flew open.

Impact. Someone took her at the waist and they fell sideways into a different flat, skidding across bare boards.

Miles. Of course.

He kicked the door shut and shoved a bookcase against it. Wood splintered. The floodlight swung.

“You arrogant, suicidal lunatic,” he snapped. “I told you it was a trap.”

“You’re welcome,” she said. Her ear burned. When she touched it, her fingers came away red.

He caught her chin in one paw, turning her face to the light. His touch was gentler than his voice.

“Just a graze,” he muttered. “You’ll live. For the next ten minutes, anyway.”

“Where’s your backup?” she asked.

He snorted. “Compromised. CIA ground team’s been leaking to the Soviets or Stasi or both. Langley thinks I’m the leak. Your people think you’re the leak. It’s a party.”

Footsteps thundered past in the stairwell. Someone tried the door, felt the resistance, moved on. For now.

“So my handler sells me out,” she said. “Yours sells you out. The Spindle gets a neat little two‑for‑one.”

Miles’s eyes met hers. Something shifted between them—not trust, not yet, but the beginnings of a shared target.

“We’re done working for them,” he said. “We work for us. For whoever doesn’t want a new war as a retirement plan.”

The doorframe cracked. Dust fell. Lillian raised her gun. Miles raised his.

When the door finally splintered, they fired together.

## 7. Temporary Alliance  
CIA Bolt‑Hole, West Berlin — 04:43

The safehouse had once been a bakery. The ovens were cold now, the glass cracked, the display cases empty except for dust. Someone had left an old kitchen timer on the counter; its tick was the loudest thing in the room.

They had escaped. Just. Fire escape, alley, backstreets. No sirens. The Spindle didn’t need sirens.

Lillian sat at a metal table, torn sleeve rolled up so Miles could clean the wound properly. He worked in silence, movements precise as if he were repairing a machine.

“Two rounds in the wall,” he said. “Kalashnikov, close range. Not police.”

“So they knew the drop site, the timing, the cover identities,” she said. “And they brought military hardware.”

“Somebody wanted you dead,” he said. “I was collateral. Flattering.”

“I’ll embroider you a medal,” she said.

He re‑taped the bandage and went to put the kettle on. The ritual helped. Boil, pour, wait. Time measured not in minutes but in what you could confess before the whistle.

“There’s more,” he said finally, back still to her. “You mentioned a month ago you’d picked up something. A phrase.”

“Operation Albatross,” she said. The words tasted metallic even here. “Not Soviet, not American. Routed through a channel that shouldn’t exist. Deep black. Off‑ledger. When I pushed, my handler started acting like I’d asked him if he enjoyed strangling kittens.”

“You traced it to London?” he asked.

She nodded. “Internal. Somebody running a programme beyond oversight. Spindle‑adjacent or something worse.”

He turned, mug in paw. “You realise what that means.”

“That my own service tried to kill me to tidy up a loose thread? Yes, Miles. I do occasionally follow the plot.”

He set the mug down in front of her, then leaned against the counter.

“Then we can’t go up the chain,” he said. “Not through MI6. Not through Langley. If Albatross is sitting above them, every phone call we make becomes a confession.”

She wrapped her paws around the mug for warmth that had nothing to do with tea.

“So what do we do?” she asked. “Go home? Pretend we didn’t see the strings?”

He hesitated. When he spoke, his voice had lost its professional rasp.

“We go sideways,” he said. “Vienna. There’s a guy there—Collins. CIA, technically, but he doesn’t mind losing friends. If anyone can dig into a black op without tipping the monster that owns it, it’s him.”

“You trust him?” she asked.

“More than I trust us,” he said. “Which is saying something.”

The radio in the corner coughed into life. A burst of static, then dots and dashes in a pattern her muscles knew before her mind did. She crossed the room, grabbed the cipher pad, scribbled as the code flowed.

Vienna. Hotel name. A date. A word: BLINDFALL.

“They’re waiting for us,” she said.

Miles exhaled, almost a laugh. “Then we’d better bring them something worth the welcome.”

Their paws brushed as she handed him the decoded note.

“You’re not in this alone, Miles,” she said, surprising herself with how much she meant it.

He looked at her. “Neither are you,” he said.

For two people all the agencies had decided were expendable, it felt like a start.

## 8. Vienna, Blindfall  
Vienna, Austria — March 1988, 21:12

Vienna did a good impression of civilisation. Gilded hotels, cobbled streets, diplomats in good coats. Underneath it all, the same old trade: secrets for leverage, leverage for survival.

Lillian’s fingers trembled as she lit a cigarette. Cold, not nerves. Mostly.

“One courier,” Miles said beside her in the alley. “One briefcase. The CIA says it’s a simple intercept. Nothing about Albatross. Langley’s pretending not to know the word.”

“London’s pretending not to know my name,” she said. “We’re in good company.”

She watched the end of the alley, where a black Mercedes had just rolled to a stop. A young man climbed out, courier bag slung over one shoulder, the anxious bounce of someone who’d been told this was important but not life‑ending.

“He’s early,” Miles said.

“He’s scared,” Lillian replied. “Early is what you do when you still think being prompt impresses someone.”

They waited until he reached the broken café table that was tonight’s dead drop.

“Hey,” Lillian called in German.

He spun, eyes wide. “They said Americans,” he blurted.

“They lied,” she said.

Miles stepped out of the shadow, gun low but obvious. “Who gave you the order?”

The courier’s eyes flicked towards the boarded‑up gallery. In the reflection of a darkened window, Lillian saw the shadow move.

“Ambush,” Miles snapped.

Glass burst outward. Muzzle flash. Silenced shots still made sound if you knew what to listen for. Lillian dove behind the table, wood splintering above her. Miles fired once, twice. A figure in a black coat jerked and went down.

One more shot; the courier folded, clutching his chest. Lillian grabbed the case as it slid from his hand.

“Go,” she said.

They went. Down side streets slick with rain, over a low fence into a courtyard, through the back door of a building that smelled of cabbage and cheap soap. No sirens. No uniforms. Just the whisper of boots and the knowledge that somebody was ticking boxes in a control room.

Hours later, in a safehouse three tram stops away, Lillian dumped the contents of the briefcase onto a table.

Typed pages. Diagrams. A Polaroid of a NATO nuclear facility, its perimeter fences neat as graph paper. On the back, in red ink: OPERATION BLINDFALL. CONTINGENCY RELEASE. GENEVA, 7 MARCH.

Miles stood over her shoulder, fur still damp. “That’s not targeting,” he said. “That’s escalation protocol. Somebody’s building a legal path to launch.”

She traced a finger along the red circle around one phrase: AUTHORISED ESCALATION.

“Peace was never the point,” she said.

“It’s leverage,” he said. “Albatross was never just a spy game. It’s a set of keys. Whoever holds them can convince both sides to jump.”

She reached for the phone on the wall. He caught her wrist.

“You call London, it vanishes,” he said. “You call Langley, it vanishes faster. This goes to people who can’t bury it.”

“The press,” she said. “Or someone who still remembers what conscience feels like.”

“You know any of those?” he asked.

She thought of students she’d never taught, lives she’d never had. Then she thought of the outpost under the train station, of Evelyn back in some other version of her life giving speeches about care. Of bodies in alleys that never made the news.

“Maybe one,” she said.

They sat on a bench by the Danube at dawn, briefcase between them, city waking up around them like nothing had happened. A tram rattled past. A jogger ran by with headphones in, insulated from history.

“Once we do this,” Miles said, “we don’t go back. Not to files. Not to covers. Not to pretending we don’t know where the bodies are buried.”

“We were never going back,” she said. “We were just slow to admit it.”

They didn’t hold hands. That would have been sentimental, and sentiment got people killed.

They watched the river move, swollen with snowmelt and secrets. Somewhere behind them, two governments tried to decide whether losing two deniable assets was cheaper than admitting they’d nearly lit the fuse.

Sometimes being a spy meant keeping the story straight. Sometimes it meant tearing it out of the archive and nailing it to a door.

Lillian took a breath that tasted of cold water and cigarette ash.

“Ready?” she asked.

Miles nodded once.

They stood, picked up the case, and walked towards a café where a journalist who owed nobody anything nursed his first coffee of the day.

Whatever came after would not be tidy. The systems they’d just kicked would not fall over politely.

But the thing about weapons, Lillian thought, was that if you knew their shape well enough, you could sometimes choose which way they pointed.
