# HARD FEMME

*A Lillian Edgecombe Mystery*

## ACT II: THE BODY AND THE BLADE

Dawn came to the city like a reluctant witness, pale and hesitant, painting the sky in shades of ash and old blood. Lillian stood at the window of her apartment above the club, watching the sun struggle through the smog and smoke that perpetually shrouded the urban landscape. She'd changed out of her performance dress into something more practical—black slacks that hugged her curves like a second skin, a charcoal blazer that concealed more weapons than a small army, and boots with heels sharp enough to puncture a man's dreams.

Miles Ferguson sat at her kitchen table, looking like a man who'd spent the night wrestling with demons and losing. She'd cleaned the blood from his face, revealing features that were more interesting than handsome—a strong jaw offset by gentle eyes, a mouth that looked like it smiled more often than it frowned, and the kind of weathered hands that spoke of honest work in a dishonest world.

"Coffee?" she asked, though it wasn't really a question. In her experience, men facing murder charges needed caffeine the way drowning men needed air.

"Please." His voice was rough with exhaustion and fear, but there was something else there now—a spark of determination that hadn't been present the night before. Sleep, even the fitful kind he'd managed on her couch, had given him back some of his backbone.

Lillian poured two cups of coffee black enough to strip paint and strong enough to wake the dead. She'd learned to make it that way during her early days in the city, when weakness was a luxury she couldn't afford and sleep was a privilege she rarely enjoyed.

"Tell me about Victor Castellanos," she said, settling into the chair across from him. "And don't give me the newspaper version. I want the real story—the one they don't print because it's too ugly for decent folks to read over their morning eggs."

Miles wrapped his hands around the coffee cup like it was an anchor in a storm. "He was a bastard," he said simply. "The kind of man who'd foreclose on an orphanage and then charge the kids rent for sleeping in the street."

"That's a start. Keep going."

"He owned half the factories on the east side, and he ran them like prison camps. Twelve-hour shifts, no breaks, safety regulations that existed only on paper. Workers got hurt, they got fired. Workers complained, they got blacklisted. Workers tried to organize..." Miles shrugged. "They had accidents."

Lillian nodded. She'd heard similar stories about Castellanos, whispered in the dark corners of the city where desperate people gathered to share their misery. "So why did he call you? A man like that doesn't usually associate with honest mechanics."

"That's just it—he didn't call me. At least, not directly." Miles took a sip of coffee and winced at the strength. "I got a call yesterday afternoon. Woman's voice, said she was Castellanos's secretary. Said his car was making strange noises and he needed it looked at right away. Offered to pay double my usual rate if I could come to his office after hours."

"And you went."

"I went." The words carried the weight of regret heavy enough to sink ships. "Money's been tight lately. My sister... she's got medical bills, and the insurance company's been playing games with the payments. I needed the work."

Lillian filed that information away for later examination. In her experience, family was always a complication, and complications had a way of turning simple cases into complex nightmares. "What happened when you got there?"

"The building was mostly empty. Just a security guard at the front desk, and he was half-asleep. Took the elevator to the fifteenth floor, found Castellanos's office. The door was unlocked."

"And?"

Miles closed his eyes, and Lillian could see him reliving the moment in vivid, unwanted detail. "He was sitting at his desk, but he wasn't sitting straight. His head was... wrong. Tilted back at an angle that heads don't tilt. There was blood everywhere—on the desk, on the walls, pooled on the carpet like spilled paint."

"You touch anything?"

"I... yeah. I checked for a pulse, even though I could see he was dead. Got blood on my hands, my clothes. Then I heard footsteps in the hallway and panicked. Tried to clean up, but that just made it worse. By the time I realized how bad it looked, the footsteps were getting closer, so I went out the window."

"Fifteen floors up?"

"Fire escape. Castellanos's office had access to the emergency stairs. I climbed down, but I cut myself on some broken glass along the way. That's where the blood on my face came from."

Lillian studied him over the rim of her coffee cup. The story had the ring of truth, but truth was a slippery thing in her line of work. "Anyone see you leave?"

"I don't think so. It was dark, and I stayed in the shadows. But..." He hesitated, then forged ahead. "There was a car parked across the street. Black sedan with tinted windows. It was there when I arrived, and it was still there when I left."

"You get a license plate?"

"No. I was too scared to think straight."

Lillian stood and moved to the window, her mind already working through the possibilities. A setup this elaborate required planning, resources, and connections. Someone had wanted Castellanos dead and Miles Ferguson blamed, which meant someone knew enough about both men to orchestrate the perfect frame.

"We need to see the crime scene," she said, turning back to face him. "And we need to do it before the cops finish processing it and seal it up tighter than a banker's heart."

Miles looked at her like she'd suggested they take a stroll through hell during rush hour. "Are you insane? The place will be crawling with police."

"Sugar, I've been called a lot of things in my time, but insane ain't usually one of them." Lillian's smile was sharp enough to cut glass. "Besides, the cops finished their initial sweep last night. They'll be back this morning, but right now, there's a window of opportunity, and I intend to crawl through it."

She moved to her bedroom and returned with a hat that looked like it had been designed by someone who understood that fashion could be a weapon. The brim was tilted at an angle that cast shadows across her face, and the black felt seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it.

"What about me?" Miles asked. "I can't exactly walk around in broad daylight. Half the city's looking for me by now."

Lillian opened a closet that contained more disguises than a theater company's wardrobe department. She selected a pair of coveralls, a baseball cap, and a pair of glasses with thick frames. "Put these on. You're going to be my assistant—just another working stiff trying to make an honest living in a dishonest world."

Twenty minutes later, they were walking through the city's financial district, two more shadows in a landscape built from concrete and broken dreams. The morning rush was in full swing, and the sidewalks teemed with people who had learned not to see too much or ask too many questions.

Castellanos's building was a monument to capitalism's excesses—thirty stories of steel and glass that scraped the sky like a middle finger extended toward heaven. Police tape fluttered around the entrance like yellow prayer flags, and a single uniformed officer stood guard with the enthusiasm of a man counting down the minutes until his shift ended.

Lillian approached the guard with the confidence of someone who belonged exactly where she was. "Morning, Officer Martinez," she said, reading his name tag with the casual ease of someone who made it her business to know things. "Terrible business about Mr. Castellanos."

Martinez looked her up and down, taking in the expensive clothes and the air of authority that surrounded her like expensive perfume. "You are?"

"Lillian Edgecombe, private investigator." She produced a license that looked official enough to fool anyone who didn't examine it too closely. "The insurance company hired me to assess the scene for their claim investigation."

It was a lie wrapped in enough truth to make it believable. Insurance companies did hire private investigators, and they did care about crime scenes that might affect their payouts. The fact that no insurance company had actually hired her was a detail that didn't need to be shared.

Martinez glanced at the license, then at Miles, who was doing his best impression of someone who belonged in coveralls and carried a toolbox. "And him?"

"My assistant. We need to check the building's security systems, make sure there weren't any... irregularities that might affect the claim."

The officer hesitated, clearly torn between following procedure and avoiding the paperwork that would come with making waves. In the end, laziness won out over diligence, as it usually did. "Make it quick," he said, stepping aside. "Detectives will be back within the hour."

They took the elevator to the fifteenth floor in silence, the only sound the mechanical hum of cables and gears. When the doors opened, they stepped into a hallway that smelled of industrial cleaner and lingering death.

Castellanos's office was at the end of the corridor, behind a door marked with his name in gold letters that had already begun to tarnish. The police tape across the entrance was more suggestion than barrier—a polite request to stay out rather than a serious attempt to keep people away.

Lillian produced a set of lock picks from her purse and had the door open in less time than it would have taken to find the right key. "After you, sugar," she said to Miles, gesturing him inside.

The office was a testament to wealth and power, decorated in shades of mahogany and leather that spoke of old money and older sins. Floor-to-ceiling windows offered a view of the city that stretched to the horizon, while the walls were lined with photographs of Castellanos shaking hands with politicians, judges, and other members of the city's power elite.

The desk where he'd died was a massive thing, carved from a single piece of wood that had probably been a tree when the city was still a collection of mud huts and broken dreams. The blood had been cleaned up, but the stain remained—a dark shadow on the expensive wood that no amount of polish would ever completely erase.

"Show me exactly what you saw," Lillian instructed, her voice dropping to a whisper that seemed appropriate for the scene of a violent death.

Miles moved to the desk with reluctant steps, his face pale as old bone. "He was sitting in his chair, but slumped back. His head..." He gestured vaguely. "The bullet went in through his forehead, came out the back. There was blood on the wall behind him, brain matter on the window."

Lillian examined the scene with the methodical precision of a scientist studying a particularly interesting specimen. The angle of the blood spatter, the position of the chair, the way the light fell through the windows—every detail told part of the story, and she was fluent in the language of violence.

"He was shot from the front," she said, more to herself than to Miles. "Close range, probably three to four feet. The killer was standing right about..." She moved to a spot in front of the desk. "Here."

"How can you tell?"

"Blood doesn't lie, sugar. It tells the truth even when everything else is a fabrication." She knelt beside the desk, examining the carpet with the intensity of a jeweler appraising diamonds. "Your fingerprints were on the desk?"

"Yeah. I touched it when I checked for a pulse."

"Anywhere else?"

"The door handle, maybe the chair. I was trying to see if he was really dead."

Lillian nodded, filing the information away. She moved around the office like a predator stalking prey, examining everything with eyes that missed nothing and forgot less. The safe in the corner was closed but not locked. The filing cabinets showed signs of having been searched, but carefully, by someone who knew what they were looking for.

"This wasn't a robbery," she said finally. "And it wasn't a crime of passion. This was an execution, pure and simple. Someone wanted Castellanos dead, and they wanted it to look like you did it."

"But why me? I barely knew the man."

"That's exactly why." Lillian moved to the window and looked out at the city sprawling below them like a cancer made of concrete and steel. "You were perfect—a nobody with no connections, no alibi, and no resources to fight back. The ideal patsy."

She turned back to face him, and her expression was grim as a funeral director's smile. "The question is, who benefits from Castellanos being dead and you taking the blame?"

Before Miles could answer, the sound of voices echoed from the hallway—multiple voices, getting closer with each passing second. Lillian's head snapped toward the door, her body tensing like a cat preparing to spring.

"Cops," she whispered. "Earlier than expected."

They moved toward the window with the fluid coordination of dancers who had rehearsed this routine a thousand times. The fire escape was exactly where Miles had said it would be, a metal skeleton clinging to the building's exterior like a parasite made of rust and determination.

They climbed down three flights before the voices from Castellanos's office became audible through the windows. Detective Kowalski's gravelly tones mixed with the higher pitch of his partner, Detective Chen, a woman who had learned to be twice as tough as her male colleagues just to be taken half as seriously.

"...fingerprints match the ones we found on the desk," Kowalski was saying. "Miles Ferguson, thirty-four, mechanic, no priors except a drunk and disorderly from five years ago."

"Too clean," Chen replied. "Guy with no criminal history doesn't usually graduate straight to murder."

"Maybe he had help. Or maybe Castellanos pushed him too far. Rich bastard had a talent for making enemies."

Lillian and Miles reached the alley behind the building without being spotted, emerging into a world of dumpsters and broken dreams. The morning sun had given up its struggle against the smog, and the sky had settled into the perpetual gray that served as the city's natural color scheme.

"We need to find out who made that phone call," Lillian said as they walked away from the building with the casual pace of people who had every right to be exactly where they were. "The woman who claimed to be Castellanos's secretary."

"How do we do that?"

"We start by talking to his real secretary. And then we work our way through everyone who had access to his schedule, his office, and his enemies." Lillian's smile was sharp as a blade and twice as dangerous. "In my experience, murder is like a stone thrown into still water—it creates ripples, and if you know how to read them, they'll lead you right back to the source."

They spent the rest of the morning moving through the city's underworld like sharks through bloody water. Lillian had contacts in places that didn't appear on any map—informants who traded information for favors, sources who dealt in secrets the way other people dealt in commodities.

The picture that emerged was complex and contradictory, painted in shades of gray that would have made a philosopher weep. Castellanos had been involved in everything from labor disputes to land deals, from political corruption to industrial espionage. He'd had more enemies than a politician during election season, and any one of them might have wanted him dead.

But as they dug deeper, a pattern began to emerge. Three weeks before his death, Castellanos had been asking questions about a series of factory accidents that had occurred over the past year. Workers had been injured, equipment had been sabotaged, and production had been disrupted at facilities across the city.

"He was investigating his own factories?" Miles asked as they sat in a diner that served coffee strong enough to dissolve steel and food that had given up any pretense of being edible.

"Looks that way." Lillian stirred sugar into her coffee with the mechanical precision of someone whose mind was elsewhere. "Question is, what did he find?"

The answer came from an unexpected source—a waitress named Dolores who had worked at the diner for twenty years and had the kind of ears that picked up conversations the way magnets picked up metal shavings.

"You talking about Victor Castellanos?" she asked, refilling their coffee cups without being asked. "Man came in here about two weeks ago, looking like he'd seen a ghost. Sat right where you're sitting, matter of fact."

Lillian looked up with the intensity of a hawk spotting prey. "He say anything interesting?"

"Said he'd been played for a fool. Said someone had been using his factories for their own purposes, and when he tried to stop it, they decided he was more trouble than he was worth." Dolores glanced around the diner, making sure no one else was listening. "Said he had proof, but he didn't know who he could trust."

"What kind of proof?"

"Documents. Photos. Said he'd hidden them somewhere safe, somewhere no one would think to look." The waitress leaned closer, her voice dropping to a whisper. "Said if anything happened to him, the truth would come out anyway."

Lillian and Miles exchanged glances across the table. If Castellanos had hidden evidence of whatever conspiracy had gotten him killed, finding it might be the key to clearing Miles's name and identifying the real killer.

"Any idea where he might have hidden it?" Lillian asked.

Dolores shook her head. "Man was paranoid as a cat in a dog pound. Could be anywhere."

They left the diner with more questions than answers, but at least now they had a direction. Somewhere in the city, Victor Castellanos had hidden evidence that could expose his killers. The trick would be finding it before the killers found them.

As they walked through streets that seemed to grow darker with each passing hour, despite the sun's position in the sky, Lillian found herself studying Miles Ferguson with new eyes. He moved through the urban landscape with the easy confidence of someone who belonged there, but there was something else—a watchfulness, a awareness of his surroundings that spoke of experience with danger.

"You're not just a mechanic," she said as they paused at a street corner, waiting for the light to change.

Miles looked at her with surprise that seemed genuine enough. "What do you mean?"

"The way you move, the way you watch the shadows, the way you knew exactly where the fire escape was in Castellanos's building." Lillian's eyes were sharp as surgical instruments. "You've done this before."

For a moment, Miles looked like he was going to deny it. Then his shoulders sagged, and some of the tension went out of his posture. "Army," he said simply. "Two tours overseas, military police. I know how to watch my back and how to spot trouble before it spots me."

"Why didn't you mention this before?"

"Because in my experience, telling people you're ex-military just makes them ask questions I don't want to answer." His voice carried the weight of memories that were better left buried. "I came back from the war wanting to fix things instead of breaking them. Cars don't shoot back, and they don't have families that mourn them when they don't come home."

Lillian nodded, understanding more than he probably realized. Everyone in the city carried scars, some visible and some hidden beneath layers of carefully constructed normalcy. The trick was learning which scars made people dangerous and which ones just made them human.

They spent the afternoon following leads that led nowhere and asking questions that generated more questions. By evening, they were no closer to finding Castellanos's hidden evidence, but they had managed to compile a list of people who might have wanted him dead that read like a who's who of the city's criminal elite.

As the sun set behind a wall of smog and broken promises, they found themselves back at the Crooked Halo. The club wouldn't open for another hour, but Lillian had keys and the kind of authority that came from owning a piece of the city's soul.

The empty club felt different in the daylight—smaller somehow, less mysterious, like a magician's trick examined too closely. The stage where Lillian had been performing when Miles burst through the door looked ordinary now, just wood and metal and the accumulated dreams of people who had learned to find beauty in dark places.

"We're missing something," Lillian said as she moved behind the bar and poured herself a whiskey that was older than some countries and smoother than a politician's lies. "Something obvious."

Miles sat at the bar, his reflection multiplied in the mirrors that lined the wall. "Maybe we're overthinking it. Maybe Castellanos was killed by someone he screwed over in business, and I just happened to be in the wrong place at the wrong time."

"No." Lillian's voice was firm as granite and twice as unyielding. "This was planned too carefully, executed too precisely. Someone wanted you specifically to take the fall, which means they know you. Or they know someone who knows you."

She paused, the whiskey glass halfway to her lips, as a thought struck her like lightning in a clear sky. "Your sister. The one with the medical bills. What's her name?"

"Sarah. Sarah Ferguson. Why?"

"What kind of medical bills?"

Miles's face went pale as old bone. "Cancer. Ovarian cancer. She's been fighting it for two years, but the treatments are experimental, and the insurance company keeps finding reasons not to pay."

Lillian set down her glass with the careful precision of someone who had just realized they were standing in a minefield. "Where does she work?"

"She's a nurse. Works at Mercy General Hospital." Miles's voice was barely above a whisper. "Why are you asking about Sarah?"

Instead of answering, Lillian moved to the phone behind the bar and dialed a number from memory. The conversation that followed was brief and conducted in a code that would have been meaningless to anyone who wasn't fluent in the language of favors and debts.

When she hung up, her expression was grim as a funeral director's smile. "Mercy General Hospital gets most of its funding from a charitable foundation," she said. "A foundation that's controlled by a board of directors that includes some very interesting names."

"Such as?"

"Such as the woman who owns this building. The woman who's been trying to buy me out for the past five years. The woman who would benefit greatly from having me distracted by a murder investigation while she consolidates her control over the city's entertainment district."

Miles stared at her with the expression of a man who had just realized he was a pawn in a game he didn't understand. "You think she's behind this?"

"I think we're about to find out." Lillian reached under the bar and retrieved a gun that looked like it had been designed by someone who understood that sometimes the only way to solve a problem was to put a bullet in it. "But first, we need to have a conversation with your sister."

The drive to Mercy General Hospital took them through neighborhoods that grew progressively more desperate with each passing block. The hospital itself was a monument to good intentions and insufficient funding, a place where miracles happened daily despite the best efforts of bureaucracy and budget constraints.

Sarah Ferguson was working the night shift in the oncology ward, and when they found her, she was sitting at the nurses' station, filling out paperwork with the mechanical precision of someone who had learned to find meaning in routine.

She looked like Miles, but softer around the edges, as if life had worn her down gradually rather than hitting her with a sledgehammer. Her hair was the same brown as her brother's, but streaked with premature gray, and her eyes held the kind of exhaustion that came from fighting battles that couldn't be won, only delayed.

"Miles?" She looked up from her paperwork with surprise and concern. "What are you doing here? The news said you were wanted for—"

"I didn't kill anyone, Sarah." Miles's voice was firm but gentle, the tone of someone who had spent years protecting his sister from the world's sharper edges. "But someone wants people to think I did."

Sarah's eyes moved from Miles to Lillian, taking in the expensive clothes and the air of danger that surrounded her like expensive perfume. "And you are?"

"Someone who's trying to help your brother," Lillian said. "But I need to ask you some questions, and I need you to answer them honestly, even if the truth is ugly."

For the next twenty minutes, they sat in a break room that smelled of disinfectant and broken dreams while Sarah told them about the phone calls she'd been receiving. Calls from people who claimed they could help with her medical bills, who said they had connections with the insurance company, who promised that all her problems would go away if she just provided them with some simple information.

Information about her brother. His schedule, his clients, his habits. Innocent questions that seemed harmless when asked individually but formed a dangerous pattern when assembled together.

"I thought they were trying to help," Sarah said, tears streaming down her face like rain on a broken window. "They said if I cooperated, they'd make sure my treatments were covered. I never thought... I never imagined..."

Lillian reached across the table and took Sarah's hand in hers. The gesture was surprisingly gentle for someone who made her living in the city's darker corners. "You couldn't have known, sugar. These people are professionals. They know how to use love against us, how to turn our best instincts into weapons."

But even as she offered comfort, Lillian's mind was working through the implications. Someone had been planning this for months, gathering information, setting up the pieces, waiting for the perfect moment to spring their trap. The level of organization required suggested resources and connections that went far beyond simple criminal enterprise.

They left the hospital with more pieces of the puzzle, but the picture they formed was still incomplete. As they drove through streets that seemed to grow more dangerous with each passing hour, Lillian found herself thinking about the gun that had killed Castellanos.

Her gun. Stolen from her collection.

The realization hit her like a physical blow, and she had to pull over to the side of the road to catch her breath. She'd been so focused on helping Miles that she'd missed the most obvious clue of all.

"What is it?" Miles asked, concern evident in his voice.

"The murder weapon," Lillian said, her voice barely above a whisper. "It was stolen from my collection. Which means whoever killed Castellanos has been in my apartment, in my private space. They've been watching me, studying me, learning my habits and my weaknesses."

She turned to look at him, and her eyes were hard as diamonds and twice as sharp. "This isn't just about framing you for murder, sugar. This is about destroying me. You're just the weapon they're using to do it."

The pieces of the puzzle were finally starting to come together, forming a picture that was uglier than either of them had imagined. Someone had orchestrated an elaborate conspiracy designed to destroy both their lives, and they'd been dancing to that person's tune from the very beginning.

But Lillian Edgecombe hadn't survived fifteen years in the city's underworld by being easy to destroy. She'd built her reputation on being harder to kill than a cockroach and twice as persistent. If someone wanted to play games with her life, she was more than happy to show them how the game was really played.

## ACT III: VELVET AND STEEL

The storm that had been threatening all day finally broke as they drove back toward the city center, rain hammering against the windshield like bullets from heaven's own gun. Lightning split the sky in jagged white scars, and thunder rolled through the streets like the laughter of angry gods.

Lillian drove through the deluge with the steady confidence of someone who had navigated worse storms than this one. Her hands were steady on the wheel, but Miles could see the tension in the set of her shoulders, the way her jaw clenched when she thought he wasn't looking.

"So what's the plan?" he asked as they pulled into the alley behind the Crooked Halo. The club's neon sign reflected in the puddles like spilled blood, and the rain turned everything into an impressionist painting of violence and desire.

"The plan," Lillian said, turning off the engine and listening to the rain drum against the roof, "is to give our mysterious puppet master exactly what she wants."

"Which is?"

"A confrontation. A showdown. A chance to finish what she started." Lillian's smile was sharp as a razor and twice as dangerous. "The only difference is, this time, we're going to be the ones pulling the strings."

They made their way through the rain to the club's back entrance, water streaming from their clothes like they were refugees from a flood. Inside, the Crooked Halo was preparing for another night of sin and secrets. The staff moved through their pre-opening routines with the mechanical precision of people who had learned to find rhythm in chaos.

Lillian led Miles to her office, a sanctuary behind the stage that was decorated in shades of black and silver with accents of deep red. The walls were lined with photographs—not the usual celebrity shots that adorned most nightclub offices, but pictures of the city's power brokers, politicians, and crime bosses. Each photo was signed, and each signature represented a debt, a favor, or a secret that could be called in when needed.

"Impressive collection," Miles said, studying a photograph of Lillian shaking hands with the mayor at some charity function. "You know all these people?"

"Know them, own them, or have enough dirt on them to bury them six feet under." Lillian moved to her desk and began pulling items from the drawers—documents, photographs, and what looked like recording equipment. "In this city, information is currency, and I've been saving up for a rainy day."

She spread the materials across her desk like a general planning a battle. "Victoria Blackwood," she said, tapping a photograph of a woman who looked like she'd been carved from ice and ambition. "Owns half the real estate in the entertainment district, including this building. She's been trying to buy me out for years, but I've always refused to sell."

Miles studied the photograph. Victoria Blackwood was beautiful in the way that expensive things were beautiful—perfect, cold, and ultimately hollow. Her blonde hair was pulled back in a style that suggested military precision, and her eyes held the kind of calculation that could reduce human lives to numbers on a balance sheet.

"She's behind this?"

"She's behind everything." Lillian pulled out another photograph, this one showing Blackwood at a charity gala surrounded by the city's elite. "She sits on the board of the foundation that funds Mercy General Hospital. She has connections to the insurance companies that have been jerking your sister around. And she has the resources to orchestrate something this elaborate."

"But why frame me specifically?"

"Because you're perfect for the role. No criminal record, no connections, no resources to fight back. And your sister's medical situation gave them the perfect leverage to manipulate you into position." Lillian's voice was cold as winter rain. "They've been planning this for months, maybe longer. Gathering information, setting up the pieces, waiting for the right moment to spring their trap."

She moved to a wall safe hidden behind a painting of a woman who looked suspiciously like herself, but dressed in the fashion of a century past. The safe opened to reveal more weapons than a small army would need, along with stacks of cash and documents that would have made a federal prosecutor weep with joy.

"The murder weapon," Miles said, understanding dawning in his eyes. "Your gun. How did she get it?"

"That's the million-dollar question, sugar." Lillian selected a pistol from the safe, checking the chamber with practiced efficiency. "But I have a pretty good idea. Someone's been in my apartment, in my private space. Someone who knows my habits, my routines, my weaknesses."

She turned to face him, and her expression was grim as a funeral director's smile. "Someone I trusted."

The realization hit Miles like a physical blow. "Someone on your staff?"

"Someone close enough to have access, smart enough to plan this elaborate setup, and ruthless enough to use an innocent man as a weapon." Lillian's voice was barely above a whisper, but it carried the weight of betrayal heavy enough to sink ships. "Someone who's been playing a very long game."

As if summoned by their conversation, a knock came at the office door. Lillian's hand moved to her gun with fluid grace, but she didn't draw it. Not yet.

"Come in," she called, her voice steady as stone.

The door opened to reveal Whiskers, the scarred tomcat who tended bar at the Crooked Halo. He looked nervous, his usual confidence replaced by something that might have been fear or guilt or both.

"Boss," he said, his voice rough as sandpaper. "We got a problem. There's someone here to see you. Says it's about the Ferguson situation."

Lillian's eyes narrowed to slits. "Who?"

"Victoria Blackwood. She's waiting in the main club, and she ain't alone."

Miles felt his blood turn to ice water in his veins. "It's a trap."

"Of course it's a trap, sugar." Lillian's smile was sharp enough to cut glass and twice as dangerous. "The question is, who's walking into it?"

She moved to her desk and activated a recording system that looked like it had been designed by someone who understood that evidence was only useful if it could be preserved. "Whiskers, I want you to make sure this conversation gets broadcast through the club's sound system. Every word, every confession, every threat. Can you do that?"

The tomcat nodded, his scarred face grim with determination. "Already set it up, boss. Soon as you give the word, every speaker in the place will be live."

"Good man." Lillian checked her appearance in the mirror, adjusting her hat to the perfect angle and making sure her gun was concealed but accessible. "Miles, you stay here. This is between me and her."

"Like hell." Miles stood up, his jaw set with determination. "She used my sister to get to you, used me as a weapon to destroy your life. I'm not hiding in the shadows while you face her alone."

Lillian studied him for a long moment, taking in the set of his shoulders and the steel in his eyes. "You sure about this, sugar? Once we walk out that door, there's no going back. This ends tonight, one way or another."

"I'm sure."

She nodded, understanding passing between them like electricity through water. "Then let's go have a conversation with the devil herself."

They walked through the club's main room like gunfighters approaching high noon. The staff had made themselves scarce, disappearing into the shadows with the survival instincts of people who had learned to recognize danger when it walked through the door.

Victoria Blackwood sat at a table near the stage, surrounded by three men who looked like they'd been carved from granite and bad intentions. She was dressed in white—a color that should have suggested purity but instead made her look like a predator wearing the skin of an angel.

"Lillian," she said, her voice smooth as silk and twice as deadly. "How lovely to see you again. And Mr. Ferguson, I presume? You've been causing quite a stir."

Lillian approached the table with the fluid grace of a dancer and the controlled menace of a loaded gun. "Victoria. I'd say it's a pleasure, but we both know I don't lie unless I'm getting paid for it."

"Always so direct. It's one of your more charming qualities." Victoria's smile was perfect and empty, like a mannequin's expression. "I suppose you're wondering why I'm here."

"Not really. I figure you're here to gloat, maybe tie up some loose ends, possibly put a bullet in my brain." Lillian settled into a chair across from Victoria with casual confidence. "The only question is whether you're planning to do it yourself or let your boys handle the dirty work."

Victoria laughed, and the sound was like crystal breaking in a church. "Oh, Lillian. You always did have such a flair for the dramatic. I'm not here to kill you. I'm here to make you an offer."

"I'm listening."

"Sell me the club. Sign over the deed, walk away with enough money to disappear somewhere warm and sunny, and Mr. Ferguson's little problem goes away. The evidence against him disappears, the charges are dropped, and everyone lives happily ever after."

Miles stepped forward, his hands clenched into fists. "You killed Castellanos. You framed me for murder. You used my sister's illness to manipulate me into position."

"I did what was necessary to achieve my goals," Victoria replied, her tone as casual as if she were discussing the weather. "Victor Castellanos was a liability. He'd discovered some... irregularities in the way his factories were being used, and he was threatening to expose operations that were far more valuable than his continued existence."

"What kind of operations?" Lillian asked, though she suspected she already knew the answer.

"The kind that generate enormous profits for people smart enough to see opportunities where others see only problems." Victoria's eyes glittered with the cold light of avarice. "Weapons manufacturing, drug processing, human trafficking. Castellanos's factories were perfect fronts, but the man himself had developed an inconvenient conscience."

Lillian felt a chill that had nothing to do with the storm raging outside. "So you had him killed and framed Miles to cover your tracks."

"I had him eliminated, yes. And Mr. Ferguson provided the perfect scapegoat—a nobody with no connections and no resources to fight back. It was elegant in its simplicity."

"And the gun? My gun?"

Victoria's smile widened, and for the first time, it reached her eyes. "That was a stroke of genius, if I do say so myself. Using your own weapon to commit the murder, then letting the police discover the connection. It implicated you in the crime while simultaneously destroying your credibility as a witness."

"How did you get it?"

"Oh, that was easy. I had someone on the inside, someone with access to your apartment, your routines, your private spaces." Victoria's voice dropped to a whisper that carried the weight of betrayal. "Someone you trusted."

Lillian's blood turned to ice water in her veins as the pieces of the puzzle finally clicked into place. She turned slowly, her eyes moving to Whiskers, who stood behind the bar with his hand resting on something hidden beneath the counter.

"Hello, boss," he said, his voice carrying the weight of years of deception. "Sorry it had to end this way."

The betrayal hit Lillian like a physical blow, but she didn't let it show on her face. She'd survived fifteen years in the city's underworld by learning to control her emotions, to turn pain into power and betrayal into fuel for revenge.

"How long?" she asked, her voice steady as stone.

"Three years," Whiskers replied. "Ever since Victoria made me an offer I couldn't refuse. My daughter needed surgery, and the insurance wouldn't cover it. Victoria paid the bills, and in return, I kept her informed about your activities."

"The recording system," Miles said, understanding dawning in his eyes. "You didn't set it up to help us. You set it up to help her."

Whiskers nodded, his scarred face grim with regret. "Every word you've spoken tonight has been transmitted directly to Victoria's people. They know everything you know, every plan you've made, every move you're planning."

Victoria stood up from her chair with fluid grace, her white dress rustling like the wings of a predatory bird. "It's over, Lillian. You've lost. Sign the papers, take the money, and walk away. It's the only way this ends without more bloodshed."

But Lillian Edgecombe hadn't survived fifteen years in the city's underworld by giving up when the odds were against her. She'd built her reputation on being harder to kill than a cockroach and twice as persistent. If Victoria Blackwood wanted to play games with her life, she was more than happy to show her how the game was really played.

"You know what your problem is, Victoria?" Lillian said, standing up with the fluid grace of a predator preparing to strike. "You're so smart, so clever, so sure you've thought of everything. But you made one crucial mistake."

"And what's that?"

Lillian's smile was sharp as a razor and twice as dangerous. "You assumed I trusted Whiskers with the real recording system."

Victoria's perfect composure cracked for the first time, confusion flickering across her features like shadows on water. "What?"

"Sugar, I've been running this establishment for fifteen years. You think I don't know when one of my employees is playing both sides of the fence?" Lillian's voice was honey over broken glass. "I've known about Whiskers's little arrangement with you for over a year. The recording system he set up? It's a decoy. The real system has been broadcasting every word of this conversation to a very different audience."

As if summoned by her words, the club's main doors burst open, and Detective Kowalski strode in with a team of officers behind him. His face was grim as a funeral director's smile, and his hand rested on his service weapon.

"Victoria Blackwood," he said, his voice carrying the weight of authority and justice. "You're under arrest for conspiracy to commit murder, accessory to murder, and about a dozen other charges that are going to keep you busy for the next twenty to life."

Victoria's face went white as her dress, and for the first time since Miles had met her, she looked genuinely surprised. "This is impossible. The recording system—"

"Was feeding you exactly what Lillian wanted you to hear," Kowalski said as his officers moved to surround Victoria and her men. "The real system has been broadcasting to police headquarters for the past hour. We've got your confession, your admission of guilt, and enough evidence to put you away until the sun burns out."

Victoria's men reached for their weapons, but they were outnumbered and outgunned. The brief firefight that followed was more sound and fury than actual violence, ending with all three of Victoria's bodyguards on the floor and the lady herself in handcuffs.

"How?" Victoria asked as Kowalski read her rights. "How did you know?"

Lillian moved to the piano and sat down at the bench, her fingers finding the keys with practiced ease. "Because, sugar, in this business, paranoia isn't a character flaw—it's a survival skill. I've had backup recording systems in place for years, and I've been feeding Whiskers false information for months, waiting to see what you'd do with it."

She began to play "Blue Velvet Gun," the same song she'd been performing when Miles first burst through the door. But this time, the melody was different—triumphant rather than sultry, victorious rather than seductive.

"As for Whiskers," she continued, her voice carrying over the music, "well, let's just say that loyalty is a two-way street. He chose his side, and now he gets to live with the consequences."

The scarred tomcat was already in handcuffs, his head hanging in shame and defeat. "I'm sorry, boss," he said as the officers led him away. "I never wanted it to go this far."

"I know, sugar," Lillian replied, her voice surprisingly gentle. "But wanting and doing are two different things, and in the end, we all have to answer for our choices."

As the police led Victoria and her accomplices away, Miles found himself standing alone with Lillian in the empty club. The storm outside was beginning to subside, and the first hints of dawn were starting to creep through the windows.

"It's over," he said, and the words carried the weight of relief heavy enough to sink ships.

"This part is," Lillian agreed, her fingers still moving across the piano keys. "But there's always another case, another mystery, another dance with danger. That's the nature of this business."

Miles moved to stand beside the piano, watching her hands create music from chaos. "What happens now?"

"Now? Now you go back to your life. Fix cars, take care of your sister, try to forget that any of this ever happened." Lillian's voice was soft as velvet and twice as dangerous. "The charges against you will be dropped, the evidence will disappear, and you'll be free to live whatever life you choose."

"And you?"

"I'll do what I always do. Survive. Adapt. Wait for the next storm to blow through." She looked up at him, and for a moment, her mask slipped, revealing something vulnerable beneath the armor of cynicism and control. "Unless, of course, you'd like to stick around and see what other kinds of trouble we can get into."

Miles studied her face in the dim light of the club, taking in the silver-streaked fur and the eyes that had seen too much and forgiven too little. She was dangerous and beautiful and completely unpredictable, the kind of woman who could save a man's soul or damn it to hell, depending on her mood and the phase of the moon.

"I might be interested," he said finally. "But I have one condition."

"Which is?"

"Next time someone bursts through that door bleeding and desperate, we call the cops first and ask questions later."

Lillian laughed, and the sound was like music in the empty club. "Sugar, if we did that, where would be the fun?"

Outside, the storm was finally breaking, and the first rays of sunlight were beginning to pierce the clouds. The city was waking up to a new day, and somewhere in its concrete and steel heart, justice had been served and truth had prevailed.

But in the Crooked Halo, where shadows danced with light and danger waltzed with desire, Lillian Edgecombe continued to play her piano, her voice weaving through the melody like smoke through silk.

*"Blue velvet gun, you're my favorite sin,
Blue velvet gun, let the games begin..."*

In this town, innocence was just guilt with better lighting. But sometimes, if you were very lucky and very careful, you could find something real in all the artifice. Something worth fighting for, worth bleeding for, worth risking everything to protect.

Miles Ferguson had come to the Crooked Halo as a desperate man running from a crime he didn't commit. He was leaving as something else entirely—a man who had found his place in a world that didn't make sense, standing beside a woman who had taught him that sometimes the most dangerous thing you could do was trust someone with your life.

And in the growing light of dawn, as the city stretched and yawned and prepared for another day of sin and secrets, that seemed like enough.

The music played on, and the dance continued, and somewhere in the distance, thunder rolled like the laughter of gods who had finally found something worth celebrating.

## ACT I: THE GIRL IN THE SMOKE

The spotlight carved through the cigarette haze like a blade through silk, and in its harsh white circle stood the most dangerous woman in the city. Lillian Edgecombe didn't just sing—she weaponized melody, turned rhythm into ammunition, made every note a promise she might or might not keep. The badger's voice wrapped around "Blue Velvet Gun" like smoke around a corpse, sultry and suffocating.

She was built like a fortress—five-foot-one of solid curves and harder edges, three hundred and twenty pounds of controlled power in a dress that cost more than most people made in a month. The fabric clung to her like a second skin, black as midnight with crimson accents that caught the light like fresh blood. Her fur was silver-streaked charcoal, and her eyes were the color of gunmetal in winter.

The Crooked Halo wasn't just a nightclub—it was a confession booth for the damned, a place where secrets went to die and sins went to breed. Men in fedoras whispered in corners, their faces half-hidden in shadow, while women watched Lillian like she was delivering a sermon from the mount. She was a legend in this city of broken dreams and bent morals, the badger who never smiled unless she meant it, and when she meant it, somebody usually ended up bleeding.

The piano keys beneath her fingers were ivory stained yellow with age and nicotine, each note she struck echoing off walls that had absorbed more confessions than any cathedral. The bass line thrummed through the floorboards, vibrating in the bones of everyone present, a heartbeat for the heartless.

*"Blue velvet gun, pressed against my spine,
Blue velvet gun, your trigger finger's mine..."*

Her voice was whiskey and smoke, honey and razor wire. It could seduce a saint or damn a sinner, depending on her mood and the price offered. Tonight, she was in a particularly dangerous frame of mind. The kind of mood that made smart men nervous and stupid men dead.

The audience hung on every word, every breath, every pause between verses. They knew they were witnessing something rare—not just a performance, but a ritual. Lillian Edgecombe didn't just entertain; she hypnotized, she controlled, she owned every soul in that room for the duration of her song.

The club's décor was pure noir poetry—black leather booths that had seen more deals than a Wall Street trading floor, chrome fixtures that reflected distorted faces like funhouse mirrors, and neon signs that painted everything in shades of electric blue and arterial red. The air was thick enough to chew, heavy with smoke, perfume, and the metallic taste of impending violence.

Behind the bar, glasses caught the light like crystal bullets, and the mirror reflected a world turned upside down, where right was wrong and wrong was profitable. The bartender, a scarred tomcat named Whiskers, polished glasses with the mechanical precision of a man who'd learned not to ask questions and not to remember answers.

*"Blue velvet gun, you're my favorite sin,
Blue velvet gun, let the games begin..."*

Lillian's fingers danced across the keys with the grace of a pickpocket and the precision of a surgeon. Each chord was calculated, each melody line designed to burrow into the listener's brain and set up permanent residence. She was a master of her craft, and her craft was manipulation set to music.

The song was building to its climax when the back door exploded inward like the gates of hell had been kicked off their hinges. The music died with a discordant crash as Lillian's hands froze on the keys, her head snapping toward the disturbance with predatory alertness.

A man stumbled through the doorway, and he brought chaos with him like a plague carrier brings disease. He was tall and lean, built like a scarecrow that had learned to walk, with grease-stained coveralls and hands that shook like autumn leaves in a hurricane. Blood painted abstract patterns across his face, and his eyes held the wild look of a rabbit that had just realized the fox was already in the henhouse.

"Please," he gasped, the word torn from his throat like a confession under torture. "I didn't... I couldn't... They're coming."

The club's patrons reacted like a flock of birds sensing a predator—sudden stillness, then explosive motion as chairs scraped back and hands moved toward concealed weapons. In a place like the Crooked Halo, unexpected visitors usually meant unexpected bullets, and nobody wanted to be caught in the crossfire.

But Lillian didn't move. She sat at her piano like a queen on her throne, studying the intruder with the cold calculation of a chess master examining a new piece on the board. Her eyes took in every detail—the way he favored his left leg, the defensive hunch of his shoulders, the particular shade of terror that painted his features.

"Miles Ferguson," she said, her voice cutting through the tension like a scalpel through skin. It wasn't a question. In her line of work, knowing names was as essential as knowing which end of a gun to hold.

The mechanic's head jerked up at the sound of his name, and for a moment, hope flickered in his eyes like a candle in a hurricane. "You... you know me?"

"I know everyone in this city worth knowing, sugar." Lillian's voice was honey over broken glass. "And some who ain't worth the powder to blow them to hell. Question is, what brings a grease monkey like you into my establishment, bleeding like a stuck pig and looking like death's accountant?"

Miles stumbled forward, his legs threatening to give out with each step. The blood on his face was fresh, still wet enough to catch the neon light and throw it back in crimson reflections. "They're saying I killed him. Victor Castellanos. They're saying I put a bullet in his brain and left him to rot in his own office."

A murmur rippled through the crowd like wind through wheat. Victor Castellanos was a name that carried weight in this city—the kind of weight that could crush a man's bones or buy his soul, depending on the circumstances. He was an industrialist with fingers in more pies than a baker's convention, and enemies in more places than a politician's conscience.

"And did you?" Lillian asked, her tone conversational, as if she were asking about the weather or the price of gin.

"No!" The word exploded from Miles like a gunshot. "I never... I wouldn't... I fix cars, lady. I don't fix people. Permanently or otherwise."

Lillian studied him with the intensity of a scientist examining a particularly interesting specimen. There was something in his eyes—not just fear, though there was plenty of that. Not just desperation, though he had that in spades. There was truth, raw and unfiltered, the kind of truth that was rarer than honest politicians and twice as valuable.

The sound of sirens began to wail in the distance, growing closer with each passing second. The mechanical howl cut through the night like the cry of electronic wolves, and everyone in the club knew what it meant. The law was coming, and in a place like the Crooked Halo, the law was about as welcome as a plague of locusts.

"Cops," someone whispered, and the word spread through the crowd like wildfire through dry grass.

Miles's face went white beneath the blood, and his knees finally gave out. He collapsed onto the stage, his hands pressed against the black wood like a penitent at an altar. "They'll kill me," he whispered. "If they don't arrest me, they'll kill me. And if they do arrest me, I'll die in a cell before I ever see a courtroom."

Lillian looked at him for a long moment, her expression unreadable as a poker player's bluff. The sirens were getting closer, and soon the club would be crawling with badges and bullets. She had a choice to make, and in her experience, the choices that mattered most were the ones that had to be made in seconds rather than hours.

She stood up from the piano bench with fluid grace, her dress rustling like silk over steel. Every eye in the club was on her, waiting to see which way the wind would blow. Lillian Edgecombe was a force of nature in this city, and when she moved, smart people got out of the way.

"Whiskers," she called to the bartender, her voice carrying the authority of a general commanding troops. "Kill the lights. All of them."

The tomcat's paw hit the switches, and the club plunged into darkness thick as velvet and twice as soft. Emergency lighting kicked in a moment later, bathing everything in a red glow that made the whole place look like the inside of a beating heart.

Lillian moved through the darkness like she owned it, which in many ways, she did. Her heels clicked against the floor with metronomic precision, each step calculated and purposeful. She reached Miles in three strides and hauled him to his feet with surprising strength.

"This way, sugar," she murmured, her voice barely above a whisper. "And try not to bleed on my carpet. It's imported."

She led him through a door marked 'Private' in faded gold letters, down a hallway that smelled of old perfume and older secrets. Her dressing room was at the end of the corridor, a sanctuary decorated in shades of black and silver with accents of deep red. Mirrors lined the walls, reflecting infinite versions of themselves in the dim light.

"Why?" Miles asked as she pushed him into a chair that had seen better decades. "Why are you helping me?"

Lillian moved to her vanity and began removing her jewelry with practiced efficiency. Each piece was worth more than most people's cars, but she handled them like they were costume trinkets. "Maybe I like lost causes," she said without looking at him. "Maybe I'm feeling charitable. Or maybe I just don't like being told what to think by men with badges and small minds."

She caught his reflection in the mirror, and for a moment, their eyes met in the silvered glass. There was something there, a connection that neither of them fully understood. It was dangerous and electric, the kind of spark that could light a fire or blow up a building, depending on how it was handled.

"Besides," she added, turning to face him directly, "in my experience, when the cops are that eager to pin a murder on someone, it usually means they're trying real hard not to look at who actually pulled the trigger."

The sound of heavy boots echoed from the main club, accompanied by gruff voices and the metallic click of weapons being readied. The law had arrived, and they were in no mood for subtlety.

Lillian moved to a panel in the wall that looked like part of the decoration but opened at her touch to reveal a hidden compartment. Inside were things that would have made a police evidence locker jealous—guns, ammunition, documents, and enough cash to buy a small country or a large politician.

She selected a small pistol, chrome-plated and deadly as a viper's kiss, and checked the chamber with the casual expertise of someone who knew her way around firearms. "Stay quiet, stay still, and try not to do anything stupid," she instructed Miles. "I'm going to go have a conversation with our uninvited guests."

"What if they search back here?"

Lillian smiled, and it was the kind of smile that made smart men nervous and stupid men dead. "Sugar, I've been running this establishment for fifteen years. If I couldn't hide a body when I needed to, I'd have been out of business a long time ago."

She moved toward the door, then paused and looked back at him. "Miles Ferguson," she said, tasting his name like fine wine. "You better be worth the trouble you're about to cause me."

Then she was gone, leaving him alone in the red-lit sanctuary with nothing but his fear and the distant sound of voices raised in anger. Through the thin walls, he could hear Lillian's voice, smooth as silk and twice as strong, weaving lies and half-truths with the skill of a master craftsman.

Miles pressed his back against the chair and tried to make himself invisible. His hands were still shaking, and the blood on his face had dried to a rusty brown that made him look like a casualty of war. Which, in many ways, he was.

Outside, in the main club, Lillian faced down three detectives with the casual confidence of a woman who had stared down worse things than badges and lived to tell about it. The lead detective was a bulldog of a man named Kowalski, with a face like a clenched fist and eyes that had seen too much and forgiven too little.

"Evening, Lillian," he said, his voice carrying the weight of old grudges and older debts. "Nice show tonight. Real moving."

"Detective Kowalski," she replied, her tone as neutral as Switzerland and twice as dangerous. "What brings you to my establishment at this ungodly hour? Surely not the music."

"We're looking for someone. Miles Ferguson. Mechanic. About six-two, brown hair, probably bleeding from a recent disagreement with a window. You seen him?"

Lillian's expression didn't change by so much as a twitch. "Can't say that I have, sugar. But then, I see a lot of faces in my line of work. They tend to blur together after a while."

Kowalski's eyes narrowed. He knew Lillian well enough to know when she was lying, but he also knew her well enough to know that proving it would be harder than nailing smoke to a wall. "Mind if we take a look around? Just to be thorough."

"I mind plenty," Lillian said, her voice dropping to a temperature that could freeze hell. "But I suppose you'll do it anyway, warrant or no warrant. Just try not to break anything expensive. My insurance doesn't cover police brutality."

The detectives spread out through the club like a plague of locusts, turning over tables, checking behind the bar, and generally making themselves as unwelcome as possible. Lillian watched them with the patience of a spider waiting for flies, her expression never changing, her hands never moving toward the gun she carried.

They searched for twenty minutes, finding nothing more incriminating than watered whiskey and marked cards. When they finally gave up and headed for the door, Kowalski paused and looked back at Lillian with something that might have been respect if it hadn't been wrapped in so much suspicion.

"This isn't over, Lillian," he said. "Ferguson's going to surface eventually, and when he does, we'll be waiting."

"I'm sure you will be, Detective," she replied. "You always were persistent. It's one of your more annoying qualities."

After they left, Lillian waited another ten minutes before returning to her dressing room. She found Miles exactly where she'd left him, looking like a man who'd just watched his own funeral and wasn't sure if he was supposed to applaud.

"They're gone," she said, settling into the chair across from him with fluid grace. "For now."

"Thank you," he said, and the words carried more weight than a freight train. "I don't know why you helped me, but thank you."

Lillian studied him in the red-tinted light, taking in the details she'd missed in the chaos of his arrival. He was younger than she'd first thought, maybe thirty-five, with the kind of lean build that came from honest work rather than gymnasium vanity. His hands were calloused and stained with motor oil, the hands of a man who made his living fixing things that were broken.

There was something about him that reminded her of herself at that age—lost, desperate, and running from something that couldn't be outrun. It was a dangerous kind of recognition, the sort that made smart women do stupid things and careful women take foolish risks.

"Don't thank me yet, sugar," she said, her voice soft as velvet and twice as dangerous. "We haven't even started dancing, and already the music's getting complicated."

She leaned forward, her eyes locked on his with predatory intensity. "Now, why don't you tell me exactly what happened tonight? And don't leave out any of the interesting parts. In my experience, the devil's always in the details, and right now, we need all the devils we can get."

Miles took a shaky breath and began to talk, his words tumbling out like water through a broken dam. He told her about the phone call that had brought him to Castellanos's office, about finding the industrialist's body sprawled across his desk like a broken marionette, about the way the blood had pooled in the expensive carpet like spilled wine.

As he spoke, Lillian listened with the intensity of a confessor, filing away every detail, every inconsistency, every moment where his story didn't quite add up. She was a student of human nature, and she knew that everyone lied—the only question was what they were lying about and why.

But as Miles Ferguson told his story in the red-lit sanctuary of her dressing room, with the taste of danger still thick in the air and the sound of sirens fading into the distance, Lillian found herself believing him. Not because his story was perfect—perfect stories were always lies—but because it was flawed in all the right ways.

And that, she knew, was going to make everything infinitely more complicated.

The night was young, and already it had brought her a mystery wrapped in blood and tied with a bow of desperation. In her experience, that was usually just the beginning. The real fun wouldn't start until someone else turned up dead.

She smiled, and it was the kind of smile that would have made angels weep and devils applaud. Lillian Edgecombe was back in business, and business was about to get very, very interesting.
