Scene 1: The Field of Ash

The barrage had ended, but the earth had not yet stopped screaming.
Miles lay twisted among the dead—some with their mouths open, some with no mouths at all. A fine grey powder coated everything: flesh, bone, uniform, metal. The battlefield was a canvas of ruin, cratered and smoking, trees blackened to charred ribs. Somewhere distant, a shell thudded like a god turning in his sleep, followed by the wet slap of debris falling back to earth.
He was alive. He wasn't sure why.
His leg was mangled, flesh shredded down to exposed muscle, and his greatcoat had fused with the wound in places. The metallic tang of blood mixed with the acrid bite of cordite in his nostrils. Flies were already finding him, their buzzing a constant drone against his ears. He tried to move, but the pain came like fire in reverse—cold and deep and hollowing. His paw curled weakly, fingers crusted with dirt and blood that had dried to a rust-brown crust. He wanted water. He wanted his mother. He wanted to die, maybe. It would be easier.
The crows came first. They circled with the patience of saints, black smudges against a sulphur-yellow sky. One landed on the breastplate of a soldier beside him—a young otter with a bullet-hole between the eyes—and pecked without malice. The sound was soft, wet, deliberate.
Miles watched. He couldn't close his eyes anymore. Couldn't look away.
Then: a rustle. Not of wings. Not of flame.
Boots. Steady. Cautious. The squelch of mud beneath leather soles.
He barely managed to tilt his head. The world blurred and rang, his ears still echoing with phantom artillery. His vision narrowed to a slit of color through dust: grey-blue skirt, heavy apron, bandaged wrists. A woman. A dog, tall and broad-shouldered, young but not soft. Her fur was sandy, though ash dulled it to the color of old parchment. Her eyes were fixed on him. One paw clutched a medical satchel; the other, a knife that caught the weak sunlight.
German.
Their eyes met.
His mouth parted. No sound came. Just a raw gasp of breath that tasted of copper and smoke.
She didn't move for a long time. Not a twitch. The wind carried the stench of cordite and decay between them.
Enemy.
She had orders. She had duties. She had every right to let him rot.
And yet.
Slowly, she crouched beside him, face unreadable, the wind lifting the hem of her coat with a soft whisper. Her eyes swept over the wreck of his body, the exposed tendons, the ruined uniform. Her nose twitched once—scenting blood, infection, fear. Then she dropped the knife into the mud with a wet thud and reached into her satchel.
Her voice was low. Not gentle. Not cruel. Measured. Deciding.
"Still alive," she said—in French, imperfect but clear. "Merde."
Miles blinked once. The crows screamed above them, their cries sharp against the sulfurous air.
Then he passed out.


Scene 2: The Barn

The barn was barely standing.
Its spine sagged inward where fire had kissed the rafters. One side was collapsed entirely, replaced by a wall of crates, hay bales, and rusted tools scavenged into barriers. It smelled of iodine, rot, and old smoke—the acrid sweetness of burned wood mixing with the sharp chemical bite of antiseptic. The kind of place where wounded men came to linger, not to heal. Dust motes danced in shafts of pale light that leaked through gaps in the walls, and somewhere in the shadows, mice scurried through dry straw.
She dragged him in with both arms, panting from the effort, boots slipping in the slush that had formed where rain leaked through the damaged roof. Her uniform was stained at the hem and soaked through, the wool heavy with moisture and the metallic scent of blood. Her breath came in tight, short bursts—not from fear, but from urgency. The way one moves when they've made a choice they can't unmake.
She laid him on a straw pallet near the back, where the shadows were thickest. His weight had nearly broken her shoulders. He was bigger than her. He bled more than she had expected, the crimson seeping through the straw beneath him with a soft, persistent drip.
Her name was Liliane.
She lit a lantern, the glow trembling against wood and fur, casting dancing shadows that made the barn seem alive. The flame hissed softly, and the smell of burning oil joined the complex bouquet of decay and medicine. Then she opened her kit—scissors, rags, gauze, tinctures. Her paws, usually so steady, hesitated just for a moment above his leg. The wound was worse than she thought. Pus. Bone. Torn skin. The kind of injury the war liked to give—unfixable, unclean. The smell of infection was already beginning, sweet and nauseating.
"God forgive me," she whispered, and poured disinfectant into the open flesh.
Miles screamed.
Not a soldier's scream. Not dignified. A raw, breaking howl that came from somewhere deeper than language, echoing off the barn walls and sending the mice scattering. He flailed, tried to push her away with what little strength he had. His eyes flew open, wide and wild, and he shouted—panicked, slurred French.
"Non! Arrêtez—par pitié! Don't—je veux pas mourir—"
She pressed his shoulders down, gently but firmly, her paws warm against his fevered skin. "Shhh. You won't."
His breath came in ragged gasps. His chest rise and fall like a bellows, each exhale carrying the taste of fear and pain. His eyes—amber, fever-glazed—searched her face as if trying to decide what she was. Who she was. Why she wasn't letting him die.
"Pourquoi…?" he asked, voice cracked and wet. "Why?"
Liliane didn't answer.
She only dipped a cloth into warm water, wiped his face, then his chest, then his bloodied paws. The cloth came away stained dark. Her hands were surer now. Not soft. Trained. Not military.
Midwife's hands.
The lamp flickered, casting their shadows large and wavering against the barn walls. He went still. His body trembled with the aftermath of pain, but the fear in his gaze began to dim. Just a little.
She didn't smile. She didn't speak again.
Outside, the wind passed over the field like breath through teeth, whistling through the gaps in the barn walls. Inside the barn, two enemies shared silence, the first fragile shelter either had known in weeks.


Scene 3: The Silence Between Them

The days bled together.
Rain drummed on the sagging roof. Rats scurried behind crates. The front lines shifted, but the barn stayed still—a bubble of breath in the collapsing lung of Verdun.
Miles slipped in and out of fever. He muttered in his sleep, sometimes words, sometimes sounds. Once, he sobbed. Another time, he tried to sit up and barked an order to men long buried. Each time he thrashed, she was there. A cool cloth to the brow. A whispered shushing. A needle, sharp and swift, to bring him back down.
Liliane worked in silence.
She fed him broth from a tin cup, spoon by spoon. Cleaned his leg, changed the dressings. When he could no longer bear the pain, she slipped opium into his water. Her hands became more confident with each pass—lifting, turning, checking for rot. Midwife's hands. Soldier's discipline.
He began to watch her. Not with suspicion, but with the quiet curiosity of someone who'd been returned to the world and didn't know why.
She hummed while she worked. Lullabies, mostly. Minor keys, old and sad.
On the fourth day, he found his voice again. Weak, but real.
"What… day is it?"
She looked up from the bandages, surprised. "Thursday."
He nodded, then winced. "You always hum the same tune."
"I know," she said.
A pause. The wind outside rattled the wood slats.
"That notebook…" His voice rasped. "Are you a nurse?"
She shook her head. "Midwife. Studied in Freiburg. Then the war came."
He hesitated. Then: "It's beautiful. The drawings."
She glanced down at the small leather notebook in her lap—edges curled and blackened by ash, a few pages stuck together with blood. Inside: pencil sketches of wildflowers, anatomical diagrams, hastily scrawled German. Names. Lists. One page held only a pressed violet and a date: October 1914.
Her fingers curled around the edges as if afraid it might vanish.
"My brother was the artist," she said at last. "Before he died. In Belgium. First year of the war."
Miles blinked, mouth dry. "I'm sorry."
She nodded once. Then: "He looked like you."
He didn't know what to say. So he said nothing.
Outside, a shell cracked in the distance. Inside, she returned to her work. But her hands moved slower now, as if memory had made them heavy.
And for the first time, the silence between them wasn't hollow. It was shared.


Scene 4: Trust

The rain had turned to thunder.
The barn creaked with every roll of it, timbers aching like old bones. Outside, the forest steamed. Inside, Miles was trying to sit up.
His breath came hard, teeth gritted against the scream rising in his throat. One arm braced behind me, the other clenched into the straw. His leg—still bandaged, still weeping—dragged like dead weight. But he moved.
Liliane turned from the water basin. “Stop.”
“I have to get up.”
“You don’t.”
His brow furrowed beneath the matted fur, sweat darkening the lines of his face. “I can’t just lie here like a pig fattening for slaughter. I’m not—” He gasped as his hand slipped, and he crashed back into the pallet. “Damn it!”
“You’ll tear the stitches.”
“I don’t care.”
“You should.”
“I need to walk. I need to get back. My people think I’m dead.”
She crossed the space between them, her apron damp and stained, and knelt by his side. Her expression was unreadable, but her ears tilted low—alert, but tired.
“If you try to go now, you’ll die out there,” she said. “That’s not a metaphor. That’s the mud, the cold, the bullets. They don’t care what colors you wear.”
He looked at her, breath heaving.
“I have to try. I can’t stay here, hidden like a child. I’m a soldier.”
“No,” she said. “You’re a man with a ruined leg and a fever who pisses into a bucket. There is nothing noble in dying stupid.”
He flinched, more at the truth than the words. “You’re German. Why do you even care if I live or die?”
That struck something. She stood suddenly, fists clenched at her sides.
“I care because I chose to,” she snapped. “And if they find you here—if they find me with you—I’ll be shot for treason.”
The thunder broke again, louder this time. The rafters shuddered.
He looked up at her—breathing hard, mouth still open to argue—but something in him softened. The rage drained out. What was left beneath it was smaller. Bruised. Almost young.
“I didn’t ask for this,” he murmured. “I just wanted to fix bicycles. That’s all.”
Liliane’s shoulders dropped. The storm outside hissed against the stone.
“I wanted to be a rural doctor,” she replied. “Deliver babies. Tend gardens. Brew elderflower tea for the old men in my village.”
Their eyes met. For a moment, nothing moved.
Then, quietly, she knelt again and reached for his hand—not to scold, not to pull him back down, but to hold it. Steady. Present.
The storm went on. But the air between them had shifted.
Not peace. But something close.


Scene 5: Stories at Dusk

The light came slow that evening.
Thick clouds had drifted east, and in their wake the sky flushed pink and bruised. Through a hole in the barn wall, the sunset spilled like spilled wine over the straw. Dust floated in it, gold-edged. Quiet, fragile.
Liliane lit a stub of candle on a crate between them. The flame bent toward her as if listening.
Miles lay propped on his side, his leg stiff beneath the blanket. He watched her as she cleaned the last of the day’s tools—needles, cloth, a bent scalpel. Her movements were automatic now. Precise. There was something holy in the way she worked. Like she believed every act of care was a kind of defiance.
“Tell me something,” he said.
She glanced over. "What manner of thing?"
"Before this. Before uniforms and mud and screaming. What did you wish for?"
She paused. Her paw lingered on a stained cloth. Then: "I was studying medicine. In Freiburg. Rural work, mostly—small towns, poor villages. I preferred birth to death. Babies arrive pink and furious. One can still mend things, then."
She set down the cloth and looked toward the candle flame. "My mother died birthing my youngest brother. The midwife was... inexperienced. I was fourteen, watching from the doorway. I swore that day I would learn to do better." Her voice grew quieter. "I had nearly finished my training when the war began. Two more months, and I would have had my certification. Instead..." She gestured at the makeshift medical supplies around them.
"The professors said women had no place in proper medicine—that we lacked the constitution for surgery, the intellect for diagnosis. But when the men marched away, suddenly our 'delicate sensibilities' became quite useful." There was a bitter edge to her voice. "Funny how war changes what society deems proper."
He smiled faintly. "You speak as though you miss it."
She shrugged. "I remember it like a house I once dwelt in. Still there, but no longer mine. But perhaps... perhaps this is still medicine, in its way. Just under different circumstances."
Silence fell again, but it was a different kind—expectant.
She asked, "And yourself?"
Miles looked toward the rafters, exhaled through his nose.
"I mended bicycles. My father kept a shop in Rouen. Nothing grand. Just gears and grease. Folk came in with wheels falling off, and I'd send them out smooth as silk." He grinned. "Didn't pay much, but everyone knew me. Especially the children."
Liliane raised an eyebrow. "Did they pay you in sweets?"
"No," he said, mock-affronted. "But I did receive a few drawings. And a poem once, from a girl who wanted her chain oiled." He sighed. "She was ten. It rhymed. Barely."
Liliane laughed.
Not a chuckle. Not polite. A real one—sudden and bright, like something that had been trapped under ice. She covered her mouth too late. Her eyes crinkled at the corners.
Miles blinked, stunned. Then he laughed too, softer.
It felt foreign in his ribs. Like using a muscle that had nearly atrophied.
She wiped her eyes and stood to fetch water. He watched her—how her tail swayed when she wasn't thinking, how the candlelight gilded the side of her face in a way that made her look younger, softer.
For a moment, he forgot where he was. He forgot the wound in his leg, the distant rumble of guns, the smell of iodine and mildew.
There was only her. The candle. The sound of her paw against the ladle. The faintest trace of her humming again.
Just a woman and a man. A midwife and a mechanic. Caught in a world that hadn't completely burned yet.
The moment stretched between them like a held breath. Miles watched the candlelight dance across her face, memorizing the curve of her smile, the way her ears twitched when she was thinking. Outside, the evening settled into deeper quiet—no shells, no distant screaming. Just the whisper of wind through broken boards.
Then the world intruded.

Scene 6: The Patrol

The crunch of boots.
Liliane froze mid-step, the ladle still in her paw. The sound came from the east—branches snapping, low murmurs in clipped German. The barn’s candle flickered in the draft that followed.
Then: a knock.
Not gentle. Not asking.
She turned to Miles. He was already sitting up, pain flashing across his face.
“Go,” she whispered, already lifting the loose board behind the crates.
He dragged himself off the cot, half-crawled with her help. Beneath the straw was a pit—shallow, lined with old crates and folded canvas, barely large enough. She had dug it days ago, just in case. Just in fear.
He grunted as she helped him in. She covered him with hay, blankets, a scattering of broken planks. Only his eyes were visible before she tugged the last cloth into place.
“Breathe slow,” she mouthed.
The barn door opened with a groan.
Three soldiers entered. One bore the black-and-silver insignia of an Oberleutnant. Another held a lantern. The third stayed at the door, rifle ready.
"Fräulein," the officer drawled. He was a Dobermann—tall, lean, uniform too clean for the mud outside. His collar bore the twin silver pips of his rank, and the red medical corps armband marked him as someone with authority over field hospitals. "My apologies for the interruption. Routine inspection."
Liliane stood straight, apron tidy despite the smudges. She nodded once, cool and professional. "The wounded were moved east this morning. Only medical supplies remain."
"A pity," he said, pacing toward her. "I had hoped for some company. It is a lonely war."
She kept her face blank. "Would you care to inspect the inventory?"
He smirked. "Perhaps. But first—your name?"
"Liliane Becker. Midwife. Unit 42, Second Medical Company." She kept her voice steady, professional. The additional detail established her legitimate position within the military structure.
He stepped closer, lantern held near her face. She didn't flinch.
"You have kind eyes, Fräulein Becker." His gaze drifted lower. "A shame they are wasted on dying men."
Liliane held his stare. "With respect, Herr Oberleutnant, my supplies are limited. Do you require medical attention?"
The Dobermann's grin faltered. He knew she was within her rights to question his medical needs—field medics, even civilian auxiliaries, had certain protections under military protocol. He turned, waved lazily at his men.
"Check the crates. Quickly."
They rummaged. Kicked straw. One paused near the pit. Miles held his breath beneath the weight of his own heartbeat.
“Nothing here, sir,” came the report.
The officer sighed, mock disappointment heavy in his voice. “Very well. But if you do find someone—say, a straggler from the wrong side—I trust you’ll be patriotic.”
She inclined her head. “Of course.”
He lingered a beat too long. Then turned and left, boots echoing.
The door shut. Silence.
Liliane waited a full minute before moving. Then she dropped to her knees, heart hammering, and began pulling back the hay.
Miles gasped as air hit his face. His eyes were wide, bloodshot.
“I thought—” he began, but she was already hauling him up.
He leaned on her shoulder. Together they half-walked, half-fell back to the cot.
And then her knees gave out.
She collapsed into him, her breath sharp, chest trembling. Not sobbing—but close. Her forehead against his collarbone. His arms around her without thinking.
They stayed that way, wrapped in the echo of what might have been the end.
Outside, the night returned to crickets. But the war had pressed its thumbprint deep into the wood of the barn.


Scene 7: The Choice

The guns had changed.
You could hear it in their rhythm—closer now, frantic and proud. From the west came the staccato rattle of French rifles, the low whump of mortars. Even the wind smelled different. Smoke, not rot. Home.
Miles sat upright, clothed in a stolen German coat too broad in the shoulders. His leg is stiff but holds his weight now. A cane made from a broken broomstick rested beside him.
Liliane laid the forged papers on the crate: identification, orders, ration card. The ink was still drying.
“I copied them from a deserter’s pocket,” she said. “He won’t miss them.”
Miles took the papers slowly. His paw brushed hers.
She gave him a rolled map next. “Avoid the roads. Take the woods north until you reach the river. Then follow the train lines east. There’ll be scouts—French ones.”
He looked down at the bundle in his lap. Then up at her.
“No.”
Liliane’s brow creased.
“You’re coming with me,” he said. “You have to.”
She didn’t answer. She stepped away, tied her apron tighter. Her mouth was hard.
“You’ve done everything for me,” Miles said. “You saved my life. Twice. More. I’m not leaving you here. They’ll shoot you if they find out—”
“They won’t,” she snapped, turning. “Because they won’t find out.”
His voice broke. “Liliane—please.”
Silence swelled.
She stepped closer, took his paw gently. Her thumb brushed over his scarred knuckles. Then, slowly, she brought his hand to her mouth and kissed it.
Warm. Tender. Final.
“I belong to the dead,” she whispered. “But you—don’t you dare stop living.”
He couldn’t speak. His throat burned.
She pulled away before he could reach for her. Walked to the barn door and opened it just enough for moonlight to fall on the straw. The world outside was chaos—distant fires, the occasional crack of a rifle—but it was also freedom. His only chance.
Miles stood.
He lingered a moment. One breath. Two.
Then he stepped into the light.
She watched until he vanished into the trees.
And when she turned back to the empty barn, there were tears on her cheek—but no regret in her spine.


Scene 8: Years Later

Paris, 1952.
The gallery was small, tucked between a bookstore and a florist off Rue Saint-Antoine. Inside, the light was soft, the walls pale, the air faintly scented with lavender and varnish. Paintings and photographs lined the space—faces frozen in grain and sepia, soldiers and nurses and mothers, hands held or blurred mid-motion.
Miles stood near the back, cane in one hand, hat in the other.
He was older now. The fur on his muzzle had faded to a noble grey, and his shoulders stooped with the quiet weight of time. But his eyes—still sharp, still amber—were fixed on one photograph.
It was small. Tucked between larger frames. Black and white.
A young German Shepherd woman stood in front of a barn. Apron stained. Arms crossed. A line of dirt down her cheek. Behind her, barely visible through a gap in the wooden wall, was a man’s silhouette.
The caption read:
The Angel of Verdun. Name unknown. Photo recovered 1917. Presumed killed in action.
Miles didn't move.
He didn't need to.
The noise of the city had vanished. So had the voices of visitors, the ticking of the gallery clock. There was only her. Frozen in silver nitrate, forever looking out into a world she never left.
He reached out, one trembling paw grazing the glass.
Then: a sound behind him. A young couple, murmuring in admiration.
“She looks strong,” the woman said.
“She looks sad,” the man replied.
Miles smiled faintly. “She was both.”
They didn’t hear him. He didn’t need them to.
He placed his hat back on, nodded once to the image, and turned to leave—cane tapping against the floor, slow and steady.
Outside, the wind rustled the gallery sign. A few petals from the florist’s stand blew past his feet.
The war was long over. But some ghosts chose to stay kind.
