# The Thin Years
*A Novella*

---

## Chapter 1: The Weight of Snow

The stones. Count them. Seventeen along the eastern wall, where the wind cuts through. Eighteen if you count the broken one, but broken things—do they count? Lillian's broad paws trace the rough surface, feeling for gaps with the sensitive pads that can detect the smallest flaw in mortar. The mortar crumbles between her clawed fingers like old bread. Like the bread that isn't there.

Her glasses, wire-rimmed and slightly fogged from the cold, catch what little light filters through the December clouds. She pushes them up her snout with the back of a paw—a gesture so habitual she barely notices it anymore. Her long black hair, streaked with the first silver of middle age, falls in waves past her shoulders, catching snowflakes that melt against the warmth of her fur.

The cottage breathes around her. In, out. The way buildings do when they're dying. When they're being abandoned. When they're being claimed.

She is claiming this place.

Her five-foot-one frame fills the doorway completely, blocking the December light that struggles through the Atlantic clouds. Two hundred and ninety pounds of badger-woman, solid as the stones themselves, warm as the hearth that hasn't been lit in—how long? The ashes are cold. Cold as the child she hasn't found yet but knows she will find.

*Will find.*

The floorboards sag under her weight. Good. They hold. They will hold the children too, when they come. And they will come. The abandoned always find each other, like water finding the lowest place, like snow finding the ground.

She moves through the cottage with the methodical precision of someone who has learned that survival lives in the details. Her claws click softly against the wooden floor—not the sharp click of predator claws, but the blunt, sturdy sound of digging claws, built for earth and endurance rather than hunting. The back room: smaller, warmer, away from the wind. The main room: space for a fire, for gathering, for the stories that will keep them alive when the food runs out. The lean-to: storage, if there's anything to store.

There isn't.

But there will be.

Her thoughts move like her body—steady, purposeful, carrying weight. *Where to put the potatoes.* The few that remain, wrinkled and soft but not yet rotted. *How to block the wind.* Rags, perhaps. Stones. Mud mixed with straw if there's straw to be found. *How to make this place hold warmth the way her body holds warmth.*

The way her mother's body held warmth.

*Don't think of mother. Think of now. Think of stones. Think of gaps.*

But the mind wanders where it will, like children wander, like the lost wander. Her mother's paws, broad like hers, capable like hers. Her mother's voice: *"Lillian, love, you're built for enduring. Built for carrying what others cannot carry."*

Built for this.

She finds the first sign in the main room—a bowl, wooden, carved with care. Half-filled with something that might once have been soup. Nettle soup, from the smell. Her sensitive nose, black and wet at the tip of her elongated snout, picks up the story the scent tells: left in haste, left in hope of return, left in the terrible mathematics of who stays and who goes.

Someone chose to go.

Someone chose to leave the soup.

Someone chose to leave—

The sound comes from the back room. Small. Barely there. Like breathing, but wrong. Like breathing that's forgotten how to be breathing.

Lillian moves toward the sound with the careful grace of large things that have learned to be gentle. Her footsteps make the floorboards sing—low notes, bass notes, the deep music of weight distributed with love. Her fur, thick and coarse, provides natural insulation against the cold that seeps through every crack in the cottage walls.

The roof beam has fallen. Not completely—it hangs at an angle, supported by the wall, creating a small cave of shadow and safety. And in that cave, curled like a question mark, like a prayer, like a thing that has given up hoping but hasn't yet stopped breathing—

A child.

Boy. Seven, perhaps eight. Hard to tell when hunger has been doing the aging. His lips are blue—not the blue of cold, though there's that too, but the blue of something deeper. The blue of a body that's been asking for things it cannot have for too long.

Seamus. She knows his name before he tells her, the way mothers know things, the way the earth knows what seeds it's carrying.

"Seamus," she says, and her voice fills the small space like warmth, like soup, like the promise of soup. The words emerge with the slight lisp that comes from speaking around elongated canine teeth, but the sound is gentle, maternal. "I'm Lillian."

His eyes open. Brown eyes, too large for his face, too old for his years. They focus on her with the careful attention of prey animals, of things that have learned that survival depends on reading the intentions of larger things.

But she is not a larger thing to be feared. She is a larger thing to be trusted.

She kneels—carefully, slowly, letting her knees announce themselves against the floor so he knows where she is, what she's doing. Her paws, when she extends them, are empty. Palm up, the pink pads visible, the claws retracted. The universal gesture: *I have nothing to hurt you with. I have everything to help you with.*

"Are you hurt, love?"

He tries to speak. His voice comes out like winter wind through broken glass. "Cold," he manages. "So cold."

"I know." And she does know. She knows cold the way stones know pressure, the way water knows ice. Her glasses have fogged again, and she pushes them up her snout with a gentle paw. "I'm going to move this beam, and then I'm going to get you warm. Can you help me? Can you tell me if anything hurts when I move it?"

He nods. Such a small movement, but it contains everything: trust, hope, the decision to keep living.

The beam is heavy, but she is heavier. She is built for lifting, for carrying, for bearing weight that would break smaller things. Her powerful shoulders, thick with muscle beneath the coarse fur, take the load. Her legs drive up from the earth, and the beam shifts, groans, settles against the wall where it can do no more harm.

Seamus unfolds slowly, like a flower that's forgotten how to bloom. His clothes—what's left of them—hang on his frame like flags of surrender. But he's not surrendering. He's here. He's breathing. He's looking at her with those too-old eyes and waiting to see what happens next.

What happens next is warmth.

Lillian gathers him against her chest, and he disappears into her embrace like a stone disappearing into deep water. Her body heat, trapped by the thick layer of fur beneath her clothes, surrounds him, penetrates him, begins the slow work of bringing him back from the edge of everything. Her long black hair falls around them both like a curtain, creating a small world of safety and warmth.

"There," she murmurs into his hair, her breath warm against his scalp. "There, love. You're safe now."

And for the first time in months—maybe years—it's true. He is safe. In the circle of her arms, against the solid warmth of her body, in the cottage that she has claimed and that has claimed her, he is safe.

But safety is not enough. Safety is just the beginning.

## Chapter 2: Small Gatherings

The village breathes like a dying thing—shallow, irregular, each exhalation carrying the scent of endings. Lillian moves through Skibbereen's broken streets with Seamus's small hand tucked safely in her large paw, her claws retracted to avoid frightening him, the soft pads of her fingers providing warmth against the December cold.

Her long black hair, braided now to keep it from her face, catches what little light filters through the perpetual gray of winter sky. The wire-rimmed glasses perched on her elongated snout fog with each breath, and she pauses periodically to clean them with a corner of her coat—a gesture that has become as natural as breathing, as necessary as the careful attention she pays to every shadow, every doorway, every sign of life in this landscape of abandonment.

Three days of soup and stories have transformed Seamus from the hollow-eyed wraith she found under the fallen beam into something that remembers how to be a child. His step has steadiness now, his cheeks have color, his eyes have begun to hold light instead of merely reflecting it. But more than that—he has begun to trust. To believe that the large, strange woman beside him will not disappear, will not leave him, will not prove to be another cruel trick played by a world that has already taken everything he thought was permanent.

"Where are we going?" he asks, his voice carrying the careful curiosity of someone learning that questions might be answered instead of punished.

"Looking," Lillian says, her words shaped by the slight lisp that comes from speaking around elongated canine teeth. "Listening. Seeing what needs to be seen."

What needs to be seen is everywhere. The workhouse squats at the village's heart like a malignant growth, its walls bulging with the weight of too many bodies, too much desperation, too much of the terrible mathematics that reduce human beings to numbers, to calculations of cost and benefit, to the cold arithmetic of who lives and who dies.

The smell reaches them from a hundred yards away—unwashed flesh, sickness, the sweet-sick scent of people dying in close quarters. Lillian's sensitive nose, black and wet at the tip of her snout, picks up the individual threads of misery woven into that overwhelming tapestry of despair: fever, infection, the particular odor of hope slowly rotting away.

"Three thousand," says a voice behind them. "Maybe more."

Lillian turns, her thick fur rustling beneath her coat, her bulk moving with the careful grace she has learned to cultivate—the art of being large without being threatening, of taking up space without crowding others out of theirs. The woman who has spoken is perhaps forty, perhaps sixty—hunger ages people in ways that make counting years meaningless, that collapse the careful categories by which the comfortable world measures time.

Her clothes hang on her frame like flags of surrender, but her eyes—her eyes still hold fire. The kind of fire that burns when everything else has been consumed, when there is nothing left to fuel it but pure, stubborn refusal to be extinguished.

"Three thousand?" Lillian asks, pushing her glasses up her snout with the back of a paw, the gesture automatic, comforting in its familiarity.

"In the workhouse. Built for eight hundred, they say. But who's counting?" The woman's laugh is sharp as broken glass, bitter as winter wind. "I'm Mary Fitzgerald. Was Mary Fitzgerald. Don't know what I am now."

"Lillian. This is Seamus."

Mary's eyes fix on the boy with the intensity of someone who has learned to catalog loss, to measure it, to understand its weight and texture and the particular way it settles into the bones. Then her gaze travels up to study Lillian's face—the elongated snout, the wire-rimmed glasses that catch and scatter light, the way her long black hair frames features that are unmistakably both human and something more, something other, something that exists in the spaces between the categories the world uses to sort and label and understand.

"Yours?" she asks, and the question carries layers: biological, legal, emotional, the complex web of relationships that bind people together when blood and law and custom have all failed.

"Mine now," Lillian says, and her voice carries the authority of someone who understands that claiming and being claimed are acts of will, choices made in defiance of a world that would prefer to leave the lost ones lost.

"Ah." Mary nods as if this explains everything, as if the mathematics of survival have become so simple that love can be reduced to possession, that family can be created by declaration, that the bonds that matter are the ones forged in the crucible of shared need rather than the accident of shared blood. "You're the one, then. The badger-woman."

"I'm sorry?"

"Word travels, even when there's nowhere for it to go. Woman built like a badger, they say. Strong as stone, gentle as rain. Takes in the lost ones. Feeds them somehow, keeps them warm somehow, makes them believe that tomorrow might be worth seeing." Mary's eyes narrow, studying Lillian's face with the careful attention of someone who has learned to read truth in unusual places, to find hope in unexpected forms. "You're her."

Lillian feels something shift inside her chest—not unpleasant, but strange, like the sensation of discovering that you have been walking in someone else's footsteps without knowing it, that your life has become a story other people tell, that your choices have rippled outward in ways you never intended or imagined.

She has been many things—daughter, survivor, wanderer, the woman who counts stones and measures gaps and makes soup from nothing. But she has never been a legend walking around in her own skin, never been a myth that breathes and bleeds and pushes wire-rimmed glasses up a badger's snout with the back of a clawed paw.

"I take care of who needs taking care of," she says, her voice steady, matter-of-fact, refusing the weight of legend, the burden of being more than human. "Nothing more complicated than that."

"Same thing," Mary says, and her smile carries the weight of every impossible choice, every calculation that reduces love to arithmetic, every moment when the heart's mathematics prove more complex than the world's simple equations of scarcity and survival. "You want to see what needs taking care of, look there."

She gestures toward the workhouse, toward the sea of mud and misery that surrounds it, toward the people who huddle against its walls like debris washed up by some terrible tide. "Children mostly. Parents bring them, leave them, hope someone else can do what they can't. Hope someone else can perform the miracle of making something from nothing, of feeding the hungry when there is no food, of warming the cold when there is no fuel."

"Leave them?" The words emerge from Lillian's throat like a physical pain, like something sharp and jagged that has to be coughed up before it can do more damage.

"What choice is there?" Mary's voice carries the weight of every impossible decision, every moment when love proves insufficient to the demands of survival, when the heart's desire to protect collides with the body's need to endure. "Feed one child or watch three starve. Keep the family together and bury them together, or split them up and maybe—maybe—save some."

The mathematics of love in a time when love is a luxury no one can afford. The terrible arithmetic that reduces parents to accountants, children to line items in a ledger written in tears and blood and the kind of desperation that makes monsters of saints and saints of monsters.

They walk closer to the workhouse, Lillian's bulk creating a windbreak for Seamus, her presence a wall between him and the worst of what they are about to witness. The sounds that emerge from within are not quite human—moaning, crying, the wet cough of lungs giving up their fight against the inevitable. But underneath, threading through the despair like a melody played in a minor key, she hears something else.

Children's voices. Singing.

"They do that," Mary says, noticing her attention, understanding the way hope can catch in the throat like a fishbone, sharp and unexpected and impossible to swallow. "The little ones. Sing to keep the fear away. Sing to remember what it felt like before."

Before. The word that divides the world into two countries: the place where people lived, and the place where they survive. The border between them is marked not by geography but by memory, by the moment when the future stopped being a promise and became a threat, when tomorrow stopped being something to anticipate and became something to endure.

"I want to see," Lillian says, her glasses catching the weak light as she turns her head toward the workhouse yard.

Mary shakes her head, but not in refusal—in recognition, in the understanding that some things must be witnessed even when witnessing them changes you, even when seeing them makes it impossible to unsee them, even when knowledge becomes a burden too heavy to carry but too important to put down.

"You don't want to see," she says. "But you need to see. Different thing entirely."

The workhouse yard is a sea of mud and misery, a landscape painted in shades of brown and gray and the particular color of hope slowly dying. People huddle against the walls, wrapped in whatever they could carry, waiting for admission, for food, for death—whichever comes first. Children dart between the adults like small fish in a dark pond, their games a desperate parody of the games children played before, their laughter sharp and brittle as ice that might crack at any moment.

Lillian sees her immediately.

A girl, perhaps five years old, sitting apart from the others with the careful isolation of someone who has learned that connection leads to loss, that love leads to abandonment, that the safest place to be is alone. She holds something in her arms—a doll, carved from wood with the kind of care that speaks of better times, painted with colors that have faded but not disappeared, worn smooth by small hands and the kind of love that polishes what it treasures.

The doll's face is a map of devotion, its features softened by countless touches, countless whispered secrets, countless nights when it served as the only barrier between a child and the darkness that wants to swallow everything whole.

The girl herself is silent. Not the comfortable silence of contentment, not the peaceful quiet of someone at rest, but the silence of something broken in a way that makes sound impossible, that turns the voice into a luxury too dangerous to afford.

"Brigid," Mary says, following Lillian's gaze, understanding the way certain sights can catch the heart like hooks, can pull at something deep and fundamental and impossible to ignore. "Been like that since they brought her. Won't speak. Won't eat unless you put the food directly in her hands. Just sits there, holding that doll like it's the last real thing in the world."

"Where's her family?"

"Dead. Fever took them last month—mother, father, two older brothers. She was under the bodies when they found her, still breathing, still holding that doll. Been silent ever since."

Lillian feels something pull tight in her chest, the same something that pulled tight when she found Seamus under the fallen beam, the same something that has been pulling tight her entire life when she encounters things that are broken and might, with care and time and the kind of stubborn love that refuses to give up, be mended.

She approaches slowly, the way she would approach a wounded animal, her footsteps careful and deliberate, her bulk moving with the practiced grace of someone who has learned that size can be either threatening or comforting depending on how it's used, how it's offered, how it's received.

Brigid's eyes track her movement but show no fear, no hope, no recognition that another person is a thing that might matter, that might make a difference, that might be anything other than another source of potential pain in a world that has already provided more pain than any five-year-old should be asked to carry.

"Hello, love," Lillian says, settling herself on the ground beside the girl, her bulk creating a windbreak, a wall between Brigid and the chaos of the yard. Her long black hair falls forward like a curtain, creating a small space of privacy, of intimacy, of the kind of safety that exists not in the absence of danger but in the presence of someone who will stand between you and whatever wants to hurt you.

Brigid's arms tighten around the wooden figure, her small fingers pressing into the worn surface as if she could somehow merge with it, become part of it, disappear into the safety of being an object that cannot be hurt because it cannot feel.

"That's a beautiful doll," Lillian continues, her voice carrying the gentle authority of someone who understands that healing begins with recognition, with seeing what is precious in what the world has discarded, with acknowledging the value of what others might dismiss as worthless.

Silence. But not empty silence—full silence, pregnant with possibility, heavy with the weight of words that want to be spoken but haven't yet found their way to the surface.

"What's her name?"

More silence, but Brigid's grip on the doll shifts slightly, becomes protective rather than desperate, as if the question has reminded her that the doll is not just an object but a being with its own identity, its own story, its own right to exist and be acknowledged.

"My name is Lillian. This is Seamus." She gestures to the boy, who has settled on her other side, instinctively understanding the need for gentleness, for patience, for the kind of careful attention that allows broken things to begin the slow process of healing. "We live in a cottage not far from here. It's warm there. There's soup."

Still silence, but something flickers in Brigid's eyes—not hope, because hope is too dangerous, too likely to be disappointed, too much like the promises that have already been broken. But attention. The kind of attention that says: *I am listening, even though I cannot respond. I am here, even though I cannot speak. I am still alive, even though I wish I weren't.*

"Would you like to come with us? You and your doll?"

The question hangs in the air between them like a bridge that might be crossed or might collapse, like an offer that might be accepted or rejected, like a possibility that might transform everything or change nothing at all.

Brigid looks at her for a long moment, studying the unusual face with its elongated snout, the wire-rimmed glasses that catch and scatter the weak winter light, the long black hair that frames features both strange and somehow comforting, both alien and deeply familiar, both frightening and safe.

Then, slowly, carefully, as if the movement might shatter something precious, she nods.

The claiming is that simple. That complicated. That necessary.
