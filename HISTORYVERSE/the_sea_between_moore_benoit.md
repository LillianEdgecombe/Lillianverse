# The Sea Between

## 1. Embarkation

1953, Southampton. The air on the dockyard tasted of coal smoke, wet rope, and boiled cabbage from the canteen. On the water, the *Aurelia* sat in her berth like an idea someone had insisted on finishing properly: all white paint and polished brass, a radio mast like a neat exclamation mark against the low, stubborn sky.

Miles Ferguson checked his watch, then the gangway, then the passenger list folded in his pocket, because habits in peacetime had to come from somewhere.

He wore the uniform of a navigation officer, which never quite stopped feeling like a costume. Dark wool, brass buttons, cap brim spoiling his peripheral vision. Out of uniform he was a tall, rangy German Shepherd in his late thirties, handsome in a slightly over-specified way—ears that had never agreed with gravity, shoulders that hadn’t learned how to relax, a mouth that smiled more easily for passengers than for cameras. In uniform he was a symbol: reassurance in neat stitching, a way for people paying for first-class cabins to imagine that someone sensible was between them and the sea.

He moved along the embarkation deck with the brisk, contained energy of someone used to tight corridors and sudden alarms. The war had taught him that nothing good came of letting your attention drift when people were moving in and out of metal boxes.

The passengers arrived in careful sequence: families with too much luggage, businessmen with too much confidence, widows with too much jewellery. The crew at the gangway checked names, tickets, passports. Miles watched the way people stepped onto the ship—the hesitation or lack of it, the way they glanced up at the funnels as if measuring their own lives against steel—and made private notes.

He had been doing this job for three years. The pattern never stopped changing, and never really changed.

“Officer Ferguson,” a voice called.

He turned. The cruise director, a fox named Ellis whose smile did most of his work for him, was weaving through the crowd with a clipboard and the air of a man who had promised several different people that they were all his top priority.

“We’ve added a late booking,” Ellis said without preamble. “Cabaret singer. Replacement. The last one took ill.”

“Took ill,” Miles repeated, because some phrases deserved the dignity of quotation marks, if not full cross-examination.

“Ran off with a trumpeter,” Ellis said, and ticked a box. “Same outcome for our purposes. Here—”

He handed over a thin file. On top, a typed sheet: “LILLIAN EDGECOMBE – ENTERTAINER (VOCALIST)”. Under it, a photograph paper-clipped at the corner.

Miles glanced at the picture out of habit and because Ellis clearly expected him to. It was a studio headshot, slightly too bright, the edges of the paper worn. Lillian Edgecombe looked straight at the camera, which was unusual. Most performers looked just above or just to the side, angling for some invisible audience. She looked as if the photographer had been caught trying to explain something, and she’d made a decision about him.

Badger, late thirties by the look of her, strong jaw, heavy-lidded eyes, fur patterned like someone had carefully shaded in confidence. Curvy, as the form said in professional euphemism; solid in a way that made the flimsy suit jackets and cocktail dresses boarding around her look like temporary arrangements. The note next to “Image” read in neat pencil: “Authentic wartime glamour. London. RAF clubs.”

Ellis followed his gaze.

“You know the type,” he said. “They want nostalgia with their cocktails. We had to scramble to find someone who still knows all the songs without looking like their own chaperone.”

“What did she do during the war?” Miles asked.

Ellis raised an eyebrow. “Besides this?”

“This during the war meant something different,” Miles said. He was aware, as he said it, that he sounded older than he was.

Ellis shrugged, a small show of surrender.

“It’s in the file,” he said. “Look, can you make sure she gets her safety briefing? The last singer nearly fainted in the lifeboat drill, and I cannot repeat that conversation with the insurance people.”

“Of course,” Miles said, because that was his job: make sure the ship reached New York upright, and if possible, that no one wrote a letter afterwards describing a preventable embarrassment.

Ellis vanished back into the throng. Miles watched the gangway again.

She appeared twenty minutes later, in the second wave of latecomers: a woman in a dark coat, hat pulled low, luggage modest. The photograph had been kinder about lighting, less so about reality. In person, Lillian Edgecombe looked exactly as if someone had drawn her from memory after a long night—lines at the corners of her mouth, a small scar along one brow, shoulders that carried things she had not been paid to lift.

He recognised something else, too, beneath the professional poise: the particular way people who had worked through the Blitz moved. A fraction faster under arches. A habit of counting exits. An instinctive, almost impolite attention to other people’s faces, as if noting who might need to be carried.

She handed her ticket to the gangway steward, smiled for the benefit of the couple behind her, and stepped onto the ship with the deliberate calm of someone boarding a fragile truce.

Miles marked her name off on his list.

“Welcome aboard, Miss Edgecombe,” he said, because it was what you said. “I’m Officer Ferguson. I’ll be giving you your safety briefing later this afternoon.”

Her gaze flicked to the side of his cap where his rank insignia sat, then up to his eyes. There was an almost imperceptible pause, as if she had expected him to be someone else entirely and was adjusting.

“Lillian,” she said. Her voice carried easily over the noise, low and trained to disappear into orchestrations. “Unless you prefer formal address on the high seas.”

“Miles,” he said, surprising himself. “Unless the captain is listening. In which case I am very impersonal and very busy.”

She smiled, briefly. Not for him, not yet, but at the shape of the exchange. Then she moved on, into the ship’s cool interior, and he went back to his list, his watch, and the quiet arithmetic of distance.

Out in the Channel, the wrecks from another decade lay under the surface, unseen. The sea didn’t care about passenger manifests. It remembered hulls, not names.

Miles folded the file with her photograph and slipped it into his jacket pocket, telling himself he was only being thorough.


## 2. Routines at Sea

By the second day out, the *Aurelia* had settled into her patterns.

Morning: breakfast for the passengers, coffee for the crew, a steady procession of complaints at the purser’s office ranging from the understandable (“the cabin is too hot”) to the barely comprehensible (“the ocean is too loud at night; can it be turned down?”). Miles took his turn on the bridge, eyes moving from horizon to instruments, from instruments to weather reports, from weather reports to the captain’s brief nod. The sea rolled under them, a vast, indifferent platform for human rituals.

Afternoon: lifeboat drills, deck chairs, the first layer of gossip forming like salt on rails. Lillian did her safety briefing, standing in a life jacket that sat awkwardly over her curves, listening to Miles explain escape routes and muster stations.

“And if the worst happens,” he said to the little group of entertainers and bar staff, “you follow crew instructions and do not improvise.”

“Even if the choreography would be better?” she asked, dryly.

There was a ripple of laughter. It took the edge off the seriousness, which was serviceable, if not ideal. He had learned during the war that people retained information better when they weren’t completely terrified, but there was always the risk they stopped taking the information seriously at all.

He held her gaze for a moment longer than the others.

“Even then,” he said. “Sinking ships don’t respond well to improvisation.”

She nodded, thoughtful, and adjusted the straps of her life jacket with efficient hands.

Evening: the ballroom.

The *Aurelia*’s ballroom was one of those spaces that existed to convince people they were living in a better version of the world for at least three hours a night. Chandeliers, polished dance floor, an orchestra that did its best with the tastes of the paying public. Lillian took to the small, gleaming stage with poise that suggested she preferred more smoke and less chandelier, but would accept whatever the job offered.

Miles did not, as a rule, attend the performances. His shifts didn’t allow for it, and in any case, he had spent enough nights in wartime clubs pretending the world wasn’t ending to feel uneasy when peace tried to recreate the pose.

On the third night, a gale convinced him to break his own rule.

“We’ll be running on reduced power for a spell,” the chief engineer announced on the bridge, voice too calm for the way the ship shuddered. “There’s a fault in the secondary generator. We can maintain navigation and vital systems. Non-essential circuits will need to be taken offline until we fix it.”

“Define non-essential,” the captain said.

“Ballroom lights,” the engineer said. “Perhaps also some of the decorous frivolities.”

Miles watched the radar as the storm front moved over them—a mild thing compared to what he had seen from cockpits, but enough to make the *Aurelia* creak in ways that made passengers clutch their armrests. He pictured people in evening wear attempting to dance through a blackout and decided to take his break below, where trouble was likely to concentrate.

He arrived at the ballroom just as the first flicker hit.

The band was halfway through the second chorus of “We’ll Meet Again,” which was either nostalgia or poor imagination. Couples moved on the dance floor in a slow, sticky waltz: elderly men in formal wear revisiting air-raid shelter romances, younger ones performing a version of history they had never lived. Lillian stood at the microphone in a dark green dress that caught the light, shoulders bare, fur catching the glow.

For a moment, the scene held.

Then the chandelier lights went out.

The room fell into blackness.

There was a single, collective intake of breath—the air thickening with fear, memory, preparation. Somewhere at the back, a woman shrieked once, short and sharp, as if out of loyalty to her younger self.

The band faltered. The pianist struck one wrong chord, hands jolting in the dark.

Lillian did not move.

“We’ll meet again,” she sang, without amplification now, voice cutting clean through the dark. “Don’t know where, don’t know when…”

Miles felt the hairs on his arms stand up under his uniform.

The song, sung without the trappings, changed shape. It lost the bright, resilient bravado that had made it a propaganda staple and became something quieter, stranger. A woman’s voice in the dark, acknowledging that she didn’t know, actually, and that this ignorance was the point.

The passengers listened. The ship groaned. Somewhere underfoot, the engineers swore affectionately at reluctant machinery.

Lillian sang the verse through to the end, not rushing, not slowing, as if the blackout had been part of the act all along. When she finished, there was a silence that felt more like respect than uncertainty.

Emergency lights blinked on along the walls: dull red strips, casting everyone in the kind of colour that made funerals look theatrical.

Ellis appeared at Miles’s shoulder, whispering a prayer in mixed currencies.

“Everything’s under control,” he announced brightly, stepping forward as if he had personally negotiated with the storm. “Just a brief reduction in lighting for atmosphere. Ladies and gentlemen, please remain calm and seated while our capable crew attends to a trifling electrical matter—”

Miles caught Lillian’s eye as she stepped back from the microphone, letting the band take over with a less literal selection. She gave him a small nod that conveyed, with admirable economy, “I saw that coming” and “Don’t you dare apologise for the weather.”

Afterwards, when the ballroom had emptied and the engineer reported that the fault was isolated and the ship was, in fact, not going to die in the middle of the Atlantic out of an excess of drama, Miles tracked her down backstage.

She was sitting on a crate, rolling a cigarette with slow, practised movements. The dressing room smelled of powder, sweat, and the kind of perfume that had been fashionable ten years earlier and hadn’t noticed the calendar.

“You didn’t panic,” he said, leaning in the doorway.

“Sure I did,” she said. “In a very controlled, useful way. What else was I going to do? Scream? There’s a queue.”

He smiled, tired.

“You kept singing,” he said.

“Habit,” she said. “The lights went out a lot in London. If you stopped every time, you’d never finish a set. Besides, people like continuity. Makes them feel the universe knows what it’s doing.”

“Does it?” he asked.

She lit her cigarette, exhaled smoke in a neat line toward the ceiling.

“No,” she said. “But we’re all doing our bit to cover for it.”

He thought of his own job, of charts and radio signals and the illusion that if you calculated correctly enough, nothing bad would happen. Of the idea that any one person’s diligence could make up for structural chaos.

“You worked the clubs during the war,” he said.

She looked at him over the ember of the cigarette, measuring.

“I sang in some,” she said. “I poured drinks in others. Sometimes I hid under tables. You?”

“Flew over Hamburg,” he said. “And other places that didn’t deserve it.”

She absorbed that, eyes flicking briefly to his hands as if expecting to see residue.

“You navigated?” she asked.

“And occasionally steered,” he said. “Although the way they talk about it in the club stories, you’d think all we did was smoke and flirt with the pianist.”

“We appreciated the smoking,” she said. “Made it harder to smell the damp.”

They sat in a pocket of shared knowledge, not quite comfortable. The war was a presence in the room, like a fourth person who declined to be introduced.

“I liked the song better in the dark,” he said, finally.

“So did I,” she said. “But don’t tell the guests. They’re paying for chandeliers.”

He almost asked her something then—about the nights under the Blitz, about the men who didn’t come back from the dances, about how you learned to make your body part of the décor and still keep it for yourself. Instead, he said, “Thank you,” which was simpler and not nearly enough.

She waved it away with the cigarette.

“I like knowing where the exits are,” she said. “You make sure we don’t hit anything. I make sure they don’t think about drowning. Everyone contributes.”

Later, alone on the deck, the storm easing around them, Miles leaned on the rail and listened to the engines. The night sky was empty of searchlights now. The ocean shone with nothing but moonlight and the ship’s own reflected light, cut into neat rectangles by railings.

He thought, not for the first time, of Karl.

The thought arrived the way it always did: a flicker of a face at the edge of memory, a voice mostly reconstructed from their mother’s accent, the blurred idea of a uniform that had been on the wrong side of every story Miles had been told as a child.

He pictured his own cockpit over Germany, instruments glowed red, flak rising, the radio crackling with clipped instructions. He imagined, because he had never been able not to, another cockpit somewhere in that blackness, another young man in another uniform, climbing through the same skies. The maps had called them enemy. No map could convince him the clouds recognised the difference.

The engines hummed under his feet. The ship moved forward, despite everything.


## 3. Karl, a Draft

In 1953, there were things people did not say in public, not because they were forbidden by law, but because the silence around them had acquired the weight of custom.

Miles did not say, for instance, that his mother had been German. That she had met his father in 1919, when the world was briefly pretending to be tired of war. That his younger brother had grown up on stories told in two languages and had chosen, with the blunt logic of adolescence, the one that felt more endangered.

He certainly did not say that he had spent the years since 1944 carrying the possibility that he, personally, had shot Karl out of the sky.

It wasn’t that he had evidence. Memory did not operate like a court. It operated like a draftsman with a faulty eraser. He knew his own missions. He knew the dates and times. Somewhere over the Ruhr, somewhere over the grey skeletal outlines of what had once been cities, he had dropped flares, marked targets, listened to the bombers behind him.

He had also, on occasion, fired at shapes in the sky that moved wrong.

These were facts, of a sort.

The rest was geometry. Karl had been posted to a Luftwaffe squadron assigned to night fighters. Miles had been posted to routes that those fighters had harassed. The lines on the map could have intersected. It took very little work, if you were so inclined, to draw an arrow from one to the other.

Miles had done that work many times. It had become a private ritual, performed mostly in the quiet moments between radar contacts, between a captain’s order and its execution. A proof written on the inside of his skull, erased, re-written, always stopping just short of the final line because that line was not survivable.

His father, who had died in 1942 in a hospital corridor that smelled of carbolic and rationed soap, never knew. His mother had gone quiet when the war started, quieter still when news of Karl’s posting came, and then silence became something else entirely when the telegram arrived. She had not asked whether Miles had been in the air that night. He had never volunteered anything beyond that he was stationed elsewhere, which was true enough if you blurred the edges of “elsewhere” to match the shape of denial.

The letters he did not write to her formed a stack in his mind, just as tangible as the ones she had burned after reading, making sure no paper trail survived their mixed history.

The sea was a refuge from that geometry because it offered a different kind. Distances were measured in miles and knots, not in flight paths and kill reports. A ship’s route was written down in clear lines, not third-hand recollections. You could stand on the bridge and say, with some degree of confidence, that between here and there there was nothing but water.

He had taken the job on the *Aurelia* because he was good at it. At least, that was the version he offered to colleagues and to himself on practical days. On less practical days, like nights when the weather pulled at the hull and his mind had no obstacles between thoughts, he suspected he had chosen it because it was the farthest he could get from runways while still being responsible for other people’s lives.

In quiet moments, he tried to imagine what Karl would have made of it.

A sea voyage, for a boy who had idolised the Luftwaffe while playing with paper planes on their Manchester street. When Miles had written about the job in one letter, lightly, his mother had replied with a single line: “Your brother always preferred the idea of looking down rather than looking out.”

That had been before Karl enlisted. Before the uniforms diverged. Before their mother’s accent became a thing she trimmed away on buses, in queues, at the grocer’s.

He sometimes pictured Karl standing on the *Aurelia*’s deck, hands on the rail, face turned up to the wind, making jokes about officers and trying to coax the engine room staff into illicit card games. It was easier to imagine that than the other version: Karl strapped into a cockpit, oxygen mask on, seeing nothing but tracer and fire as some unknown British pilot lit up the sky around him.

The past, he told himself, was a set of closed hatches. You could bang on them as much as you liked; the only thing you’d achieve was bruised hands.

He believed this on roughly three days out of seven.

On the others, he thought of a specific night in 1944 when the flak had been heavier than usual, when he had seen a shape cross his path and fired at it without truly seeing it, and he wondered whether the hatches were, in fact, leaking.


## 4. Deck Chairs and Small Puzzles

On the fourth day, the sea calmed down and the passengers followed suit. The sky lightened to the tired blue of washed denim. Deck hands unfolded chairs on the promenade, and people in suits and dresses contended with the existential problem of where to sit in a way that signalled both relaxation and access to gossip.

Miles took his turn on deck duty, which was mostly a matter of looking official and available while quietly enforcing the pretense that no one ever drank too much before noon.

He found Lillian on the leeward side, a book on her lap, a shawl around her shoulders despite the mild weather. Her fur moved almost imperceptibly whenever the ship swayed, balancing with the ease of someone who had made many journeys on less predictable floors.

She was not reading. She was watching the other passengers over the top of the book in a way that would have been obvious if she hadn’t been so good at it.

“Good afternoon,” Miles said. “I see we survived last night’s attempt at dramatic flair.”

“The sea has a sense of timing,” she said. “If it had cut the lights in the middle of ‘A Nightingale Sang in Berkeley Square’, I’d have accused it of sentimentality. This way, it just looked like resentment.”

“Resentment?” he asked.

“We keep using it as a backdrop,” she said. “I can’t imagine that endears us to it.”

He smiled.

“Are you enjoying the voyage?” he asked. It was the kind of question his role required.

She tilted her head, considering.

“I’m getting paid to sing to people who aren’t being shelled,” she said. “That’s already ahead of several other gigs.”

“High bar,” he said.

“The war raised it,” she said. “It’s an ungrateful employer.”

He shifted his weight, feeling the ship’s movement through his boots.

“You went back to London after?” he asked.

“Eventually,” she said. “After a tour of all the places that were supposed to be more modern and less tired. Turns out people are people everywhere. London at least has better jokes.”

“And here you are,” he said.

She closed the book around one finger, marking her place even though she hadn’t turned a page.

“Here I am,” she said. “Booked as ‘authentic wartime glamour,’ which is a phrase designed by someone who never had to choose between mascara and a gas mask.”

He laughed, startled.

“The brochure did you a disservice,” he said.

“The brochure is very keen on narratives,” she said. “They want a certain kind of story: plucky, brave, a bit of lipstick, a few tasteful tears, then victory and cocktails. The fact that I also spent a year pouring pints for Americans who thought every British woman wanted to marry their G.I. does not make it into the copy.”

He watched her as she watched the sea. There was a small, deliberate distance in the way she held herself, as if she had stepped back from her own story and was evaluating the framing.

“Do you mind?” he asked. “The narrative.”

She shrugged.

“It pays the bills,” she said. “It gets me a cabin of my own and a chance to avoid some of the post-war poverty of hope. But sometimes I wonder if, by playing along, I’m helping them forget the awkward parts.”

“Them,” he said.

“The people who can afford nostalgia,” she said. “Which, to be fair, includes me, some days.”

He thought of the passengers dancing in the blackout, pretending that a song could bridge a decade. He thought of his own job, smoothing routes so that no one had to think about shoals.

“For what it’s worth,” he said, “I thought your blackout version was more honest than the lit one.”

“That’s because you were on the bridge thinking about how easily we could have been sitting in a lifeboat instead,” she said. “Honesty is very attractive to people who’ve had to lie to themselves for a living.”

He opened his mouth to object, then realised she had him there.

“I read faces for work,” she said, more gently. “Wartime entertainments were mostly about reading what people needed you to be. You look like you know how to arrange shadows for other people. Difference is, they put you in a uniform for it.”

Her book slid a little on her lap. He caught the title: a well-handled edition of a recent novel about post-war reconstruction, with reviews that had alternated between calling it “necessary” and “treacherous.”

“Do you ever get tired of curating it?” he asked. “The version of events people can bear to hear.”

“Constantly,” she said. “And then I remind myself that if I stop, someone else will step in and do it worse. Besides—”

She looked out over the rail, where the sea stretched in every direction, maddeningly unedited.

“Besides,” she said, “every so often someone actually listens to the lyrics.”

“What about you?” she added after a moment. “Do you enjoy this?”

“The sea?” he asked.

“The pretending we are all just going somewhere for pleasure,” she said. “No one trying to outrun anything, no one carrying anything they can’t afford to declare at customs.”

He thought of Karl. Of his mother’s accent. Of his father’s silence. Of his own file, with its neat summaries of missions flown, stripped of the sounds and smells.

“I like knowing where I am on a chart,” he said. “It feels like an improvement.”

“Over?” she prompted.

He almost said “over guessing whether I’ve killed my own brother,” which would have been more honesty than a deck chair deserved.

“Over looking down and not recognising anything,” he said instead.

She nodded once, as if she understood both the answer given and the one withheld.

“You should come to the show tonight,” she said. “I’m trying a new song. Less nostalgia, more bad decisions.”

“Is that a theme?” he asked.

“It’s a type,” she said. “We attract them.”


## 5. A Name in the Lounge

The passenger’s name was Vogel.

He arrived on the fifth day, figuratively speaking. Physically, he had boarded with the others at Southampton: a wolf in a well-made but slightly unfashionable suit, travelling alone, with two suitcases and a German passport that had prompted a brief but pointed check from the immigration officer at the gangway. Miles had noted him at the time—as one of several German nationals aboard, which made sense in 1953—and then filed him away with the rest of the manifest.

It was only in the mid-Atlantic lounge, under the influence of whisky and boredom, that Vogel stepped forward from the anonymous “passengers” category into something sharper.

The lounge was half-empty. The seas had grown choppy again, and people unused to the way a ship’s movement reprogrammed your stomach had retreated to their cabins. The ones who remained were either inveterate sailors or unwilling to admit weakness in front of the bar staff.

Miles sat at a small table near the back, a break he had justified by the logic that officers seen in public fostered trust. He nursed a drink more slowly than protocol allowed and tried not to listen to the radio in his head, which was playing the weather forecast on repeat.

Vogel approached with a traveller’s easy entitlement: scanning for empty seats, noticing a fellow solitary, deciding that company was part of the ticket price.

“Is this free?” he asked.

His English was precise, with a faint accent that matched his passport.

“Of course,” Miles said. “Please.”

Vogel sat, ordered whisky in halting bar vocabulary, and made a small production of lighting a cigarette in the way of those who believed they were in a film.

“Rough seas,” he said.

“They’ll settle,” Miles said. “The Atlantic likes to remind us we’re not in charge.”

Vogel smiled.

“Ah,” he said. “You are one of the officers.”

Miles touched his jacket out of reflex.

“Navigation,” he said.

“Important work,” Vogel said. “Without you, the ship might end up somewhere… unintended.”

“I’d rather not test that,” Miles said lightly.

Vogel studied him with the frank curiosity of someone testing a theory.

“Ferguson,” he said, nodding at the nameplate. “English.”

“Mostly,” Miles said.

“Mostly,” Vogel repeated, amused. “These are complicated times to be mostly.”

He took a slow drag of his cigarette, exhaled toward the ceiling.

“I had a friend,” he said casually. “In another life. In the Luftwaffe. He had an English name like yours.”

Miles’s throat went tight.

There were many English names. Not every one needed to be a ghost dragging a chain.

“Did he?” Miles said.

“Yes,” Vogel said. “He grew up in Germany, but his mother was British. He would joke that he was amphibious.” He smiled at his own joke. “His name was Karl Ferguson.”

The room did not tilt. The ship did that sometimes, but not now. The room remained stubbornly upright. It was his perception that slid.

“He—” Vogel continued, then paused, perhaps registering something in Miles’s face.

“Excuse me,” Miles said, because that was what polite men said when their internal structure had just been reclassified as rubble.

He stood too quickly, nearly upsetting his drink. Vogel reached out automatically, steadying the glass.

“Are you all right, Mr. Ferguson?” Vogel asked.

The use of “Mr.” was a small, absurd kindness.

“I… knew someone by that name,” Miles said. “A long time ago.”

“Yes?” Vogel’s eyes sharpened. “Ah. Family, perhaps?”

Miles wanted to say no. Denial rose out of habit, carrying with it all the reasons he had avoided this conversation for nearly a decade.

“My brother,” he said. The word came out flatter than he intended.

Vogel sat back.

“Ah,” he said again, softer. “Then we should talk.”


## 6. A Training Crash

They found a quieter corner at Vogel’s insistence: a table further from the bar, near a porthole where the sea pressed in, flattening waves into a dark green disc.

“We served together briefly,” Vogel said. “Karl and I. A training unit, near Kassel. He was very proud of his mixed heritage. He said it would make him a better pilot, seeing from both sides.” He smiled, rueful. “We were all very stupid, then.”

Miles listened, jaw tight.

“I did not know him long,” Vogel continued. “He arrived in late ’43. The war was already… you could smell it, going wrong. But we pretended. He had a photograph he kept in his locker—Manchester, I think. Row of houses, washing in the yard. He said if he survived, he would take his mother home and buy her a real garden.”

Miles knew the photograph. He had taken it, borrowed his father’s camera and climbed onto a wall to get the angle. He remembered the way Karl had insisted on standing on the end of the row, as if any composition that did not feature him prominently was a waste of film.

The image rose in his mind with such clarity that for a moment he was afraid it would appear on the porthole glass.

“What happened to him?” he asked.

Vogel’s cigarette had burned down to a long, precarious ash. He tapped it into the tray with exaggerated care, buying himself a second.

“Training accident,” he said. “Officially. We were flying night exercises. Cloud cover, poor maintenance. His instruments failed on approach. He went into a hill just short of the runway.”

Miles blinked.

“Not combat?” he asked. His voice sounded strange to his own ears, as if he had borrowed someone else’s throat.

“No,” Vogel said. “He never saw the enemy. Unless you count gravity.” He smiled without humour. “You look… surprised.”

Surprised was an inadequate word. Relief and guilt collided in Miles’s chest with such force that for a moment he thought he might actually be sick. The geometry he had built—the lines between the Ruhr and a nameless dot on a map—shattered. In their place, a smaller, more humiliating tragedy: faulty instruments, bad weather, the banal cruelty of a training schedule written by men who had never flown.

He had not killed Karl.

He had only killed men he did not know, whose names had never reached him.

“I had been told,” he said carefully, “that he was lost in action.”

“Many were,” Vogel said. “It was simpler to say it that way to families. More… heroic. They did not want to admit how many of us died within sight of our own airfields.”

He watched Miles with a kind of polite curiosity, as if aware that he was rearranging someone else’s life and unsure of the etiquette.

“You thought you had shot him down,” Vogel said. It was not a question.

Miles laughed once, a short, ugly bark that made the couple at the next table glance over.

“I thought I might have,” he said. “I thought it was… possible.”

“And you have been worrying about this for how long?” Vogel asked.

“Nine years,” Miles said, and heard how absurd it sounded only after he’d said it.

Vogel nodded, slow.

“We all make strange maps to live in,” he said. “For what it is worth, his death was immediate. No one suffered long. And he was… very proud. Of you, I think, though he said little. He kept your letters.”

Miles swallowed.

He had written those letters in the same clipped, careful sentences he used in his logbooks, afraid that any hint of doubt or fear would somehow betray him to censors or to his own conscience. He had tried to be the older brother who knew what he was doing. It had never occurred to him that Karl might die believing the fiction.

“Thank you,” he said.

The words felt inadequate and yet enormous.

Vogel stubbed out his cigarette, as if ending something.

“We are, none of us, innocent,” he said. “But perhaps you can put this one ghost down.”

Miles nodded. He did not, could not, say “yes.” The word would have been dishonest. You did not unlearn nine years of guilt in ten minutes. The mind was not that efficient.

But a door had opened somewhere in his internal architecture. Air moved through, unsettling the dust.

Later, alone in his cabin, he sat on the bunk with his back against the bulkhead and let the details settle: instruments failing, a hill, a crash. A brother who had died not as an enemy in his sights, but as another victim of the same indifferent machinery.

It was not absolution. But it was different.


## 7. Confessions in the Dark

He found Lillian on the aft deck, smoking under a no-smoking sign. The sign itself was small, tired, and clearly accustomed to being ignored.

The night was cold, the kind of cold that crept around coats and up sleeves. The ship’s wake glowed faintly under the stern lights, froth trailing into black.

“Do you ever obey signs?” he asked.

She flicked ash over the rail without looking at him.

“I’m very obedient,” she said. “I just like to choose my authorities.”

He joined her at the rail, hands in his pockets. The cold bit at his ears. The smell of her cigarette, cheap and assertive, overlaid the salt.

“You look like someone has told you something helpful and you haven’t decided if you’re grateful,” she said.

He huffed a laugh.

“A passenger knew my brother,” he said. “He told me how he died.”

She turned her head slightly.

“And?” she asked.

“Training accident,” he said. “Faulty instruments. I did not shoot him down.” The last sentence came out more brittle than he had expected.

“You thought you had,” she said.

He nodded.

“For nine years,” she said. “That’s… a long time to carry that.”

“Habit,” he said. “I needed something to explain the shape of things. ‘I might have killed my brother’ was a story that made a certain kind of sense. It made the war personal. It made my guilt efficient.”

She blew out smoke, thoughtful.

“Does this new version feel better?” she asked.

“Different,” he said. “Less… heroic. More ridiculous. Faulty instruments. Bad weather. Not even a proper villain.”

“Most stories don’t have proper villains,” she said. “Just people too tired or frightened to imagine other options.”

He thought of Karl in his cockpit, trusting the dials. Of the ground crew who had signed off on maintenance. Of the officers who had set the training schedule and never flown it themselves.

“I spent years thinking I was the worst thing that could have happened to him,” he said. “Turns out I was just… incidental.”

“You’re allowed to feel both relieved and insulted,” she said.

He laughed, properly this time.

“I suppose I am,” he said.

They stood in companionable silence for a moment, watching the wake.

“Do you ever find out what happened to the people you sang for?” he asked.

“Sometimes,” she said. “They come up after shows. They tell me about nights I barely remember. They say, ‘You got me through,’ as if I had anything to do with their survival beyond providing background noise while they worked out how to go on. It’s flattering. It’s also… unnerving.”

“Because you don’t recognise them?” he asked.

“Because they’re rewriting me into their stories,” she said. “I become a symbol of their courage, their youth, their lost possibilities. It’s tempting to accept the role. It’s also a lie.”

He glanced at her.

“What’s the truth?” he asked.

She stubbed out the cigarette on the rail, then flicked the butt into a pocket rather than overboard, as if aware that the ocean had had enough of other people’s debris.

“The truth is I was young and tired and sometimes resentful,” she said. “I wanted to be an artist and ended up being an accompaniment. I cared about them, some of them, and the rest I tolerated. The truth is I was also… flattered, and vain, and occasionally cruel in my head. That doesn’t fit nicely into their narratives.”

He heard, in her voice, the Margaret Benoit register he did not know by name but recognised by weight: the analytic self-indictment, the refusal to accept tidy absolution.

“You could write your own version,” he said. “Memoirs of a Cabaret Singer Who Was Not Your Mascot.”

She smiled, crooked.

“I write in other ways,” she said. “Tiny edits. Songs sung a beat too slow, jokes made at different expense. Quiet corrections. It’s… petty. It helps.”

The wind tugged at her shawl. He resisted the urge to adjust it. They were not that close.

“And what will you do with this new story of your brother?” she asked. “Will you treat it as gospel? Or as another draft?”

He thought about it.

“I don’t know,” he said. “I think I’m going to have to live with the fact that both are true in different ways. I didn’t kill him. I’ve lived as if I did. That’s… not nothing.”

“No,” she said. “It isn’t.”

He could feel her not touching him; the space between their shoulders was a deliberate act of politeness.

“Tell me about him,” she said quietly. “Not how he died. How he lived.”

He hesitated. The request felt like an intrusion and a gift at once.

“He was loud,” he said. “He thought he was funnier than he was. He could never accept losing, even at cards, which he was terrible at. He spoke German with our mother when he was trying to get away with something. He loved heights. He used to climb onto the roof of our building and pretend he could see across the Channel.”

“And you?” she asked. “Did you love heights?”

“I loved maps,” he said. “He wanted to jump off them. I wanted to draw them more accurately.”

She nodded, satisfied.

“There,” she said. “Now he’s not just a corpse in a story about your guilt.”

He swallowed.

“You’re very good at this,” he said.

“At what?” she asked.

“Interrogating without making it feel like an interrogation,” he said.

“Women had to learn useful skills,” she said. “We weren’t often given the rank to ask direct questions.”

The ship moved forward. Somewhere, in the dark under them, the Atlantic kept its own counsel.


## 8. Landfall

The morning they sighted New York, the passengers assembled on deck as if for a spectacle. Coats were buttoned, hats adjusted, children hoisted onto shoulders. The skyline rose out of the mist in stages: first a smudge, then lines, then the familiar shapes from newsreels and posters.

For many aboard, it was arrival at something they had been rehearsing in their heads for months. New starts, new jobs, new marriages, new chances to pretend the last decade had not happened.

Miles stood on the bridge, watching the harbour take shape through the windows. He gave the necessary orders, answered the captain’s clipped questions, relayed the pilot’s instructions. His hands moved with reassuring familiarity. His mind, unhelpfully, insisted on writing about it as if it were another chapter in a book he was both reading and trying to edit.

At the edge of his vision, the Statue of Liberty came into view. He had seen it enough times that the symbolism had worn thin, but the passengers reacted with the appropriate quota of awe. Several cried. He tried not to resent them their simple narratives.

When his shift ended, he went down to the main deck. It was important, he told himself, to be visible. To be part of the picture they would take home.

He found Lillian near the stern rail, away from the majority of the crowd. She wore a coat that had seen better tailoring, collar turned up against the wind. Her expression was difficult to read. Not awe. Not indifference, either. Something like a private argument.

“You’re not pushing to the front,” he said.

“I’ve seen postcards,” she said. “Besides, if I stand behind them, I get to watch their faces, which is better.”

He followed her gaze. A young couple with a baby. An older man in a worn overcoat, clutching a hat like a talisman. A woman in a fur stole that had survived the war more intact than most people.

“Do you ever get tired,” he asked, “of people thinking the arrival solves everything?”

“It’s necessary,” she said. “Without the illusion, they wouldn’t get on the boat. But yes. The problem with docks is they look like endings in photographs and beginnings in reality.”

He thought of all the arrivals the war had produced—troop ships, hospital ships, refugees. None of them had corresponded neatly to the posters.

“Will you stay in New York?” he asked.

She shook her head.

“No,” she said. “I’m on the return voyage. They like the continuity. Same singer both ways. Gives the brochures a sense of narrative closure.”

He tried, and failed, to imagine her settling in any one place now.

“Do you want to?” he asked.

She considered the question seriously, which he appreciated.

“I don’t know,” she said. “Sometimes I think about it. An apartment, a job in a bar or a small theatre. A life where the walls don’t move. Engaging in the radical act of buying furniture. Then I remember what it felt like the first time I left port after the raids and think, ‘No, thank you.’”

“So you keep moving,” he said.

“So I keep moving,” she said. “What about you? Any plans to stay on this side?”

“No,” he said. “I’ve grown attached to the channel fog.”

“It’s flattering light,” she said.

They watched the city loom closer. The harbour noise rose: horns, shouted instructions, the mechanical clatter of cranes. The smell of the sea shifted, taking on the tang of oil and industry.

“Will you tell your mother?” she asked, suddenly.

He did not ask what. There were only so many significant pronouns available.

“About Karl?” he said.

She nodded.

“She died last year,” he said. He had not meant for it to sound so simple.

“I’m sorry,” she said.

He shrugged.

“She spent the last years in a boarding house that smelled of boiled vegetables and disinfectant,” he said. “She forgot most everything except how to ask for tea and whether the windows were closed. I don’t know if this would have helped her.”

“It might have helped you to tell her,” she said.

“Perhaps,” he said. “Perhaps not. I told myself I was protecting her. It’s possible I was just protecting the story I’d written about my own terrible importance.”

She smiled, slowly.

“Self-importance is a sturdy lifeboat,” she said. “It leaks, but people cling to it.”

He thought of letters he had not written. Of conversations he had rehearsed and never had. Of the fact that the only person who had actually told him the truth about his brother was a stranger in a ship’s lounge.

“What will you do with it?” she asked again. “The information.”

He looked at the city ahead, at its promise of anonymity.

“I’ll try to remember that not everything revolves around my guilt,” he said. “And that some tragedies are banal. I don’t know if I’ll succeed.”

“That’s all right,” she said. “Most of us are works in progress.”


## 9. The Sea Between

Disembarkation was chaos in formal attire. The gangways were crowded, porters overwhelmed, customs officers attempting to look both efficient and welcoming. People said goodbye or pretended to. Addresses were exchanged with the casual fervour of those who would never write.

Lillian did not join the crush. She remained aboard, technically still on duty, watching from the upper deck as passengers became small figures on the dock.

Miles finished his part in the ritual—checking lists, answering last-minute questions, assuring a nervous man that the ship would not, in fact, leave before his trunks had been located—and made his way up.

He found her leaning on the rail, arms crossed, eyes narrowed against the light bouncing off the water.

“You don’t wave,” he observed.

“I’ve learned not to,” she said. “It encourages them.”

“Them?” he asked.

“The ones who cry and say dramatic things about never forgetting,” she said. “It’s very flattering until you realise you’re just the part of their holiday they can tell people about without sounding crass.”

“You’re very cynical,” he said.

“I’m very experienced,” she said. “Those two often look alike.”

He stood beside her. The harbour smell—diesel, tar, fish—wrapped around them.

“Do you regret it?” he asked. “The path you chose?”

“You mean singing for drunks and officers instead of marrying a greengrocer and having three children?” she said.

“Something like that,” he said.

She thought for a moment.

“Sometimes,” she said. “When I’m tired. When I think about old age and how it looks on women like me compared to women who chose… safer stories. But then I remember the way it felt, the first night I sang after a raid, the room full of people who shouldn’t have been alive and were, and how they looked at me like we were all getting away with something. I don’t think the greengrocer would have topped that.”

He tried to imagine her behind a produce stall in a bombed-out street. The image refused to cooperate.

“What about you?” she asked. “Do you regret taking to sea instead of staying in the air?”

He shook his head.

“No,” he said. “I’ve had enough of looking down at things I can’t fix.”

They fell quiet, watching the last passengers file down.

“Will you be on this run for long?” she asked.

“As long as they keep paying me,” he said. “As long as the charts don’t change too much.”

“You know they will,” she said. “Everything does.”

“I prefer it when the changes are slow enough to redraw on paper,” he said.

She smiled.

“Spoken like a man who trusts ink more than people,” she said.

“Ink doesn’t argue with you in the middle of the night,” he said.

“It does if you’re a songwriter,” she said.

On the dock, Vogel paused at the bottom of the gangway. He looked up, as if expecting to see someone. Miles raised a hand in a small, ambiguous gesture that could have been greeting or farewell. Vogel nodded once, then turned away, swallowed by the crowd.

“Is that him?” Lillian asked.

“Yes,” Miles said.

“Do you want to go down?” she asked.

He considered it. Considered walking up to Vogel in the noise and saying, “Thank you for dismantling the worst thing I believed about myself.” Considered the awkwardness, the intrusion, the risk that saying it aloud would make it too real.

“No,” he said, finally. “It’s enough that he said what he did. Not everything needs witnesses.”

She nodded.

“I find it’s the things without witnesses that cause the most trouble,” she said. “But it’s your ghost.”

The dockworkers shouted. Lines were cast off. The pilot boat nudged them from the berth. The *Aurelia* began to move again, slowly, like someone waking with reluctance.

“You’re not going ashore at all?” he asked.

“No,” she said. “I’ve seen New York. It’s loud and smells of ambition. I’m not in the mood.”

“You could find a new audience,” he said.

“I have one,” she said. “They’re trapped with me for another week. Low competition.”

He laughed.

“When we get back,” he said, “will you be on board?”

“If they haven’t discovered I can’t dance,” she said. “Yes.”

He leaned on the rail, not quite touching her.

“Then I’ll see you there,” he said.

It was not a promise, exactly. More an agreement to keep existing in the same moving box for a few more crossings, to carry on having appropriately oblique conversations about truth and story and the ways guilt reconfigured itself at sea.

As the city slipped away behind them and the open water widened, the space between them on the rail remained carefully measured. Too close would have invited complications neither of them had the appetite for. Too far would have acknowledged that they were only ever going in the same direction by accident.

The sea between where they had been and where they were going widened, deep and indifferent. Under its surface, wrecks from previous years lay quietly. Above it, the *Aurelia* traced a line that would be recorded in logbooks and forgotten by everyone who had not stood on her decks.


## 10. Years Later, a Letter

Years later—how many was it? Enough that fashions had cycled, that the music on the radio had changed tempo, that people spoke of “the war” with an air of indulgent exasperation, as if it were an elderly relative who refused to die on schedule—Miles received a letter.

It arrived at the flat he shared with a kitchen table and an accumulation of books. He had left the *Aurelia* by then, taken a shore job with the shipping company, sitting in an office that smelled of ink and cold tea, drawing routes for ships he would never see. His shoulders had learned, finally, how to slump without expecting an air raid.

The envelope bore a foreign stamp and his name, written in a hand that was unfamiliar but careful.

He opened it at the table, mug in hand, the afternoon light the colour of old paper.

Inside was a single sheet, typed, with one paragraph in German and one in English. It was from Vogel.

Dear Mr Ferguson,

We met some years ago on the *Aurelia*. I hope you remember. I am writing because I have recently been in contact with a former mechanic from our unit. He has confirmed what I told you then: your brother’s instruments were, indeed, faulty, and there was an investigation after his crash. I am enclosing a copy of the report, in case it is of use to you.

Below, in German, Vogel elaborated: times, dates, the mechanic’s testimony. The language was dry, official, full of phrases like “equipment failure” and “no pilot error detected.”

At the bottom, in English again, a single line:

I hope this helps you live a little more lightly.

The report itself was photocopied, grainy. It smelled faintly of toner and cigarette smoke. It reduced Karl’s life and death to sections and signatures. It was, in its own way, obscene.

Miles read it twice. The first time, he absorbed only the structure: headings, bullet points, the way bureaucracies tried to cage reality with Helvetica. The second time, he read the sentence that mattered: “The accident is attributed to instrument malfunction during night training. The pilot is absolved of responsibility.”

He put the paper down, fingers trembling.

Absolved of responsibility.

The phrase should have made everything easier. It did not. It raised, instead, the question of what you did with guilt when it had nowhere to live.

He had built himself, in the years since the war, around the idea that he might have done the worst thing a person could do unknowingly. He had atoned in small, private ways: double-checking routes, staying late to help junior officers, writing letters to families of crew who had died in accidents, even when the company had delegated that task to others. It had been, he admitted to himself now, a form of self-importance dressed up as penance.

If he was not, in fact, the accidental executioner of his brother, then what was all that seriousness for?

The question was ridiculous and essential.

He poured another cup of tea, because some rituals had to be honoured while dismantling others. The kettle’s whistle cut through his thoughts. The sound pulled him back to the small, present room, the table, the letter.

He thought of Lillian, on the *Aurelia*’s deck, saying that most of them were works in progress. He had not seen her in years. Their paths had diverged somewhere between Southampton and whatever came next. He imagined her in some other port, still singing, still adjusting narratives with small, precise cuts.

He sat at the table until the light shifted and the letter blurred. Then he did something he had not done when his mother died, when the telegram for Karl came, when the war ended.

He cried.

It was not cinematic. There were no sobs. Just tears that slid down his muzzle and into his fur and would not stop for a while. He let them. No one was watching. There was no audience to manage.

When he finished, he wiped his face with his handkerchief, blew his nose, and folded the letter back into its envelope. He put it on the mantel, propped against a photograph of the *Aurelia* that he kept there partly as memory and partly as evidence that a period of his life had not been invented.

He did not feel absolved. He felt, for the first time, accurately burdened.

There were still other ghosts: the unnamed men in cockpits he had lit up, the cities he had flown over, the civilians who had not made it to shelters. Those responsibilities did not evaporate because one brother’s death had been reclassified as a technical failure.

But Karl—loud, reckless, amphibious Karl—had died in a training accident because men in offices had signed off on instruments they should have checked more carefully. That was the story now. It was as banal and as terrible as most truths.

He picked up a pen.

Dear Mr Vogel,

Thank you for your letter and for the report enclosed. It was more useful than you perhaps know.

He hesitated, then added:

I hope you, too, are able to live more lightly.

He did not mention Lillian. He did not mention the songs she had sung, or the way she had insisted on turning Karl back into a person rather than a vector for guilt. That was a different story, one he was not ready to share in the language of correspondence.

He sealed the envelope, stuck on a stamp, and put it on the table by the door, ready to post in the morning.

The sea between his past and his present did not shrink. But as he looked at the grey sky outside the window and listened to the ordinary sounds of the building—footsteps on the stairs, a radio down the hall, someone banging a pot in the flat below—he thought, cautiously, that perhaps he could stop using it solely as a graveyard.

There were other things in it: routes, possibilities, ships carrying people who believed in postcards and those who didn’t. There had been a woman leaning on a rail, telling him that honesty was attractive to people who had lied to themselves for a living.

He put the kettle on again.

Some things, after all, remained.
